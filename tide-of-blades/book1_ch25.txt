CHAPTER TWENTY-FIVE
The Dockyard Afternoon



The fishmonger's boy was eating a meat pie when Zima took the filleting knife.

He sat on a barrel behind the stall with his legs crossed and the pie held in both hands, grease on his chin, attention on the food. The leather knife roll hung from a nail on the stall's back post — six hooks, four knives, the roll tied with a thong that had been retied so many times it barely counted as a knot. Zima walked past the post, pulled the thong, palmed the smallest knife, and was three stalls away before the boy's next bite.

The knife was good. Narrow blade, eight inches, the edge still sharp under a film of fish oil. She wiped it on the inside of her coat and slid it into the seam she kept along the collar. It sat against her neck like a cold finger.

The morning was bright for the first time in days — the fog had pulled back to the harbour mouth and the streets had the scrubbed, exposed quality of a city that had forgotten what sunlight looked like. People walked faster in the light. They held their parcels differently. The gangs were less visible when the fog wasn't there to blur their edges.

She ate nothing. The bread was gone. She had no coppers and the ration queue wasn't until the seventeenth hour. The hunger sat in her stomach like a stone — not sharp yet, not the kind that made your hands shake, but present. A reminder that the clock was running on every plan she'd made.

The dockyard at noon. She walked the long way, along the harbour wall and down the sea steps to the loading quay. The cranes stood in their row of four, their arms reaching over the empty water, cables hanging slack. No boats. No workers. The Hook boys who used the quay as a gathering spot were absent — she'd timed this correctly. The afternoon dead period.

She counted the windows that overlooked the quay. Eleven. Three were boarded. Five showed no movement behind the glass. The remaining three had curtains drawn, which meant either privacy or absence. She watched them for two hundred breaths. Nothing moved.

The fourth crane was her target. It stood at the far western end of the quay, partially screened from the street by a warehouse with a collapsed roof. The timber frame was twenty feet tall, weathered to the colour of old bone, the iron winch at its base seized with rust. The cable ran from the winch drum up through a pulley at the arm's tip and down to a hook that hung six feet off the ground. The cable itself was good — tarred hemp over a hemp core, thick as her thumb, the tar mostly intact despite the salt air and neglect.

She tested the frame. The timber was sound — the base was bolted to a stone platform, and the bolts held when she pulled. The cross-braces creaked but didn't shift. She'd climbed worse things in worse conditions. At nine years old Garrick had put her on the roof of the Salt Lane tenement to access a window that a grown man couldn't reach, and the drainpipe she'd climbed had been half-rotted and swaying in a harbour wind. This was solid by comparison.

She climbed. Hand over hand up the cross-braces, finding footholds in the joints where the horizontal timbers met the verticals. The wood was rough under her palms — splinters and salt crystals and the dried residue of tar. At the arm she straddled the beam and looked down.

The quay was empty. The harbour stretched out below her, flat and grey, the blockade ships motionless on the water. From this height she could see the pattern of the orc encampment in the hills — tents in ordered rows, firepit smoke rising in thin columns, the catapult emplacements along the ridgeline. Fifteen thousand soldiers. She counted the catapults instead: seven. The same seven she'd counted from the harbour wall, but from this angle she could see the eighth, partially hidden behind a fold in the terrain. Eight catapults. She filed it.

The cable.

She drew the filleting knife from her collar seam and began cutting. The tarred outer layer parted easily — the knife was sharp and the tar was brittle from years of exposure. Beneath it the hemp core was dense and tight, the fibres packed so close they resisted the blade. She sawed. The motion was awkward — sitting on the beam with her legs wrapped around it, one hand on the cable and the other on the knife, cutting at an angle that strained her shoulder.

The first strand parted. Then the second. The cable was three-strand construction, each strand as thick as her little finger. She cut through the first section — two feet of cable, freed from the drum — and coiled it over her shoulder. Then the next section. And the next.

The work was slow. Each two-foot section took roughly forty breaths to cut — the hemp resisted, the knife slipped on the tar, the angle was wrong. She adjusted. Moved further along the arm, found a position where she could brace her back against the pulley housing and pull the cable toward her instead of reaching for it. Better. Thirty breaths per section.

She needed fifty feet. Twenty-five sections. At thirty breaths each, that was seven hundred and fifty breaths. Fifty minutes. She had three hours.

She cut. The coil on her shoulder grew heavier. The cable's weight pulled at her balance on the beam. After the tenth section she climbed down, dropped the coil on the quay, and climbed back up. Her palms were bleeding — the tar and the hemp fibres had worn through the skin at the base of her fingers, and the filleting knife's handle was slippery with it. She tore a strip from the hem of her shirt and wrapped her right hand. Cut with the wrapped hand. The grip was worse but the pain was manageable.

Fifteen sections. Twenty. The cable on the quay was a substantial pile now — more rope than she'd ever owned, more than she'd ever touched in one piece. She was on the twenty-third section when she heard the sound.

*          *          *

Below her. Inside the warehouse with the collapsed roof.

Scraping. Metal on stone. Not the sound of rats — too heavy, too deliberate, with a rhythm that suggested something large moving slowly across a hard floor. Then a second sound: a thud, like a body settling against a wall. Then silence.

Zima stopped cutting. She held the knife still and listened. The harbour wind pushed through the crane's frame, making the timber creak. The pulleys above her clicked. She breathed through her mouth to quiet the sound and counted.

Twenty breaths. Nothing.

Twenty more. The scraping came again — shorter this time, followed by a sound she couldn't identify. Fabric on stone, maybe. Or skin.

She looked down at the warehouse. The collapsed roof had left a gap in the eastern wall where the timbers had fallen outward, creating an opening large enough for a person to walk through. The interior was dark. From the crane arm she could see the first few feet of floor — stone, cracked, littered with roof debris and the grey dust of old mortar.

Something moved in the dark. A shape. Large — much larger than a person. The shape shifted and settled, and the scraping sound came again, and Zima understood. Not metal on stone. Bare feet on stone. Very large bare feet.

She finished the last two sections of cable quickly, not caring about noise now. The coil on the quay was sixty feet — more than she needed. She climbed down, gathered the cable into a rough bundle, and carried it to the warehouse wall where the collapsed roof created an overhang. She set it down in the shadow and stood still.

The sounds from inside had stopped. The silence was deliberate — the silence of something that had heard her and was waiting.

She could leave. The rope was cut, the mission was done, and whatever was inside the warehouse was not her concern. She had a drain cover to open and a vault to find and a debt that was ticking over at a rate that would reach Garrick's patience well before it reached zero. The smart thing — the Garrick-trained thing — was to take the rope and go.

She stepped through the gap in the wall.

The warehouse interior was larger than it looked from outside. The collapsed roof had taken down the upper floor, leaving a single open space the size of a market hall. Dust and light filtered through the broken timbers overhead, falling in columns that cut the darkness into sections. The floor was covered in debris — broken wood, masonry fragments, the rusted remains of cargo hooks and chain. Against the far wall, where the shadows were deepest, something sat.

It was too dark to see clearly. She stood inside the gap and let her eyes adjust. The shape resolved slowly — edges first, then mass, then detail.

Large. Very large. Sitting against the wall with its knees drawn up, its back straight, its head turned toward her.

Two points of light in the dark. Eyes. Amber-coloured, catching the daylight that came through the roof and holding it.

She didn't move. She counted. Her hand was on the filleting knife in her collar seam, but the knife was eight inches long and whatever was sitting against that wall was seven feet tall at least, and eight inches of steel against seven feet of anything was arithmetic that didn't favour her.

The eyes watched. They didn't blink. They didn't look away.

Zima took one step forward. The light from the roof gap fell across the figure, and she saw grey-green skin and the scarred, shaved dome of a skull and a jaw that was wider than a human jaw and a body that was built for a different scale of violence than anything Ashport's gangs could produce.

An orc.

She took a breath and held it and took another step.

He didn't move. He sat against the wall with the patience of a stone, watching her approach the way she watched marks in the ration queue — with calculation, not urgency. His clothes were military — a leather vest, heavy trousers, the kind of belt that held a sword harness but bore no sword. The insignia on the vest had been cut away. Clean cuts, deliberate. Not torn in a fight. Removed on purpose.

No boots. Bare feet on the stone floor, which explained the scraping sound. His feet were enormous — flat, wide, calloused to the consistency of leather.

A clay jug sat beside him. A bedroll. Nothing else.

She stopped at twelve feet. Close enough to see his face in the filtered light. Far enough to turn and run if she had to. She looked at him and he looked at her and the warehouse held its silence like a cupped hand holding water.

"This is a strange place to sleep," Zima said.

Nothing. No response. The amber eyes stayed on her, steady and unreadable.

"You're from the army." She nodded at the cut insignia. "And you're not with them anymore."

The eyes tightened. The first reaction — a contraction around the sockets that could have been anger or recognition or the effort of not showing either.

"Can you speak?"

He opened his mouth.

The light caught it. The cavity where a tongue should have been — dark, healed, the stub of the root visible at the back of the throat. Not a wound. A cut. Deliberate and precise, the kind of surgery that was done to prisoners or deserters or whoever the orc army decided should be alive but silent.

His mouth closed. The amber eyes didn't waver.

Zima stood in the broken warehouse with the rope coiled behind her and the disc warm against her ribs and looked at a seven-foot orc deserter who couldn't speak and who had been living in the only part of the dock district that nobody else visited.

She should leave. The rope was cut. The afternoon was burning. She had a drain cover and a vault and a debt and none of those things involved an orc sitting in a warehouse with no tongue and no boots and no weapon.

"I'll come back tomorrow," she said.

She didn't know why she said it. The words came out before the calculation caught them, the way her hand had closed on the courier's disc before thought caught action. Reflex. Or something deeper than reflex — the part of her that had slept under a bridge for six months and understood what it looked like when someone was surviving alone in a city that didn't want them.

She picked up the rope from the overhang shadow, slung it over her shoulder, and walked out through the gap in the wall. She didn't look back. She carried the bundle through the dock district by the alley route — quieter, longer, away from the patrol paths — and stashed it in the space beneath the Coppergate bridge where she'd slept before the tanner's loft, in the gap behind the stone abutment where the cats used to gather.

The filleting knife went back into her collar seam. Her hands stung where the tar and hemp had worn them raw. She washed them in the harbour shallows, the saltwater biting into the abrasions, and wrapped them in strips torn from the same hem she'd used on the crane.

The disc pulsed. Steady. Warm.

She'd found rope. She'd found something else. She didn't know yet what the something else was worth, but she knew what seven feet of muscle and no other options looked like, and she knew what partnership meant in a city where everyone who survived was surviving alone.

Tomorrow. Bread for the orc. And then the bakery, for Lirra's patrol map.

One thing at a time.
