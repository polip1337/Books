CHAPTER SIX
The Ashford Market



The Ashford Market opened at dawn and by the time Zima got there it had been running for an hour and had already developed the particular layered chaos that made it both harder and more profitable than the ration queues.

The market filled a cobblestoned square that had been a cattle yard before the siege and was now the city's largest trading space — roughly eighty marked pitches, sixty-odd of them occupied, arranged in districts that had formed through habit rather than planning, the way paths form through grass when enough people walk the same route. Food at the centre, because food drew the densest traffic and the traders who sold it wanted to be where the feet were. Cloth and tools along the eastern edge, where the morning light was strongest and you could check a weave or a blade's temper without squinting. Medicine on the north side, where the drainage was poorest and the smell from the old canal kept the pitch prices low enough for traders who couldn't afford prime ground. And through all of it, around it, the crowd — three hundred people at peak, fewer now as the siege thinned everything, moving in currents that eddied around stalls and pooled at corners and flowed in patterns that changed by the hour depending on what was for sale, who was selling, and which parts of the square the gangs had claimed that morning.

She bought a bread heel from a stall near the entrance. Two coppers. The baker didn't look at her. He was counting his remaining stock with the expression of a man doing mathematics he didn't enjoy. She ate standing in the shadow of the old tannery wall, watching the crowd the way she watched all crowds: for shape, for rhythm, for the places where attention gathered and the places where it didn't.

Market days were different from ration-queue days. The queue was a fixed line — everyone facing the same direction, moving at the same speed, held in place by the simple geometry of a single point of distribution. You read it from the back and you worked it front to back and the variables were limited. The market was fluid. People eddied, reversed, stopped suddenly to argue prices, drifted sideways to examine something they hadn't planned to examine. The whole mass moved in loose currents that shifted when a stall dropped its prices or a new cart arrived or a fight broke out three rows over. Reading the market was harder. You couldn't predict it. You had to feel it — the density, the direction, where money pooled and where it flowed. But it paid better, because market people had come to spend, and people who'd come to spend carried their coins where spending put them: in outside pockets, in open pouches, in hands that were busy holding goods and not watching what else their bodies were doing.

She spent the first twenty minutes reading.

*          *          *

The Viper presence was concentrated around the food stalls in the centre. Two men at the southern entrance, arms folded, watching the crowd with the flat expression of men who were being paid to stand and had opinions about it. One more leaning against a post near the grain seller, chewing something, his eyes doing steady circuits of the nearby rows. A fourth walking the eastern aisle with the studied casualness of a man who wanted to look idle and wasn't. They wore the muted greys and charcoals the Vipers preferred — nothing conspicuous, no marks, but the collars had a cut to them that was recognisable if you knew what to look for. Zima knew.

On the western side, the Hook boys held the corners. Three of them clustered near the old brick archway that framed the entrance to Fishmonger's Lane — the lane had flooded in the sixth month and the city had sealed it with planking, and the archway had become a landmark, the western border of Hook territory in the market. Their mark was visible on the brickwork, the curved hook scratched in charcoal, fresh over the faded remnants of last month's mark. The boys had the posture of territory-holders: feet planted, shoulders angled outward, heads turning to track anyone who passed within ten feet. They were young. The Hooks recruited young, younger than the Vipers, and the boys at the arch had an energy that said they hadn't yet learned that standing still was harder than it looked.

Between the Viper stalls and the Hook corners lay the buffer zone. Three rows of independent stalls that belonged to neither side — the chandler, a cobbler, two food sellers with carts, and the medicine stalls at the northern edge. Neutral ground. The traders there operated under an understanding that both gangs tolerated their presence because the buffer served a purpose: it kept the two sides from touching. As long as the gap existed, the Vipers and the Hooks could work the same market without the friction that proximity produced, and the independent traders paid for their safety by being useful as a space between. It was not a comfortable arrangement. It was the kind of arrangement that worked until it didn't, and the traders in the buffer zone knew this, and you could see it in the way they set up their stalls each morning with the careful, provisional air of people building something they might have to abandon by noon.

Three rows. It had been four rows three weeks ago. The buffer was thinning.

She finished her bread. Wiped her hands on her coat. Started working.

*          *          *

The spice merchant's apprentice was her first take.

He was young — sixteen, maybe seventeen — with the soft hands of someone who hadn't worked outdoors and the distracted air of someone who'd rather be anywhere else. He stood behind his master's stall arranging jars of dried herbs while his master was two stalls over in conversation with another trader, and on the crate beside him was a cloth pouch, half-open, set down while he argued with a woman over the price of rosemary. The woman was insisting the rosemary was stale, which it was — Zima could smell it from here, the faded, papery smell of herbs that had been dried too long. The argument had his full attention. Both hands on the counter. Eyes on the woman. The pouch was within arm's reach but it wasn't within his awareness, and the gap between the two was where Zima worked.

She moved past the stall in the current of the crowd. Didn't slow. Her left hand dipped to the crate, found the pouch by touch, closed on it, lifted it in one continuous motion that was part of the same forward movement — hand out, hand in, the pouch gone from the crate to her coat pocket in the time it took to take two steps. She didn't look back. The boy was still explaining that the rosemary was perfectly fresh.

The second take was the man examining boots.

He was at the cobbler's stall in the buffer zone, holding up a pair of heavy work boots, turning them this way and that, checking the soles, flexing the leather the way men flexed leather when they wanted to look like they knew what they were checking for. His coat was open and the pocket on his right side was gaping — the pocket mouth stretched from carrying too many heavy things for too many months, the fabric permanently distended. Three coppers sat at the top of the pocket on a fold of cloth, visible if you were the kind of person who looked at other people's pockets, which Zima was.

She timed it to his reach. When he lifted the boots higher, turning them to catch the morning light, his right arm rose and his body turned left, and the gaping pocket swung toward her. She walked past, shoulder to shoulder with the crowd, and her right hand went in and out in a motion so brief that her own fingers barely registered the contact. Three coppers. Clean.

She circled the market. Let the crowd carry her through the food district and out past the cloth stalls and back around toward the north side, moving with the current, part of the traffic, a body among bodies. On the return pass, near the chandler's stall, something glinted between two paving stones. A half-silver. Dropped, not placed — it sat in the groove of the mortar, half-covered by a piece of straw from someone's basket. She stepped on it, felt it under her boot, and crouched to adjust her bootlace. When she stood, the half-silver was in her palm.

Not theft, strictly. Salvage. The distinction mattered to the part of her that still kept track of such things, which was a smaller part than it used to be and shrank a little more each month.

*          *          *

The chandler was an old woman named Britta, though nobody used the name. She was the chandler the way the harbour was the harbour — a fixture, an institution, a thing that had been there so long it had become its own category. She sold candles made from rendered fat she collected from the butcher's scrap barrels and whatever other animal waste she could source, which meant the candles smelled like old meat when they burned and produced a thin, yellowish light that was barely better than darkness but was, crucially, not darkness, and in a city where the lamp oil was running out the difference between barely-better-than-darkness and actual darkness was worth four coppers per candle, which was twice what they'd cost before the siege and half what they'd cost next month if the oil kept disappearing.

She sat behind her stall with the patience of a woman who understood that she sold the one commodity a besieged city would never stop needing. Her eyes were sharp and they tracked the space around her goods with the territorial precision of a raptor watching a field. Zima had never tried to steal from the chandler. Not from principle — from professional respect. Some targets were marks. The chandler was a fortress.

Past the chandler, on the north edge, the medicine stalls ran in a row of four. Two were Viper fronts — they sold at prices Garrick's network set, the stock came through Viper supply chains, and the men behind the tables had the flat expression of employees who didn't care about the product because the profit went elsewhere. The third was empty today, the trader either sick or gone. The fourth was Cael's.

Cael answered to nobody. That was the fact about her that Zima had noticed first, six months ago, and it was still the most interesting one. In a market where every stall existed by permission — the Vipers' permission, the Hooks' permission, the grudging mutual tolerance of two armed factions who could shut the whole thing down in an afternoon if they chose to — Cael operated in a pocket of her own making. Her stall was orderly, her stock varied, her prices fair. She mixed her own compounds, working with mortar and pestle behind the counter with the unhurried focus of someone who knew exactly what each ingredient did and didn't need to consult a recipe. Zima suspected she was better than she let on. Considerably better. The kind of better that you hid in Ashport because competence attracted attention, and attention attracted the gangs, and the gangs attracted obligations she apparently had no interest in carrying.

Today the medicine stalls were telling a story that Zima read the way she read everything: looking for what had changed.

Three months ago the stock had been mostly tonics — liquid remedies in stoppered bottles, treatments for fever, for gut complaints, for the low-grade persistent ailments of a population that wasn't eating enough and sleeping worse. Tonics were for illness. Illness was a slow problem, the kind the body produced when you gave it bad inputs for long enough. You treated it with liquids and time.

The stalls had shifted. The tonics were still there, pushed to the back, but the front displays were dominated by poultices — thick pastes spread on cloth and folded into packets, designed for wounds. Not knife wounds, not the clean damage of a blade fight gone wrong. These were poultices for crushing injuries. For compressive trauma. The kind of damage that happened when masonry fell on you, or a wall section gave way, or the ceiling of the building you'd been sleeping in since the siege began decided it was done holding the weight and came down on your body at three in the morning.

The city was breaking. The catapult strikes from the early months had weakened structures that Ashport couldn't afford to repair, and eleven months of deferred maintenance and dwindling labour had done the rest. Walls cracked. Floors sagged. Roofbeams rotted in the salt air and nobody replaced them because the timber was needed for the harbour defences and the harbour defences were needed because the orc warships sat past the breakwater and occasionally lobbed something at the dock district to remind everyone they were still there. The injuries were getting dirtier because rubble wounds were dirty by nature — stone dust in the flesh, mortar fragments, the ground-in filth of a city that had stopped cleaning itself. The poultices were the market's way of documenting a problem that nobody with authority was addressing: the city was coming apart, one building at a time, and the people inside the buildings were absorbing the damage.

She stood at the edge of the medicine row and did the calculation she always did: what does this mean for me? More injuries meant more distracted people. More distracted people meant easier marks. But it also meant more desperate people, and desperate people were volatile — quicker to anger, quicker to violence, quicker to notice a hand in a pocket and respond with a fist or a blade. The two effects didn't cancel each other. They layered.

*          *          *

She worked the midday crowd and added to her take. Two more coppers from a woman's basket in the food district — the woman had stopped to talk to someone she knew, and the conversation had her full attention, and the two coppers sitting on top of a folded cloth in the basket's open mouth were barely even a challenge. She also sold the apothecary paper from yesterday — three coppers, to a man near the grain seller who listened to her description of the order and nodded and paid without haggling, which meant the information was worth more than three coppers and she'd underpriced it. She filed that. Next time, four.

By early afternoon the day's total was twelve coppers and a half-silver in new takes, plus the three coppers from the paper. Fifteen coppers and a half-silver gained. Added to what she'd had, that put her roughly where she'd been yesterday but with the clock one day shorter, which was the particular mathematics of Ashport: you ran to stay in place and you stayed in place to avoid falling behind and you fell behind anyway because the ground was moving.

She sat on the low wall at the market's eastern edge and watched the square. Sixty-three stalls she could count from here. Two months ago there had been seventy-one. The attrition was steady — one or two stalls gone each market day, the traders behind them absorbed into the city's thinning population, their absence filled by the crowd closing the gap the way water closes around a stone pulled from a stream.

The buffer zone. Three rows. Eleven stalls. She counted them to be sure, because being sure was what she did. Three rows, same as last week. The Hooks hadn't pushed further east. The Vipers hadn't pushed further west. The independent traders sat in between and sold their goods and the arrangement held because everyone needed it to hold, because the market was the last place in Ashport where the city still worked as a city — a place where strangers came together and exchanged things of value and walked away with what they needed, and if that stopped, then what remained wasn't a city anymore. It was a pen. A holding space. A collection of people trapped inside the same walls with nothing between them but hunger and the geography of who had a blade and who didn't.

The afternoon turned grey. The fog built at the harbour's edge, long banks of it rolling in over the breakwater. The crowd thinned as it always did past midday — the serious buyers had bought, the browsers had browsed, and what was left was the residue of a market day winding down, the stall holders beginning to pack, the last transactions of the afternoon conducted with the weary efficiency of people who'd been standing since dawn.

She climbed down from the wall. Pocketed her count. Walked home through the thinning crowd with the day's take distributed across three pockets and the familiar, low-grade dissatisfaction of a day that had been productive but not productive enough.

Behind her the market went on buying and selling and being the last working thing in a city that was running out of working things. The chandler sat behind her candles. Cael's stall was being tidied, the poultice packets arranged in neat rows for tomorrow. The spice apprentice's master had returned and was berating the boy for something — the missing pouch, probably, or the rosemary argument, or both. The cobbler was packing up his boots. The food sellers were covering their remaining stock with cloth. The Viper men at the southern entrance were changing shifts, one pair arriving as another left, the transition smooth and professional and continuous, the machinery of control operating the way machinery operates — without pause, without reflection, without caring whether the people inside it wanted it to stop.

The buffer zone held. The stalls were open. The market worked.

She walked through the market and it was whole. The buffer held. The chandler overcharged for her fat candles. The spice boy argued about rosemary and lost. The whole fragile, ordinary, unsustainable arrangement kept turning because nobody had yet told it to stop.

She walked south through the fog. The drums started — three slow beats from the hills, heavy and patient, then the silence, then the seven fast ones rolling through the thickening air. She listened to them and kept walking and the market was behind her, still open, still functioning, still pretending it would be there tomorrow exactly as it was today.

It wouldn't. But that was tomorrow's knowledge, and tonight she had coins to count and a bridge to sleep under and an arithmetic problem that was not going to solve itself, and the fog came in, and the drums fell silent, and the last normal day ended the way all the normal days had ended — quietly, unremarkably, with no sign on it that said: remember this.
