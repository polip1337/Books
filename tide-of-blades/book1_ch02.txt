CHAPTER TWO
The Ledger



Her spot was under the Coppergate bridge, in the alcove where the drainpipe met the foundation wall.

She'd found it six months ago, in the fifth month of the siege, when the tanner's loft where she'd been sleeping became too crowded and too watched. The loft had been safe enough for a while — Brennick the tanner charged two coppers a night and didn't ask questions and didn't let anyone in after dark. But the fifth month was when the Hooks started expanding west, and expansion meant scouts, and scouts meant strangers walking through neighbourhoods where strangers got noticed. Brennick's loft was three streets from Hook territory. Two of the rough-sleepers had disappeared in the same week — not dead, probably, just gone somewhere safer or less safe depending on how you measured. Zima had measured by counting the new faces in the district and finding three she didn't recognise, and she'd left the loft that night with everything she owned, which fit in her coat pockets.

The Coppergate bridge crossed a drainage channel that ran from the old tannery district down to the harbour. In dry weather the channel carried a trickle of rust-coloured water that smelled of chemicals and dead things. In wet weather it carried the same trickle plus whatever the rain washed off the streets, which was worse. The bridge was stone, old enough that nobody remembered who'd built it, with three arches and a road surface that had been patched so many times the patches had patches. Under the second arch, on the south side, a drainpipe came out of the foundation wall at chest height and left a gap between itself and the stone — narrow, maybe three feet wide and five feet deep, sheltered from rain on three sides and from view on all four if you knew how to climb in.

She'd found it by accident. She'd been walking the drainage channel looking for somewhere to sleep and she'd noticed the drainpipe and the gap behind it and she'd thought: that's a terrible place to sleep. And then she'd thought about it more and decided that a terrible place to sleep that nobody else wanted was better than a good place to sleep that someone might take from her. She'd climbed in that night with her coat pulled tight and her back against cold stone and she'd slept badly and woken stiff and stayed anyway, because the gap was invisible from the bridge road and invisible from the channel unless you knew exactly where to look, and invisibility was the only luxury that mattered.

Six months later it was still terrible. Cramped and damp and it smelled of low tide and old mortar and whatever came through the drainpipe on wet nights, which she'd stopped trying to identify. But it was hers. Not in any way that mattered legally — nothing in Ashport belonged to anyone legally anymore, not since the garrison stopped enforcing property claims in the seventh month. Hers in the only way that counted: she slept there and nobody disturbed her and when she left in the morning her things were still there when she came back.

That last part was the expensive part. Three people knew about the alcove, and she paid each of them two coppers a month to forget it existed.

Brennick the tanner was the first. He'd seen her leave the loft with her pockets full and her eyes checking corners, and he'd followed her — not out of malice, but because Brennick was curious about everything and discreet about nothing until you gave him a reason. She'd caught him watching from the bridge two mornings later and she'd gone up to him and said: two coppers a month and you never saw this place. He'd taken the coppers and he'd kept quiet, and she believed he would continue keeping quiet because Brennick understood that information was a commodity and a commodity you'd already sold lost its value if you gave it away for free.

The second was a girl named Maren, thirteen or fourteen, one of the bridge-kids who slept in the archways on the north side. Maren had sharp eyes and no loyalty to anyone, which made her dangerous and predictable in roughly equal measure. She'd spotted Zima climbing out of the alcove one morning and had stood on the bridge railing looking down with the expression of someone calculating a price. Zima gave her two coppers and a warning: if someone comes to this alcove who isn't me, I'll know who sent them. Maren had nodded, pocketed the coppers, and never mentioned it again. Zima checked the alcove for signs of entry every time she came back, and so far the only visitors had been the cats.

The third was a man called Docent, which wasn't his real name. He was old, maybe sixty, and he sat at the north end of the Coppergate bridge most days with a bowl and a piece of cardboard that said VETERAN in smudged charcoal, and he saw everything that happened on or near the bridge because that's what you did when you had nothing else — you watched. Zima had approached him with two coppers and a bowl of the thin fish soup from the dock stalls. He'd taken both. He'd said, "Girl, I don't remember things unless someone pays me to." She'd taken that as agreement.

Six coppers a month. It was the most expensive thing she owned, and there was nothing to show for it except the continued absence of other people in her alcove, which was not the kind of value you could hold in your hand but was the kind that let you sleep.

*          *          *

She sat with her back against the stone and counted the day's take.

The labourer's purse: nine coppers, one half-silver. Better than she'd guessed. She lined them up on the flat stone beside her knee — coppers in a row, the half-silver by itself, catching the faint grey light that came through the gap between the drainpipe and the wall.

The man in the wool coat: four coppers, the useless key she'd already ditched.

The market woman: six coppers, a brass button, and a folded square of paper with an address on Cutter Street that she couldn't read in this light. She kept the paper. Addresses were sometimes worth something.

Total: nineteen coppers and a half-silver. She picked up each coin and felt its weight and its edges and put it down and picked up the next one, because counting by feel was the only kind of counting that worked in the dark under a bridge. The coppers were mostly genuine — two were clipped, which meant someone had been shaving them, and one had a soft edge that might mean it was base metal under a copper wash. She put the questionable ones in a separate pile. Fifteen good coppers, four suspect, and the half-silver.

Plus the eight coppers and two bent silvers she'd had from yesterday. The bent silvers wouldn't pass at face value — a good fence would take them at maybe sixty percent, a bad one at forty, and the ration table guards wouldn't take them at all. Call it one silver's worth if she was lucky. She counted it at face because Garrick's ledger didn't distinguish — a silver was a silver, bent or not. That made twenty-seven coppers, twenty-three good, four bad, and roughly two and a half silvers.

Garrick's three-week tithe came to roughly nine silvers. She had two and a half.

She looked at the coins on the stone beside her knee and did the arithmetic she'd been doing every night for three weeks. Nine minus two and a half was six and a half. Six and a half silvers was sixty-five coppers at the official exchange rate, more like seventy or seventy-five at the rate the fences actually paid. She averaged maybe fifteen good coppers a day from the queues on a good day, and a bad day was closer to eight. Call it twelve. But she needed to eat. She needed to pay her silence-coin. She needed to replace the ration tokens that expired at the end of the week. After all of that she kept maybe five coppers a day toward the debt.

Seventy-five divided by five was fifteen days. She didn't have fifteen days. She'd known this for about a week. She kept doing the arithmetic anyway because the arithmetic was something to do with the fear, and doing something with the fear was better than sitting with it.

*          *          *

She'd been running the queues since twelve, taught by a man named Garrick who ran the Ash Vipers and who had found her sleeping in a doorway on Wick Street three years before that.

She'd been nine. She'd been starving — not the slow, grinding hunger that everyone in Ashport knew now, but the clean kind, the kind with an edge, the kind that happened to children who had no one and no system and no idea what came next. She remembered the doorway on Wick Street with a clarity that ten years hadn't dulled. She remembered the cold. She remembered the stone step pressing into her hip bones where there wasn't enough of her to cushion the contact. She remembered the smell of the street — piss, cooking fat, the sourness of a drain that hadn't been cleaned since before she was born. And she remembered Garrick's boots first, good leather, clean, which was unusual on Wick Street, and then his voice, which was calm and certain in the way that certain voices are when they belong to people who've already decided what's going to happen next.

Come with me. I'll feed you.

She'd gone, because that was not a complicated decision when you were nine and the alternative was the doorway. He'd fed her. Bread and fish stew, in a room at the back of a warehouse near the docks that was warm and smelled of tar and money — actual money, copper and silver and the particular mineral tang of coins kept in quantities large enough to have their own atmosphere. She'd eaten until she was sick and then she'd slept on a straw pallet against the wall, and in the morning she'd woken up and Garrick had been sitting at a table across the room, writing in a book.

That was the ledger. She didn't know it then.

The warehouse was Garrick's counting house, the place where the Vipers' money moved through on its way from the streets to wherever Garrick kept it. She'd slept there for two years on the pallet that smelled of tar and tallow and copper dust, and during those two years she'd learned the shape of the system she was inside without understanding that it was a system at all. There were other children — five or six at any given time. They came and went. Some she remembered. A boy named Pike who was quick with his hands and quicker with his feet, gone after eight months when he ran. Nobody said where. A girl named Tulla who never spoke and who worked the markets until she caught the flux and died in the back room while Garrick's men carried on counting money on the other side of the wall. The counting didn't stop. It never stopped. The counting was the point.

The food was real. The roof was real. The debt was real, from the first day, written in the ledger in Garrick's precise, slanted hand. Room: two coppers a night. Food: three coppers a day. Clothing, when he gave her a coat in the first winter: one silver. Training, when she started learning the queues at twelve: one silver per month, for the privilege of being taught by Garrick's best table-man, a fingersmith named Vetch who had eight digits and should have had ten but had been careless once and the two stumps served as his most persuasive lesson. Protection, for the privilege of working Garrick's territory without interference from other Vipers: half a silver per month. Interest on the balance: ten percent per month, calculated on the last day, added to the principal.

She'd been twelve when she first understood the ledger. Garrick had shown it to her — held it open across the table, turned it so she could read the column of numbers next to her name. She'd learned numbers before letters, because numbers were useful on the street and letters were not, and so she could read the amounts clearly enough. What she couldn't read was the mechanism. The way interest compounded. The way charges for room and food and protection grew faster than anything she could earn. The way the numbers in the right-hand column climbed month after month after month, and the numbers in the left-hand column — payments, her payments, the coppers she brought in from the queues and poured into the machine — never climbed fast enough.

She'd asked to hold the ledger once. Garrick had closed the book and put it in the drawer and locked the drawer and said, "When your balance is zero, you can hold whatever you like." He'd smiled when he said it. Garrick smiled the way other people used punctuation — to mark the end of a statement that did not require a response.

By fifteen she owed enough that walking away was the same thing as running, and running from Garrick was the same thing as bleeding, slowly, in an alley somewhere, while one of his boys went through your pockets for the repayment. She'd seen it happen. A man named Creel who'd owed three silvers and who'd tried to leave through the sewer exits at the coast. They'd found him two days later and brought him back, and what happened after that happened in the back room of the warehouse. Zima had been sleeping on the pallet on the other side of the wall. She'd heard enough to understand that three silvers was not worth what Creel had paid instead.

She was nineteen now. She'd been off the pallet for four years — Garrick didn't house his workers once they were old enough to fend, because housing cost money and the cost went into the ledger and the ledger was supposed to grow, not shrink. She'd slept in alleyways, in the tanner's loft, in abandoned shops, and now under a bridge, and each move was a step down that she'd told herself was a step sideways, because sideways was a direction you could survive and down was a direction you couldn't.

The tithe was sixty percent of her take, paid weekly, with the remaining forty notionally hers except that hers meant hers minus living expenses, minus interest, minus whatever adjustments Garrick decided to apply. The adjustments were the mechanism within the mechanism, the part that made the system truly inescapable. Last month he'd added a charge for territory maintenance — half a silver. The month before, a fee for ledger administration — another half. She'd asked what ledger administration meant and the man she'd asked, one of Garrick's table-workers named Dust, had looked at her with something close to pity and said, "It means he hasn't forgotten about you."

The tithe. The interest. The fees. The adjustments. Nine silvers this month. Maybe ten next month. Maybe twelve the month after. The ledger was a machine designed to produce debt, and the debt was a machine designed to produce compliance. Not the money — the money was small, the earnings of pickpockets and street-runners, barely worth collecting on its own. The real product was the network: a hundred people who owed Garrick and who therefore did what he said and went where he sent them and did not leave. Because leaving was Creel in the back room. Because the ledger was always heavier than the door.

*          *          *

The orc warships sat out past the breakwater — she couldn't see them in the fog but she could feel them, or imagined she could, the way you feel a hand near your face in the dark. They'd been there for eleven months. The fishing boats went out at night through the gaps in the blockade and brought back enough to keep the city technically alive, and the orc ships let them. Or couldn't stop all of them. Or didn't care. She'd spent a long time thinking about which of those it was.

The cats came. Two of them, lean harbour toms with notched ears and the suspicious, wary expressions of animals who'd survived eleven months of siege by being smarter than the average citizen. They'd learned that this alcove occasionally produced someone who'd share food. She broke off a corner of the stale bread she'd been carrying in her coat pocket since morning and set it on the stone. The cats took it without gratitude, which she respected. She had never seen a cat pretend to be something it wasn't.

One of them — the grey one, the bigger one, with a scar across its nose that ran from the bridge to the right nostril — settled beside her feet and began to wash itself. The other took its bread to the far side of the alcove and ate in the dark. She didn't name them. Naming things was an attachment, and attachments in Ashport had a way of disappearing. You came to rely on a face in the queue, a stall at the market, a route through the streets that was safe, and then the face was gone and the stall was closed and the route had been claimed by one gang or the other, and the space where the attachment had been was just another gap in the architecture of daily life.

She thought about the doorway people. She'd passed six of them on the walk from the ration queue — six people sitting in doorways with their knees drawn up and their faces empty, not sleeping, not awake, occupying space the way stones occupy space. She'd recognised two of them. The man outside the boarded cobbler's shop had been a dockworker three months ago; she'd lifted a purse from him in the eighth month and he'd had decent coin. The woman in the customs house entrance had been a market regular, basket and all, maybe as recently as last month. Now they sat and they didn't check their pockets because there was nothing in them and they didn't watch the ration queue because the queue required a token and getting a token required registering with the garrison and the garrison had stopped registering new names in the ninth month.

The doorway people were what happened when the system stopped pretending to include you. They were the remainder — the part left over after the gangs had their cut and the garrison had its cut and the merchants had their cut and the food was distributed to the people who still had tokens, and whatever was left, which was nothing, went to the doorways. They sat with their backs against other people's buildings and their eyes open and their mouths closed, and the city moved around them the way water moves around a rock — without acknowledgment, without effort, as though they were a feature of the geography.

She wasn't one of them. She kept telling herself that. She still worked. She still planned. She still counted her coppers every night and did the arithmetic and arrived at the same answer and got up the next morning and did it again. That was the difference — the doing, the repetition, the insistence on continuing to function even when the function produced nothing sufficient. The doorway people had stopped. She hadn't. The distance between stopping and not stopping was the width of a decision she made every morning when she crawled out of the alcove and stood in the drainage channel and stretched and told herself today would be different.

But the distance was also shrinking. She could measure it the same way she measured everything — in numbers, in coins, in the arithmetic that ran underneath every day like the trickle of water in the channel. Five coppers a day toward the debt. Fifteen days to pay it. A debt that grew faster than she could shrink it. Eventually the arithmetic wouldn't work anymore, and when the arithmetic stopped working, the doing stopped, and when the doing stopped, there was the doorway.

*          *          *

The fog thickened. The sounds from the harbour grew muffled and then the harbour disappeared entirely and there was only the alcove and the stone and the coins and the cats and the drainpipe running its thin cold trickle into the channel below.

She gathered the coins and put the good coppers in her left pocket and the questionable ones in her right and the silvers in the pouch she kept inside her shirt, against her skin, because silvers were worth killing for in Ashport and she'd rather feel them than wonder about them.

Garrick's ledger. The nine silvers she didn't have. The doorway people and their empty faces. Ten years of sleeping in other people's spaces and eating other people's food and working other people's queues, and here she was: twenty-seven coppers and an alcove under a bridge and the persistent, low-grade awareness that she was not in charge of her own life and hadn't been for as long as she could remember.

Something had to change. She didn't know what. She knew what happened to people in Ashport who waited for something to change without doing anything about it: the same thing that happened to the walls, the same thing that happened to the shops, the same thing that happened to the granary after the orc catapults found their range in the second month. Slow collapse. The mortar gives. The stones shift. And one morning you look up and the structure that was holding you together is gone.

She pulled her coat tight and pressed her back against the cold stone and listened to the harbour and the distant drums — three slow, then seven fast, then nothing — and she closed her eyes with the coins in her fist because that's what you did when your pockets weren't deep enough.

In the morning she'd figure something out.

She told herself that most nights. She was starting to suspect it was a lie, but it was a useful one, the kind that got you through to morning, and in Ashport a lie that got you through to morning was the closest thing to truth that mattered.
