CHAPTER THIRTEEN
The Tannery Man



The tannery market had nine stalls left.

There'd been fourteen before the fight at the ration tables, but five had closed in the three days since — the tanners on the eastern row pulling out after the Hooks planted men at both ends of the lane, claiming the territory the way they claimed everything: by standing in it and daring someone to ask them to move. The remaining nine stalls operated in the shrunk space between the Hook perimeter and the alley that led to the harbour road, which meant the market that used to span two blocks now fit in about forty yards, and the smell — tanning was not a clean trade — had concentrated proportionally.

Zima came in from the south, through the alley behind the cooperage, because the main approach from Bleach Lane was Hook-watched and she didn't want her face logged at another checkpoint. She had three items in her coat: a brass clasp she'd taken during the market fight, a bone-handled folding knife she'd lifted from a sleeping man in the ration queue two days ago, and a thin silver chain that had come off a woman's neck so cleanly the woman hadn't noticed until Zima was four bodies deep in the crowd. The clasp was decent. The knife was average. The chain was good — real silver, not plated, with a small garnet pendant that a jeweller would price at two silvers if the jeweller was honest and four if he wasn't.

She didn't need a jeweller. She needed Dav.

Dav's stall was the seventh from the south entrance, except now with the five closures he was the fourth. He ran his fence operation from a tanner's workbench that was exactly that — a real workbench, real tools, real hides, the apparatus of legitimate leather-working arranged around him like a stage set. If you came to him with something to sell, he'd look at it the way a tanner looked at a hide: professionally, without judgement, with the detached interest of a man who knew what things were worth because knowing what things were worth was how he stayed alive.

She'd been coming to Dav for three years. He was mid-forties, heavy through the shoulders and arms, narrow through the face, with hands that were mapped in scars from the knife-work tanners did and from the other kind of knife-work that fences occasionally had to do. He had a gold tooth — upper left canine — that he'd won in a card game from a Hook lieutenant six years back, and which served as both a conversation piece and a quiet reminder that he'd been sitting at tables where people bet their dental work and he'd walked away with the winnings. He was careful. He was fair, within the bounds of what fair meant when you were converting stolen property into coin. He didn't ask where things came from. He didn't report to Garrick, or to the Hooks, or to anyone else Zima had been able to identify in three years of watching him. In Ashport, that made him approximately the safest person she knew, which was not the same as safe.

The lane smelled of lime and urine and the particular sweetness of leather at the scraping stage, when the hair was off and the flesh was off and the hide was just skin and chemicals, drying on the frames. Zima had hated the smell for the first year. Now she associated it with money, which had improved the smell considerably.

"Morning, girl." Dav didn't look up from the hide he was trimming. The knife in his hand was a half-moon blade, the kind tanners used for scraping, and he drew it across the leather with the casual precision of a man who'd done this ten thousand times and could do it ten thousand more without cutting himself. "Buying or selling?"

"Selling." She put the three items on the bench, laid out in a row, left to right, in descending order of value. The silver chain. The brass clasp. The folding knife. She always presented them this way — best piece first, so the fence spent most of his attention on the thing that was worth the most, which meant he spent less attention on the things that were worth less, which meant the total price ended up slightly higher than it would have if she'd started with the knife. Small advantage. The kind of thing that added up over three years.

Now he looked up. His eyes went to the chain first — of course they did — and then to the clasp, and then to the knife. He picked up the chain. Held it to the light. The grey afternoon came through the tannery canvas in flat bars, and the silver caught it the way real silver did: clean, without the yellowish tint of plate.

He tested the weight. Ran a thumbnail along the links to check the joins. Held the garnet up and turned it.

"Where'd you get the garnet?"

"Ration queue."

"She know it's gone?"

"She will by now."

He set the chain down. Picked up the clasp. Turned it twice. Set it down. Didn't bother picking up the knife — he could see from two feet what it was worth, which was not much.

"Two silvers for the lot."

"Two and a half."

"Two and two coppers."

"Two and four."

He looked at her over the chain. The gold tooth showed when he almost smiled. "Two and three. I'm being generous because you're consistent."

"Done."

He counted the coin from a leather pouch he kept in his boot — everybody in Ashport kept their money in their boots, it was the city's one universal — and set it on the bench. Two silvers, three coppers. She took the coins and put them in her coat and didn't count them in front of him because that would have been an insult to a man who'd never shorted her in three years. She'd count them later, under the bridge, the way she counted everything.

The transaction was done. The real business hadn't started.

*          *          *

She stayed at the bench.

Normally she'd have left — you didn't linger at a fence's stall, for the same reason you didn't linger at a privy, because the longer you stood there the more obvious it became that you were doing something you didn't want watched. But the market was thin today. The nearest occupied stall was three down, and the tanner there was bent over a frame with his back to them, and the lane's foot traffic was sparse enough that she could count every person in sight. Seven, including Dav. Including herself, eight.

"Can I ask you something?"

He didn't look up. The half-moon blade went back to the hide, scraping in long, even strokes. "You're going to whether I say yes or not."

"If someone came to you with something old — genuinely old, pre-Founding era — what would it be worth?"

The blade paused. A small pause, barely a hitch in the rhythm, but she saw it. She was trained to see pauses. Pauses were information. He resumed cutting.

"Depends," he said. "Pre-Founding objects are rare. Genuinely rare, not market-rare — not the rarity of a clean purse in a thin queue, but the rarity of a thing that mostly doesn't exist anymore. Most of what was made before the city was built got melted down or lost or broken in the first century. The things that survived are in private collections outside the walls, or they were." He scraped another long strip from the hide. "I don't know what the siege has done to the collector market. Nothing good, I'd imagine."

"But if someone had one. Metal. Small. What would it be worth to the right buyer?"

He set the blade down. Wiped his hands on the scrap of hide he used as a cloth. His scarred fingers moved slowly, deliberately, and she understood that the slowness was communicative — Dav thinking, and wanting her to see him thinking, which was how he signalled that a question had weight.

"Inside the walls? Hard to say. The market for curiosities isn't what it was. People want food, fuel, medicine. Nobody's collecting historical artefacts when they can't collect bread." He picked up the blade again but didn't resume work. Held it. "Outside the walls — if you could get a piece to a collector in Thornreach or one of the university cities — you'd be looking at real money. Tens of silvers. Gold, possibly, for the right piece. Pre-Founding metalwork is the kind of thing scholars and rich men fight over, and scholars fight harder because they can't afford to lose."

Tens of silvers. Gold. Zima filed the numbers the way she filed all numbers: precisely, without visible reaction. The disc sat against her ribs, warm through the canvas lining, a steady low heat she'd been living with for days and which came back to her attention every time she thought about the disc directly, the way a sound you've been ignoring becomes loud the moment someone mentions it.

"Who in the city studies old things?" she said. "Old metal. Old markings. Someone who'd know what they were looking at."

Dav was quiet. The market sounds filled the gap — two women arguing over the price of a belt three stalls down, a dog nosing at the gutter, the distant muffled cadence of the drums starting their evening cycle from the hills. Three slow beats. She counted them out of habit. Then the silence, then the seven fast ones rolling into the dark.

"The man at the Wayfarers Hall," Dav said. "Eldrin. You remember — I mentioned him, that day on Wick Street."

"You mentioned a scholar."

"Same one. Ironmore scholar, or was." He scratched the edge of his jaw with the flat back of the blade handle — a habit she'd seen before, something he did when he was assembling information in the order he wanted to present it. "He came to me six months ago with a piece of old script on vellum. Wanted to know if I could sell it. I told him I fence stolen goods, not library scraps." The ghost of a smile. The gold tooth. "But he knows the old writing. Tidal Script, he called it. The script on the seawall carvings, the ones nobody can read. He can read them, or he says he can. I've no way to verify and no reason to care, but he seemed certain."

"What else do you know about him?"

"Brilliant. Impoverished. The university let him go because his findings were — disturbing, he said." Dav shrugged, a motion that involved only his left shoulder, the right one staying still on the hide. "He didn't elaborate. I didn't press. I'm a tanner and a fence, not a librarian." He went back to his work. The blade found the hide. "He looks like he hasn't eaten properly in a year, which in this city puts him in the majority. He's thin in the way scholars get thin — not starving, just neglected, the body of a man who forgets meals because his mind is somewhere else. He keeps a corner table at the Wayfarers Hall, near the back, piled with his papers. He's there most afternoons. I think the Hall lets him stay because he's quiet and he doesn't drink much and they don't have enough paying customers to turn anyone away."

Zima absorbed this. She built the picture the way she built every picture — detail by detail, testing each piece against the others for fit. An Ironmore scholar, loose in Ashport. Let go from a university for findings that were disturbing, a word that could mean anything from heresy to accuracy, depending on the university. Six months in the city. Knew Tidal Script, which was old — pre-Founding old, the script on carvings that predated the walls. Impoverished, which meant he needed money, which meant he could be hired, if hiring was the right word for asking a man to look at a thing and tell you what it was.

"Has anyone else asked you about him?"

"No." Dav looked at her. His expression was careful — not suspicious, exactly, but adjacent to suspicious. The expression of a man who sold information as a sideline and who could recognise when someone was buying more than they were paying for. "Nobody asks about Eldrin. Nobody knows he's here, or if they know they don't care. He's a scholar in a city that's stopped caring about scholarship. He's about as threatening as wet paper."

"Good."

She should have left it there. She'd got what she came for — a name, a location, a rough assessment of the man's capabilities. But Dav was watching her, and she could see the question forming behind his eyes — the question he wouldn't ask because asking would violate the professional code between a thief and her fence, a code built on the understanding that you didn't enquire into the reasons behind the things people brought you. You weighed them. You priced them. You paid. The reasons were their business.

But he said it without asking it. "Whatever you've found, be careful who you show it to."

She waited.

"Old things have old owners," he said. The blade was still in his hand. He wasn't using it. "And old owners tend to have opinions about who holds their property."

It was the closest thing to a warning Dav had ever given her. In three years of transactions — brass clasps and silver chains and the occasional good piece that he'd pause over and weigh with that expression of professional respect — he had never once offered unsolicited advice. He'd answered questions. He'd quoted prices. He'd kept his silence. That was the arrangement. But this was outside the arrangement. She could hear it in his voice — not concern, not exactly. Something closer to the instinct of a man who'd survived in Ashport by knowing when a situation was carrying more weight than its surface could hold, and who recognised the look on a person's face when they were carrying something they hadn't told him about.

"I haven't found anything," she said.

He looked at her. The gold tooth was hidden — he wasn't smiling.

"Course you haven't," he said. "Nobody finds anything in this city. Things just turn up."

She took the coin and left the stall.

*          *          *

The walk back took her south along the harbour road, past the fish market — three vendors now, down from seven at the start of the month — and the chandler's shop that had been boarded since month nine of the siege. The fog was early today, coming in from the harbour in low banks that smelled of salt and tar and the particular brackish rot of a port that hadn't been properly dredged in a year. She walked through it and she thought.

Two things she needed. Information and knowledge. Different commodities, acquired from different sources, serving different functions.

Information meant intelligence: what the Vipers knew, how they were searching, how close they were to the disc. Without intelligence she was operating blind. She knew they were looking — the questions at the ration table, the tightened patrols in the eastern blocks where Pell had died, the way the two Viper men at the distribution point had marked her face and tracked her as she walked away. But she didn't know the shape of their search. She didn't know if Garrick had connected the disc to something specific or if he was still working backwards from Pell's body, pulling threads, trying to reconstruct the chain of hands the disc had passed through. She didn't know who else might be looking for it. She didn't know how much time she had.

Intelligence meant Lirra.

The baker on Mercer Lane. Zima had never been to the bakery — had never needed to, not directly — but she knew about Lirra's operation the way she knew about most of the unofficial infrastructure in Ashport's southern quarter: through accumulation, through the slow layering of overhead conversation and observed pattern that was the real education of a pickpocket. Lirra ran coded messages through her bread deliveries. That much was established — not proven, not confirmed by any source Zima trusted completely, but established through enough independent observations that the picture was solid. Notes slipped into paper wrappings. Information moved from table to table, kitchen to kitchen, disguised as the mundane commerce of a woman who baked loaves and sold them. The customers who received the special deliveries knew to look. Everyone else saw bread.

The intelligence was supposedly good. The price was supposedly fair. The security was supposedly tight — three years of operation without a single leak, which in Ashport was a record that spoke for itself. Zima needed to know what the Vipers were doing, and Lirra would know, or she'd know who knew, or she'd know how to find out.

But going to Lirra meant explaining why she needed the intelligence. And explaining meant showing her hand. And showing her hand was the thing she'd spent three days not doing because the disc in her coat was the most dangerous object she'd ever carried, and every person who knew about it was a potential leak, and leaks in Ashport didn't drip. They bled.

She weighed it. She'd go to Lirra. Not today — today she'd go back to the bridge and sit with what Dav had told her and let the pieces settle. She'd learned the hard way that moving too fast on new information was how you walked into the gap between what you knew and what you didn't know, and in that gap, all manner of things waited.

Lirra first. Then Eldrin.

The logic was clean. Intelligence before scholarship. Know the danger before you try to understand the object. Stay safe, then get smart.

The other logic, the one underneath, was harder to put into words but equally clear: she wasn't going to show the disc to anyone — not Eldrin, not Lirra, not a single person in this city — until she knew the landscape around it. Walking into the Wayfarers Hall with a pre-Founding artefact that the Ash Vipers were hunting was a move that ended with Garrick at her door, and Garrick at her door ended with the ledger, and the ledger was where your choices went to die.

Lirra first. Map the danger. Then Eldrin. Understand the object.

The harbour road was empty. The fog had thickened to the point where she could see about twelve feet in any direction, which she knew because she paced it out — a habit, compulsive and useful. Twelve feet of visibility. A short sprint. Two seconds, maybe three. Enough time to react to someone coming out of the grey. Not enough time to do anything clever about it.

*          *          *

The disc was warm. She was getting used to it, and that bothered her more than the warmth itself — the body adjusting to the abnormal before the mind had decided whether the abnormal was safe.

She'd considered, more than once in the past three days, taking the disc out and looking at it again. In the alcove. In the quiet. The way she'd done the first night — holding it in her palm, studying the markings, the fine lines etched into the dark metal that looked almost like a map if you tilted it at the right angle. She hadn't done it. Partly because any time spent examining the disc was time she wasn't watching for Vipers. Partly because the disc had pulsed that first night, once, like a heartbeat, and she hadn't known what to do with that information and she still didn't.

She turned off the harbour road onto the lane that led down to the Coppergate bridge. The bridge was empty. The alcove was hers.

She sat with her back to the cold stone and counted the day's take. Dav's payment: two silvers and three coppers. Plus yesterday's queue work: eleven coppers. The day before: seven coppers and a half-silver. She ran the total. On paper, between the satchel silver and the rest, she was close to clearing the nine. But the satchel silver was the problem — five silvers appearing the day after a Viper courier died with a pouch on his belt was the kind of arithmetic Garrick could do in his sleep. She had the money and couldn't spend it. Strip the hot silver out and she was still more than four silvers from clearing Garrick's debt, and the debt was past its extension, and Stel hadn't come back.

That kept circling. Stel. The collector who'd given her the four-day extension at the ration tables, who'd marked her face and her name and told her four days and meant it. Gone. Not at the queues. Not at the market. Not at any of the places where Garrick's men showed their faces and reminded you that the ledger was patient but not infinite. Three possibilities. One: Stel was busy elsewhere — the fight had reshuffled Garrick's operations, collectors pulled to other duties. Two: Garrick was letting the debt grow, adding silence and interest in equal measure, the way he did when he wanted a debtor desperate enough to do whatever he asked. Three: something had happened to Stel during the fighting. Four confirmed dead. Others unaccounted for. The confusion hadn't fully cleared.

She didn't know which possibility was real and she didn't have the information to find out, which brought her back to Lirra, which brought her back to the plan.

She sat under the bridge with the fog pressing in and the harbour water moving against the pilings and the disc warm against her ribs, and she let the shape of the plan settle into place. Not the shape of a completed thing — she didn't have enough pieces for that. The shape of a beginning. Two names. Two visits. Two conversations that would cost her something, because everything in Ashport cost something, and the currency wasn't always coin.

The fog thickened. The drums started their evening count from the hills — three slow beats, then the silence, then seven fast ones rolling across the rooftops. She listened and thought about a scholar at the Wayfarers Hall who could read old writing, and a baker on Mercer Lane who moved secrets through bread, and a dead courier named Pell who'd grabbed her wrist and said a word she still didn't understand, and a disc sewn into her coat that was warm when it should have been cold and heavy when it should have been light and patient in a way that metal had no right to be.

She pulled her coat tight. The disc settled against her ribs. She closed her eyes.

Tomorrow she'd start. Tonight she'd sleep, or try to, and if sleep came she'd take it and if it didn't she'd lie here and count coins and listen to the harbour and carry, against her ribs, the most dangerous thing she'd ever owned, which was not a weapon and not a fortune but something worse: a question she didn't have the answer to yet.

The fog took the city. The harbour sounded its patient note against the pilings. Somewhere in the dark, the water moved through channels she couldn't see, beneath the streets, beneath the foundations, beneath the accumulated weight of a city that had been waiting eleven months for something to change.

Zima waited with it. She was good at waiting. She was starting to suspect she wouldn't have to wait much longer.
