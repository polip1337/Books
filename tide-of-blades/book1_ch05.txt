CHAPTER FIVE
Wick Street



Wick Street had fourteen shopfronts and nine of them were boarded shut.

She counted them every time she walked it, which was most days, because Wick Street was the spine of her territory and counting was what she did instead of praying. The number had been climbing since the third month of the siege — four boarded in month three, seven by month six, nine now at eleven months. Two of the remaining five were the kind of open that meant someone still slept inside but didn't sell anything, which was a different word for closed that let you keep your pride. The butcher's had been the latest to go. He'd lasted longer than she'd expected, selling cuts of meat she chose not to identify from animals she hoped were rats but suspected were worse. One morning the shutters were nailed and there was a Hook mark scratched into the wood — the curved hook, like a fishhook drawn by someone who'd never held a rod — and that was the end of it. Eleven months of butchery reduced to a symbol on a door.

The Hook graffiti was spreading west. Three new marks since last week: one on the butcher's door, one on the old linen shop that had been empty since month two, and one on the stone wall at the corner of Wick and Tanner's Row, high up, placed by someone who'd stood on a crate or a friend's shoulders to reach it. The marks were territorial claims, or territorial ambitions, or just a boy with charcoal and an opinion about who owned what. Garrick's Vipers hadn't responded yet. They would — Garrick always responded to territorial marking, the way a dog responded to another dog's piss on its fence post — but the response took time because Garrick's responses were organisational, not impulsive, and organisational responses required meetings and men standing in back rooms arguing about which street corners were worth the cost of defending.

Zima walked the street with her hands in her pockets, shoulders neutral, pace unremarkable. A body in transit. She'd learned at fourteen that the trick to moving through Ashport unseen was to look like someone who belonged wherever she was and had no reason to be anywhere else, and the trick to that was to actually believe it, at least with the part of her that controlled her walk. The thinking part could run its own business. Today the thinking part was doing arithmetic.

Four days. Stel had given her four, delivered in the Harbour Gate queue with the kind of politeness that was worse than a threat because a threat told you what was coming and politeness just told you it was coming. This was the first morning of the first day, which meant she had the rest of today and three more after it to produce nine silvers from a city where nine silvers was more than most people held in a month. The coins in her pockets — distributed across three locations, left coat, right inner, boot, because putting all your money in one place was the same thing as putting all your trust in one seam — added up to twenty-seven coppers and two and a half silvers. She'd counted them before dawn in the alcove under the Coppergate bridge, in the thin grey light that came off the harbour, and the count had produced the same answer it always produced: not enough.

Six and a half silvers short. In four days. She'd have to earn more than a silver and a half per day, which was roughly triple her normal rate. She could work longer hours. She could take risks she usually avoided — drunks with friends, women who'd scream, the garrison soldiers who carried decent coin and indecent tempers. Or she could go to Dav and convert yesterday's goods into cash and start the day with something instead of nothing.

She walked south.

*          *          *

The doorway was still there.

Third shopfront from the south end of Wick Street, on the left. It had been a cobbler's once, before the cobbler died or left or simply stopped existing in the way people in Ashport stopped existing — gradually, by subtraction, until the space they'd occupied was just a space. The doorway was recessed about two feet into the stone facade, a shallow alcove, and when she'd been nine years old and sleeping in it the space had felt enormous. Not comfortable — the stone was cold, the street was exposed, and the rain came in sideways on nights when the wind blew off the harbour. But it had been hers. A room without a roof. A place she fit inside because she was small enough, and the smallness of her body and the smallness of the space had been a kind of match that she'd understood without thinking about it, the way a cat understands a box.

She was nineteen now and the top of the recess came to her shoulder.

She walked past it. She always walked past it. Every time she did, the doorway was a little smaller and she was a little taller and the distance between the two — between the girl who'd slept there and the woman who walked past — was a distance she couldn't measure in feet or years. It was measured in other things. In the number of purses she'd cut. In the debt she carried. In the fact that Garrick had found her in this doorway and said come with me and she'd gone, and everything that had happened since was the consequence of a nine-year-old's decision to eat instead of starve, which was no decision at all.

The doorway had a Hook mark on it now, scratched into the stone at knee height. She noticed that. Filed it. Kept walking.

Fourteen steps to the corner of Tanner's Row. She counted them.

*          *          *

The dead zone started where Wick Street opened onto Tanner's Row.

Three buildings on the north side, all dark, all shuttered, the street in front of them empty in a way that went beyond simple absence. In the seventh month a man had been stabbed there — a disagreement about territory or debt or something nobody remembered anymore, the specifics dissolved by repetition into a general legend of violence — and neither the Vipers nor the Hooks had claimed the killing. In the gap between their denials the street had become unclaimed ground. During the day it was just empty pavement, paving stones with weeds pushing through the cracks, a gutter clogged with months of refuse nobody had cleared. After dark it was something else. Not dangerous, exactly. Removed. A place the city had stopped maintaining, the way a body stops sending blood to a finger it's decided to lose.

She crossed it in twenty-two paces. The weeds were taller than last week. Nobody had walked through here recently enough to flatten them.

The tannery market was on the other side, in the open yard behind the old tannery building. The tannery itself had been dead since the siege cut off the hide supply, but the chemicals in the stone kept bleeding smell into the air — acrid, brownish, a stink that got worse when it rained and never quite left when it didn't. The yard was sheltered on three sides by the tannery walls, with clear sightlines to the street, and the independent traders had claimed it in the fourth month as a place to do business without paying Viper or Hook taxes. Independent was generous. What it meant was too small to tax. The traders here sold salvaged goods, mended tools, secondhand hardware — merchandise that existed in the margin between worthless and worth stealing, and neither gang had bothered to close that margin because the take wasn't worth the effort of collection.

Eight stalls today. Three months ago there had been twelve. The arithmetic of attrition, reliable as weather.

Dav's stall was at the yard's north end, against the tannery wall where the overhang kept rain off his goods. He was there when she arrived. He was always there. Dav's relationship with his stall was like the harbour cats' relationship with the fish carts — permanent, territorial, and based on an intimate understanding of where the food came from.

He was mid-forties, heavy through the shoulders and arms, with hands that told a story he never explained: the knuckles set at wrong angles, twice broken at least, the fingers not quite closing, the scars across his palms layered and shiny with the tissue of old cuts healed over old cuts. He had one gold tooth, left side, third from the front. He was proud of it the way another man might be proud of a scar or a title — not vanity exactly, but the satisfaction of owning something distinctive. He showed it when he smiled, and he smiled often, because the smile was a business tool. It said: I'm friendly, I'm approachable, I'm a man who appreciates the fine art of commerce between reasonable people. It did not say: I'm trustworthy. That was the part he left for you to work out on your own.

"Zima." He was arranging a display of salvaged nails — bent, various sizes, sorted by length into clay pots. The nails were worth more than they should have been because the blacksmith on Dock Lane had closed and nobody was making new ones. Supply and demand. The siege's favourite lesson. "Early today."

"I've got things to sell."

"Everyone has things to sell. That's what keeps me in the lifestyle." He gestured at the stall — the nails, a rack of secondhand tools, a tray of mismatched buttons and buckles, a wooden crate full of things that defied category. "Show me."

She laid it out on his counter. Yesterday's take from the Harbour Gate queue — the queue where Stel had found her, the queue she'd kept working after he left because panic was a waste and the marks weren't going to rob themselves: a brass button that looked like it came off a garrison officer's coat, a small knife with a chipped tip that she'd found in the wool-coat man's purse, a copper ring with an intact band, and a folded paper with a Cutter Street address she'd been able to read that morning. An apothecary's supply order. Willow tincture and rendered fat in quantities that suggested burn patients.

Dav picked through the items with the practised hands of a man who'd been appraising other people's losses for longer than she'd been alive. He held the brass button to the light. Turned the ring. Unfolded the paper and read it without his expression changing, which meant it was either worthless or interesting and he wasn't going to tell her which until he'd set a price.

"The button's two coppers. Garrison brass, common issue. He's got six more on his coat." He set it down. "The ring is copper, not bronze — the colour's wrong. One copper, and that's generous because the band's intact." He tapped the paper. "This is more interesting. Supply order from the Millhouse Apothecary on Cutter Street. They're ordering willow and rendered fat in bulk, which means they're compounding burn salve, which means they have burn patients, which means someone's paying them to treat burns quietly, which means someone got burned doing something they shouldn't have been doing."

"What's that worth?"

"To me? Nothing. I don't deal in intelligence. To someone who wants to know who's burning and who's paying to hide it? Three coppers, maybe four." He folded the paper and slid it back to her. "Sell it yourself. My cut doesn't apply to goods I don't handle."

"You just told me what the intelligence was worth."

"Professional courtesy." The gold tooth appeared. "And an advertisement. The knife's worthless with that tip. I'll take it for a copper so you don't have to carry it."

She pushed it across the counter. "Four coppers for the lot, then. Minus your twenty percent."

"Comes to three coppers and change. I'll round to three." He counted them out from the clay pot he kept under the counter — his paying pot, separate from his stock, separate from his display, because Dav understood the architecture of money and knew that coins you could see were coins you spent. "You're a reliable girl. I appreciate reliable. Reliable means I don't check the goods twice."

She pocketed the coppers. Three wasn't much. Three was barely an hour's work in a good queue. But three coppers from Dav came without risk — no crowds to read, no hands to dodge, no garrison men with short tempers and shorter patience for thieves. Fencing was clean money, or as clean as money got when it had been stolen first.

"Anything else moving?" she asked.

"Everything's moving. Nothing's selling." He rearranged the nail pots. "Business has been — you know how business has been. Market's thinner every week. I had a man in yesterday trying to sell me a door. A door. Pulled it off the hinges himself, carried it here on his back. Wanted eight coppers."

"Did you buy it?"

"Gave him three. Doors are useful. Doors are always useful." He paused in his arranging. "Speaking of useful. If you ever come across anything old — metalwork, stone with markings on it, that sort of thing — I know a man who'd pay to look at it. Not buy it, necessarily. Look at it."

"What kind of markings?"

"The old kind. Pre-founding. The script on the harbour wall carvings, down by the breakwater — that style. There's a man at the Wayfarers Hall, used to be at Ironmore University. Reads old script, appraises objects when he can stomach it. Came to me twice with items to sell." Dav shrugged, a motion that involved more shoulder than most people's shrugs, the way everything about Dav was slightly more than expected. "Academic type. Thin, nervous. Looks like he'd blow away in a harbour wind. Name's Eldrin. He hasn't got money, but he's got knowledge, and in a city where nobody reads anymore, knowledge is the kind of commodity that's either worthless or priceless, depending on the day."

She filed the name. Eldrin. Wayfarers Hall. Ironmore University. Old script. She filed it in the place where she kept things that might matter later — not a plan, not an intention, just a piece of information with potential weight, stored alongside the apothecary paper and the Hook graffiti count and the slow, polite cadence of Stel's voice measuring the days she had left. She collected details the way Dav collected buckles: sorted, stored, waiting for the buyer who needed them.

"I'll keep it in mind," she said.

"You do that." He turned back to his display. "And Zima. Whatever's got you out here at dawn — be careful this week. The Vipers are running tighter. Something's got Garrick's attention and when Garrick pays attention the things he's looking at tend to bruise."

"I'm always careful."

"You're always quick. That's different." He held up a salvaged hinge, examined it, set it aside. "Quick is what you are when you're young and your hands work. Careful is what you learn to be when they don't." He held up his scarred, wrong-angled hands. The demonstration was unnecessary but effective.

*          *          *

She left the tannery market with thirty coppers and two and a half silvers.

Three more coppers than she'd had that morning. The apothecary paper was still in her coat — she'd sell it at the market tomorrow, find someone in the medicine-stall crowd who'd know what a bulk burn-salve order meant and pay for the information. Three more coppers from that, if Dav's estimate was right, and Dav's estimates were usually right because Dav was a man who understood value the way Zima understood pockets — professionally, precisely, without sentiment.

She walked back through the tannery yard. The eight remaining traders were conducting their quiet commerce — a woman mending a coat while a customer waited, two boys selling clay jugs of harbour water to people who were either desperate or didn't know the difference between harbour water and well water, a man sharpening knives on a whetstone with the careful, repetitive strokes of someone for whom the motion had become its own purpose. Small operations. Marginal lives. The kind of economic activity that existed because nobody had bothered to end it.

She crossed the dead zone. Twenty-two paces. The weeds brushed her boots.

Back on Wick Street, past the boarded shopfronts, past the Hook marks on the linen shop and the butcher's and the corner wall. Past the doorway. She didn't look at it. She didn't need to. It was there the way the debt was there and the four-day countdown was there and the nine silvers were there — present, constant, part of the landscape she moved through whether she looked at it or not.

Fourteen shopfronts. Nine boarded. Two more effectively closed. Three still open, barely, selling things nobody wanted at prices nobody could afford. The street had been a street once — a proper street with proper shops and proper traffic, a place where people came and went and the coming and going was itself a kind of life. Now it was a record of closures and a measure of the slow westward advance of Hook graffiti, and the territory it represented — her territory, the ground she walked because Garrick had found her here and the finding had made this ground hers in a way she'd never asked for and couldn't refuse — was smaller this week than last week, and would be smaller next week than it was now.

The fog was thickening early. The harbour smell came with it — salt and tar and the underlying rot that was Ashport's constant companion, the smell of a port city whose port had stopped working and whose city had nearly stopped being one. From the hills to the north, too early for the proper sequence, a single drum beat sounded. One heavy stroke, holding in the thickened air. Then nothing. A test, perhaps. Someone's hand finding the drumhead.

Tomorrow was market day at the Ashford. Bigger crowds, bigger purses, more motion. She'd work it hard — harder than usual, taking chances she usually saved for weeks when the numbers were already bad, because this was that kind of week and the four days were burning and the arithmetic was not going to solve itself through caution. Six and a half silvers in three days. The gap between what she had and what she owed was a gap she was going to have to jump, and the ground on the other side was not visible from here.

She walked south through the fog toward the harbour. Toward the Coppergate bridge and the alcove and the coins she'd count again tonight, because counting was what she did, and the counting always said the same thing, and the thing it said was not enough, and the not enough was getting louder, and the fog was getting thicker, and the territory behind her shrank a little more in the grey morning air.

Not all at once. Not dramatically. One shopfront at a time. One graffiti mark at a time. One closed door after another, until the space you thought was yours turned out to be a space you were only borrowing, and the rent was due, and the landlord had a ledger with your name in it, and the ledger didn't forget.
