CHAPTER TWENTY-EIGHT
The Wayfarers Hall



The hand went into her pocket on Chandler's Row, between the boarded chandlery and the soap maker's stall.

Good technique. Two fingers along the seam, the touch so light she might have mistaken it for the brush of a passing coat. But the disc pulsed — a quick, sharp burst of heat that shot from her left ribs across her body like a warning — and her hand closed on the wrist before the fingers found anything worth taking.

Thin. The wrist was thin enough that her fingers overlapped. The bones moved under the skin like the mechanism of a badly made clock. She turned and looked at the face attached to the wrist.

Male. Young — twenty-five, maybe, or thirty pretending to be twenty-five. Gaunt. The cheekbones visible, the jaw sharp, the eyes large in a face that had lost its padding. Grey eyes, the colour of harbour water under cloud. The hair was dark and unwashed and too long, falling across his forehead in a way that might have been deliberate once and was now just neglect.

Scholar's hands. She could feel them in the wrist she held — the fine bones, the long fingers, the calluses on the middle finger and thumb where a pen sits. The coat was patched at both elbows. The boots had been resoled with different leather. Everything about him said he'd once owned better things and had worn them out.

"Let go," he said.

"You're in my pocket."

"I was looking for a— I wasn't—" He stopped. His grey eyes moved from her face to his own wrist, held in her grip, and something in his expression rearranged itself. The panic fell away and left something flatter. Resignation, maybe. Or the specific exhaustion of someone caught doing something they know they're bad at.

"You were picking my pocket," Zima said.

"Attempting to."

"Badly."

He didn't argue. She held the wrist for another count of three, reading him the way she read marks in the queue. His breathing was fast but getting slower — he wasn't going to fight or run. His other hand hung at his side, open, no weapon. His body weight was forward, leaning into her grip instead of pulling away, which meant he'd already calculated that pulling would hurt more than waiting.

She let go.

He stepped back and rubbed his wrist. The red marks of her fingers were visible on the pale skin. He looked at them with the detached interest of someone examining a specimen.

"You approached from the wrong side," Zima said. "The right pocket is where I keep nothing. You wanted the left, where the coat is heavier. But you came in on my right because the foot traffic was thicker and you thought the crowd would cover you."

He stared.

"Your fingers were too high. The seam sits at hip level, not waist level. You were reaching into the fold of the fabric, not the pocket. And you telegraphed with your shoulder — you dropped it half an inch before your hand moved. Anyone who knows what a pickpocket looks like would have caught it."

"You know what a pickpocket looks like."

"I know what a good one looks like. You're not one."

He rubbed his wrist again. The grey eyes studied her with something that wasn't embarrassment — it was the look of a student being corrected by a master, the specific attention of someone who wanted to learn.

"I watched you," he said. "At the Harbour Gate queue. Three days ago. You worked two purses and a coat pocket in twenty minutes. I watched your hands. I thought—" He stopped. "I thought I could learn the technique."

"From watching?"

"From watching."

"You can't learn a pocket from watching. You learn it from doing, and you start with practice targets, not live marks. How long have you been at this?"

"A week."

"A week." She looked at him — the scholar's hands, the patched coat, the boots with mismatched soles. "What were you before the week?"

Something in his face closed. A door shutting behind the grey eyes. "A researcher. Ironmore University. Ancient languages and pre-Founding inscriptions."

The name sat in the air between them. Zima had known — from the moment the disc pulsed, from the moment she'd felt the scholar's calluses on the thin wrist — but hearing it was different from knowing.

"Eldrin," she said.

The door behind his eyes opened again. Surprise. "How do you know my name?"

"A man named Dav told me about a scholar at the Wayfarers Hall who reads old scripts. A fence at the tannery market. He said you'd been at Ironmore. That you'd been sacked. That you appraised objects when you could stomach it."

Eldrin's face went through several things in rapid sequence. She counted three she could identify — recognition of Dav's name, discomfort at the word sacked, and something else, something darker, at the mention of old scripts.

"Dav talks too much," he said.

"Dav talks the right amount. He's a fence. Talking is inventory." Zima looked at the street around them. Chandler's Row was busy — mid-morning foot traffic, a handcart with grain sacks, two women carrying a shared basket of root vegetables. Nobody was watching them. The gangs didn't mark this street heavily because there was nothing on it worth marking.

"Buy me a meal," Eldrin said.

"What?"

"Buy me a meal and I'll tell you why you should be afraid of that thing in your coat." His grey eyes dropped to her left side. "The thing that got hot when I touched your pocket. The thing that's been warm since you sat down at the queue three days ago. I could see the heat distortion from twenty paces."

*          *          *

The Dock Lane vendor was still serving. Zima bought two bowls of stew with coppers she'd taken from the Harbour Gate queue that morning — the last of them, the bottom of the well. She was out of money after this. Completely out. The debt to Garrick was abstract; the absence of coppers in her pocket was immediate.

They sat on the harbour wall. The fog was in, thick and low, the blockade ships invisible. The stew was the same thin broth she'd brought Torv — grey potato, grey fish, grey water with the memory of flavour. Eldrin ate it fast, scooping with a piece of bread that Zima didn't know where he'd found. The bread was stale, the kind that comes from the bottom of a day-old basket, and he ate it as though it were the best thing he'd had in a week.

It might have been.

"Tidal Script," Eldrin said, between mouthfuls. "That's what the markings on your object are. I haven't seen it in person for two years. The last time was at the university, on a fragment of wall coping that a dock worker brought in from a collapsed cellar near the harbour."

"You recognised it from twenty paces."

"I recognised the heat. Tidal Script artefacts generate warmth when they're active. It's documented — there are references in three pre-Founding texts that describe objects that hold heat in the presence of a..." He paused. Looked at his stew. "In the presence of a compatible bearer."

"Compatible."

"The script doesn't activate for everyone. The texts call it attunement. The object chooses. Or the object's creator chose, in advance, by setting conditions that the bearer must meet." He set the bowl down. His hands were shaking — not from cold. From something else. "You're attuned to whatever's in your coat. That's why it's warm. That's why it pulsed when I touched it. It was warning you, or warning itself."

Zima ate her stew slowly. She could feel the disc against her ribs, warm and steady, its rhythm unchanged by the conversation. It had pulsed when Eldrin touched her pocket — a quick, sharp heat, not the slow warmth she was used to. Warning, he'd called it. Or recognition.

"What happened at Ironmore?" she asked.

Eldrin's grey eyes went to the harbour. The fog sat on the water like a ceiling, and beneath it nothing was visible — no ships, no shore, just grey in all directions.

"I was researching pre-Founding binding contracts. Legal documents written in Tidal Script — the oldest form of the script, predating the common era by two centuries. The contracts were unusual. They weren't symbolic. The language was functional."

"Functional how?"

"They did things. The words on the page didn't describe an agreement — they were the agreement. Breaking the contract wasn't a legal violation. It was a physical impossibility. The script enforced itself."

He picked up his bowl again and drank the broth. His hands had stopped shaking. The act of explaining his own work was steadying him — giving him a framework to stand in, the way the bakery gave Lirra a framework.

"I published a preliminary paper. My department head read it. He called it dangerous speculation and had it pulled. I pushed back. He had my access to the archive revoked. I pushed back harder. The university terminated my position. That was two years ago."

"Because you said the script was functional."

"Because I said the script was still functional. Two hundred years old and still enforcing contracts written before the city had walls." He set the bowl on the wall between them. "The university's position was that pre-Founding artefacts are historical curiosities. My position was that they're active legal instruments. One of those positions is comfortable. The other one is true."

Zima looked at him. The gaunt face, the grey eyes, the long hands wrapped around an empty bowl. Two years since the university. Two years of the Wayfarers Hall and appraising objects for Dav and learning to pick pockets from watching because the skills he'd built his life on were no longer welcome in the only institution that valued them.

"I have something I need you to read," she said.

"I know." His eyes went to her left side. "That's why the thing in your coat was warm when I came near it. It wasn't just warning you. It was calling me."

The fog moved. The harbour was invisible. Somewhere beyond the grey, the drums went three slow, seven fast, and the blockade ships sat patient on the water, and fifteen thousand orc soldiers waited in the hills for something that hadn't happened yet.

"Not here," Zima said. "Tomorrow. The harbour wall, but further east, past the crane yard. Somewhere quiet."

"I'll be there."

"Eldrin."

He looked at her.

"Don't try to pick anyone else's pocket between now and then. You're terrible at it. Someone less patient than me will break your fingers."

The grey eyes held hers. For a moment something moved behind them — not the flat resignation she'd seen when she caught his wrist, not the scholar's excitement when he talked about scripts, but something older and more uncertain. The look of a man who'd been alone with his thoughts for two years and had just been invited back into the world.

"Tomorrow," he said.

He walked east along the harbour wall, his patched coat catching the fog like a sail. Zima watched him go. He walked like a man who'd forgotten how to be watched — hunched, narrow, occupying as little space as possible. The opposite of Torv, who filled every room he entered. The opposite of Lirra, who occupied space with the precision of someone who'd measured it first.

Three people. Three kinds of lonely.

The disc pulsed. Maela's voice came, thin and distant: "He's frightened of it. But he'll read it. Frightened scholars are the most honest kind."

Zima didn't answer. She sat on the harbour wall in the fog and thought about a crew of four — a cutpurse, a baker, an orc, and a scholar — and wondered what Maela had been, two centuries ago, when she'd built passages and sealed vaults and set conditions that would choose a bearer she'd never meet.

The stew bowls were empty. The fog was thick. The drums went on.

She'd find out tomorrow.
