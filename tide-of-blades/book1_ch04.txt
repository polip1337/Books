CHAPTER FOUR
The Collector



The Harbour Gate ration queue was shorter than the Mercer Lane queue and worse in every other way.

Thirty-one people. Zima was nineteenth. She'd arrived early — an hour before distribution, which was unusual for her and deliberate. The Harbour Gate drew from the dock district and the southern waterfront, which meant dockworkers, fishwives, deckhands from the boats that still ran, and the particular kind of person who lived near the harbour because the harbour was the last place in Ashport where money still moved in something resembling a current. The pockets were better here. The marks were harder. Dock people kept their coin close and watched each other with the professional attention of people who made their living near valuable things that weren't theirs.

The queue ran along the inside of the harbour wall, in the shadow of the gate itself — a massive timber-and-iron structure that hadn't opened since the second month of the siege, when the garrison commander ordered all city gates sealed and the ironmonger's guild welded the bolts shut as a gesture of civic resolve that had impressed nobody and inconvenienced everyone. The gate was thirty feet high and it didn't move and it hadn't moved and the rust on its hinges had gone from orange to brown to the dark, permanent colour that meant the iron underneath was being consumed. Some mornings Zima looked at the gate and thought about the effort that had gone into building it — the timber cut and seasoned, the iron forged and fitted, the hinges cast, the locks set, all of it designed to open and close, to permit and prevent — and now it stood shut and rusting and its only function was to provide shade for a ration queue. A thing built for one purpose, repurposed by circumstance into something lesser. She could relate.

The guard at the distribution table was Henner, one of the garrison regulars she'd seen often enough to recognise but not often enough to predict. Some guards watched the queue with attention. Some watched with boredom. Henner watched with the careful disinterest of a man who was thinking about something else entirely — his pay, or his debts, or whatever arrangement he'd made with whichever gang was paying him this week. Two of Garrick's men flanked the table. Different men from the Mercer Lane pair, same expressions. The Vipers' presence at the ration tables was as standardised as the tables themselves — faces changed, the function didn't.

She'd been working on her second mark of the morning — a woman in a salt-stained coat with a drawstring purse at her hip — when she saw Stel.

*          *          *

He was coming from the east, along the harbour wall road, walking at the pace of a man who had nowhere urgent to be and intended everyone to notice. Mid-thirties. Thin in the way that some men were thin in Ashport — not starved but economical, as though his body had decided to carry only what was strictly required and had filed everything else under unnecessary. Clean-shaven, which was notable because razors cost money and the hot water to use them cost more and the vanity to care about either was the most expensive thing of all. He wore a coat that was slightly too good for the district — dark wool, well-fitted, recently brushed. Not Viper colours. Stel didn't wear colours. Colours were for the street-level boys who needed people to know who they worked for. Stel needed people to know nothing until he was already in front of them, at which point the knowing was too late to be useful.

She let the woman with the drawstring purse go. Couldn't work a mark with Stel approaching — her hands needed to be clean and still when he reached her, because Stel noticed hands the way she noticed ankle-checks, and getting caught working a queue while you owed Garrick three weeks of tithe was not a conversation she intended to have.

She watched him close the distance. Forty paces. Thirty. His stride didn't change. His eyes moved once — a sweep of the queue, the kind of look that took in everything and settled on nothing — and then found her, and his face did something that wasn't quite a smile but occupied the same territory. Recognition. Arrival. The settling of a man who'd come to do a specific thing and had found the person he needed to do it with.

He stopped six feet from her. He didn't cut into the queue. He stood beside it, at the edge, hands in his coat pockets, head tilted slightly to one side.

"Zima." He said her name the way you'd say good morning — easy, pleasant, no weight behind it. "You're looking well."

She wasn't. Nobody in Ashport was looking well. The bones in her wrists were more prominent than they'd been three months ago and her skin had taken on the grey cast that came from too little food and too much fog. But Stel said it the way he said everything — as if the words were currency he was placing on the table between them, and the denomination didn't matter because both of them knew the real transaction was happening somewhere beneath the surface.

"Stel."

"Garrick mentioned you haven't been by in a while."

Three weeks. She knew the number. He knew she knew the number. He said a while instead of three weeks because precision was something Stel avoided in conversation the way she avoided it in crowds — it left no room to manoeuvre, and room to manoeuvre was the whole point.

"I've been busy," she said.

"Everyone's busy." He looked at the queue. His gaze moved over the line the way light moves over water — touching everything, settling on nothing. "The market situation. The Hooks pushing west. You know how it is. Garrick's had his hands full."

She waited. Stel's conversations had a rhythm, and the rhythm was: pleasantry, context, the thing he'd come to say. He was still on context.

"He does keep track, though." Stel returned his eyes to her. Brown eyes, unremarkable, the kind you wouldn't remember in a description. "He's organised that way. Keeps the books current."

The ledger. There it was. Not named but present — sitting in the conversation the way the warships sat in the harbour, visible if you knew where to look.

"I know he does," she said.

Stel nodded. A small nod, acknowledging her acknowledgment, nothing more. This was what made him good at his job. This was what made him better than good.

*          *          *

Garrick employed three kinds of collectors. The first kind were the rough ones — Breck, and Wallis, and the big man whose name nobody used because his name was less memorable than his hands. The rough collectors were for the people who owed large and were likely to run. They grabbed arms. They stood too close. They spoke loud enough for the nearest twenty people to hear, which meant the threats they made had to be carried out, because a threat that's heard and not honoured becomes a joke, and a joke in Garrick's operation was more dangerous than a knife. The rough collectors created scenes, and scenes drew attention — the garrison, the Hooks, ordinary citizens who wanted violence kept away from their ration queue — and attention meant complications, and complications cost money, and Garrick did not enjoy spending money to collect money. It offended his arithmetic.

The second kind were the invisible ones. Couriers, mostly. They found you and handed you a folded piece of paper with a number on it and walked away, and the number was what you owed and the paper was the message and the message was: we know where you are. These worked on the middle tier, the debtors who wouldn't run but needed reminding, the ones whose debt was large enough to matter and small enough to pay.

The third kind was Stel. There was only one of him, and Garrick used him carefully, the way a good carpenter uses a fine tool — not for every job, but for the ones that required precision. Stel was for the people who were smart enough to know what they owed and scared enough to do something about it but hadn't yet, because the gap between knowing and doing was where Garrick made his real money. Stel's job was to stand in that gap and close it. Gently. Politely. Without raising his voice or balling his fists or doing anything that would give the debtor a reason to escalate, because escalation was the enemy of collection and Stel understood this the way Zima understood pockets — intuitively, professionally, as a matter of craft.

She'd watched him work other people. She'd seen it from across ration queues — Stel standing beside someone, talking, quiet and unhurried, and the other person's face changing as the conversation progressed. Not fear, exactly. Something worse. The flat understanding that this man was not going to go away, and the thing he was asking about was going to have to be addressed, and the addressing was going to happen on terms that had already been set by someone who wasn't present. Nobody ran from Stel. Running from the rough collectors was possible because anger made them clumsy and noise covered your exit. Running from Stel was like running from arithmetic. The numbers would still be there when you stopped.

"I've got something working," she said.

"Oh?"

"A job. Bigger than the queues." She didn't specify. Specifying was dangerous — Stel would remember the details and Garrick would hear the details and if the details didn't check out, the debt would upgrade from money owed to trust broken, and trust broken was a different category entirely.

Stel waited. His expression didn't change. This was the other thing about him — he didn't help you. The rough collectors would argue, which gave you something to push against, which gave you room to negotiate. Stel didn't argue. He let you talk and he listened and the silence he left between your words was designed to make you fill it, which made you say more than you meant to, which was exactly the point.

She kept her mouth closed. Two could wait. She'd learned it from the queue, where silence was the natural state and speech was what you did when you'd already decided what to say.

After a moment, Stel said, "How long?"

"A few days."

"A few."

"Four." She picked the number because it was specific enough to sound like a plan and short enough to sound reasonable and long enough to maybe be useful. Four days was not enough to earn nine silvers from the queues. She knew that. Stel probably knew it. But four days was not the point. The point was the extension itself — the agreement that the debt existed and that she intended to address it and that Garrick's operation would allow her the time to do so, and this agreement was the real transaction, happening above the money and below the words, in the place where Garrick's actual business lived.

"Four days." Stel said it back to her the way you'd taste something you weren't sure about. Then he nodded — the same small nod as before. "I'll let him know. He'll remember after that."

She heard what he was saying. After four days, it wasn't Stel anymore. After four days it was someone from the first category — Breck, or Wallis, or the man with the hands — and the conversation would not be about the weather and it would not take place beside a ration queue where other people could see.

"Four days," she said.

He looked at her for a moment longer. Then a smile — brief, professional, a smile with nothing behind it except the acknowledgment that a transaction had been completed and terms had been set. "Take care of yourself, Zima. The queues aren't as safe as they used to be."

He turned and walked away along the harbour wall road. Same unhurried pace. Hands in pockets. Coat well-fitted, well-brushed, slightly too good for the district. She watched him go and counted his steps — a habit, nothing more — until the curve of the road took him and he was gone.

*          *          *

She didn't go back to working the queue. Her hands weren't steady enough and her mind was too full of numbers.

Four days. Ninety-six hours. She walked the arithmetic the way she walked the streets — automatically, thoroughly, without expecting to arrive anywhere new.

Nine silvers owed. Two and a half on hand. Six and a half short. At the exchange rate the fences were offering this month — and the rate dropped every month because the coins were scarcer and the fences were greedier and the relationship between supply and demand in Ashport had been simplified by the siege into something brutal and clean — six and a half silvers was roughly seventy coppers. Her average daily take from the queues, after eating and silence-coin and the other small necessities of remaining alive, was five coppers toward the debt. Seventy divided by five was fourteen. She had four.

She could work harder. More queues, longer hours, riskier marks. The Merchant Council ration point was the most lucrative because the crowds were largest and the guards were worst, but it was Hook territory and a Viper cutpurse working Hook queues was an invitation to the kind of attention that ended with someone face-down in a drainage channel. She could try the market — market days were decent earning — but the next market day was two days away and the market had been getting thinner and more volatile since the Hook expansion.

She could try to borrow. Except there was nobody in Ashport who lent money outside the gang system, and the fences who advanced against future goods charged thirty percent interest due in a week, and borrowing from a fence to pay a gang was the financial equivalent of cutting off a finger to feed yourself — technically possible, briefly effective, and certain to make the larger problem worse.

Four days to earn what she couldn't earn in four weeks.

The queue moved. She moved with it, mechanically, not working, just standing in line like the other thirty people, waiting for eight ounces of grain, and the waiting felt different now than it had an hour ago. An hour ago she'd been a pickpocket in a ration queue, doing what she did, working the problem one copper at a time. Now she was a pickpocket in a ration queue with a clock running, and the clock changed everything. The slow, grinding arithmetic of daily survival — which was bad but familiar, a wheel she knew how to push — had been replaced by a deadline, and a deadline was a wall, and walls were the thing Ashport had too many of already.

She reached the table. Henner checked her token. A guard scooped grain into her bag. Eight ounces. She took it and walked.

*          *          *

She walked west along the harbour road and thought about Garrick's system, because thinking about the system was easier than thinking about what the system was going to do to her in four days.

The debt wasn't just money. She'd understood this for years, in the way you understand that fire is hot — you know it, you can say it, but you don't truly feel it until you're standing close enough to burn. The debt was the mechanism. Every cutpurse, every runner, every dock-watcher and table-guard and corner lookout in the Ash Vipers owed Garrick something, and the something was recorded in the ledger, and the ledger was the real power — not the men with knives, not the territory, not the ration tables. The ledger. Because a person who owed you could be directed. A person who owed you could be placed. A person who owed you would do what you told them because the alternative was the debt being called, and the debt being called was the end of the line. Not death necessarily, though death was available. The end of everything that made daily life possible. Your ration token revoked. Your sleeping spot claimed by someone else. Your name spoken to the garrison, or the Hooks, or whoever would find the information most useful and most damaging.

Garrick didn't need to hurt you. He just needed to stop protecting you, and in Ashport the absence of protection was its own kind of violence — slower than a knife, quieter than a beating, and more thorough than either.

If she paid the nine silvers — all of it, somehow, in four days — the debt would reset to zero. For a week. Maybe two. And then the charges would start again. Territory maintenance. Ledger administration. The tithe on every copper she earned. The interest on every copper she didn't. The ledger wasn't designed to reach zero. It was calibrated to hover at the precise level that kept the debtor working — not so high that hope collapsed, not so low that freedom became real. Nine silvers was the number that kept her in that range, and if she paid it, the number would grow back to nine silvers within a month because that was the equilibrium the system was built to maintain.

She'd thought about this. She'd thought about it for years, in the back of her mind, in the place where the problems lived that were too large to solve and too true to ignore. The ledger was not a record of debt. It was a record of ownership. Garrick owned her the way you owned a tool — not with affection, not with cruelty, but with the simple expectation that the tool would function as designed and could be replaced if it didn't.

The four-day extension was part of the design. Stel hadn't granted it because Zima was persuasive. He'd granted it because Garrick's system ran on controlled pressure, and controlled pressure required relief valves, and the extension was a valve — it released enough pressure to keep her functioning while allowing the debt to grow by four days' worth of interest and fees. She'd asked for time and what she'd received was more debt dressed as mercy.

She knew this. She took the four days anyway, because four days of additional debt was a future problem and no days was a current one, and current problems were what killed you in Ashport. Future problems were what you worried about at night under a bridge while you pretended the arithmetic would change by morning.

*          *          *

The harbour road led west and then north as it curved away from the waterfront. The afternoon was grey and cold and the city was doing what it always did — the small transactions of survival, the queues and the stalls and the careful exchanges of food and fuel and information that kept people moving through their days. She passed a woman selling salt fish from a handcart, the fish laid out in rows on a piece of canvas, each one approximately the size and colour of a man's hand. She passed two garrison soldiers sitting on a low wall eating something from a shared bowl, their weapons leaned against the stone beside them with the casual disregard of men who'd stopped expecting to use them. She passed a narrow alley where a man sat in a doorway with his knees drawn up and his eyes open and nothing behind them, and she looked at him and he didn't look at her and she walked on.

The city was getting hungrier. She could feel it — not in any single thing but in the aggregate, the weight of eleven months pressing down on every street and every face and every queue. The gangs were tightening their grip because a tighter grip meant more control and more control meant more of a shrinking supply, and the mathematics of that — more control over less — was the mathematics of collapse, and everyone in a position to do the mathematics was doing it, and nobody was changing their behaviour because changing behaviour required an alternative and there were no alternatives. The garrison was thinning because the soldiers were leaving for the gangs. The ration lines were getting shorter because fewer people had tokens and longer because the people who did had nothing else to wait for. The shops were closing. The doorway people were multiplying. The whole city was a system in the late stages of a process that had only one outcome, and the process was not going to stop because no one with the power to stop it had a reason to.

She walked. Four days. She had two and a half silvers and a skill that earned coppers in a city that required silver and a debt that grew faster than she could cut it and a man who would remember her in ninety-six hours and a system that was designed, from the ground up, to make sure that whatever she did next would not be enough.

Something had to change. Not the vague, night-time something — the useful lie, the formless intention that dissolved in morning light. Something specific. Something outside the system. A door she hadn't seen, or a number she hadn't counted, or a piece of luck she hadn't earned, because she'd been inside the system long enough to know that the system didn't produce exits. It produced debt and compliance and the slow, steady grinding of people into shapes that fit the machine.

Four days.

She walked north through the dock district and the light was failing and the fog was settling over the dock district in its usual grey blanket and the drums in the hills had been silent all afternoon, which was unusual, which was probably nothing, which she filed away anyway because filing things away was what she did and because in a city where the enemy's patience was the most dangerous thing on the horizon, a change in the pattern of drums was worth remembering even if it meant nothing.

Four days. The clock had started.

The fog took the harbour. The fog took the streets. The fog took the last of the light and Ashport settled into another evening, and somewhere in the Coppergate district a girl with two and a half silvers and a deadline was walking toward a bridge, and the bridge was the same bridge as last night and the alcove was the same alcove and the arithmetic was the same arithmetic, and the only thing that had changed was that the arithmetic had a number attached to it now, and the number was four, and four was not enough, and she was going to have to find another way or become another doorway person with empty pockets and an empty face and a ledger entry that nobody would ever close.

She kept walking.

This time was different. The lie wasn't going to be enough to get her through to morning.
