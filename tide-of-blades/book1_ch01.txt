CHAPTER ONE
The Ghost of Ration Row



The evening ration line was fifty-two people long and Zima was forty-third.

She knew the number because she'd counted twice. The first time she lost track at thirty-seven when the man in front of her shifted his weight and she had to lean back to keep his elbow out of her teeth, so she started over, and the second count came to forty-three. Behind her the queue had thickened to nine more, which meant the distribution table hadn't opened yet. Which meant the guards were running late. Which meant something had gone wrong at the granary or the harbour checkpoint or one of the twelve other places between there and here where Ashport's food supply could snarl itself into a problem, and the fifty-two people in this queue would stand here in the fog getting older until someone sorted it out.

The queue ran along the east side of Ration Row, which was what people called the stretch of Mercer Lane between the old chandlery and the bricked-up entrance to the customs house. Before the siege it had been a street. Now it was a system. Two ropes strung between posts to mark the line. A distribution table at the north end where the garrison guards sat behind grain measures and ledgers. A fire barrel halfway down that nobody ever lit because fuel was worth more than warmth. And at the edges, leaning against the customs house wall or sitting on the chandlery steps, the watchers — Garrick's men, mostly, with a pair of Iron Hook scouts at the far end of the street keeping count of something Zima didn't want to think about.

She stood with her hands at her sides and let the fog settle on her face and thought about hands.

Not her own. She was thinking about the cooper's widow six places up, the one in the yellowed headscarf, who kept her ration token in her left boot and checked it every few minutes by lifting her leg and touching her ankle with two fingers. Quick touch. Barely noticeable if you weren't watching. But Zima had been watching the cooper's widow's ankle since the third month of the siege — not to steal from her, though the opportunity was there every time the woman bent, but because the widow was a useful barometer. When she checked her ankle three times in under five minutes, it meant the crowd had got restless. When she went six or seven minutes without checking, things were calm. Right now she was running a check every four minutes, which put the mood of the queue somewhere between nervous and bored.

Bored was better. Nervous crowds turned inward, tucked themselves tight, moved their valuables to inner pockets. Bored crowds forgot where their hands were.

Zima studied the line from her position and catalogued what she could see. The dock labourer seven places ahead — thick arms, flask at his hip, purse on a single leather thong at his belt. The man in the heavy wool coat four places up — interior pocket, left side, based on the way he kept brushing his lapel. The woman with the market basket three places ahead — coin purse in her front apron pocket, visible as a bulge when she turned sideways. Three marks in a fifty-two-person queue was about average. Some days the queue gave her more. Most days it gave her exactly what the city could afford to lose, which was less every month.

Between the queue and the harbour, a row of buildings stood in various stages of abandonment. The cobbler's shop had been boarded since the fourth month. The print shop next to it still had glass in its windows but the door was nailed shut from the inside, which meant someone was living in there, which meant they'd stopped answering when people knocked. The greengrocer on the corner had been claimed by a Hook crew — their mark scratched into the doorframe, a curved line like a fishhook, rough and unmistakable. Beside it, an unmarked door that could have meant anything: owner dead, owner gone, owner sitting inside in the dark with nothing left to sell and no reason to open.

The fog was doing its usual work, coming in off the harbour in thick banks that smelled of salt and tar and something rotten underneath that she'd never been able to identify — not fish, not sewage, something older, like the harbour itself was decaying from the bottom up. In the early months of the siege, someone had told her it was the kelp beds dying because the orc blockade had disrupted the tidal patterns. She didn't know if that was true but it sounded like something that could be true, and in Ashport the distinction between true and plausible had stopped mattering around the sixth month.

By full dark the fog would fill the streets to the second storey and everything above that would be clear and cold and starlit, and everything below would be grey and muffled and close, and the people in the queue would pull their collars up and hunch and not look at each other because the fog made strangers of everyone. Zima had lived through eleven months of Ashport fog and she'd come to the conclusion that the fog was the only honest thing about the city. It didn't pretend you could see further than you could.

From the hills to the north, the drums started.

Three beats, heavy and slow, each one sitting in the air for a moment after the sound should have died. Then a silence — long enough to make you think they'd stopped. Then seven fast beats rolling together like stones tipped down a slope, and then nothing. The whole sequence took maybe fifteen seconds and it happened every evening at dusk and it had happened every evening at dusk since the orc army came down from the Thornback Hills and settled around Ashport like a hand closing slowly around a throat.

Nobody in the queue reacted. Eleven months. The drums had become weather.

Zima thought about the drums sometimes, about what they meant. The garrison said they were a psychological tactic — designed to keep the city on edge, to wear people down through repetition and dread. The gangs said they were signal-drums, communicating between orc positions in the hills. Zima thought both explanations were probably half right and that the real answer didn't matter, because the effect was the same either way: every evening at dusk, the hills reminded Ashport that they were there, and every evening at dusk, Ashport pretended not to hear.

She let the silence after the seven beats stretch and settle and then she started working.

*          *          *

The labourer was first.

She'd been watching him since she joined the queue — the way he stood, weight on his right leg, left hand free and wandering, right hand lifting the flask to his mouth every few minutes. The purse hung from his belt on a single leather thong, heavy enough to sway when he shifted his weight. She didn't look at it. Looking at what you were about to steal was the first mistake and the last one, because eyes are louder than hands. She looked at the distribution table at the front of the queue, where two of Garrick's men stood behind the garrison guards with the blank, proprietary expressions of people who collected a tax on every face that passed.

Two of the city's four ration points were controlled by the Ash Vipers. One was controlled by the Iron Hooks, the other gang, who operated out of the dockyard warehouses and had been pushing west into Viper territory for three weeks. The fourth ration point was administered by the Merchant Council, which was a polite way of describing whoever had brought the most armed men that morning. Ashport's governance had been simplifying itself since the siege began. First the port authority collapsed. Then the magistrate's office closed. Then the garrison commander started taking payments from the gangs for the privilege of guarding the city walls, and after that, the only real difference between a government and a gang was that the government had nicer stationery.

The line lurched forward three places and Zima let the motion carry her half a step left, closer to the labourer. His head tilted back for another pull from the flask. His left arm went wide for balance. She cut the thong with the small knife she kept in her right sleeve and caught the purse in her palm before it fell. She pocketed it and moved back to her place in the queue in the same motion, and nobody looked at her because nobody was looking at anything except the front of the line.

She felt the weight of the purse against her thigh. Decent heft. Probably eight or ten coppers and maybe a half-silver, which was a good take from a man who'd been drinking — drinkers either had nothing or they had whatever they hadn't spent yet, and this one had payday weight.

She should have felt something about it. She'd been doing this for five years and she used to feel something — not guilt exactly, more a tightness behind her ribs that she could identify now as the remnant of an idea she'd had about herself when she was younger, some notion that she was going to do something different with her life. The tightness had been gone for about a year. She wasn't sure if that was better.

*          *          *

The distribution table opened and the queue began to move, and she moved with it, and she took two more purses before she reached the front.

The first was the man in the heavy wool coat, who turned out to have a pouch with four coppers and a key to something she'd never find. The key she dropped into the drain at the edge of the street as she passed. Keeping stolen keys was stupid — you didn't know what they opened and neither did the fences, and anyone caught with someone else's key got asked questions she didn't want to answer.

The second was trickier. The woman with the basket, market regular, coin purse in her front apron pocket. Zima had to wait for the right moment — the woman kept lifting the cloth from her basket and reaching in, adjusting whatever was inside, and each time she did it her left side opened for about two heartbeats. On the third time, Zima was close enough. She bumped the woman's right arm with her shoulder, said sorry, and took the purse with her left hand while the woman was looking right. Clean.

She pocketed it and kept her face empty and waited her turn at the table.

The guard at the front was a man she didn't know — they rotated the garrison soldiers every few days to keep anyone from building relationships with the crowd, which was a policy that made sense on paper and in practice just meant you never knew which guard was going to be a problem. This one wasn't. She handed over her ration token, he checked it against a list, another guard scooped a measure of grain into the canvas bag she'd brought. Eight ounces, maybe. Enough for two days of thin porridge if she stretched it with water and didn't think about what porridge was supposed to taste like.

She took the bag and walked.

*          *          *

Garrick's men at the table didn't stop her. They logged her face — she saw one of them mark something on a tally sheet — but they let her through. They always did. Stopping someone at the ration table was conspicuous, and conspicuous was not how Garrick operated. He preferred to meet people in quiet places, after dark, where the conversations could go whichever direction they needed to without an audience.

She knew both of them by sight. The one with the tally sheet was Ferren, who was tall and had a face like a shovel blade — flat and hard and useful for one thing. He'd worked Garrick's ration tables since the second month of the siege, and in all that time Zima had never seen him speak to anyone in the queue. He wrote names. He remembered faces. That was his function. The other was Colm, newer, maybe six months on the tables, still young enough to look uncomfortable doing it. He'd made eye contact with her once, early on, and she'd held it just long enough to make him look away. She hadn't looked at him since.

She turned south along the harbour wall. The fog was heavier now, filling the street up to her chest, her chin, her eyes, until she was moving through it by feel and memory. She knew these streets the way she knew her own breathing — automatically, thoughtlessly, knowledge paid for in repetition. Turn left at the blocked drain. Twenty paces, then right at the cooperage. Straight past the boarded shop fronts, the one with the handprint painted on the door that meant a Hook crew had claimed it, the one with no mark at all that meant the owner had died or left and nobody cared enough to take it.

The harbour wall curved south and then west, following the waterline. At this hour it was empty — the garrison sentries wouldn't start their evening patrol for another half hour, and the dockworkers had gone home or gone to whatever passed for home. She walked along the top of the wall where the stone was worn smooth by generations of boots and cart wheels, and below her on the right the harbour water slapped at the pilings with a sound like slow, wet applause.

Ashport was a city of about twenty thousand people and it was dying by inches. That was the thing people from outside wouldn't have understood, if there'd been anyone from outside — the siege wasn't a single catastrophe. It was a slow subtraction. Every month, a little less food. A little less fuel. Another shop closed, another family gone — where they went, nobody asked, because the answers were limited and none of them were good. Either they'd found a way out through the sewer exits along the coast, which the orcs patrolled but not perfectly, or they'd tried and been caught, or they'd gone somewhere inside the city that was worse than where they'd been and stopped being visible.

The population was officially twenty thousand but Zima figured the real number was closer to seventeen, and of those seventeen thousand, maybe half were fed enough to work and maybe a quarter were fed enough to fight and the rest were the people you saw sitting in doorways with the hollow faces and a stillness she recognised. They'd stopped planning for tomorrow. She'd started calling them the doorway people, though only in her own head, because giving them a name made them a category and categories were easier to think about than faces.

She passed the old harbourmaster's office, its windows dark, its door hanging from one hinge. She passed the row of net-drying racks that hadn't held nets since the fishing fleet was reduced to six boats running the blockade at night. She passed the place where the wall had cracked in the third month when one of the orc catapults found its range, and the crack had been patched with rubble and tar and a prayer, and the patch was still holding, and nobody talked about what would happen when it didn't.

She didn't sit in doorways. She still planned for tomorrow. But she was aware, in a flat and unpleasant way, that the distance between herself and the doorway people was measured in weeks, not months. If Garrick called in the three weeks she owed him — and he would, eventually, because that's what the ledger was for — she had two options. Pay up or run. She couldn't pay. She didn't know where to run.

So she cut purses in the ration queue and she counted her coins every night and she told herself she'd think of something, because she always had before, and the fact that what she'd thought of before was always just another version of the same thing — steal a little more, owe a little less, keep moving — was a problem she'd learned to not look at directly.

The fog took her. She walked south and the street disappeared behind her and the street appeared ahead of her and the two streets were the same street and she was the only moving thing on it. From the hills, nothing. From the harbour, the slow pulse of water against stone. From the city, the sound a place makes when it's drawing itself inward for the night — doors closing, shutters latching, the small careful noises of people making themselves small.

She kept walking, into the fog, into the evening, and behind her the ration queue dispersed and the distribution table closed and Garrick's men folded their tally sheets and the street went dark, and Ashport settled into another night like all the other nights, and the drums in the hills kept their own time, patient and repetitive and certain, the way only the enemy could afford to be, because the enemy didn't have to hope for anything. The enemy only had to wait.
