CHAPTER NINETEEN
Three Options



Morning came with fog and the sound of someone being sick in the street above the bridge.

Zima listened to them for a while — the retching, the pause, the retching again, the eventual silence that meant either they'd finished or they'd moved on — and she thought about how sound carried in Ashport in the early hours. The fog muffled horizontal sound and amplified vertical sound, so a person vomiting twenty feet above her sounded as if they were standing next to her, while the harbour fifty yards away was a whisper. The city's acoustics changed with the weather. She'd noticed this in her first month sleeping under the bridge and she'd filed it in the part of her mind that stored useful environmental data, alongside the tidal patterns and the patrol schedules and the knowledge of which drains flooded when it rained.

She hadn't slept. The map was in her coat pocket, folded, charcoal-marked, as real and as dangerous as the disc it described. She'd spent the hours between drawing it and dawn lying on the stone, eyes open, running calculations that didn't resolve.

Now it was morning and the calculations still didn't resolve, but the light was coming in through the fog and her body was stiff from a night on cold stone and she needed to eat and she needed to think, and she could do both at the same time if she found somewhere to sit that wasn't the underside of a bridge.

She climbed out of the alcove. The person who'd been sick was gone. The embankment was wet with fog-damp and the first grey light of a day that would be overcast and cold. She walked south toward the harbour, bought a bowl of thin porridge at the dock-front stall for two coppers — the stall woman was one of the last holdouts, cooking grain on a wood fire in an iron pot, selling to the dockworkers and the early-morning fishermen and anyone else who had two coppers and a stomach — and she carried the bowl down to the harbour wall and sat on the stone with her legs hanging over the edge and the harbour below her, invisible in the fog, audible in the lapping and the creak of moored boats.

She ate. She thought.

*          *          *

She had three options. She'd known this since Maela first spoke, and she'd been circling them for days, examining each one from different angles, testing them the way she tested a purse before cutting it — not the cut itself, but the geometry around it. The approach, the exit, the consequences. A good pickpocket didn't just evaluate the lift. She evaluated everything the lift would set in motion.

Option one: ignore.

Throw the disc in the harbour. Walk away from the passages, the vault, the ghost, the map, all of it. Go back to the queues. Pay off Garrick somehow — work harder, steal better, beg Stel for another extension that Garrick would grant because a longer debt was a tighter leash. Live the life she'd been living. The same queues, the same marks, the same count every night under the bridge. Eleven coppers becoming twelve becoming fifteen becoming not enough, and the silvers accumulating with the slow hopelessness of water rising in a hull.

She considered this option. She gave it the time it deserved, which was about thirty seconds.

It wasn't that ignoring was wrong. It was that ignoring was the option a sensible person would take, and sensible people in Ashport were the people who stood in the ration queues and accepted the cuts and adjusted their expectations downward one increment at a time, because that was what sensible people did when the world got smaller. They contracted. They took up less space. They became the version of themselves that fit inside the shrinking room, and they told themselves they were being practical, and the room got smaller, and they got smaller, and one day they were the doorway people — sitting in the grey morning with hollow faces and a stillness that came from having contracted so far that there was nothing left to contract.

Zima knew sensible people. She walked past them every day. She respected their survival instinct and she feared their trajectory, because the trajectory was hers if she let it be. Every day she didn't change the equation was a day the equation changed her, and the direction of the change was always the same: less. Less food, less money, less space, less room between where she was and where the doorway people were. The arithmetic was simple and the arithmetic was merciless and the arithmetic said that ignoring the disc was the same as accepting the current trend line, and the current trend line ended in a doorway.

No.

Option two: sell.

Find a buyer. Take the money. Clear the debt. Let someone else deal with the dead queen and her vaults and her underground passages and her two-hundred-year-old escape plan. This was the smart option. She knew it was the smart option because it was the option that Garrick would have chosen — Garrick, who understood the value of everything and the meaning of nothing, who would have looked at the disc and seen currency and calculated its worth to the nearest half-silver and sold it before the body of the courier was cold.

She could price it. Or she could try. Dav would know the market for pre-Founding artefacts — there were collectors in Ashport, even now, people with money and eccentricity who accumulated old things the way other people accumulated food or influence. The disc would be worth something to them. How much depended on what they thought it was: a curiosity, a relic, a key to something larger. If she sold it as a curiosity — a piece of old metal with interesting markings — she might clear the debt. If she sold it as what it actually was — the key to a vault full of supplies under a dying city — she'd be killed for it long before any money changed hands.

But the problem with selling wasn't the logistics. The logistics were solvable. The problem was simpler and harder and sat in her chest in the place where the disc's warmth had settled.

She'd heard Maela's voice.

That changed things. Not in the dramatic way that stories described — no revelation, no conversion, no sudden understanding that her life had a purpose she hadn't known about. It changed things in a smaller, more specific way: she now knew what the disc was. She knew who had made it. She knew why it existed and what it was meant to do. And selling a thing you understood was different from selling a thing you didn't, the way selling a person's coat was different from selling it while they stood there watching you.

Maela was watching. Or she would be, the next time the voice came. Zima couldn't sell the disc and then face the dead woman's presence in her skull. Not because of guilt — guilt was an emotion she'd learned to manage a long time ago, the way you learned to manage cold or hunger, by acknowledging it and then proceeding. Not because of the promise to remember, though that was part of it. Because selling the disc would make her a specific kind of person — the kind who heard a dead woman's voice and calculated its resale value — and she wasn't that. She was a thief and a liar and a debtor and a lot of other things that she'd stopped apologising for, but she wasn't that.

A week ago, before the voice, selling would have been easy. A week ago the disc was just a warm piece of metal with markings she couldn't read, and the smart move was the profitable move, and she would have done it without looking back. But a week ago felt like a different country now, a place she'd left and couldn't return to, because the border crossing was a dead woman saying pay attention, girl and the passport was stamped and there was no going back through the gate.

No.

Option three: explore.

Go down. Find the access points. Enter the passages. Navigate the branches. Find the vault. Open the door — or the doors, plural, because Maela had said there were two, the first for supplies and the second for the ship. See if the vault was real. See if the contents were real. See if the ship was real, sitting in its underground harbour, canvas and wood and rope preserved for two centuries by whatever engineering Maela had used to keep iron from rusting and stone from crumbling and a dead woman's pattern from fading into nothing.

This was the dangerous option. This was the option that required going underground in a city where two gangs were fighting for control of the surface, where Garrick's Vipers were actively searching for the disc she'd be using to open the door, where the garrison was corrupt and the walls were failing and the orc army in the hills was doing whatever the orc army in the hills was doing. This was the option that could get her killed in a dozen ways she could list and probably a dozen more she couldn't.

She'd already chosen it.

She'd chosen it the way she chose most important things: not through deliberation but through the recognition that the deliberation was performance, a show she put on for the part of her mind that wanted to believe decisions were rational. The rational mind had examined the three options and was still weighing them. The rest of her — the part that cut purses and climbed cranes and slept under bridges and had survived nineteen years in a city that was trying to subtract her — had decided days ago. Maybe the first night, when the voice came. Maybe before that, when the dream of underground water left a map in her head that refused to dissolve. The choice had been made somewhere below the level of conscious thought, in the place where survival calculations happened too fast for words, and the deliberation was just her conscious mind arriving at the station after the train had left.

She sat on the harbour wall and she ate her porridge and she watched the fog and she acknowledged what she'd already done.

*          *          *

The question shifted. Not whether, but how.

She needed things. Practical things, specific things, the kind of things that turned an intention into an operation. She'd been assembling the list in her head since drawing the map, and now she went through it again, item by item, the way she went through a mark's pockets — systematically, nothing skipped, nothing assumed.

Light. The passages would be dark. Not the dark of a room at night, where your eyes adjusted and shapes emerged — the dark of underground, the absolute dark, the kind where your hand in front of your face was a theory. She needed lanterns. Two, at minimum — one primary, one backup. She needed oil. The market oil sellers had stock, though the price had doubled since the ration cut because oil was now competing with food as a survival resource. She had enough coin for one lantern and enough oil for maybe six hours of burn time. That would have to be enough for a first expedition.

Rope. Fifty feet, strong, capable of bearing a person's weight. The market rope sellers were Hook-affiliated, which meant her purchases would be noted and possibly reported. She'd have to find another source. The dockyard cranes still had rigging — she'd thought about this before, in the abstract way you think about things you might need eventually. Now she thought about it specifically. The fourth crane on the eastern quay had cable rigging that looked sound. She could cut a fifty-foot length with her belt-knife if she had an hour and the dock district was empty, which meant an afternoon job during the gap between the morning workers and the evening rough-sleepers.

Patrol intelligence. This was the critical piece. She needed to know which access points were watched, by whom, on what schedule. She needed to know when the patrol gaps occurred — the windows between shifts, the moments when the streets were empty, the fifteen-minute intervals that a person could slip through if she knew exactly when they opened. She needed to know whether Garrick's search for the disc had intensified or slackened, whether the Vipers were still actively looking or had moved on to other priorities, whether the gang fight at the market had redirected their attention or sharpened it.

That was Lirra. The baker on Mercer Lane with the coded bread and the intelligence network and the mismatched delivery schedule. Lirra had the information. Lirra lived in the independent district, near the access point Zima had identified as the best approach route. Lirra was smart and careful and connected to a web of informants that Zima couldn't replicate on her own in the time she had.

Someone she could trust. She needed at least one other person. Maybe more, depending on what the vault contained and how much of it she wanted to move. Someone who could carry things she couldn't carry. Someone who could watch a tunnel entrance while she went deeper. Someone who could read the old script on the disc, because Maela's explanations were useful but partial, and the markings on the disc face might contain information the ghost hadn't mentioned or didn't remember or chose not to share.

Three roles. Three people she didn't have.

Lirra for intelligence. She was the closest thing Zima had to a working relationship in the independent district — eight months of buying bread, occasional exchanges of information, the kind of cautious mutual awareness that passed for trust in Ashport. Lirra wouldn't do it for free. She'd want to know what it was for. Zima would have to tell her enough to make the ask worthwhile without telling her everything, and Lirra was smart enough to know the difference, and the negotiation would be its own kind of dangerous.

Someone strong. She didn't have a name for this. She didn't know anyone strong enough and available enough and desperate enough. The dockworkers she knew were either Viper-affiliated or too visible to recruit. The independent labourers were scattered and unreliable. She'd need to find someone, and finding someone required looking, and looking required time she was running out of.

Eldrin for the script. The scholar at the Wayfarers Hall. Dav's description: Ironmore University, ancient languages, Tidal Script, sacked, impoverished, sleeping at the Hall. She hadn't met him. She didn't know if he'd help or if he could be trusted or if his expertise was real. But he was the only lead she had on someone who could read what was written on the disc, and the disc's markings might be the difference between opening the vault and standing in front of a locked door in the dark.

Three people. Three unknowns. Three conversations she hadn't had yet, each one carrying the risk of exposure — the risk that the person she approached would talk, or sell the information, or go to Garrick, or simply refuse and leave her worse off than before.

She finished the porridge. The bowl was clay, chipped at the rim. She set it on the wall beside her and looked at the fog where the harbour should have been.

*          *          *

The thing that tipped the balance — the thing underneath the three options and the logistics and the lists — was curiosity.

She didn't use that word for it. Curiosity was a word for scholars and children, for people who had the luxury of wondering about things without needing the answers to survive. What she had was something harder, more specific: a need to know. Not a want. A need, the way hunger was a need, the way the body's requirement for water was a need. She needed to know what was in the vault. She needed to know if the ship was real. She needed to know what Maela had built and why and whether any of it could change the arithmetic that was slowly subtracting her from existence.

Garrick had given her most of what she was. The craft, the debt, the territory, the understanding of how a city worked at the level of pockets and ledgers and the exchange of small violences for smaller obediences. He'd taken a nine-year-old from a doorway and installed in her the tools and the obligations that made a useful Viper asset, and she'd spent ten years becoming what he'd made her, and the debt was the proof that she was still his, still inside his system, still running on the track he'd laid.

But the need to know — the thing that made her lie awake thinking about underground passages instead of sleeping, the thing that made her draw maps instead of counting coins, the thing that made her choose the dangerous option over the safe one and the smart one without hesitation — that was hers. Garrick hadn't put it there. The street hadn't put it there. The siege hadn't put it there. It existed in the part of her that predated Garrick's doorway and Garrick's warehouse and Garrick's ledger, the part that had been a nine-year-old sitting in a doorway on Wick Street and looking at the city and wanting to understand how it worked, not to exploit it but to know it, the way you wanted to know the shape of the room you were locked in.

The disc had found her because of something the mechanism recognised. Maela couldn't explain what. The terms were two hundred years old and the mechanism interpreted them in its own way and the locksmith didn't know every key. But Zima thought she knew, or she thought she could guess. The mechanism had tested everyone who touched the disc — the warmth, the dreams, the voice — and most people had been the wrong shape. Wrong for the lock. They'd felt the warmth and pocketed the disc as a curiosity, the way Pell had pocketed it, and the mechanism had registered them and found them insufficient and the disc had moved on.

Zima hadn't pocketed the disc as a curiosity. She'd pocketed it as a problem. She'd examined it, measured it, categorised its wrongness, and then she'd waited — not to sell it, not to use it, but to understand it. The need to know. The mechanism had found that and recognised it and the rest had followed: the dream, the map, the voice, the conversation with a dead woman who'd built a city and been forgotten and wanted someone to remember.

She sat on the harbour wall and she thought about this for a while. The fog shifted. The harbour sounds changed as the morning traffic began — the fishing boats returning, the dock crews stirring, the slow resumption of activity that passed for normal in a city that was running on a quarter of its capacity.

She didn't believe the disc had been looking for her specifically. She didn't believe in destiny or fate or the grand cosmic machinery that the temple stories described, where every person had a purpose and the gods arranged events to fulfil it. She believed in timing and position and the willingness to act when the moment arrived. The disc had circulated for two hundred years and she'd been the person touching it at the right moment, and the right moment was defined by whatever criteria Maela had embedded in the mechanism, and one of those criteria — maybe the primary one, maybe the only one — was the need to know what was underneath.

That was enough. It was the only explanation she had and it would have to be enough, because the alternative was believing that a piece of metal had opinions about her character, and she was willing to accept a lot of strangeness — the warmth, the dreams, the ghost — but she drew the line at being flattered by an object.

She picked up the bowl. She carried it back to the stall and set it on the counter and the stall woman took it without speaking, because speech was expensive in the early morning and neither of them was spending.

Zima walked south.

*          *          *

Lirra first. The bakery. The back door. Tomorrow.

Not today. Today she'd do the groundwork. She'd walk the route from the Coppergate bridge to Mercer Lane and time it. She'd note the patrol positions — which corners had Viper men, which had Hook men, which had neither. She'd pass the bakery without going in, checking the hours, the foot traffic, the pattern of customers at the front and whoever came and went at the back.

She'd walk past the old city access point too. South of Mercer Lane, Maela had said. She didn't know the exact location — the dream-map placed it in the old city district but the specificity she'd had for the dock entrance was absent here. She'd have to look for the same signs: older stone, a drain cover or similar access point, the darker grain of Maela's original construction. That might take hours. It might take days.

But she had the map, and the map had given her a starting district, and a starting district was better than nothing, and nothing was what she'd had two weeks ago. Two weeks ago she was a pickpocket with a debt and a bridge. Now she was a pickpocket with a debt and a bridge and a map and a ghost and a piece of dark metal that talked to her, and the difference between those two states was that the second one had direction. Not safety. Not comfort. Not the resolution of any of her immediate problems. But direction — a line on the map of her life that pointed somewhere other than the doorway she was heading for.

She walked south along the harbour wall. The fog was lifting — not clearing, just rising, the layer between the rooftops and the ground thinning enough that she could see thirty feet ahead instead of twenty. People were on the streets now. The ration queues would form in an hour. The gangs would resume their territorial choreography. The garrison soldiers would walk the walls and pretend the walls were holding and the orc army in the hills would beat its drums and pretend the drums were patience and the city would go on dying by inches, the same way it died every day.

And Zima would go to the bakery. Tomorrow. The back door. She'd ask Lirra for intelligence and she'd frame it as a job she was working and Lirra would know there was more and Lirra would wait, because Lirra was patient in the way that smart people were patient — not passively but strategically, the patience of someone who knew that information given freely was less valuable than information earned.

Tomorrow was the beginning of something. She could feel it the way she could feel the disc — not in her mind, not in her thoughts, but in her body, in the low persistent warmth below her ribs where Maela's key sat against her skin through the canvas. The beginning of an operation. The first move in a sequence that would either lead somewhere or collapse, and the only way to know which was to make the move.

She turned up Harbour Lane. The fog took her footsteps and gave back nothing, the way it always did, and the drums hadn't started yet, and the morning was quiet and grey and cold, and Zima walked through it with a folded map in one pocket and a dead woman's key in the other and the specific, sharp-edged awareness that she had just committed herself to something she couldn't take back.

Lirra first. Then the rest of it.
