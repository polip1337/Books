CHAPTER TWENTY-TWO
The Coded Loaf



Lirra was waiting at the back door when Zima arrived the next morning. Not baking. Sitting on the worktable with her boots crossed at the ankle and two cups of chicory already poured.

"Close the door," Lirra said.

Zima closed it. The latch was a simple hook — the kind that kept out wind but not people. She sat on the stool and took the cup Lirra pushed toward her. The chicory was bitter and slightly burnt, the way Lirra always made it, as though the burning were deliberate.

"I checked something," Lirra said. "Garrick has been visiting the harbour records building. Three times in the last three weeks. Always in the evening, always with Stel. The harbour clerk is named Marten. He charges two silvers an hour for after-hours access to the founding-era documents."

Zima set the cup down. "Founding-era."

"Maps. Property records. Construction orders. The kind of documents nobody looks at because nobody cares about two-hundred-year-old drainage plans." Lirra paused. "Except apparently Garrick does."

"How do you know this?"

"Because Marten is one of my six." Lirra said it without emphasis, the way she might have said Marten buys rye. "He gets a loaf on Tuesdays and Fridays. The Tuesday loaf has a clay pellet inside with the week's harbour manifests. The Friday loaf has patrol rosters for the dock-district garrison."

Zima stared at her.

"You bake intelligence reports into bread."

"I bake bread. The intelligence is a secondary product." Lirra's real eye held steady. The glass one caught the morning light through the back window and returned it fractured. "Six clients. Specific addresses on specific days. Each one gives me something and receives something. Marten gives me harbour records. I give him garrison gossip from two other clients. Everybody trades in what they know."

"Who else?"

"That's not yours."

"It is if we're working together."

"We're not working together yet. We're having chicory."

Zima drank. The bitterness settled in her throat. She studied the room — the shelves, the proving baskets, the ledger on the stool where she'd sat yesterday. The ledger was open today. The page showed delivery addresses in a neat, small hand, with numbers beside each one. Loaf counts. Or something that looked like loaf counts.

"The pellet I found," Zima said. "Three months ago. In a loaf I bought from the front counter."

Lirra went still. Not the controlled stillness of someone preparing a lie — the sharp stillness of someone caught.

"That wasn't for you."

"I know. It was for whoever bought the loaf before me. Dock Street, the boarding house on the corner. I'd been watching your route. You made six deliveries that day, but five of them were to addresses on the regular list. The sixth was new."

"You counted my deliveries."

"I count everything."

Lirra's mouth moved. The almost-smile from yesterday. "What did you do with it?"

"Put it back. Bought the same loaf the next day and left it at the boarding house door." Zima set the cup down. "I didn't know what you were doing, but I knew it was something, and I knew interfering with something you don't understand is a good way to make enemies you can't see."

Lirra looked at her for a long time. The oven wasn't lit this morning — the room was cooler, the light thinner. Without the heat the bakery smelled of dry flour and old wood and the faint chemical bite of the lye Lirra used to clean the worktable.

"That was the correct decision," Lirra said.

"I know."

"And the incorrect conclusion. I don't work for anyone. The network is mine. I built it, I maintain it, and I decide what moves through it."

"Everyone works for someone."

"Then I'm the exception that should worry you."

The words sat between them like a coin on a table — offered but not yet picked up. Zima turned her chicory cup in her hands. The clay was rough, unglazed, the kind the potters on Salt Lane made for a half-copper each.

"The pellets," she said. "How do they work?"

Lirra hesitated — not the hesitation of someone deciding whether to lie, but the hesitation of someone deciding how much truth to release. She reached under the worktable and brought out a small clay ball, no bigger than a thumbnail. Cracked it open between her fingers. Inside: a curl of paper the width of a bootlace, covered in tiny marks.

"Fired clay," Lirra said. "Baked inside the loaf. The heat doesn't reach the paper — the clay insulates. To read it you have to break the bread, find the pellet, crack it. If you don't know it's there you eat around it. If you do know, you break it open and read."

"And the marks?"

"Mine. Shorthand I developed. Nobody else reads it."

Zima picked up the clay fragments. They were light, porous, the colour of river mud. The paper inside was thin enough to see through. The marks were tiny — not letters, not numbers, something between the two.

"How long have you been doing this?"

"Four years. Since the siege started, the information market got interesting." Lirra took the fragments back and dropped them in her pocket. "People who know things want to know other things. I'm the exchange. The bread is the delivery method. The bakery is the cover."

"And nobody's caught on?"

"Two people have. You're one of them." The real eye and the glass eye both aimed at Zima. "The other one stopped being a problem."

She said it flat, without weight. Zima didn't ask what stopped being a problem meant. She filed it.

*          *          *

They sat in the cool room and Lirra talked.

Not freely — each piece of information was considered, weighed, and released like a portion of dough cut from a larger batch. But she talked.

Marten at the harbour records building: Garrick had asked about founding-era construction. Specifically, about underground works predating the current sewer system. Marten had pulled three sets of plans, all incomplete, all showing fragments of a passage network that didn't connect to anything the city currently maintained.

"Garrick drew on the plans," Lirra said. "Marten watched him. He was connecting fragments. Making a map."

The sentence landed in Zima's stomach like cold water.

"He's looking for the same thing I found."

"He's looking for something. He doesn't have what you have." Lirra nodded at Zima's left side. "That thing in your coat is the difference between looking and finding."

"But he knows the passages exist."

"He knows something exists. He had the courier — Pell — carrying the disc somewhere, which means he found it first and was moving it to someone who could read it. A scholar, an appraiser, I don't know. The market fight happened. Pell died. The disc went into your hand instead."

Zima thought about that. Garrick had possessed the disc. Had held what she now held. Had felt the warmth, the weight that was wrong. Had he heard Maela? She didn't think so. The voice had come to her after days, and it had addressed her specifically — pay attention, girl — as though Zima were the intended recipient and everyone before her had been postage.

"How much does he know?" Zima asked.

"More than you'd like. Less than he needs. He knows the passages exist. He knows the disc is a key. He doesn't know where the access points are — the founding-era plans don't show them. And he doesn't have the disc anymore."

"Which is why he's searching."

"Which is why he's searching quietly, with Stel, through channels that don't involve the rest of the Vipers knowing what he's actually after." Lirra finished her chicory. "He hasn't told his own people what the disc does. He told them it's a valuable artefact. Stolen property. He wants it back for personal reasons, not gang business."

"Personal reasons."

"Garrick is a businessman who runs a gang. The gang is the instrument. The business is the point. Whatever's in those passages, he wants it for himself, not for the Vipers."

Zima turned the empty cup in her hands. The clay was warm from her palms. The disc against her ribs pulsed — slow, slow, like the beat of a sleeping animal.

"I need to get down there before he figures out the access points."

"Yes." Lirra set her cup on the table. "You do."

*          *          *

They didn't discuss terms that day. Lirra sent her away with a loaf — a real one, no pellet — and told her to come back when she'd decided how much to tell.

Zima walked back through the thinning fog. It was midmorning now and the streets were filling with the cautious traffic of a city under siege — people moving in pairs, staying close to walls, carrying what they'd queued for in bags pressed tight against their bodies. The ration cut had changed the geometry of crowds. People were thinner and quicker and held their parcels differently, with both hands.

She found a sheltered doorway on the harbour wall and sat with the loaf in her lap. The bread was good — dense, slightly sour, the crumb tight enough to last three days if she rationed it. She ate a quarter and wrapped the rest in the cloth Lirra had tucked around it.

Below her the harbour lay grey and flat under the overcast sky. The orc blockade ships sat beyond the breakwater in the same positions they'd held for eleven months — heavy-hulled warships with iron plating on the waterline, riding low, patient. Fifteen thousand soldiers in the hills. Two hundred and some days. No attack. Just the drums and the ships and the slow economic strangulation that was doing what catapults hadn't.

She counted the ships. Fourteen. The same fourteen. One of them had raised a new pennant — dark red, which she hadn't seen before. She filed it.

The disc pulsed against her ribs. Not the slow sleeping rhythm but something quicker, a double-beat that she associated with proximity. Maela's voice came, faint and thin, like something heard through several walls.

"She's sharp."

Zima didn't answer aloud. She'd learned that the voice didn't need speech — thought was enough, or at least strong thought directed inward with intention.

You were listening.

"I'm always listening. The bridge is narrow but it works both ways." A pause. The disc cooled slightly, as though the effort of speaking drew warmth away from the metal. "She has the right kind of mind. Practical. She won't panic when the passages get dark."

Zima tore another piece of bread. You could have told me Garrick was researching the passages.

"I didn't know. I can feel the city — walls, water, stone. I can't read documents. I knew someone was probing, the way you'd feel a hand on a door you were standing behind. I didn't know it was your Garrick."

He's not my Garrick.

"He owns your debt. That makes him yours, whether you like it or not." The voice faded. The disc settled to its baseline warmth.

Zima ate bread and watched the harbour and thought about a man who ran a gang like a business and researched founding-era documents after hours. Garrick wasn't stupid. She'd known this. But the gap between knowing someone was intelligent and understanding that they were pursuing the same object you carried against your ribs — that gap was the width of a knife, and she'd been walking along its edge without looking down.

She wrapped the remaining bread, tucked it inside her coat on the right side — the opposite side from the disc, a separation she maintained without examining why — and walked back toward the dock district.

Tomorrow she'd return to the bakery. Tomorrow they'd talk numbers.

Today she needed to look at a drain cover.
