CHAPTER EIGHTEEN
Drawing the Map



She went to the dock district. She found the alley. She found the drain cover.

It took an hour of walking and another hour of looking, because the old customs house wasn't called that anymore and hadn't been for at least a generation. The building she was looking for turned out to be a long stone structure on the harbour side of Dock Lane, three storeys, half its windows boarded, currently serving as an overflow storage for the fishing fleet's diminishing catch. Nobody had called it the customs house in living memory. But the stone at its base was different — darker, denser, with a visible grain that ran horizontal instead of the vertical grain of the later construction. Older. Maela's stone.

The alley ran along the building's east side, narrow, choked with debris — broken crate slats, a coil of rotted rope, the bones of a fish that had been dragged there by a cat and forgotten. The drain cover was at the far end, set into the ground where the alley met the harbour wall. Round, iron, with a lifting ring sunk flush into its surface. The stone around it was the same darker stone as the customs house foundation. Seamless. The drain cover and the building and the stone were all one piece of construction, all built at the same time, all older than the walls.

Zima crouched beside it. She touched the iron. Cold, salt-rough, spotted with surface rust but not deeply corroded. Two hundred years in harbour air should have eaten the iron to nothing, but this ring still held its shape, the way the disc still held its warmth. Maela's engineering.

She didn't try to lift it. She was alone, in daylight, in a district the Vipers controlled. Two of Garrick's dock men worked the warehouse three buildings north, and their afternoon circuit brought them past this alley at irregular intervals. She'd timed them on the way in — roughly every forty minutes, give or take the amount of time they spent smoking at the corner. She had maybe twenty minutes left in the current gap.

She looked at the drain cover. She memorised its position — sixteen paces from the alley mouth, four feet from the harbour wall, two feet below the foundation line of the customs house. Then she stood and walked out of the alley and back through the dock district, keeping her pace unremarkable, and she was three streets away before the patrol came back around the corner.

Verification: one access point confirmed. Three to go.

*          *          *

She didn't sleep that night.

The map was in her head and it was growing. Every time she closed her eyes, the underground network was there — the four branches radiating from the central vault like the spokes of a wheel, the access points at the surface like manholes into a system nobody knew existed. The dream-version had been abstract: shapes, directions, a sense of where things connected. But now she'd stood above one of the access points. She'd touched the iron ring. She'd seen the stone. And the abstract map was becoming specific, gaining texture and dimension, the way a sketch becomes a painting when you start adding the details that make it real.

She lay under the bridge and she tried to sleep and the map wouldn't let her.

At some point — an hour after full dark, maybe, the harbour sounds settling into their nighttime rhythm of lapping water and distant creaking — she sat up and reached into her coat for the piece of paper she'd been carrying since the first week. The market woman's purse, the one from Chapter One: six coppers, a brass button, and a folded square of paper with an address on Cutter Street. She'd kept the paper because addresses were sometimes worth something. The address side was still illegible in the dark, the ink faded and the fold worn, but the back was blank.

She had a piece of charcoal. She carried it in the same pocket as the paper, because charcoal was useful for marking routes, leaving signals, and writing numbers on walls — the small practical utilities of someone who navigated the city by memory and needed to leave notes for herself in places no one else would think to look.

She smoothed the paper on the stone beside her. She held the charcoal the way she held the small knife — between thumb and forefinger, light grip, precise control. And she drew.

The vault first. A circle in the centre of the paper, the size of a thumbnail. She didn't know the vault's exact shape but she knew its position — below everything, dead centre, the hub that the passages radiated from.

The four branches. Lines extending from the circle toward the edges of the paper, one in each direction. One toward the harbour. One northeast toward the Harbour Gate and the northern wall. One west toward the market. One south toward the old city. She drew them as she'd dreamed them — not straight, not perfectly symmetrical, but with the organic curves of passages that had been dug through bedrock by people who followed the stone's grain rather than fighting it.

The four access points. Small squares at the end of each branch, where the underground passages met the surface. She placed them carefully, thinking about what she knew.

North: the drain cover she'd found today. The dock-district alley, behind the old customs house. Viper territory. She wrote a small V beside it.

East: the Harbour Gate. She hadn't scouted this one yet, but Maela had said it was there, and the dream-map placed it at the base of the gate, near the wall's foundation. This was contested ground — the gate itself was sealed, the wall walk above it manned by garrison soldiers, and the streets around it shifted between Viper and Hook influence depending on the day. She wrote a small C beside it. Contested.

West: the market's northern edge. She thought about this one carefully. The market geography had changed since the gang fight — the Hooks had expanded west, pushing into territory that had been neutral a month ago. The northern edge of the market was now Hook-controlled, or close to it. She wrote a small H.

South: the old city, south of Mercer Lane. This was the interesting one. Mercer Lane sat in a district that neither gang had fully claimed — a narrow strip of old city between the Viper core and the Hook expansion, populated by independents and small traders and people like Lirra, who operated in the spaces between factions. Not safe, exactly, but not owned. She wrote a small I. Independent.

She sat back and looked at the map.

*          *          *

It was rough. Crude. A few lines of charcoal on the back of a stolen piece of paper, drawn in the dark under a bridge by a woman who had no training in cartography and was working from a dream and a dead woman's description. It looked like something a child would draw — the kind of map you'd find in a child's game, the kind where X marked a spot that didn't exist.

But it was also, she was fairly certain, accurate. The dream had given her the layout with the specificity of direct knowledge — not a suggestion, not an impression, but the hard clarity of something seen. And Maela's description matched. Two independent sources saying the same thing was either corroboration or conspiracy, and if a dead queen's ghost was conspiring with a pickpocket's dreams, the situation was beyond her ability to evaluate and she might as well treat the information as reliable until it proved otherwise.

She studied the map. She overlaid it, in her mind, on the city she knew.

The access points sat under the oldest parts of Ashport. That made sense. Maela had said she built the passages before the walls, which meant the passages predated the current city — they were part of the original construction, the bedrock layer that everything else had been built on top of. The dock district, the Harbour Gate, the old market site, the old city south of Mercer Lane — these were the founding-era districts, the places where Maela's fourteen-year construction project had started, where the stone was different and the foundations went deeper.

The vault was in the centre. Below the newer construction, below the sewer system that the city engineers had laid over Maela's original drainage channels, below the foundations of buildings whose builders had never known what they were building on top of. Two centuries of city stacked above a room full of supplies and a ship in a sea cave, and nobody had found it because nobody had been looking and nobody had had the key.

She thought about the sewer system. Ashport's sewers were a later addition — built in the century after Maela's death, according to the kind of general knowledge that filtered through a city without anyone trying to learn it. The sewers used the natural drainage channels as their foundation, which meant they intersected with Maela's passage network at certain points. The drain cover in the dock district was one of those intersections — a place where the old passages and the later sewers met, where two eras of construction overlapped.

That meant the passages might be partially flooded. Sewer water running through channels that Maela had built for drainage and ventilation, two centuries of city waste and harbour overflow finding its way into a network designed for something else. The passages would be wet. They might be blocked. They might be dangerous in ways that had nothing to do with gangs or guards.

She made a note on the map: a small W beside each access point. Water. She'd need to account for that.

*          *          *

Then she started thinking like a pickpocket planning a job.

A job had three phases: approach, execution, departure. The approach was how you got to the mark without being noticed. The execution was the lift itself — the cut, the dip, the moment of contact. The departure was how you got away clean. Every phase had its own risks and every risk had its own mitigation, and the difference between a good pickpocket and a dead one was the quality of the planning in each phase.

Approach. Which of the four access points could she reach without being seen? The dock-district entrance was in Viper territory — she'd confirmed that today. Getting to it required navigating Garrick's patrols, which ran on a roughly forty-minute cycle with enough variation to make prediction unreliable. The Harbour Gate entrance was contested and manned, which made it the worst option. The market entrance was Hook-controlled, which was less dangerous for her specifically — the Hooks weren't looking for her, or weren't looking for her as hard as the Vipers were — but Hook territory had its own surveillance, its own patrols, its own ways of noticing people who didn't belong.

The old city entrance. South of Mercer Lane. Independent territory. No gang patrols, or sporadic ones at most. The streets down there were quieter, emptier, populated by the kind of people who minded their own business because minding other people's business required energy they didn't have. The old city was the closest thing Ashport had to ungoverned space.

That was the approach. The old city entrance, south of Mercer Lane.

Execution. Getting underground required opening the access point, which presumably required the disc. The drain cover in the dock district had an iron lifting ring — she assumed the other access points would be similar, though she hadn't verified. She'd need light. She'd need rope. She'd need to move through the passages without getting lost, which meant memorising the map she'd drawn or — she looked at the paper in her hand — carrying it. She'd need to find the vault. She'd need the disc to open it.

She could do most of that alone. The light and the rope were procurement problems — solvable, with effort and time. The navigation was a memory problem, and her memory was good. The disc was in her coat and the vault would open or it wouldn't, and if it didn't then none of the planning mattered anyway.

What she couldn't do alone was the departure. If the vault contained what Maela described — supplies, currency, equipment, a ship — then getting it out required more than one pair of hands. She'd need help carrying things. She'd need someone watching the access point while she was underground. She'd need intelligence about what was happening on the surface, because the gangs didn't stop moving just because she was in a tunnel.

Departure required people. People she could trust. People who could carry things and keep secrets and move through the city without attracting the kind of attention that got everyone killed.

She looked at the map again. The four access points. The four branches. The vault in the centre, holding everything Maela had prepared. A two-hundred-year-old escape plan, designed by a dead queen, accessible through a disc that a pickpocket carried in her coat lining.

She thought about the people she knew.

Dav the fence: useful, connected, amoral. He'd sell her out if the price was right. Not an option.

The silence-payment people — the three who knew about her alcove: two dockworkers and a woman who sold candles at the harbour market. They were paid to forget she existed, not to help her exist. Not options.

Garrick's people: obviously not.

The Hook network: she didn't have contacts there. The Hooks were the enemy of her enemy, which in Ashport's geometry made them people who might kill her slightly later than the Vipers would. Not options.

Lirra. The name rose in her mind with a specificity that surprised her. She'd been thinking about Lirra without realising it — the way a problem you've been working on in the background suddenly surfaces with a shape you didn't expect.

Lirra ran the bakery on Mercer Lane. Zima had never been inside — had never had reason to, not directly — but she knew about Lirra's operation the way she knew about most of the city's unofficial infrastructure: through the slow accumulation of overhead conversation and observed pattern. A woman who baked bread and moved secrets through her deliveries, coded notes slipped into paper wrappings, information traded kitchen to kitchen.

Lirra was smart, from what Zima had observed at a distance. The bakery's deliveries varied in ways that didn't match normal commercial patterns — extra loaves to addresses that weren't regular customers, timing shifts that corresponded to events in the city. Zima had watched the operation from across Mercer Lane on three occasions and concluded that bread wasn't the only thing moving through those routes. An intelligence network, disguised as commerce. The kind of operation that survived in Ashport by being invisible to anyone who wasn't looking.

Lirra's bakery was on Mercer Lane, in the independent district, near the old city access point that Zima had identified as the best approach route.

If Zima needed patrol intelligence — Viper movements, Hook positions, garrison schedules — Lirra was the person who had it. And Lirra's bakery was six streets from the access point Zima wanted to use, which made her geographically convenient as well as informationally useful.

Zima wrote a small L beside the southern access point on the map. Lirra. Then she put the charcoal down and looked at what she'd drawn.

*          *          *

She also needed someone who could read the old script.

The disc had markings. Maela had explained what they meant in broad terms — a tidal chart, a passage schematic, an inventory — but Zima couldn't read them herself. She'd looked at the markings a dozen times since taking the disc, and they remained what they'd been the first time: geometric patterns that suggested meaning without revealing it, the visual equivalent of a language she didn't speak.

Dav had mentioned a scholar. Eldrin. Ironmore University, sacked, sleeping at the Wayfarers Hall, appraising objects when he could stomach it. Dav had described him as someone who read old scripts — specifically Tidal Script, the pre-Founding writing system that appeared on the disc's face. Dav had also said that Eldrin had left the university because his findings were disturbing, which was the kind of detail that could mean anything from academic disagreement to something considerably worse.

She hadn't met Eldrin. She knew his name, his location, and his area of expertise, which was enough for preliminary contact but not enough for trust. She wrote a small E on the map, off to the side, not connected to any access point. A resource, not yet a participant.

And she needed someone strong. The vault, if it contained what Maela described — crates, chests, sealed containers — would require physical labour to empty. Zima could carry her own weight and had the endurance of someone who walked ten miles a day through a city that was mostly uphill, but she weighed a hundred and twelve pounds and her upper body strength was adequate for cutting purses and climbing cranes and exactly nothing else. She needed someone who could lift heavy things and carry them through underground passages and do it without complaining or collapsing.

She didn't have a name for that. She didn't know anyone strong enough and trustworthy enough and desperate enough to go underground with a pickpocket on the word of a ghost. That was a gap in the plan, and gaps in plans were how plans failed.

She looked at the map. The charcoal lines were crude but legible, even in the dim light that filtered under the bridge from the harbour. The vault. The four branches. The four access points with their territorial markers — V, C, H, I. The initials — L for Lirra, E for Eldrin. The small W beside each access point for water.

She looked at it for a long time.

This was the first time the thing in her head had become a thing in the world. For three days the map had existed only in the space between her skull and the disc — a shared hallucination, a dream that two sources confirmed, an architecture that she could feel but not prove. Now it was on paper. Charcoal on the back of a stolen address from a market woman's purse, rough and imprecise, but real. Real in the way that things you could touch and fold and put in your pocket were real, which was the only kind of real that had ever mattered to Zima.

It looked different on paper. Smaller. The vast underground network that filled her dreams and pressed against the inside of her skull was, in charcoal, a few lines and a few squares on a piece of paper the size of her palm. The vault was a circle the size of her thumbnail. The passages that she imagined as dark stone corridors stretching into the earth were lines no longer than her finger.

Smaller. More possible. Also more dangerous, because a thing that fit on a piece of paper was a thing that someone else could find. She folded the map and tucked it into her coat, in the inner pocket opposite the disc. She felt the paper settle against the fabric and the disc pulse once through the canvas — a single beat of warmth, like an acknowledgment. Or she imagined it. The line between the disc doing things and her imagining the disc doing things had been blurring since the first night, and she wasn't sure she'd ever find it again.

The fog was thinning. Dawn was coming — she could feel it in the air, the slight warming, the shift in the harbour sounds as the fishing boats began their pre-dawn preparations. Somewhere in the hills, the drums would start again soon. Somewhere in the city, Garrick's men would resume their search for a disc that was sewn into her coat, four inches from a map that could lead them to everything the disc was meant to open.

She closed her eyes. She counted her breathing. She had a map and a ghost and a piece of metal and the beginning of a plan, and tomorrow she'd start turning the beginning into a middle.
