CHAPTER FIFTEEN
The Voice



Two nights after the dream, the disc started getting warmer after dark.

She noticed it on the first night — a slight increase, barely perceptible, like the difference between holding a stone that's been in your pocket and holding one that's been sitting near a fire. The warmth climbed for an hour or so after full dark, reached a peak she could track through the canvas of her coat, and then settled back to its usual resting temperature, the steady ambient heat she'd reluctantly come to think of as normal. She noticed the increase and she filed it and she didn't do anything about it, because doing something about it would have required knowing what something to do was, and she didn't.

The second night, it was stronger.

She'd worked the afternoon queue at the Harbour Gate. Thin crowd, thin takes. She'd eaten the ration bread on the walk back and counted her coins under the bridge — total holdings: four silvers, eleven coppers, which put her five silvers and one copper short of Garrick's debt, and Stel still hadn't come — and then she'd lain down in the alcove with her coat pulled around her, the disc against her ribs, and tried to sleep.

The fog was thick. A proper Ashport night, the kind where the city disappeared into itself and the only things that existed were the immediate: the stone at her back, the damp in the air, the taste of salt, the sound of the harbour. She was thinking about nothing in particular. The day's take. The split in her left boot sole, which had widened by a quarter inch since yesterday and was going to let water in the next time it rained. Whether the bread she'd bought from the woman on Harbour Lane had been made with cut flour — it had tasted wrong, gritty and flat, and she'd eaten it anyway because gritty flat bread was still bread and bread was bread.

The disc was warm. Warmer than yesterday. Warmer than the daytime normal, which she'd calibrated over six days of wearing it and which she now carried in her mind the way she carried the price of salt fish or the distance between the bridge and the Harbour Gate — precise, unconscious, a reference point she didn't have to think about to use.

She'd been lying there for about an hour — she knew it was an hour because she'd counted the intervals between the harbour bell, which rang every quarter, and four quarters had passed since she'd lain down — when the temperature climbed again. Not gradually this time. A step-change. The way a coal shifts from smouldering to hot when the draught catches it. The heat went from noticeable to insistent, pressing through the canvas into her skin, and she put her hand over the disc through the coat and felt the warmth in her palm and thought: something is happening.

Then the air changed.

Not the fog. Not the temperature. Something else — a thickness, like the air in the alcove had gained weight, had become denser without becoming heavier, the way water is denser than air without being heavier drop for drop. She felt it on her skin. She felt it in her ears, the way your ears feel when you drop below the surface of a pool — a pressure, a fullness, the sense of being enclosed by something she couldn't see.

And then the thickness resolved.

Very slowly, the way a face resolves out of fog. A sound. Then a sound with shape. Then a sound with shape that was a word. Then a sentence.

Pay attention, girl. I don't have the patience to say things twice.

*          *          *

Zima sat up. Her hand went to her belt-knife. The blade cleared the sheath before her mind had caught up with her arm, and she was on her feet with her back to the wall and the knife in front of her in the time it took to breathe twice. Two breaths. She counted them.

The alcove was empty.

She scanned it. Left to right, floor to ceiling, the way she scanned every space she entered — automatically, thoroughly, the eyes doing their work while the rest of her held still. The alcove was eight feet deep and five feet wide and she knew every surface. The drainpipe on the left. The crack in the wall on the right where the mortar had separated. The low shelf of stone where she kept her water jug. The darker patch of ground where the earth showed through. Nothing had changed. Nothing was out of place. No footprints in the dirt. No shadow where a shadow shouldn't be.

She looked up. The bridge above was empty — the underside of the arch, grey stone, the seam where the bridge met the embankment. Nobody could stand there. Nobody could hang there. She looked at the street beyond the alcove's mouth. Fog and silence. The distant harbour. The faint glow of a watch-fire somewhere on the northern wall, orange through the grey.

Nobody was there.

She held the knife and she listened. Her breathing was fast — not racing, she told herself, not panicking, just quick. The steady acceleration of someone who was frightened in a practical way and dealing with it. She counted: three breaths in, four breaths out. A method she'd learned years ago. The fastest way to die in a fight was to forget to breathe. The second fastest was to breathe too fast. She slowed herself. Three in. Four out. Five repetitions. Ten.

The knife was steady. Her hand was steady. Her heart was going, but she'd made her peace with that — you couldn't slow your heart the way you slowed your breathing, you could only wait for the breathing to do the work, and after ten cycles her heart began to settle into something manageable.

She listened for footsteps. For breathing. For the scrape of a boot on stone, the creak of weight on timber, the small involuntary sounds that a human body made when it was close and trying to be quiet. She knew these sounds. She'd spent five years listening for them in queues and crowds and alleys, the sounds that told you someone was near and meant harm or meant theft or meant both.

Nothing.

The harbour moved against the pilings. The fog pressed in. A ship's bell rang, distant, one of the fishing boats at its mooring. That was all.

The words replayed in her head. Pay attention, girl. I don't have the patience to say things twice.

A woman's voice. She was certain of that, even though she'd only heard it for a moment, even though it had come from nowhere she could point to. A woman, older than Zima, with the flat authority of a person who expected to be listened to and who would not ask again. The words hadn't come from the left or the right. Hadn't come from above or below. Hadn't come from the street, or from the bridge, or from any direction she could identify. They'd come from everywhere at once, or from nowhere, or — and this was the thought she held at arm's length like a thing that might bite — from inside.

Inside her head. In the place where her own thoughts lived.

She stood against the wall for a long time. The knife didn't go away. She didn't sit down. She waited, and she listened, and the disc was hot against her ribs, and the alcove was silent.

*          *          *

She didn't sleep. She sat with the knife across her knees and her back to the wall and the disc pressing its heat into her side and she watched the alcove's mouth and she waited.

The harbour bell rang. Quarter past. She counted.

The bell rang again. Half past.

She thought about explanations. She lined them up the way she'd line up stolen goods on a fence's bench — one by one, in order, checking each for value.

One: she was hearing things. Sleep deprivation, stress, the accumulated toll of a week of bad rest and constant fear. Auditory hallucination. The brain, running out of resources, producing input on its own — a voice, a sentence, the kind of phantom sound that exhaustion manufactured when the real world wasn't providing enough stimulation. This was possible. It was rational. It required nothing to be true that wasn't already true. She'd been sleeping badly. She'd been eating badly. She'd been carrying a stolen object that people were looking for, in a city under siege, with a debt she couldn't pay and a map in her head she couldn't explain, and all of that was enough, probably, to make a brain start talking to itself.

Two: someone was in the walls. The bridge had hollows — old drainage channels, gaps between the stone courses, the kind of spaces that accumulated in two-hundred-year-old masonry. Someone could be hiding in the structure, speaking through a crack, projecting their voice into the alcove. A trick. A manipulation. Maybe the Vipers had found her and this was their approach — not a knife in the dark but a voice, a performance, a way of unsettling her before they moved in. It was the kind of thing Garrick would think of. Not practical, not efficient, but theatrical, and Garrick had always had a sense of theatre.

Three: the disc.

She held the third option in her mind and looked at it. The disc had given her a dream. The disc had placed a map in her head. The disc was warm. The disc pulsed. The disc did things that metal was not supposed to do. If the disc could reach into her sleeping mind and plant a map, could it reach into her waking mind and plant a voice?

She didn't answer. She put the question aside and she sat and she waited and the bell rang three-quarter.

The night air was cold. The fog moved. A cat padded past the alcove entrance — one of the harbour toms, lean and purposeful, on its nightly circuit. It didn't pause. It didn't look at her. It moved through the fog the way cats moved through everything, as if the world existed for their convenience.

She replayed the words again. Pay attention, girl. I don't have the patience to say things twice. The phrasing was specific. Not vague, not dreamlike, not the garbled half-language of a hallucination. It was a sentence spoken by a person with a personality, a person who was annoyed and who wanted to be heard and who had used girl the way you used it with someone you considered subordinate — not cruel, not dismissive, but hierarchical. The voice of someone who gave orders.

She almost put the knife away. She was starting to think she'd imagined it — that the first explanation was the right one, that the voice was a product of exhaustion and stress, that in the morning she'd feel foolish about sitting in the dark with a blade across her knees because her own brain had talked to her.

The bell rang the hour.

Good. You're awake. Now listen.

*          *          *

This time it was clearer.

Not louder — she didn't think it had a volume, not the way sounds had volume. But more resolved. The first time had been like hearing someone through water. This time was like hearing someone through a closed door: muffled at the edges but coherent at the centre, the words fully formed, the voice fully present. She could hear the texture of it now. A woman's voice — low, roughened, the kind of voice that had spent years in rooms where decisions were made and had done most of the making. Not a young voice. Not old, either. The voice of someone in the hard middle of life, the years where authority was earned and not granted, where the roughness came from use rather than age.

And it was inside her head. Not outside. Not traveling through air, not arriving through her ears, not a sound in the conventional sense. Inside. Behind her eyes. In the architecture of her thinking, in the private space where her own thoughts lived. As if someone had found a door that shouldn't have existed, opened it, walked in, and sat down.

The wrongness of it was absolute. She'd felt wrongness before — the map in her head, the dream that wouldn't dissolve, the disc's warmth. But those had been wrongnesses of degree. Too vivid. Too persistent. Too warm. This was a wrongness of kind. A person was in her head. A person who was not her was occupying the space reserved for her, speaking from behind her eyes, using the medium of her own consciousness the way a stranger uses your kitchen. The violation of it was so total that for a moment she couldn't respond. She sat with the knife and the wall at her back and the voice in her head and she was afraid. Properly afraid, in a way she hadn't been in years.

Then the fear passed, because fear was a luxury and she couldn't afford luxuries.

She said, out loud: "Who are you."

Not a question. A demand. She said it the way she'd say it to a stranger in the dark — flat, direct, a voice that said answer me and meant it.

The answer came without hesitation.

Maela. And you already know that, if you'd stop being frightened long enough to think.

The name landed. Zima processed it the way she processed all information — automatically, checking it against what she already knew, looking for the match. Maela. She'd heard the name. Not recently. Not in any conversation she could place. Somewhere in the background of the city's ambient knowledge, the things you absorbed without studying, the way you absorbed the shape of the skyline or the schedule of the drums. Maela. Ashport's founder. The woman who'd built the walls and the harbour, two hundred years ago, whose name was carved into the seawall in a script nobody could read.

"You're dead," Zima said.

Obviously. The voice carried a quality of impatience that was almost comforting, because the ghosts in stories — the ghosts in the stories she'd half-heard as a child, the kind old women told to scare children into sleeping — were supposed to be solemn and tragic and wreathed in sorrow. This one sounded like a woman who'd been kept waiting at a shop counter while the clerk looked for something in the back. I've been dead for a long time. The disc woke me. Or it woke you, which amounts to the same thing. Listen — there's something you have to hear and I don't know how long I can hold this.

"Hold what?"

This. The disc flared warm — a sharp pulse of heat, enough to make her flinch, the warmth spiking through the canvas into her ribs like a brand. Speaking. It costs. The disc bridges the gap between what I am now and what you are, but the bridge is narrow and I can't stand on it forever. So listen.

Zima sat very still. She was, she realised, having a conversation with a dead woman through a stolen artefact in an alcove under a bridge, and the dead woman had a schedule. She inventoried the situation the way she inventoried everything: the knife across her knees, useless. The alcove, empty. The street, empty. The voice, inside her head, which meant she couldn't run from it and couldn't cut it and couldn't do anything except listen or refuse to listen, and refusing felt both futile and unwise.

"Fine," she said. "Talk."

There was a pause. The disc's warmth held steady — high, active, the heat of a connection being maintained. Then:

There are passages beneath this city. I built them. Before I built the walls, before I built the harbour —

The voice stuttered. Not a hesitation — a break. A stutter in the signal, the way a flame stutters when the oil runs low. Zima felt it in the disc: a fluctuation in the warmth, a dip, as if something that had been flowing steadily had met a constriction.

When the voice came back it was thinner.

I can't — not tonight. I thought I could do this in one sitting but the bridge won't hold. She could hear the frustration in it. Not exhaustion — frustration. The frustration of someone who'd planned for a longer conversation and was running out of the resource that made conversation possible. I need time. The disc needs to build its charge. I'll come back.

"When?"

A day. Maybe two. You'll feel it — the warmth, the build. You know the signs now.

"I know the signs."

Good. Then the voice did something unexpected. It softened. Not by much — Maela's voice was not built for softness the way a brick is not built for floating — but the hard edge eased, and what was underneath was not kindness exactly but something adjacent. Recognition. One person seeing another. Don't be frightened of this. I know it feels wrong. It is wrong — the dead aren't supposed to talk to the living and the living aren't built to hear it. But I need you to hear it. I've been waiting a very long time for someone to hear it.

Then the voice was gone. Present, and then not. Like a flame going out — there, and then nowhere, the absence total and absolute, the space in her head that Maela's voice had occupied swept clean as if the words had never been. The disc cooled. Not gradually — the warmth dropped in seconds, the heat collapsing the way a wave collapses when it hits the shore, fast and complete, and the disc settled to a temperature below its usual resting heat. Cool. Almost cold. Almost like ordinary metal.

*          *          *

She sat in the alcove for a long time after the voice went.

The knife was still in her hand. She noticed this the way you notice a held breath — only because it had gone on too long. She sheathed the knife. Her hands were steady. Her breathing was steady. Everything observable about her was steady, and the unobservable parts — the inside, the architecture of her thinking that had just been occupied by someone who was not her — were something she wasn't ready to assess.

A dead woman had spoken to her through a piece of metal.

She said it to herself in those words, plain and flat, because she needed to hear how it sounded when you stripped the experience down to its components. It sounded like madness. It sounded like the kind of thing that people who slept in doorways said to the fog, the confident declaration of a reality that existed for one person and nobody else.

She tested the idea: I am going mad. She held it up. She looked at it.

It didn't fit. Madness didn't answer questions. Madness didn't say obviously in the exact tone of a woman who'd been kept waiting. Madness didn't say the disc needs to build its charge, which was a practical statement about a resource and a limitation, the kind of thing an engineer said when explaining why the equipment wasn't ready. Madness didn't have a personality — impatient, commanding, used to authority, frustrated by the narrow bridge between what she was and what she needed to be. Madness was formless. This had edges. This had a name.

Maela.

Ashport's founder. A name from two centuries ago, from the time before the walls, before the harbour, before the city was a city. Zima knew almost nothing about her. Nobody did — not because the information had been hidden but because nobody cared. Ashport's history had been compressed by the siege into the immediate and the useful. Who controlled which ration point. Which streets were safe. Where the blockade gaps were. Old stories, old names, old women who'd built cities and died — these had been pushed to the margins, the way furniture is pushed to the walls when you need the room for something else.

Except the old woman who'd built the city had just spoken to her and asked her to listen.

The disc was cool. Cooling further. She could feel it settling against her ribs, the warmth draining like heat from a stone taken from a fire. Resting. Recharging. Whatever Maela had used to speak, it was spent, and the disc was the vessel, and the vessel was empty.

The dream. The channels. The four branches. The map that wouldn't dissolve.

The voice. There are passages beneath this city. I built them. The sentence had been the start of something — an explanation, a story, the information she needed — and then the connection had broken. The bridge too narrow. The charge too low.

She'd said: I'll come back.

Zima believed her. She believed her because the voice had been too specific to be a hallucination and too practical to be a ghost. Because the dream and the voice confirmed each other, and two impossible things that matched were harder to dismiss than one impossible thing alone. Because the disc was a physical object with physical properties and it had served as a bridge, and bridges were things you could cross more than once.

She was talking to a dead woman.

She sat under the bridge with that fact and she tried to find the feeling that should have gone with it — awe, terror, wonder, the large response to a large event. What she found instead was the flat practicality she brought to everything. A dead woman had spoken to her, offered partial information, and promised to come back with more. That was, in its structure, an informant meeting. She'd had those before. The informant was dead and spoke through a piece of warm metal, which was new. But the structure was familiar: a person with information, offering it on terms she didn't fully understand, on a timeline she couldn't control.

She could work with that. She'd been working with worse her whole life.

She pulled her coat tight. The disc was nearly cold now, a weight against her ribs without the warmth, and the absence of the warmth was strange — she'd adjusted to it so completely that the cold felt like something missing, a gap in the landscape of her body. The fog pressed in. The harbour moved against the pilings, slow and rhythmic, and somewhere underneath it — she could hear it now, or she was admitting she could hear it — the sound of water in stone channels, muffled and directional, the underground river moving through the dark beneath the city.

She didn't sleep. She sat in the alcove with the silence where Maela's voice had been, and she waited for morning, and she thought about a dead woman who'd built a city and been forgotten and was trying, through a piece of metal and a pickpocket and a bridge too narrow to stand on for long, to be heard.
