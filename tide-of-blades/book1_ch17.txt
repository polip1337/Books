CHAPTER SEVENTEEN
Why Me



Three days after the dead woman spoke, Zima was still sleeping badly.

Not from fear — she'd moved past fear on the second night, when she lay awake waiting for the voice and it didn't come and the waiting was worse than the voice had been. Fear required uncertainty, and whatever the disc was doing to her, it was at least consistent: the warmth, the pulse, the low hum of something not quite dormant sitting against her ribs like a second heartbeat she hadn't asked for. She could account for those. She could put them in a category. Strange, impossible, not normal — but categorised, which was the first step toward useful.

What kept her awake was the questions.

She had a list. Not written down — writing things down was a habit she'd learned to avoid, because written things could be found and found things could be used against you. But the list was in her head, sharp and specific, and it had been growing since the night Maela's voice had faded out and left her sitting in the alcove with a cooling disc and no answers that added up.

Question one: if the disc chose people, how had it ended up with a Viper courier? Pell had been Garrick's man. Garrick's men didn't carry mysterious artefacts by accident — they carried what Garrick told them to carry, and they carried it where Garrick told them to carry it. That meant either Garrick knew what the disc was and had sent Pell to move it, or Pell had found it and was bringing it to Garrick because that's what you did when you found something valuable and you worked for someone who catalogued valuable things.

Either way, the disc had been in Viper hands. And Maela had said she didn't choose the courier. So how did the disc get from a dead queen's mechanism to a gang runner's pocket?

Question two: what made Zima the right person? Maela had deflected. She'd said the terms were older than she was, which was a statement that managed to be simultaneously informative and useless, like telling someone the ocean was wet. The terms existed. They worked. Maela couldn't or wouldn't explain why they'd selected a nineteen-year-old pickpocket with a nine-silver debt and a sleeping spot under a bridge.

Question three: what did speaking cost? Maela had said the bridge was narrow. She'd said she couldn't hold it. The voice had faded — not ended, faded, like a signal weakening over distance. That implied limits. Energy spent and not recovered. A finite resource being burned every time the dead queen pushed her voice through a piece of dark metal into the head of a living woman.

What happened when the resource ran out?

She lay under the Coppergate bridge on the third night and she listened to the harbour and she waited. The fog was heavy — it had been heavy for two days, a deep fog that smelled of salt and something acrid that she thought was the chemical works on Ship Street, the one that made dye from shellfish and leaked its waste into the harbour at night when nobody was supposed to be watching. The cats were absent. The coins in her pocket — eleven coppers, one bent silver, today's take — pressed against her thigh through the thin cloth. The disc pressed against her ribs through the canvas lining.

She waited.

*          *          *

The voice came an hour past midnight, or what she estimated was an hour past midnight. She'd been counting her breaths — a habit from childhood, from the years on Garrick's warehouse floor when counting was the only thing that made the dark bearable — and she'd reached somewhere around four thousand when the pressure changed.

It was different this time. Faster. The first night, Maela's voice had taken minutes to resolve, building from pressure to sound to shape to words, like something climbing up from deep water. Tonight it came in seconds. One moment Zima was lying in the dark counting breaths, and the next moment the voice was there, fully formed, inside her skull, as familiar as if it had never left.

You've been waiting.

"Three days," Zima said. She kept her voice low. Sound carried under the bridge, bounced off the stone and the water, and anyone walking the embankment above would hear a woman talking to nobody.

I know. I've been — the voice paused, searching for a word — gathering. It's faster now. The connection, or whatever you'd call it. Wider. I don't have to push as hard to reach you.

"Is that good or bad?"

It means we can talk longer. Beyond that, I don't know. I'm not working from instructions. I built the mechanism and the mechanism is running and I'm inside it, but I didn't build it to be inside it. I built it to be dead.

"That's reassuring."

A sound that might have been a laugh, or the ghost of a laugh, dry and brief. The dead queen had a sense of humour. It was the humour of someone who'd been having conversations with herself for two centuries and had developed a very specific tolerance for absurdity.

Zima sat up. She pulled her coat around her and she pressed her back against the stone and she asked the first question on her list.

"The courier. Pell. He was Garrick's man. He was carrying the disc through the city on Garrick's orders. How did it get to him?"

The disc moves. I told you that.

"You told me it circulates. You didn't tell me how."

How does a coin circulate? Someone holds it. They spend it, or they lose it, or they die and someone else picks it up. The disc has been doing that for two hundred years. I set it moving before I died. I gave it to a woman named Sera, who was the harbour-master's clerk, and told her to keep it safe. She kept it for eleven years and then she died of the coughing sickness and her son sold her belongings to a rag-man on Chandler Street. The rag-man had it for three months. He sold it to a tinker. The tinker traded it for a sack of nails in a village forty miles inland. Forty miles. It took the disc nineteen years to come back to Ashport.

"Nineteen years."

It came back in a peddler's tray. She didn't know what it was — none of them knew what it was. They felt the weight, some of them. The warmth. A few of them dreamed. But none of them were the right person at the right time, so the disc kept moving. Dropped in a gutter. Found by a child. Taken by a thief. Sold at a market stall as a curiosity. Lost in a drawer. Found in a dead man's effects. Over and over and over, for two hundred years, moving through this city like a rumour that nobody quite believes.

"And Pell?"

Pell found it in a cellar. One of your Viper's cellars — the ones where he stores things he's taken from people who can't pay. The disc was in a tin box with some copper buttons and a broken watch. Pell took the box because the tin was worth melting, and the disc was inside, and when he touched it the warmth came and he felt something and he decided to show it to your man Garrick, because he was the kind of person who brought interesting things to the person above him instead of thinking about them himself.

"You're describing Garrick's supply chain."

I'm describing how things move through a city. You know this. You pick pockets for a living. You know how objects travel — hand to hand, pocket to pocket, lost and found and lost again. The disc does the same thing, except it's looking for something specific while it travels.

"Looking for what?"

A pause. The disc's warmth shifted — not cooling, but redistributing, as if the heat had opinions about which direction to flow.

I set the terms two hundred years ago. The mechanism tests everyone who holds the disc. Most people feel nothing. Some feel warmth. A few dream. One person — one, in two centuries — gets the rest. The voice. The map. The connection. That's you.

"Why?"

I don't fully know.

"You built it."

I designed a contract. I wrote the terms. I set the conditions for who would qualify. But the mechanism interprets the terms in its own way, the way a lock interprets a key — the shape either fits or it doesn't, and the locksmith can design the lock without knowing every key that will ever be made. The voice shifted, something in its tone thinning. I specified: someone practical. Someone who survives. Someone who has nothing to lose and enough intelligence to understand what they're being given. Beyond that, the mechanism decided.

Zima sat with that. The harbour water lapped at the pilings beneath the bridge. Somewhere to the north, the drums had started again — late, later than usual — three slow beats that sat in the fog like stones dropped into water, and then the silence, and then the seven fast ones tumbling after.

"That's not an answer," she said.

It's the only one I have.

*          *          *

She'd expected that to be the end. The first time, Maela had faded after maybe ten minutes of conversation — the voice thinning, the warmth dropping, the presence withdrawing like a tide going out. But the voice stayed. The disc stayed warm. The dead queen sat in her skull and said nothing for a while, which was its own kind of communication — the silence of someone who was gathering energy for something, or deciding whether to say it.

Zima used the pause.

"You said speaking costs energy. Where does the energy come from?"

The disc. And me. What's left of me. The voice was careful with this, the way people were careful with information they'd rather not share. I'm not alive. I'm not a spirit, not in the way the temple stories describe spirits. I'm a pattern. An impression left in the mechanism I built, the way a footprint is left in clay. The disc preserves the pattern and allows it to function, but functioning wears the impression down. Every time I speak to you, the pattern degrades by a small amount.

"How small?"

Small enough that it doesn't matter tonight. Or next week. Or next month, probably. Large enough that it matters eventually.

"Eventually meaning what?"

Meaning I won't be here forever. The voice was flat. Not self-pitying — pragmatic, the way you were pragmatic about a resource you'd measured and found finite. I have enough for the job. I planned for this. The disc and the vault and the passages — they're designed to work without me once the bearer understands what they are. My voice is the instruction manual. Once you've read the manual, you don't need the author.

Zima thought about that. She thought about Garrick's ledger, and the way Garrick recorded debts, and how the ledger continued to function after the individual transactions were forgotten. The debt existed on the page whether Garrick remembered writing it or not. The mechanism persisted after the maker was done.

"You planned for your own disappearance."

I planned for everything. She sounded annoyed. Not at Zima — at the situation, at the limitations, at the narrowness of the bridge she was standing on. I had two hundred years with nothing to do but plan. I planned the vault. I planned the passages. I planned the disc and the binding and the selection mechanism and the energy budget for maintaining my pattern long enough to explain all of it to whoever the disc found. What I didn't plan for was how frustrating the actual conversation would be.

"Frustrating."

I rehearsed this. In the years after I died, when the pattern was new and the disc was strong and I had more energy than I knew what to do with — I rehearsed. I imagined the person who would carry the disc. I imagined the questions they'd ask. I prepared answers. Careful, complete, persuasive answers. And now I'm having the real conversation and it's nothing like the rehearsal, because the real person asks questions I didn't anticipate in an order I didn't prepare for, and she's suspicious of everything I say, which is reasonable but inconvenient.

Zima almost smiled. It was the most human thing the ghost had said — the complaint of someone who'd prepared a speech and found that the audience wasn't cooperating. She'd felt that herself, more than once. You planned a lift, you rehearsed the approach, you knew exactly which pocket and which angle and which moment — and then the mark turned the wrong way or the crowd shifted or a child walked between you and the target, and the beautiful clean plan became a messy improvisation. That was the gap between thinking and doing. Two hundred years of thinking hadn't closed it.

"Tell me about the vault," she said.

I've told you about the vault.

"Tell me more. What's in it?"

Supplies. Tools. Weapons. Currency. Everything you'd need to equip a departure or sustain a defence. I was thorough.

"How thorough?"

The voice was quiet for a moment. Salt and oil and spices that don't spoil. Canvas. Rope. Navigation equipment — charts, instruments, tide tables. Coin — silver and gold, sealed against damp. Blades, not many, but good ones. Black powder in sealed jars. And the secondary store.

"The secondary store."

Behind a second door. Inside the vault, at the back. The disc opens both. The first door gives you the supplies. The second door gives you the way out.

Zima waited. The disc pulsed — one long, slow beat, like a heart at rest.

There's a ship. A coastal vessel, sealed in a sea cavern below the vault. The cavern connects to the harbour through a tidal channel. At low tide, there's clearance to sail through. I built it. I provisioned it. It's been waiting there for two hundred years.

"A ship."

A ship.

Zima sat under the bridge and thought about a ship. Two hundred years in a sealed underground cavern, wood and canvas and rope preserved in the dark, a vessel designed by a dead queen for a voyage that had never happened. The idea was absurd. It was also, given everything else she'd learned in the past three days, completely consistent. Maela planned for everything. A vault without an exit was a tomb. A vault with a ship behind a second door was an escape route.

"You planned for someone to leave the city."

I planned for someone to have the option. Leaving or staying — I said that. The vault serves both purposes. But the ship — the ship was my insurance. Every city dies eventually. I wanted to make sure that when this one did, someone could get out. Someone who'd remember what this place was.

*          *          *

The voice changed after that. It didn't weaken — if anything it grew clearer, more defined, as if the dead queen had been holding something back and was now releasing it. But the quality shifted. The impatience thinned. The authority softened. What replaced them was not vulnerability — Maela was not a woman who would have tolerated that word applied to her — but something adjacent. A frankness that came from having nothing left to protect.

I built a city, she said. I dug the harbour. I raised the walls. I designed the sewer system and the market grid and the street plan and the defensive positions and the drainage channels and the loading docks. I did it in fourteen years, from nothing, from a fishing village with forty people and a beach. And when I died, the city kept going, because that's what cities do. They keep going without you. And within fifty years nobody remembered who I was. Within a hundred years my name was in records that nobody read. Within a hundred and fifty years the records were in a building that nobody entered.

She paused. The disc was very warm.

I didn't build the passages for the city. The city didn't need them — the city had walls and granaries and a harbour. I built the passages for me. For the proof that I existed. For the knowledge that somewhere under the streets I'd planned, there was a room full of things I'd put there, and one day someone would find them and know that a woman named Maela had lived and built and prepared.

The voice was thinner now. Not fading — thin by choice, the way a voice gets thin when it's saying something that matters and the speaker has decided to say it quietly.

That's what I want. I told you. I want to be remembered. Not as a name on a record in a building nobody enters. As a person who saw what was coming and did something about it. That's the only payment I can ask for, because I'm dead and dead women don't spend silver.

Zima didn't say anything for a while. She listened to the harbour. She listened to the fog settling on the stone. The doorway people — the ones who'd stopped planning for tomorrow, the ones whose faces she walked past every day without looking. They were disappearing too. Not dying, not always, but ceasing to be known. A person sitting in a doorway was a person the city was forgetting while they were still alive.

Maela had built a city and the city had forgotten her after she was dead. The difference was one of timing. The mechanism was the same.

"I'll remember you," Zima said. She said it the way she said most things that mattered — without emphasis, without ceremony, the flat tone of a statement of fact. "If the vault is real and the ship is real and any of this works, I'll remember you. That's not a condition. It's an answer."

A long silence.

That's sufficient.

And then the voice began to thin. Not abruptly, the way it had ended the first time — a slow withdrawal, the presence receding like water going out of a pool, the weight of it lifting from the inside of her skull in increments. The disc cooled. Not cold, not inert — just less warm, the way a stone cools when you take it out of the sun. The connection narrowing. The bridge getting thinner.

Before it went completely, one more thing. Quiet, nearly gone.

The access points. I told you four. The one near the harbour — the entrance is a drain cover in the dock district, in the alley behind the old customs house. The stone around it is different. Older. You'll know it when you see it.

Then the voice was gone.

*          *          *

Zima sat in the alcove for a long time after the presence left.

The disc was cooling against her ribs. She could feel it through the canvas — a steady decline, degree by degree, the heat withdrawing into the metal the way the ghost's voice had withdrawn from her head. In an hour it would be at the ambient temperature of the stone around her, just a piece of dark metal with markings, heavy and warm and inert. By morning it would be as if nothing had happened.

She pulled her coat tighter. The fog had thickened while she'd been talking — talking, she thought, to a dead woman through a stolen artefact under a bridge, which was not a sentence she could have constructed two weeks ago and was now a factual description of how she spent her evenings. The harbour was invisible. The pilings were invisible. The world had contracted to the alcove and the stone and the sound of water and the residual warmth of a conversation she couldn't prove had happened.

What Maela had said. The disc circulates. It tests. It finds. A mechanism two hundred years old and still running, still searching, passing through hands the way coins passed through hands — lost and found and lost and found, a ceaseless patient circulation that had outlasted the woman who built it and the city she'd built it for. The disc had been in a rag-man's cart. It had been in a tinker's pocket. It had been in a cellar, in a tin box, with copper buttons and a broken watch, and a Viper courier named Pell had touched it and felt something and decided to bring it to Garrick, because that was what Pell did with interesting things. And Pell had died in a market fight with the disc in his hand, and Zima had taken it, and here she was.

She didn't believe in destiny. She'd grown up in Garrick's system, in the arithmetic of debt and repayment, in a world where everything had a price and nothing happened without a transaction behind it. Destiny was a story people told when they couldn't see the arithmetic. You got lucky or you got unlucky, and the difference was timing and position and whether you were paying attention when the moment came.

But the disc was warm and the map was in her head and a dead woman had spoken to her twice now, and she couldn't make the arithmetic work. She couldn't find the transaction. The disc had circulated for two hundred years and found nobody, and then it had found her, and she was a nineteen-year-old pickpocket who owed nine silvers to a gang leader and slept under a bridge. The mechanism had tested her and she'd passed, and she didn't know the criteria, and the designer of the test didn't fully know the criteria, and that was supposed to be acceptable.

Maela's voice had gone thin when she asked to be remembered. Not commanding. Not transactional. A dead woman who had built a city and been forgotten by it, asking the one person who could hear her to keep her name alive. It was the least practical thing Maela had said. It was also the most honest. Everything else — the vault, the passages, the ship — was strategy. The request to be remembered was the thing underneath the strategy, the human need that all the planning was built on top of.

Zima understood need. She understood it the way she understood the ration queue — not as an abstraction but as a physical fact, the weight of it in your stomach, the taste of it in your mouth. She needed eleven coppers for tomorrow. She needed to clear nine silvers before Garrick's patience expired. She needed a roof that didn't leak and boots that didn't let the damp in and food that wasn't cut flour and water.

Maela needed to not be forgotten. That was a different kind of hunger, but Zima recognised the shape of it. You carried it the way you carried a debt — always there, always pressing, the press of it constant whether you were thinking about it or not.

The disc cooled another degree. The fog shifted. Somewhere above the bridge, footsteps — quick, light, someone crossing in the dark, going somewhere. They faded. The city went on doing what it did: breathing, decaying, surviving one more night because nobody had figured out how to stop.

She touched the disc through her coat. The canvas was warm where the metal sat. She could feel the edge of it, the smooth rim, the place where the markings began. In her mind, clear as the dream had been clear, the map: four access points, four branches, a central vault. And now a detail — the dock-district alley, the old customs house, a drain cover set in stone that was older than the stone around it.

She had a location. One of four, but one was enough to start with. One was all you needed when the alternative was zero.

The disc settled. Warm, heavy, quiet. No voice. No presence. Just the metal and the markings and the weight of it against her ribs, and the knowledge that somewhere inside it a dead woman's pattern was resting, conserving, waiting for the next conversation that would cost her a fraction of what she had left.

Outside the alcove, Ashport. The fog and the harbour and the walls and the hills and the drums that had stopped for the night. The ration queues that would form at dawn. The Vipers searching for a disc that was sewn into a pickpocket's coat. The debt that was growing. The time that was shrinking. The city compressing, the liveable space between factions getting smaller by the day, the neutral ground disappearing like shoreline at high tide.

And Zima, under the bridge, with a dead woman's request sitting in her chest next to a dead woman's key.

She closed her eyes. She counted her breathing. She didn't sleep — not really, not fully — but she drifted into the shallow rest that passed for sleep when your mind was too full to shut down and too tired to keep running. The disc cooled. The fog moved. The water lapped at the pilings with the patience of something that had been doing the same thing for longer than the city had existed.

She'd remember Maela. She'd said it and she'd meant it and it was the first promise she'd made in years that wasn't transactional. She didn't know what to do with that. A promise to a ghost was not a line item in anybody's ledger. It didn't earn interest. It didn't come due. It just sat there, in the space between what you owed and what you chose, and it was hers.

The fog thinned toward morning. The disc was cool. The ghost was quiet. The city stirred.

Zima opened her eyes and looked at the dark underside of the bridge and thought: the dock district. The old customs house. The drain cover in the alley.

She'd go today. Not to enter — not yet, not alone, not without rope and light and a plan. But to look. To find the older stone. To see if what the dead woman described matched what was actually there, because trust was built in increments and the first increment was verification.

She got up. She gathered her things — the coat, the knife, the coins. The disc, warm and heavy and sewn into the lining, shifted as she moved. She touched it once through the canvas. Still there. Still hers.

She climbed out of the alcove into the grey dawn light and the harbour fog and the smell of salt and tar, and she started walking.
