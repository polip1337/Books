CHAPTER TWELVE
The Buffer Zone



The cobbler's shop on the corner of Market Lane and Tallow Street had been open for as long as Zima could remember. Not open in the way that thriving businesses were open — not bright-windowed and welcoming, not the sort of place you'd notice from across the street. Open in the stubborn, quiet way of Ashport's surviving establishments: the door unlocked at dawn, the shutter folded back, the cobbler — a man named Voss, old, thin, with hands that looked like they'd been carved from the same leather he worked — visible at his bench through the doorway, bent over someone's boot with an awl and a focus that the siege hadn't managed to touch. He'd been there in month one. He'd been there in month six, when the catapults found range and the buildings on Harbour Row lost their upper storeys. He'd been there last week.

The shop was boarded up.

Zima saw it on the fourth morning after the market fight, walking south on Market Lane through the thinning fog. The shutter was closed. The door was nailed. Rough planks across the window, driven in with fourteen nails — she counted, because she counted everything, and fourteen was a strange number. You'd use twelve for symmetry or sixteen for thoroughness. Fourteen meant someone had run out of nails or patience, had driven in what they had and left.

She looked at the building beside the shop. A hook mark. Fresh, scratched into the stone at shoulder height with something sharp — a knife point, probably, or a nail. The crude angled shape that the Iron Hooks used to mark claimed territory, the simple geometry that said this belongs to us now. The mark was on a building that had been neutral three days ago. Voss's shop had stood on neutral ground. The corner of Market Lane and Tallow Street had been part of the buffer zone — the strip of streets between Viper territory and Hook territory where the city's unaffiliated population conducted its business without paying tribute to either side.

The buffer zone was shrinking.

*          *          *

She'd watched it happen over four days, the way you watch a tide come in — not in a single wave but in the accumulation of small advances, each one barely noticeable on its own, each one permanent. Water that the sand absorbs doesn't go back.

The market fight had broken something. Not a wall or a gate or any physical barrier that could be rebuilt. A convention. An understanding — unspoken, unwritten, enforced by nothing except mutual agreement and the practical recognition that some streets needed to be left alone — that said there were places in Ashport where neither gang went. Streets where people could buy and sell and walk without calculating whose flag flew from the nearest roof. The fight at the Ashford Market had ended that understanding, and the ending was the kind that didn't reverse. Things that broke in Ashport did not get repaired. They got replaced by something worse.

The Hooks had moved west. She could trace their advance through the graffiti — the hook mark appearing on buildings, on walls, on the boarded fronts of shops that had closed during the fight and hadn't reopened. Each mark was a claim. Each claim was a statement that this building, this corner, this stretch of cobblestones now answered to the Iron Hooks, and the people who lived within sight of the mark would accommodate themselves to that fact or they would leave. The marks had appeared on Tallow Street, on Market Lane's western end, on the alley behind the old chandlery where the tallow workers used to store their crates. Ground that had been neutral for months — ground where the buffer zone's unwritten rules had held through eleven months of siege and escalating tension — now carried the Hook stamp.

The Vipers had pulled back. Not retreated — the distinction mattered, because retreat was weakness and consolidation was strategy, and Garrick did not do weakness. The Viper presence on Market Lane's eastern end was denser than before the fight — more men at the corners, more charcoal vests visible in the thin crowds, the subtle thickening of territorial display that said: we're here. We've chosen to be here. This ground is ours and we are holding it. The ration tables were Garrick's core. The harbour-side warehouses were Garrick's core. The docks, the distribution points, the chokepoints where food entered the city from the fishing boats and was divided and taxed and distributed through the chain — these were his, had always been his, and the market fight had tightened his grip on them rather than loosened it.

Between the Hook advance and the Viper consolidation, the buffer zone had halved. The six blocks of neutral ground that had run from the market's western edge to the Fishmonger's Arch were now three blocks, and the three blocks were frightened. The people in them walked faster. The shops that remained open closed earlier, shutters down by midafternoon, the merchants who'd once stayed until the evening ration drums now packing their goods at the first sign of long shadows. The children who'd played in the lane behind the tallow works — she'd seen them for months, a group of five or six, ages ranging from old enough to run to young enough to be carried, supervised by an older girl who couldn't have been more than twelve — weren't playing there anymore.

Zima stood on the corner of Market Lane and Tallow Street and looked at the boarded cobbler's shop and the hook mark on the wall beside it and she thought about territory the way she always thought about it: as a pickpocket. As someone who worked the spaces between things, who survived in the gaps, who made her living from the existence of ground that nobody controlled and nobody watched too carefully. The gaps were where she operated. The gaps were where she lived.

The gaps were closing.

*          *          *

She adjusted. That was what you did. You didn't fight the geography — you couldn't fight it, not without weapons and allies and the kind of weight that pushed back against something larger than yourself. She had none of those things. She had a coat with a disc sewn into the lining and a handful of coins and a debt that was now overdue and a set of skills that depended entirely on the existence of spaces where she could work without being catalogued.

New routes first. The old path from the Coppergate bridge to the Ashford Market had taken her north on Harbour Lane and west on Market Row. That route now carried her through a block and a half of Hook territory — new territory, freshly claimed, the hook marks still sharp on the walls. She couldn't avoid it entirely, but she could shorten the exposure. North on Harbour Lane to Dock Row, then west through the tannery grounds — still unclaimed, because the tannery was abandoned and there was nothing there worth claiming and the smell kept the ambitious at bay — then north on the lane behind Cobb Street, which brought her to the market's eastern end without passing a Hook corner. The adjusted route added twelve minutes. Twelve minutes was cheap for the avoidance of questions from men standing on corners with hook marks behind them and a proprietary interest in who passed through their new domain.

New working territory. The market had partially reopened on the third day — not fully, not in the damaged western section, but the eastern stalls were trading again under the watchful presence of garrison soldiers who stood at the perimeter and did nothing useful but whose uniforms provided the appearance of order that commerce required. The stalls that had reopened were exclusively Viper-side or neutral — the Hook-affiliated vendors had moved west with the hook marks. The result was a market that was smaller, tighter, and more heavily surveilled than before.

The ration queues, though, were the safest places to work. That was the logic of the situation, and the logic had a clarity that pleased the part of her brain that liked things to make sense. Both gangs wanted the queues running. The ration distribution was the one piece of Ashport's infrastructure that everyone needed — Vipers, Hooks, garrison, civilians, the doorway people, the families, the dockhands, the laundry women, every living person in the city depended on the grain and the oil and the thin measure of whatever else the distribution tables offered that week. Disrupting the queues would trigger the one thing both gangs feared: open revolt. A hungry crowd with nothing to lose and a clear target for its anger. So the queues were protected. The queues were orderly. The queues were the last stable ground in a city that was losing stability one block at a time.

She worked them because stability was where pickpockets thrived. Chaos was where they got caught. The irony — working under Garrick's direct supervision because Garrick's territory was the safest remaining ground — was the kind of irony Ashport produced by the barrel.

New sleeping spots. The bridge was still safe. The Coppergate sat in the waterfront strip south of the main gang boundaries, in territory that neither side had bothered to claim because there was nothing there except the bridge and the harbour wall and the pilings and the fog. But she scouted alternatives. A deep-set doorway on the south end of Harbour Row with a stone lintel that blocked rain and sight lines in both directions. An alcove in the old sea wall, damp and salt-crusted, accessible only from the waterline at low tide. A corner of Aud's tannery loft, a copper a night, walls and a bar on the door. She kept three options current at all times. Three was enough. Fewer was complacent. More was paranoid. Three meant that if one spot became dangerous, she could be somewhere else within minutes.

*          *          *

On the fourth morning she walked the dock district and the market's edges and she counted what was different, and what was different was the people who were gone.

The family at the tannery market. She'd noticed them months ago — a woman with two children, a boy of maybe eight and a girl younger. They came every second day to buy cheap candles and the coarse soap from the Cobb Street makers. The woman had a careful, lined face and she held her children's hands and she counted her coppers out one by one with the slow precision of someone who knew exactly what she had and what it cost to spend it. Zima had never spoken to her. She'd noticed her because the woman's coin purse sat in her front apron pocket and would have been a straightforward lift, and she'd decided, without examining the decision too closely, not to take it. She didn't steal from women with children. It wasn't a moral position. It was a line she'd drawn because you had to draw lines somewhere or you woke up one morning and discovered you'd become Garrick.

They weren't there. Hadn't been for three days. Their absence wasn't conspicuous — nobody in the tannery market was counting faces the way she counted faces, tracking the regulars and mapping the gaps. But the gap was there. The spot where the woman usually stood, sorting through the candle crate while her children tugged at her skirt and argued about nothing, was occupied by a man selling scraps of leather trimmed from old shoes. He'd moved into the space the way water fills a depression — naturally, without thought, because the space was there and he needed somewhere.

Where had the family gone? The options were limited and none of them were good. Into Hook territory, where the rents were lower but the cost was paid in other currencies — loyalty, information, the agreement to look away when business happened on the corner that you couldn't pretend wasn't happening. Into Viper territory, where Garrick's protection was more reliable but the price was a percentage of everything you earned, recorded in the ledger, tracked with the meticulous patience of a man who believed that accounting was a form of governance. Or somewhere else — the doorways, the rough-sleeping circuit, the slow transition from housed to unhoused that Ashport's economy manufactured with the efficiency of a machine designed for the purpose.

The cobbler Voss. Gone. Fourteen nails and a boarded shop and nothing to say where he'd taken his leather and his awls and his decades of quiet, competent work.

Old Dalla, who'd sold repaired shoes from a blanket on Market Lane's western end — a thin, weathered woman with hands that had been stitching soles since before the siege, who laid her wares out on a square of canvas every morning and sat behind them with the patience of a person who had nothing else to do and nowhere else to go. Gone. The canvas was still there on the third day, folded and left on the cobblestones, uncollected, as if she'd meant to come back and then something had changed her mind.

The needle-and-thread woman on Tallow Street was still there, but diminished. She'd pulled her table inside the doorway of the building behind her stall, serving from the shadows, half-visible, the posture of someone who'd calculated that being difficult to find was worth the cost of being difficult to buy from. Her stock was thinner — fewer needles, less thread, none of the brass buttons and clasps that she'd carried before the fight. She'd trimmed her inventory down to what she could carry at a run.

A fish-seller Zima knew by sight — a man with a scarred hand who'd worked the market's neutral section for months, selling the thin catches from the night boats — had relocated three streets east, deep into Viper territory. His stall was smaller. His prices were higher by the amount of the Viper tax. He had the expression of a man who'd done the arithmetic and determined that paying protection was cheaper than not selling at all, and who hated the arithmetic and the answer it had given him but couldn't argue with either.

This was the cost. Not the dead — three people had died in the market fight, she'd heard the count from the dock district grapevine, though the number kept shifting depending on who was talking. The cost was the living. The people displaced, rearranged, compressed into smaller spaces by the expansion of territories they'd never chosen to inhabit. Every hook mark on a wall was a family deciding whether to stay or leave. Every charcoal vest on a new corner was a street getting tighter. The city's liveable space — the space where ordinary people could exist without belonging to a side — was shrinking, and the people in it were shrinking with it. Getting smaller. Quieter. Less visible. The way any population gets when the territory it occupies is reduced — the animals in a forest that's being cut, the fish in a pool that's draining, the people in a city that's eating itself from the edges inward.

*          *          *

She worked the Harbour Gate queue in the afternoon. Took one purse — four coppers, a thin take, but she needed every coin because the extension was over and Stel had not come.

The four days had expired. Yesterday had been the last day. She'd walked through it with the specific, low-level dread of someone expecting a knock that never arrived, a figure in the crowd that never materialised, a quiet voice at her shoulder saying time's up, the money's due. The knock hadn't come. The figure hadn't appeared. The voice had stayed silent.

Stel had not returned. No collector at the queue. No polite man waiting at the Coppergate bridge with his ledger and his careful phrasing and his way of making a debt conversation feel like a discussion between reasonable people about a reasonable problem. No message through the dock district channels, no word from Garrick's network. Nothing.

She turned the silence over and examined it from both sides, the way she'd examined the disc.

Option one: Garrick was distracted. The market fight, the Hook expansion, the loss of territory on the western end, the dead courier Pell, the missing disc — these were problems that demanded attention, and a pickpocket's nine-silver debt was small business in a week when the gang landscape was being redrawn. Stel hadn't come because Garrick had bigger things to manage. The debt had slipped down the list. When the territorial situation stabilised, when the search for the disc concluded, when the immediate crisis passed — then someone would come. The debt hadn't been forgotten. Garrick's ledger didn't have a mechanism for forgetting. It had only been deferred.

Option two: Garrick was done with patience. The extension had been a courtesy. Courtesies had limits, and the limit had been reached, and what came next was not another visit from polite Stel but something different. The kind of visit that happened after dark, in the quiet places, with the kind of people who didn't bring ledgers because the conversation they'd come to have didn't require documentation. The silence wasn't mercy. The silence was the gap before the door opened.

She couldn't tell which. That was the worst of it — not the danger itself but the uncertainty about its distance. She'd lived with danger her whole life. Danger was specific, measurable, something you could see coming and calculate and maybe avoid. Uncertainty was worse. Uncertainty was the fog that Ashport draped over everything — the inability to see past the next few feet, the knowledge that the thing that was going to change your life was already in motion and you couldn't see it yet and wouldn't see it until it arrived.

She kept the disc. The warmth had become constant — part of her body's landscape, noticeable only when she thought about it. The Vipers were still searching. She'd heard mentions that morning — fewer now, the search diffusing from the dock district outward into the residential streets, the questioning less urgent but more thorough. They were being systematic. They were being patient. They were doing what Garrick always did, which was to apply steady, quiet pressure until the pressure found a seam, and then apply more.

She was a seam. She hadn't been found yet. The yet was doing a lot of work in that sentence and she knew it.

*          *          *

Evening came on early. The fog rolled in from the harbour in heavy banks that smelled of salt and tar and the particular mineral decay that Ashport's waterfront produced — the smell of a harbour that had been sitting too still for too long, the biological processes of a body of water that wasn't being circulated by the traffic that used to keep it clean. She stood on the Coppergate bridge and looked south toward the breakwater and the shapes of the orc ships in the fog, grey on grey, patient, and she listened to the drums when they came.

Three slow beats. The silence. Seven fast.

She'd been hearing them for eleven months. Every evening, the same sequence. The same drums from the same hills beaten by the same hands in the same pattern, as regular as sunrise and as incomprehensible. Nobody in the city knew what the count meant. The garrison said it was a morale exercise. The gangs said it was a signal. Zima thought it was something else — a measurement, maybe. A tally. The orcs counting something that they could see from the hills and the city couldn't see from inside itself. They'd been sitting up there for nearly a year, fifteen thousand soldiers, and they hadn't attacked a city whose walls had holes in them, and the drums came every evening like a ledger entry, recording something that only the recorder understood.

Below her, the harbour. The fishing boats were going out — small lights moving through the fog toward the blockade gaps, the nightly commerce that kept seventeen thousand people technically alive. The orc ships watched them pass. The orc ships always watched them pass.

She turned from the water and looked north, toward the city. Ashport in the fog was a collection of shapes — rooftops, chimneys, the ragged line of the harbour wall, the taller buildings of the commercial district emerging from the grey like the masts of sunken ships. Somewhere in those shapes, the Hooks were settling into their new territory, painting their marks on walls that had been bare yesterday. Somewhere, the Vipers were consolidating, pulling their people tighter around the core, the ration tables, the harbour infrastructure. Somewhere between them, in the three remaining blocks of neutral ground that used to be six, the people who belonged to neither side were making their accommodations.

She was one of those people. She'd always been one of those people — Garrick's ledger notwithstanding, she wasn't Viper, not really, not in the way that Brennan was Viper or Stel was Viper, people who'd chosen the charcoal vest and the network and the belonging that came with it. She was an asset. A revenue source. A name in a book that generated a return. Garrick had found her in a doorway at nine and fed her and housed her and trained her, and what he'd created was not a gang member but a tool — a pair of hands that went into crowds and came out with coins that went into his ledger. She owed him because the ledger said she owed him, and the ledger was the only contract that mattered in Ashport, and the contract said: you are mine until the numbers say otherwise.

The numbers did not say otherwise. The numbers said: nine silvers, overdue, the extension expired, the collector silent. The numbers said: you are standing on a bridge in the fog with a stolen artefact sewn into your coat and a debt you cannot pay to a man who is looking for the artefact and has not yet connected it to you and when he does the debt will be the smallest of your problems.

She went to the alcove. The fog took the bridge. The harbour took the fog. The pilings creaked in the low tide and the water moved against them with the sound of something old and tired and too large to hurry, and Zima sat against the cold stone with the disc warm against her ribs and her coins in her pockets and her options narrowing the way the buffer zone was narrowing — slowly, from both sides, the space in which to manoeuvre getting smaller with every day that passed.

The city was compressing. She could feel it. Not the disc's warmth — something else, something larger and less specific. The pressure of a space contracting while the number of people in it held steady. The Hook marks moving west. The Viper presence thickening east. The neutral ground between them halving, and the people in that ground pressing closer together, pressing toward the walls or the water or the doorways, the whole civilian population of the buffer zone being squeezed into a corridor that was closing from both ends.

She lived in the corridor. She'd always lived there — the narrow space between power, the gap where nobody looked because nobody owned it.

And it was shrinking.

She put her hand on the disc through her coat. The warmth was steady. She waited, because waiting was what you did when the options were bad and the information was incomplete and the only move was to keep still and trust that the picture would resolve before the walls closed in completely.

She slept. The fog filled the alcove. The harbour moved below. The drums were done, and the city settled into another night, and the gap between the Hook marks and the Viper corners narrowed by another few feet in the dark. Somewhere in the shrinking buffer zone, the people who lived there were making their calculations — stay or go, pay or run, this side or that side or the doorways or the water. The calculations were getting harder. The answers were getting fewer. The space in which to ask the questions was getting smaller.

And Zima slept under the bridge with a dead man's disc against her ribs and a dead man's name in her memory and a debt she couldn't pay and a warmth she couldn't explain, and the city compressed around her the way it compressed around everyone — slowly, steadily, without mercy or malice, just the blind mechanical pressure of a system that had stopped working and hadn't yet stopped moving, grinding forward on its own momentum, crushing whatever was caught between the gears.

The fog thickened. The water moved. The drums were silent.

Tomorrow she'd start asking questions. Answers were a separate problem.
