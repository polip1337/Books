CHAPTER THREE
Fog and Drum-Thunder



She woke to the sound of gulls fighting over something dead at the waterline.

The noise came up through the stone and the drainpipe and arrived in the alcove as a thin, furious shrieking that pulled her out of sleep the way it did most mornings — not gently. She lay still for a moment with her back pressed against the wall and her hand on the coins in her pocket and waited for the rest of the world to assemble itself. The stone was cold. Her coat was damp. Her left hip ached from the position she'd slept in, which was the same position she slept in every night because the alcove only allowed one and it was not a kind one.

Grey light came through the gap between the drainpipe and the wall. Dawn, or close to it. The fog was still thick but it had that thin quality it got in the early morning, a lighter grey that meant the sun was up somewhere behind it. She could see her breath.

She sat up slowly, because sitting up too fast in a space this small meant hitting her head on the drainpipe, which she'd done twice in the first week and not once since. She checked the alcove — the flat stone, empty and clean; the corner where the cats came in, two sets of paw prints in the dust heading out, which meant they'd left before her. The entrance, undisturbed. Nobody had been here.

She pulled herself out through the gap and dropped into the drainage channel and stood in the thin trickle of rust-coloured water and stretched until her spine made a sound like someone cracking their knuckles. Nineteen years old and her body already kept a running inventory of complaints — hip, shoulders, the knuckle on her right hand that had been stiff since she'd jammed it on a mark's belt buckle two weeks ago. She gave herself a count of ten to acknowledge the worst of it, and then she stopped, because the inventory only got longer and she had somewhere to be.

*          *          *

The harbour wall was a ten-minute walk from the bridge. She went there most mornings, when the weather allowed and sometimes when it didn't, because the wall was the one place in Ashport where you could see the whole shape of the problem.

She climbed out of the drainage channel using the handholds she'd mapped in the first month — a protruding stone, a crack in the mortar, the iron bracket where a railing had once been bolted — and came up onto the Coppergate bridge road. The bridge was empty. The streets branching off it were empty. Ashport at dawn belonged to the gulls and the cats and the people who had reasons to be up before the rest of the city, and most of those reasons were not the kind you discussed.

She walked south along the channel road, then cut west through the alley between the old sailmaker's shop and the boarding house where Dav the fence kept a room on the second floor. The alley smelled of piss and lye and the sourness of laundry water used too many times. She stepped over the gutter and came out on Harbour Street and turned south, and the harbour opened up on her left — grey water, grey sky, grey fog sitting on the surface like something poured.

The harbour wall ran the length of Ashport's southern edge, from the dockyard in the east to the old fort at the western headland. It was twenty feet high on the seaward side and twelve on the city side, with a walkway on top wide enough for two people abreast and a crenellated parapet that had been decorative a hundred years ago and was structural now, or would have been if anyone had been manning it properly. She climbed at the gap near the harbourmaster's office — two loose stones that served as a staircase if you knew where to put your feet — and stood on the walkway and looked out.

*          *          *

The orc warships sat beyond the breakwater, seven of them, arranged in a loose arc that covered the harbour mouth.

She counted them the way she counted everything — left to right, each one confirmed against her memory of yesterday and the day before. Seven. Same as last week. Same as the week before that. The nearest was maybe three hundred yards past the breakwater, close enough to make out its shape through the thinning fog: a heavy-hulled vessel with a high stern and what looked like a ram at the waterline, though the distance made the details uncertain. The farthest was a grey smear at the edge of visibility, barely distinguishable from the fog itself. Between the second and third ship, measured from the west, a gap of maybe eighty yards that opened wider at night when the tide pushed the third ship east. That was the gap the fishing boats used. Six boats, running dark, slipping through after midnight when the orc watch changed. She knew this because she'd watched them go out and she'd watched them come back and she'd counted the hours between.

Seven ships. Six boats. Eighty yards of gap. These were the numbers that kept Ashport alive, and Ashport didn't know them. The garrison probably had its own count, its own measurements, but Zima had been watching the harbour every morning for eleven months and she trusted her numbers more than she trusted anyone else's, because her numbers came from patience and repetition and the garrison's numbers came from soldiers who'd rather be doing something else.

The fishing boats were one of the things about the siege that didn't make sense. Six boats, going out through a seven-ship blockade, coming back with fish, night after night for months. The orc ships could have closed that gap. She'd watched them manoeuvre in the first weeks — they were heavy and they were slow but they were competent, and seven ships patrolling a harbour mouth less than four hundred yards wide should have been able to stop six fishing boats with oars and no lights. They didn't. They let them through.

She'd thought about why. For months she'd thought about it, turning it over the way she turned coins, looking for the edge that didn't match. The obvious answer was that the orcs couldn't be bothered — fishing boats weren't military targets, and the fish they brought back kept the city alive at a subsistence level, which was maybe the point. A city that starved completely was a city that either surrendered or died, and either outcome removed whatever value the city had. A city that starved slowly was a city that remained occupied, functional, compliant. You could do things with a compliant city that you couldn't do with a dead one.

But that explanation assumed the orcs wanted something from Ashport, which raised the question of what, and that question had been sitting in her head since the fourth month without producing an answer she trusted.

Beyond the warships, past the outer harbour, the sea was open and empty and grey. No merchant ships. No navy. No relief fleet on the horizon, which was something people had stopped scanning for around the seventh month. Whoever was supposed to help Ashport had either decided not to, or didn't know, or didn't exist. The harbour mouth faced south and south was open ocean for a hundred miles before you hit the trading lanes, and the trading lanes had rerouted around Ashport the way traffic reroutes around a collapsed bridge. The city had become an obstacle in its own geography. Nobody was coming.

She turned north and looked at the hills.

*          *          *

The Thornback Hills rose behind Ashport in a long, ragged line that ran east to west, and the orc army was spread across them like a stain on cloth — visible from a distance, impossible to ignore, part of the landscape now in the way that ruins become part of a landscape if you leave them long enough.

From the harbour wall she could see the western end of their position — the siege works on the lower slopes, the catapult emplacements on the ridgeline, the trails of cook-fire smoke that caught the morning light and hung in the still air. She'd counted the cook-fires once, in a fit of restless arithmetic, and got two hundred and twelve. If each fire served a unit of fifty — which was the garrison's estimate, though the garrison estimated most things the way a drunk estimates distance — that put the army at around ten thousand on the western flank alone. The eastern flank was hidden behind the Coppergate district and she couldn't see it from this position, but Dav had mentioned the scouts put another five thousand there. Fifteen thousand total. Probably more.

Fifteen thousand soldiers, sitting in hills, for eleven months.

She'd thought about this number until it had worn a groove in her mind. Fifteen thousand people required food, water, shelter, sanitation, medicine, discipline. An army that size consumed resources faster than any city, and the Thornback Hills were not generous country — rocky, thin-soiled, forested in patches but mostly exposed scrubland that would support grazing but not agriculture. Which meant the orc army was being supplied from somewhere else. Which meant there was a supply line running through the hills or along the coast or both, a logistics chain feeding fifteen thousand mouths every day while those mouths did nothing except sit and wait and beat drums at dusk.

That wasn't cheap. That wasn't improvised. Someone was funding this siege the way you'd fund a building project — month by month, with planning and patience and the expectation of a return.

The catapults on the ridgeline had fired three times in eleven months. She'd been on the harbour wall for two of them. The first, in the second month, had hit the granary — a direct strike that collapsed the south wall and destroyed a third of the city's stored grain in one afternoon. Everyone had panicked. Everyone had expected more shots, a barrage, the beginning of the end. Nothing came. The catapults sat on the ridge and the orc engineers sat beside them and the second shot didn't come for three months. That one landed in the harbour, killed nobody, sank a rowing boat. The third, in the eighth month, overshot the city entirely and the stone dropped into the sea past the breakwater, raising a plume of white water that Zima had watched from this exact spot on the wall.

Three shots. Eleven months. From a ridgeline that could have put a stone into any district of Ashport at will. The granary shot proved they knew exactly where to aim. The other two were — what? Reminders. The same kind of reminders as the drums. We're here. We can reach you. We're choosing not to.

The siege works on the lower slopes were visible as a series of earthen walls and trenches that zigzagged up the hillside in the pattern she assumed military engineers used to approach a fortification. She'd watched them dig those trenches over the first three months — a slow, methodical advance that crept closer to the north wall by about ten yards a day. And then, in the fourth month, the digging had stopped. The trenches ended maybe three hundred yards from the wall, within bowshot if anyone had been shooting, and the orcs had put down their shovels and started waiting. The trenches were still there seven months later. Maintained. Manned. No closer.

She stood on the harbour wall and looked at the hills and tried, for the thousandth time, to understand what she was looking at.

An army of fifteen thousand against a city of seventeen thousand — mostly civilians, half-fed, defended by a garrison of maybe two thousand soldiers whose attention was split between the walls and the gangs and whoever was paying them this week. The city walls had three breaches she knew of: the crack in the harbour wall behind her, patched with rubble and tar; a collapsed section near the north gate filled with debris but never properly repaired; and a gap in the eastern wall where a postern gate had rotted off its hinges and been replaced with a barricade of old furniture and timber that wouldn't stop a determined child, let alone an orc assault team. Any one of those breaches could be forced in an hour. All three at once would break the city open before lunch.

The orcs hadn't attacked. Eleven months. Three breaches. A garrison spread so thin you could see daylight through it. And the army in the hills sat and waited and beat its drums and fired its catapults at nothing and let the fishing boats through and watched the city do to itself what an assault would have done faster and cleaner.

That was not a siege. She didn't know the right word for it. She'd settled, provisionally, on patience — the orcs were waiting for something, and whatever it was hadn't arrived. The garrison said they were waiting for the city to starve into surrender. The gangs said they were waiting for reinforcements, or winter, or a divine sign. Zima thought all of those explanations were too small. They assumed the orcs wanted something a human army would want: territory, resources, capitulation. What if they wanted something else entirely? What if they were waiting for a condition, not an event — something the city would produce or become or reveal, given enough time and enough pressure?

She didn't have an answer. She'd stood on this wall almost every morning for eleven months and she didn't have an answer. But the question kept her coming back, because the question was the only thing about the siege that felt consequential, and the feeling of consequence — the sense that something mattered beyond the daily grind of coppers and rations and debt — was a thing she protected the way she protected her alcove: quietly, stubbornly, at a cost she didn't examine too closely.

*          *          *

The fog was burning off.

It went in layers — the surface fog first, peeling back from the water and revealing the harbour in pieces: the pilings, the mooring lines, the dark hulls of the boats too damaged to fish and too heavy to move. Then the mid-level fog, thinning from grey to white to a kind of luminous nothing, letting the light through in angled shafts that hit the water and turned it from black to green to the dirty gold of morning. Finally the high fog, sitting at rooftop level and holding the light in, so that for maybe twenty minutes Ashport glowed from within like a lantern with a dirty glass, and you could almost — if you were standing on the harbour wall and not thinking too carefully — mistake it for a city that was going to be fine.

She watched the transformation and let it happen without comment, because the beauty of Ashport at dawn was not the kind of beauty that invited comment. It was the beauty of a bruise — vivid, temporary, evidence of damage.

The garrison sentries would arrive soon. She could hear them in the distance — boots on stone, the clink of belt buckles, the particular rhythm of men performing a duty they'd stopped believing in. The wall patrols had been regular in the first months. Two-man teams, four-hour shifts, continuous coverage from the dockyard to the fort. She'd timed them, because timing patrols was a habit she'd picked up from the queues — you watched the guards the same way you watched the marks, looking for patterns, gaps, the moment when attention wandered.

Now the patrols were half what they'd been. The garrison had two thousand soldiers on paper. She'd estimated maybe twelve hundred actually on duty at any given time, and of those twelve hundred, a third were running errands for Garrick or the Hooks — carrying messages, guarding supply caches, providing muscle at the ration tables in exchange for extra food and the coins the gangs paid better than the garrison commander did. The commander knew. Everyone knew. It was the kind of open secret that stops being a secret and becomes a fact, and facts in Ashport didn't generate outrage anymore. They generated shrugs.

Last week she'd timed the harbour wall patrol and found a twelve-minute gap between the eastern pair passing the harbourmaster's station and the western pair reaching the same point. Twelve minutes of wall that nobody was watching. In a city under siege by fifteen thousand soldiers with catapults and warships, twelve minutes of unguarded wall. She'd stood in that gap and looked north at the hills and thought: if they wanted in, they could come in right here, right now, and the first person to know about it would be me, and I'm a pickpocket with a bread knife.

She wasn't sure anymore whether that made her angry. Anger required the expectation that things should be better, and she'd used up that expectation around the eighth month. What she felt was something flatter and more durable — an awareness of the gap between what the wall was for and what the wall had become, and the understanding that the gap was just another version of the same gap that ran through everything in Ashport: the distance between how things were supposed to work and how they actually did.

*          *          *

The morning settled. The light strengthened. She looked at the city.

The rooftops of the Coppergate district, patched and sagging. The spire of the old customs house, its clock stopped at a quarter past two since the third month when the clockmaker died and nobody replaced him. The chimneys of the dock district, fewer of them smoking than last month because fuel was another thing the city was running out of, another line on the long subtraction that the siege was performing on every aspect of daily life. The market square, visible as a gap in the roofline, where in a few hours the stalls would open and the careful, diminished business of survival would resume.

She'd lived here nineteen years, or close enough. She didn't remember arriving. Her earliest clear memory was a room — small, dark, cold — and after that the memories jumped to the street and the doorway and Garrick's boots, and somewhere in the gaps was whatever had come before, and she'd stopped trying to recover it because recovering it wouldn't change what came after.

This was her city. Not in the way that belonged means owned. In the way that a scar belongs to the skin it's on — permanent, formative, not chosen. Ashport had made her. The streets and the fog and the queues and the gangs, the slow machinery of survival that turned every day into a copy of the one before. She was a product of this place the same way the fog was a product of the harbour — shaped by the conditions, impossible to separate from the geography.

And the city was dying. She could see it from up here — the contraction, the emptying, the slow withdrawal of life from the edges toward the centre, like a tide going out and not coming back. The outer districts were mostly abandoned. The middle ring was thinning. Only the centre held any density: the market, the ration points, the dock district where the gangs kept their strongholds. And even there, the density was a lesser thing than it had been, measured in fewer faces and shorter queues and the steady accumulation of boarded windows.

She climbed down from the wall and walked back into the city.

The transition was what it always was — from the openness of the wall, where the problem had shape and distance and the clean geometry of warships and trenches, to the streets, where the problem dissolved into its parts and each part was small enough to hold. On the wall she thought about the siege. In the streets she thought about the debt.

Garrick's nine silvers. Three weeks overdue. He hadn't sent anyone yet, and the fact that he hadn't sent anyone was itself a kind of message, because Garrick communicated through omission as fluently as through speech. The silence meant he was watching. The watching meant he was waiting. And Garrick waiting, like the orc army waiting, was the kind of patience that preceded something she'd rather not be standing in front of when it arrived.

She needed the queues today. The Harbour Gate ration point opened at midday and the crowds there were denser than Mercer Lane, which meant more marks and more risk and the particular arithmetic of a pickpocket calculating whether the extra coppers were worth the extra exposure. She walked north through the dock district and the morning was happening around her — a fishmonger setting up a handcart, a woman hauling water from the pump on Chandler Street, two garrison soldiers walking the other direction with the purposeful stride of men on an errand and the careful eyes of men who were earning their second wage.

The fog was gone. The city stood in its own thin light, ordinary and solid, and behind it the hills stood with their trenches and their cook-fire smoke and their patient, silent army, and between the city and the army was the wall that wasn't being watched, and between the city and the sea was the blockade that wasn't being enforced, and between Zima and the next few days was a debt she couldn't pay to a man who was choosing, for now, not to collect it.

From the hills, the morning drums. Three slow. Seven fast. Then nothing. She'd been too deep in thought to hear them from the wall, which was a bad habit she kept meaning to fix. You should always hear the drums. The drums were the sound of someone else's patience, and in Ashport, someone else's patience was the thing most likely to kill you.

She walked north. The enemy behind her, in the hills and on the water. The enemy ahead, in the ledger and the debt and the slow machinery of a system designed to keep her exactly where she was.

Both of them waiting.
