CHAPTER TWENTY
Cobb Street



She went to the tanner's loft that night because the bridge felt wrong.

Not dangerous — not more dangerous than usual, anyway. She hadn't seen anyone near the alcove. She hadn't spotted Viper men on the embankment or unfamiliar figures watching the Coppergate approach. The bridge was as it always was: damp, cold, smelling of low tide and old mortar, with the two harbour toms lurking at the far end waiting for scraps she didn't have. Nothing had changed in the physical arrangement of the alcove.

But she was carrying a map now. A map and a disc and a plan that had moved from abstract to specific, and the combination of those three things made the alcove feel exposed in a way it hadn't before. She'd been safe there for six months because the alcove's only value was as a sleeping spot — nobody wanted it except her, and the three people who knew about it were paid to forget. But the woman sleeping in the alcove tonight was different from the woman who'd been sleeping there a week ago. She had something people were looking for. She had something worth finding. And the alcove, which had been invisible when she was invisible, might not stay invisible now that she wasn't.

Paranoia, probably. She recognised the shape of it — the hypervigilance, the tendency to read threat into ordinary details, the specific anxiety of carrying a stolen thing that people wanted. She'd felt it before, in smaller doses, after particularly good lifts. It faded. It always faded, replaced by the dull normality of survival.

But tonight she didn't trust the fading, and she didn't trust the alcove, and she had four coppers she could spend on a roof that wasn't stone, so she went to Cobb Street.

*          *          *

The tanner's loft was above the tannery on Cobb Street, accessed by a wooden stair on the building's south side. The stair was narrow and steep, built for workers going up to store hides, not for people going up to sleep, and the third step had a crack that would break under heavy weight within the year. Zima stepped over it out of habit.

The tanner — a man named Druse, who was either fifty or seventy, depending on whether you counted the years the tanning chemicals had added to his face — charged four coppers a night for a spot on the floor. No beds. No blankets. Straw pallets that he replaced every month or so, which meant the current ones were three weeks old and smelled accordingly: stale straw and damp and the faint, persistent smell of hides and lye that drifted up from the tannery below, through the floorboards, through the straw, into your clothes and your hair and the back of your throat. You woke up smelling like leather whether you wanted to or not.

Druse took her coppers at the top of the stairs. He counted them by feel in the dark — two in his left hand, two in his right, a quick press with his thumb to check the weight and the edges — and he jerked his chin toward the interior. His mouth didn't open. Speech cost energy, and Druse, like the stall woman at the harbour, was not spending.

Zima went in.

The loft was a single room, maybe thirty feet by twenty, with the roof timbers exposed and the floor covered in the straw pallets. No windows — the only light came from a tallow candle on a shelf by the stairs, already guttering, good for maybe another hour. The air was close. Warm, comparatively — warmer than the bridge, warmer than the street, warmed by the bodies of the people already on the floor and by the tannery fires below, which Druse kept going through the night for his curing process, and the heat rose through the boards and turned the loft into something that was, if not comfortable, at least not actively hostile to human habitation.

Seven people on the floor. Zima counted them as she crossed to her usual spot — the pallet in the far corner, under the low point of the roof where the timbers angled down and the headroom was less than five feet. She preferred it because you couldn't approach it without ducking, which meant anyone coming at her in the dark would be off-balance.

Seven people. She'd learned to read them the way she read a ration queue — not by name, not by story, but by the signals they gave off. Position, posture, breathing, the small involuntary sounds that people made when they thought nobody was listening.

The woman with the children was in the middle of the room, on two pallets pushed together. Two children — a boy and a girl, Zima thought, though in the guttering candlelight and the shapelessness of their clothing it was hard to be certain. They were small. Five and seven, maybe, or six and eight. They lay on either side of their mother with the complete stillness of children who had learned to be quiet. Not sleeping — she could tell from the quality of their breathing, which was too even, too controlled, the breathing of someone who was awake and trying to sound asleep. Hungry children became quiet children. That was one of the things Ashport taught you if you paid attention: hunger had a sound, and the sound was silence.

The old dockhand was by the wall, on a pallet that had lost most of its straw. He slept on his back with his arms at his sides, straight as a plank, in the posture of a man who'd spent thirty years sleeping in narrow bunks on ships and never learned to sprawl. He'd worked the harbour cranes. Zima knew this because he'd told her once, on a previous night in the loft, in the brief and unsolicited way that lonely people sometimes told you things about themselves — not expecting a response, just needing to put the words into the air so they'd exist somewhere outside their own head. Thirty years on the cranes. Then the siege. Then the cranes went idle because there was nothing to load and no ships to load it onto, and a man who'd done one thing for thirty years was suddenly a man who did nothing, and the distance between doing nothing and being nothing was shorter than anyone wanted to admit.

The coughing man was near the stairs. She heard him before she saw him — a wet, deep cough that came in waves, three or four convulsions separated by pauses of maybe twenty seconds, a rhythm as regular as the drums in the hills. The cough had a particular quality that she'd heard before in the ration queues, in the boarding houses, in the shelters. It was the cough of the lung sickness that had been spreading through Ashport since the seventh month of the siege, the one the city's remaining doctors couldn't treat because the medicines came by ship and the ships weren't coming. He lay on his pallet with his knees drawn up and his coat pulled around him, and when the coughing took him his whole body clenched like a fist, and when it released him he was still for twenty seconds, and then it took him again.

The others: a man and a woman who might have been together, sleeping close but not touching, on adjacent pallets near the middle of the room. A young man — sixteen, seventeen — in the far corner opposite Zima's, lying on his side, facing the wall, with the rigid immobility of someone who was either asleep or pretending to be. A woman alone, older, sitting upright against the wall with her eyes closed and her hands in her lap, not sleeping, just resting, the way you rested when sleep was either impossible or unwise.

Seven people. Zima made eight. Thirty-two coppers a night in Druse's pocket, assuming everyone had paid. Druse had maybe twelve spots in the loft, which meant he was running at two-thirds capacity, which meant either business was slow or he'd turned people away.

She settled onto her pallet. The straw compressed under her weight with a dry crunching sound. She pulled her coat around her and she lay on her side with her back to the wall and the low roof timbers six inches above her head and the room dim and close and full of the sounds that seven other people made when they were trying to be quiet.

*          *          *

The people in the loft were the sliding class.

Zima had no name for them — the phrase was hers, something she'd started using in her head to describe the people who occupied the space between survival and collapse. They weren't the doorway people. Not yet. The doorway people had stopped trying — they sat in their doorways and their faces were hollow and their eyes were distant and they'd let go of whatever thread connected them to tomorrow. The people in the loft were still holding the thread. They paid four coppers a night for a roof. They went to the ration queues. They looked for work, or they maintained the pretence of looking, which amounted to the same thing in a city where the work had dried up.

But they were sliding. Every week, a little further down. The ration cut had pushed some of them here from better lodging — boarding houses that had raised their prices, rooms that had been commandeered by one gang or the other, arrangements that had held for months and then collapsed in a single day because the margin between affordable and impossible was one meal wide. The gang fight at the market had closed a boarding house on Harbour Lane — the building had caught fire in the aftermath, not badly, but badly enough that the owner had shut the doors and the twelve people who'd been sleeping there had scattered into the city's shrinking supply of alternatives.

One bad week and the loft people would be doorway people. One bad week — a lost purse, a stolen ration token, a day of sickness that kept you from the queue, an encounter with the wrong gang at the wrong corner. The distance between the loft and the doorway was measured in coppers, and coppers were measured in luck, and luck in Ashport was not a renewable resource.

Zima lay on her pallet and she looked at the woman with the quiet children and she thought: that's me. Not literally — she didn't have children, didn't have anyone — but the mechanism was the same. The woman was holding on. She paid her coppers and she fed her children and she came to the loft every night and she kept going, and the going was the thing, the continued motion forward, the daily act of not stopping. And one day the coppers would run out or the children would get sick or the ration queue would be too long or too short or too dangerous, and the holding on would become letting go, and the loft would become a doorway, and the doorway would become something she didn't want to think about.

The difference between Zima and the woman was the disc. The disc and the map and the ghost and the vault and the dangerous, unlikely, probably insane plan to go underground and find what a dead queen had left there. The woman had coppers and children and the loft. Zima had coppers and a plan. Both of them were sliding. But Zima was sliding toward something, which was different from sliding toward nothing, even if the something was a tunnel in the dark.

The coughing man coughed. The wave lasted longer this time — six convulsions, seven, the sound filling the loft with its wet regularity. Nobody reacted. The loft had absorbed the cough the way the city had absorbed the drums, turning it from an event into an environment, a thing that happened continuously and therefore stopped being a thing that happened.

*          *          *

She lay in the dark and she planned.

The candle had guttered out. The loft was black — not the grey-black of the bridge alcove, where harbour light and fog-glow filtered through, but the absolute black of an enclosed space with no windows and no flame. She couldn't see the other sleepers. She could hear them: breathing, shifting, the coughing man's rhythm, the children's silence. She could smell them: sweat and straw and lye and the particular sourness of people who'd been wearing the same clothes for too long.

She planned in the dark the way she planned everything — practically, specifically, without sentiment.

The bakery. Mercer Lane. Tomorrow.

She'd go to the back door. Not the front — the front was for customers, for people buying bread, for transactions that were public and simple. The back door was for other business. Zima had never used it, but she knew it was there — she'd watched the bakery from across Mercer Lane on three separate occasions, mapping the patterns, noting who came and went and through which entrance. The regulars used the front. The people who stayed longer than a loaf required used the back. A first approach through the back door would signal that Zima understood which kind of transaction she was proposing.

She wouldn't mention the disc. Not tomorrow. Not until she'd established the terms of the current interaction and measured Lirra's willingness to engage. Revealing the disc was an irreversible act — once Lirra knew about it, that knowledge existed in the world, outside Zima's control, subject to whatever calculations Lirra made about its value and its danger. Information, once given, could not be taken back. That was one of Garrick's lessons, one of the few she'd kept because it was true regardless of who taught it.

She'd frame it as a job. She needed intelligence: Viper patrol patterns around the dock district, specifically the area near the old customs house. She'd need the schedule — who patrolled, when, how long the gaps were between shifts. She'd need to know whether Garrick's search for the disc had expanded or contracted, whether the Vipers were still actively canvassing or had diverted their attention to the Hook situation.

Lirra would want to know why. She always did — not aggressively, not with the direct interrogation of someone who didn't trust you, but with the quiet, lateral questioning of someone who understood that the context of a request was more valuable than the request itself. She'd ask what job. She'd ask why the dock district. She'd ask, probably, why Zima was asking her instead of using her own observation, which was a fair question that Zima would need a credible answer for.

The answer: she needed coverage she couldn't provide herself. She was working a job that required moving through Viper territory on a specific schedule, and she couldn't simultaneously move and watch. She needed eyes on the patrols while she was in the area, and Lirra's network — the bread deliveries, the coded loaves, the web of informants who traded information for bread in a city where bread was scarcer than silver — had the reach to provide that coverage.

It was close enough to true. It left out the disc, the ghost, the passages, the vault. It presented the request as professional — one operator asking another for a service, with payment implied. Lirra would accept it on those terms. She might suspect there was more — Lirra always suspected there was more, because there usually was — but she wouldn't press. Not yet. She'd file her suspicions and she'd wait, and when Zima was ready to reveal the rest, Lirra would be ready to hear it, and the negotiation would begin from a position of established cooperation rather than raw disclosure.

That was the approach. Careful. Incremental. The way you approached everything in Ashport — not with trust but with the sequential extension of small, manageable risks.

She also needed to know which of the four access points was least watched.

The dock-district entrance was in Viper territory. She'd confirmed that. The patrols were regular, the surveillance was active, and the Vipers were looking for exactly the kind of activity that accessing the entrance would require — crouching in an alley, lifting an iron cover, descending into a drain shaft. Getting caught at the dock-district entrance would mean getting caught with the disc, which would mean getting dead.

The Harbour Gate entrance was the worst option for different reasons. The gate was a military installation. Garrison soldiers walked the wall above it. The streets around it were high-traffic, which was good for blending in and bad for spending time at a single location. You didn't crouch at the base of a fortified gate and pry up a drain cover without someone noticing, and the someone who noticed might be garrison or Viper or Hook or just a citizen who was angry enough and hungry enough to report you for the reward.

The market entrance was Hook territory. Less dangerous for her personally — the Hooks weren't searching for the disc, or didn't know to search for it — but Hook territory had its own patrols, its own surveillance, its own mechanisms for identifying people who didn't belong. She was known at the market as a pickpocket, not as an operative, and any change in her behaviour would register.

The old city entrance. South of Mercer Lane. Independent territory. This was the one. She'd thought so when she drew the map and she thought so now. The old city was the least governed, the least watched, the most forgiving of the four options. The streets were quieter. The patrols were sparse. The population was the kind of people who looked at their own feet when they walked, not at other people's business.

But she needed to find the exact location. She'd walked through the old city district that afternoon, slowly, observing, looking for the signs that had led her to the dock-district entrance: older stone, a cellar entrance or disused doorway, the darker grain of Maela's construction. She hadn't found it. The old city was old — that was the problem. Everything south of Mercer Lane was old stone, old foundations, old streets that had been repaired and rebuilt and patched so many times that the original construction was buried under layers of subsequent work. Finding Maela's stone in a district made entirely of old stone was a different problem from finding it in the dock district, where the older construction stood out against the later buildings like a scar against skin.

She'd go back. She'd look again, more carefully, more slowly. She'd bring the disc — not in her hand, not visible, but she'd press it against her side through the coat and see if the warmth changed when she was near the access point. The disc responded to proximity. It had been warmer in the dock-district alley than it had been on the street. Maybe it would guide her, if she let it.

Or maybe she was assigning intelligence to a piece of metal again. The line was thin, and getting thinner.

*          *          *

The disc was warm against her ribs.

She touched it through the coat. A habit now — the way you touched a sore tooth, the way you checked for your purse in a crowd, the small, repeated confirmation that a thing was still there. The canvas was warm where the metal sat. The warmth was steady, the low persistent heat that she'd come to recognise as the disc's resting state. Not the flare of Maela speaking, not the pulse of the dream-map, just the constant low temperature of a mechanism that was always on, always running, always aware of something she couldn't see.

Maela was quiet. The ghost came and went — present during the conversations, absent between them, with no warning of either state. Between contacts, the disc was just warm metal. Almost normal. Almost something you could get used to, the way you got used to the smell of the tannery or the sound of the drums or the weight of a debt you couldn't pay. You accommodated it. You made room for it in your sense of how the world worked, the way a bone sets around a break — not the original shape, but functional. Changed, but working.

She wondered what Maela did between conversations. Was the ghost conscious, waiting in the disc, aware of time passing? Or did she surface only when the energy accumulated enough to push through, rising from wherever she was the way a diver rises from depth — dark, then grey, then light, then air, then speech? Zima didn't know. She hadn't asked. She wasn't sure Maela knew either. The dead queen had described herself as a pattern, an impression in the mechanism, which sounded more like a recording than a consciousness. But recordings didn't get impatient. Recordings didn't ask to be remembered.

The other sleepers settled around her. The coughing man coughed — three waves in quick succession, then a long silence. The children breathed with the careful rhythm of the awake-and-pretending. The old dockhand snored, a low, regular vibration that came from deep in his chest and sounded, Zima thought, like a ship's hull creaking in a harbour swell. He was dreaming about cranes, maybe. Dreaming about the work he'd done for thirty years and couldn't do anymore. Dreaming about purpose.

She closed her eyes. She wasn't going to sleep yet — her mind was too full, the plan too active, the calculations still running — but she could rest. She could let her body go still while her mind worked, the way she'd learned to let her body go still in ration queues while her hands worked. Divided operation. The body at rest, the mind at full speed.

The arithmetic of survival: four coppers for the loft, two for breakfast, three for the silence-payments due this week to the people who knew about her alcove. Nine coppers gone before she earned anything. Today's take had been poor — seven coppers from the Harbour Gate queue, the marks thin and wary and keeping their coins close. Seven in, nine out. She was losing ground.

The disc was the only asset she had that could change the equation. Not by selling it — she'd decided against that, and the decision held. But by using it. If the vault contained what Maela described — supplies, currency, equipment — then the contents of the vault could clear the debt and fund whatever came next. If she could get to the vault. If she could get the contents out. If she could convert the contents to spendable coin without Garrick finding out. If, if, if. The plan was made of ifs, stacked on top of each other, each one dependent on the ones below, and if any single if collapsed the whole structure came down.

She was used to ifs. Every lift was an if. Every day was an if. The whole architecture of survival in Ashport was built on ifs — if the rations held, if the walls held, if the gangs didn't expand into your street, if the orc army stayed in the hills, if you didn't get sick, if you didn't get caught, if you woke up tomorrow and the city was still there. The ifs were the material you built with when you didn't have anything solid. You stacked them and you balanced them and you hoped the structure held long enough to stand on.

Tomorrow. The bakery. Mercer Lane. The back door.

She said it to herself like a list, like the count she did every night with her coins. A specific recitation, each element placed in order, each one leaning on the next. Tomorrow was the bakery. The bakery was Lirra. Lirra was intelligence. Intelligence was the access point. The access point was the passages. The passages were the vault. The vault was everything Maela had left.

She'd been telling herself she'd figure it out in the morning. She'd been telling herself that for weeks — months, really, since before the disc, since before the courier's hand on her wrist. The lie that got you through to morning. The useful fiction of a person who couldn't see the future and chose to pretend it would be different from the present.

But this time the lie had a specific shape. It wasn't the vague, shapeless hope of something will change, the passive waiting that the doorway people did until they stopped waiting. It was Lirra, the bakery, the passages below the city. Specific steps. Nameable actions. A sequence that started with a back door and ended — where? She didn't know. The vault, maybe. The ship, maybe. Something she couldn't see yet, beyond the tunnel and the door, in the place where Maela's planning ended and Zima's began.

Specific shapes were closer to plans than vague hopes. And plans were the difference between the people on the floor and the people in the doorways. The people on the floor still had plans, however fragile. They still paid their four coppers. They still got up in the morning and went to the queues and came back and paid their coppers and went to sleep and got up again. The rhythm was the plan, even when the rhythm was grinding them down, because rhythm was forward motion and forward motion was the opposite of the doorway.

The coughing man coughed. The children were silent. The dockhand snored. The candle was out and the dark was complete and the tannery smell rose through the boards like something buried trying to surface.

Zima closed her eyes. The disc was warm. The map was in her pocket. The plan was in her head — Lirra, the bakery, the access point, the passages — and the plan was thin and fragile and made of ifs, but it was hers, and it pointed somewhere, and somewhere was better than nowhere, and nowhere was where she'd been heading for as long as she could remember.

She slept. It took longer than usual — the calculations kept running, the list kept reciting itself, the coppers kept counting — but eventually the dark of the loft and the warmth of the disc and the rhythm of the coughing man's breath pulled her down into something that was close enough to sleep. She went under the way you went under an anaesthetic: not suddenly, not gently, but with the slow, reluctant surrender of a mind that had run out of things to calculate.

She dreamed of underground water.

She was starting to expect it. The dream came now with the regularity of the tides it depicted — the same stone channels, the same branching pattern, the same dark water moving through the dark with the purposeful patience of something that had been moving this way for a very long time. But tonight the dream was different. Tonight she was in the channels. Not observing them from above, not seeing them as a map — inside them, standing in water up to her ankles, the stone walls close on either side, the ceiling low above her head. The water was cold. The stone was cold. The air tasted of salt and mineral and the particular staleness of underground spaces that hadn't been open to the sky in centuries.

She walked forward. The channel branched and she took the left fork without thinking, because the map was in her body now and her body knew the way. The water deepened — knees, thighs, waist. She kept walking. The channel widened. The ceiling rose. And ahead, in the dark, a door.

The disc pulsed against her ribs, once, warm, and the door was there and she was there and the dream was there, and she stood in cold water in a stone channel under a city that was dying above her and she looked at the door and the door waited.

She woke with the disc hot against her skin and the loft dark around her and the coughing man coughing and the taste of salt water in her mouth.

Dawn was coming. She could feel it in the air — the slight shift in the loft's temperature, the change in the quality of the dark, the sounds of the city beginning to stir outside the walls. The other sleepers were moving. The woman with the children was sitting up, gathering her things with the quiet efficiency of someone who'd learned to pack in the dark. The old dockhand was already standing, stretching, his joints cracking in a sequence that sounded like someone walking on gravel.

Zima sat up. She touched the disc through her coat. Still warm. Still there.

Today. The bakery. The back door.

She got up. She folded the straw pallet the way Druse preferred — thirds, tucked against the wall. She went down the stairs, stepping over the cracked third step. The morning was grey and cold and the fog was thin and the tannery below was already working, the smoke from Druse's curing fires rising into the dawn air with the sour, chemical smell of hides being turned into leather.

She walked north toward Mercer Lane. The disc was warm. The map was in her pocket. The dream was fading — the stone channels, the cold water, the door — but not all the way. Not anymore. The dreams didn't fade all the way anymore. They left residue, the way salt left residue on stone when the tide went out. Each dream deposited something, and the something accumulated, and the accumulation was becoming a thing she carried with her the way she carried the disc: always present, always warm, always pointing toward whatever was underneath.

Mercer Lane was six streets away. The bakery would be open. The back door would be there, around the side, past the missing sign letters and the smell of wheat and woodsmoke that carried twenty paces.

Zima walked. The fog moved around her. The city woke. And below her feet, below the cobblestones and the sewers and the foundations of two centuries of building, the passages waited in the dark, full of water and silence and a dead woman's preparations, patient the way only underground things were patient, and ready.
