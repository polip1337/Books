CHAPTER THIRTY
The Inheritance



Morning light. The rubble shelf on the harbour wall. Eldrin with a brass-rimmed magnifying lens held six inches from the disc's reverse, his breath coming shallow and controlled, as though too much air would disturb the characters.

"Hold it steady," he said.

Zima held the disc flat on her palm, face down, the smooth reverse catching the early sun. Smooth, she'd called it. Smooth was wrong. Under the magnifying lens the surface was covered — dense, tight characters, packed so close that to the naked eye they blurred into a featureless sheen.

Eldrin moved the lens in a slow arc across the surface. His grey eyes behind the glass were enormous — the magnification made them look like something from a deep-water fish, round and pale and frighteningly focused.

"The secondary inventory continues on the reverse," he said. "More detailed than the face inscription. Quantities, conditions, preservation dates." He shifted the lens. "Navigation charts — three sets. Coastal, deep water, archipelago. Star tables for the equatorial latitudes. A pilot's log, sealed."

"A pilot's log."

"Whoever built this vault expected the bearer to sail south. The charts, the star tables, the navigation instruments — they're all oriented for a southern passage." He moved the lens again, lower, toward the disc's edge. "And here. Below the inventory. A different hand."

"Different how?"

"The inventory is written in formal Tidal Script — the same hand that inscribed the face. Clean, even, professional. This line is written in the same script but a different style. Faster. The characters are compressed. The angles are sharper. As though the writer was hurrying. Or emotional."

He brought the lens closer. The disc's warmth intensified — Zima could feel it through her palm, a slow build that pushed toward uncomfortable and held there.

"What does it say?"

Eldrin read it once silently. She watched his eyes move across the characters — right to left, the Tidal Script direction. He reached the end of the line. His face changed.

He read it again.

"Eldrin."

"The line says—" He pulled the lens away and straightened. His hands were shaking. The lens trembled in his grip, throwing a circle of light that jittered across the stone. "The line says: The bearer who opens the vault accepts the inheritance. Custodian of the city. Warden of Ashport. What was started must be completed."

The words sat in the air between them. The harbour wind took them and offered nothing in return.

"Inheritance," Zima said.

"Not in the common sense. Not wealth passed from parent to child. In Tidal Script the word is..." He searched. "Entanglement. A transfer of obligation. The inheritance is the job. The vault's contents aren't a gift — they're a payment for services that the bearer is obligated to provide."

"Services."

"Custodian of the city. Warden of Ashport." He set the lens down on the stone between them, the brass ring clicking against the rubble. "The disc is a crown, Zima. Not a key. A crown."

The disc pulsed against her palm. Warm, then warmer, then warm again. A heartbeat rhythm, but faster than before. She looked at the dark metal in her hand — the tidal chart, the access markings, the inventory in present tense — and tried to reconcile the object she'd taken from a dying courier's grip with the word crown.

"I don't want a crown," she said.

"The script doesn't ask what you want. That's the point. Tidal Script is functional. The inscription says the bearer who opens the vault accepts the inheritance. Not the bearer who agrees. Not the bearer who consents. Accepts. The act of opening is the acceptance."

"Then I won't open it."

Eldrin looked at her. The grey eyes had lost their scholarly distance. What was in them now was fear — specific, informed fear, the kind that came from understanding a mechanism rather than imagining a threat.

"You'll open it," he said. "You know you'll open it. Everything you've done since taking the disc has been moving toward that vault. You recruited a spy. You recruited a soldier. You recruited me. You have rope, a map, a patrol schedule, and a crew. The opening is already happening. The script set the conditions two centuries ago, and you've been fulfilling them since the moment the courier's hand dropped the disc into yours."

The words were a blade, and they were accurate. She looked at her own hands — small, scarred, the abrasions from the crane rope still visible at the base of her fingers. Cutpurse hands. Garrick's hands, trained and shaped and sent into pockets and crowds for nine years. The disc sat in them like it belonged there, and it was warm, and the warmth was a contract she hadn't known she was signing.

"What does Warden mean?" she asked. "In practice. What does the custodian of the city actually do?"

"I don't know." He said it flat, honest. "The texts reference the Warden system — multiple wardens, each responsible for a sector of the city's infrastructure. Passages, water, walls, harbour. The founding authority appointed them. The keys granted access and authority. But the texts don't describe the work. They describe the obligation."

"What was started must be completed."

"That's the binding clause. In Tidal Script, it's not a motto. It's a term of contract. Whatever Maela started — the passages, the vault, the system — the bearer is obligated to continue."

"Or what?"

"I don't know or what. The boundary stone on my desk enforced itself by knocking cups off tables. This is the same script at a scale I can't predict. The enforcement mechanism could be anything."

The disc cooled. The warmth dropped from uncomfortable to baseline in a count of five, as though the metal had been listening and decided the conversation had reached its necessary point.

Maela's voice came. Thin, distant, the bandwidth of a whisper carried through several walls.

"He reads well."

Zima closed her hand around the disc. You didn't tell me this.

"I told you the vault holds what you'd need. I told you the disc is a key. I told you I wanted to be remembered."

You didn't tell me about the obligation. The inheritance. The crown.

"You didn't ask the right question." A pause. The disc's warmth was steady — not apologetic, not defiant. Just steady, the way it always was. "Nobody asks the right question before they're ready to hear the answer. You're ready now."

What if I don't open the vault?

"Then the siege continues and the city starves and the supplies sit beneath it. Fifty bolts of sailcloth and three hundred fathoms of rope and enough spice and coin to buy passage to anywhere. All of it waiting. All of it useless."

That's not a threat.

"No. It's arithmetic. You're good at arithmetic."

The voice faded. The disc settled. Zima opened her hand and looked at the dark metal. The tidal chart. The access markings. The inventory that said holds instead of held.

Eldrin was watching her. His grey eyes moved between her face and the disc, reading the conversation he could only hear one side of.

"She spoke," he said.

"She spoke."

"What did she say?"

"That I didn't ask the right question." Zima put the disc back in its canvas scrap and began stitching it into the coat lining. The needle was familiar in her fingers now — she'd done this so many times the motion was automatic. "She's right. I didn't."

"The binding—"

"I heard you. I heard her. I understand what opening the vault means." The needle went through the canvas. In, out, pull tight. "I also understand what not opening it means. There are people in the tanner's loft — a woman with children, a dockhand, a man with the lung sickness. The rations were cut by a third. The gangs are compressing the city into a space where nobody can move without choosing a side. And beneath it all there's a vault with supplies that could change everything, and the only thing between that vault and those people is a girl with a nine-silver debt and a piece of metal she took from a dead man's hand."

She finished the stitching. The disc sat against her ribs. Warm. Steady. Patient the way Torv was patient, the way the vault was patient, the way the city was patient under its siege — absorbing blow after blow and waiting for something to change.

"I don't want a crown," she said. "I want to pay off Garrick. I want to stop sleeping in a loft that smells like tanning chemicals. I want to eat more than stew and stale bread." She tucked the needle into her collar seam. "But I'm going to open the vault anyway, because I was always going to open it, and the binding clause just tells me what I already knew."

Eldrin looked at her the way a man looks at someone who has read the terms of a contract and signed it with their eyes open. Not pity. Not admiration. Something between the two — the specific recognition of someone making a decision they can't undo with full knowledge that they can't undo it.

"What do you need from me?" he said.

"I need you underground. The passages will have inscriptions — Maela said there are markers at the junctions, directions, warnings. I need you to read them."

"And after?"

"After the vault, I'll need someone who understands what the Warden system means. What Maela built and what it requires. You spent your career studying this. I need you to keep studying it."

"The university sacked me for studying it."

"I'm not the university."

He looked at the harbour. The fog was thin, the light clean, the blockade ships visible as dark shapes against the grey water. The drums were quiet again — one of the silent intervals that felt like the city holding its breath.

"I'll come underground," he said. "I'll read the inscriptions. I'll tell you what the script says and what it means." He picked up the magnifying lens and turned it over in his hands. "But I want you to understand something. The binding clause isn't theoretical. What was started must be completed. If you open that vault, you accept the obligation. The script will enforce it. And I can't tell you what enforcement looks like because nothing at this scale has been activated in two centuries."

"I understand."

"You understand the words. That's not the same as understanding the thing."

"It's what I have."

He put the lens in his coat pocket. He stood. For a moment he was just a thin man in a patched coat on a broken wall, silhouetted against the grey harbour. Then he turned and looked at her, and the grey eyes were clear, and the scholar's focus was in them, and the fear was still there but arranged now — compartmentalised, put in its place, the way Zima put her debt in its place so she could function around it.

"Four people," he said. "One vault. A binding clause. And an army outside the walls."

"Yes."

"When?"

"Soon. The patrol map is done. The route is scouted. I have rope. I have you. The only thing I'm waiting for is the right night."

"You mean the night Garrick isn't watching."

"I mean the night we can get underground without anyone knowing we've gone."

He nodded. He walked west along the harbour wall, his coat pulling in the wind. Zima watched him until the fog took him.

*          *          *

She sat on the rubble for a long time after he left.

The disc pulsed slow. The harbour wind came in off the water, carrying salt and the flat mineral smell of the deep channel. The blockade ships sat motionless. The drums started again — three slow, seven fast — and the sound settled into her bones the way it had settled for eleven months, background noise that was also the most important sound in the world.

Four people. She counted them the way she counted coppers.

Lirra: intelligence, coded bread, twelve percent, practical. She'd come underground because standing on the surface while other people took risks was not something Lirra's particular pride permitted.

Torv: muscle, silence, the service passage, the lifting gesture. He'd come underground because partnership was the fists-together gesture and the gesture meant he went where she went.

Eldrin: scholarship, the Tidal Script, the binding clause, the frightened clarity of someone who understood the mechanism. He'd come underground because the thing he'd been sacked for studying was real, and he'd rather face it than live without knowing.

And herself. Zima. Cutpurse. Debtor. Nine silvers against a lifetime of picking pockets in ration queues. She'd go underground because the disc was warm against her ribs and the vault held supplies and the city was starving and the word warden meant inheritor and the inheritance was the job and the job was the city and the city was hers whether she wanted it or not.

What was started must be completed.

She stood. The rubble shifted under her feet, loose stone grinding against loose stone. She looked at the harbour one more time — the ships, the fog, the hills with their fifteen thousand patient soldiers — and she walked back into the city.

The crew was assembled. Four people, one vault, a binding clause that two centuries hadn't weakened.

She had rope. She had a map. She had a patrol schedule and a service passage and a scholar with a magnifying lens and an orc with a four-foot iron pipe and a baker who sold secrets in bread.

And she had the disc. The crown. The key. The warm metal heartbeat against her ribs that said holds in present tense and meant the vault was waiting and the vault would wait forever if it had to, because the script didn't distinguish between now and always, and always was exactly how long Maela had planned for.

The next step was the descent.

She walked through the city, counting streets, counting steps, counting the days until the right night came. The fog closed behind her. The drums went three slow, seven fast. Ashport went on being Ashport — besieged, starving, compressed, alive. And beneath its streets, sealed in stone and script and two hundred years of patient dark, the vault held.

Present tense.
