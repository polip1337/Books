CHAPTER FOURTEEN
The Dream of Channels



The night after she visited Dav, she slept properly for the first time since taking the disc.

She hadn't planned it. She'd lain down in the alcove with the usual expectation of sleeping badly — the shallow, broken rest she'd trained herself into over six months under the bridge, the kind that kept one ear open and one hand near her knife and gave her three or four hours of something that wasn't quite unconsciousness. That had been the pattern since Pell. The disc's warmth, the paranoia, the Vipers' tightened patrols — all of it had kept her at the surface of sleep, never fully under, never fully resting, and the cumulative weight of it sat behind her eyes and in her joints like a low fever. But tonight her body made a decision her mind hadn't authorised. She went down hard and fast, past the surface, past the shallow margin, into the deep water of actual sleep, and the dream was waiting for her.

Water.

Not harbour water — not the flat grey chop she saw every day from the Coppergate bridge, salt-smelling and full of debris. This was underground water. She could hear it in the dream the way you hear a river from inside a house: muffled, directional, the sound of something large and purposeful moving through stone channels in the dark.

The channels were stone. She could feel that without seeing it — the dream was dark, or nearly dark, lit by a sourceless grey light that was less illumination than permission to perceive. The stone was old. Not old the way Ashport's buildings were old, not the age of mortar and brick and timber that rotted and was replaced and rotted again. This was old the way bedrock was old — geological, patient, the kind of old that existed before anyone started counting years. But the channels had been carved into this stone by hands. Cut. Shaped. She could feel the tool marks on the walls, the grooves where chisels had bitten, the smooth sections where the stone had been dressed and finished, and she felt these things not through her fingers but through something larger, something that had no name she knew, as if her body had expanded to fill the entire passage network and every inch of stone was in contact with every inch of her.

That was wrong. She knew it was wrong inside the dream, the way you sometimes know a dream is a dream without it stopping. She was in one place — she was lying in the alcove under the Coppergate bridge with her coat pulled around her — and she was in every place at once, stretched thin across a network of stone channels, feeling water move through her the way blood moves through a body. The wrongness of it was specific and physical and she wanted it to stop and she did not want it to stop, and both feelings were true at the same time.

She knew where the water was going. The knowledge was there, fully formed, the way dream knowledge always is — no learning, no confusion, just certainty arriving complete. The channels branched in four places. One branch ran toward the harbour, and she knew it was the harbour because she could taste salt at the end of it, faint and mineral, the taste of a memory rather than a substance. One branch ran toward the northern wall, and she knew that because the stone was different there — heavier, denser, compressed by the weight of the fortification above. One ran toward the centre of the old city, where the buildings were oldest and the streets narrowest and the bedrock was nearest the surface. And one ran south, toward a place she couldn't name but could feel.

The fourth branch was the worst. The other three had destinations she could recognise — the harbour, the wall, the old city centre. The fourth had something else. Not a destination. A presence. A density, like the weight of a person in a room you haven't entered — you know they're there without seeing them, without hearing them, by the particular quality of the air and the way the silence arranges itself around the shape of a body. Something was at the end of that fourth branch. Or not at the end — at the meeting point, the place where all four branches converged, the central node of the network where the water gathered before it was distributed. A chamber. Round, she thought. Deep. Below everything else.

She tried to move toward it. In the dream, moving meant the same as wanting — she directed her attention toward the central point and the channels carried her, the water pulling her along, the stone sliding past her expanded awareness like the walls of a corridor. The density increased as she got closer. The pull strengthened. She could feel the shape of the chamber now, not with her eyes but with the same nameless sense that had let her feel the tool marks and the water: a round room, vaulted, with walls that curved inward and a ceiling lost in the dark and a floor that was not a floor but a convergence, the place where the four channels met and the water pooled and something waited.

Something that knew she was there.

She woke up.

*          *          *

The disc was in her fist.

She'd been holding it in her sleep, which she didn't remember deciding to do. The coat was open. The canvas pocket she'd sewn below her left ribs was torn — not cut, torn, the stitching ripped by a hand that had gone in hard and fast and pulled the disc free — and the disc sat on her palm, warm and dark and slick with the heat of a thing gripped for hours. Her fingers were stiff. The joints cracked when she opened her hand, and the disc stayed where it was, stuck to her skin by sweat and warmth, and the markings on its face — the fine lines she'd studied on the first night — caught the grey pre-dawn light and looked exactly like what she'd seen in the dream.

A map. Four branches. A central point.

She sat up. The alcove was cold. The fog was thin this morning — not the thick harbour kind but the high, pale scrim that let you see the shape of the bridge above and the flat grey water beyond and the dark suggestion of the pilings and the boats. She could hear the harbour bell. She could hear the seabirds. She could hear a man coughing somewhere on the street above, the productive hack of someone who'd been sleeping rough and breathing salt air for too long.

The dream was still in her head.

That was the problem. That was the specific, precise problem that turned a strange dream into something else. She sat under the bridge and she waited for it to dissolve, the way dreams dissolved — you got up, you moved, you thought about other things, and the dream came apart into scraps, losing detail, losing coherence, until by midmorning all you had was a feeling or a colour and the rest was gone. She'd had vivid dreams before. Everyone in Ashport had vivid dreams. The siege produced them — stress, hunger, the low-grade fear that ran through every day like a wire through a cable, holding everything tight. Vivid dreams were normal. They faded. The mind wasn't built to hold two realities at the same time, and the waking world always won.

This one wasn't dissolving.

She drank water from the jug she kept against the wall. She walked to the alcove's edge and stood in the morning air and breathed. She counted her breaths — eight in, eight out — and she thought about the queue she'd work today and the boots that needed resoling and whether the fish market would have mackerel because she'd been craving salt and fat for a week. Ordinary thoughts. Surface thoughts. The kind of thinking that should have pushed the dream down and covered it over the way a tide covers a shore.

The map was as clear in her head as it had been in the dream. Four branches. Four junctions. The central point, still pulling at her attention, still present, a density she could feel the way you feel a missing tooth — by the shape of the absence, by the way everything around it oriented toward the gap. Clearer than it had been in the dream, if anything, because waking had given it edges the dream hadn't provided. She could have drawn it. She could have sat down with charcoal and paper and put the whole network on a surface, accurate to the compass points, and the fact that she could do this — that a single night's dream had left her with cartographic knowledge she hadn't earned — was not normal and was not right and was not going away.

She thought: I'm going mad.

She thought: or the disc is doing something to me.

Neither option was good.

*          *          *

She re-sewed the disc into her coat. The stitching had torn when she'd pulled it out in her sleep — hard enough to rip the canvas, which meant force, which meant her sleeping body had wanted the disc in her hand badly enough to damage the pocket she'd made for it. She put in new stitches, tighter, double-threaded, the work slow and careful because her hands were stiff and the needle was dull and the morning cold was in her fingers. The disc went back against her ribs. The warmth was there. The weight was there.

She worked the morning queue at the Harbour Gate. Two hundred and eight people by her count at the halfway mark, which was twelve fewer than two days ago and consistent with the compression she'd been tracking — fewer people in the queue meant either fewer people in the district or fewer people willing to stand in a Hook-watched line, and both meant the same thing, which was that the liveable space was shrinking. She took one purse. Thin. Five coppers and a copper button she'd throw away. She ate the ration bread standing in the shadow of the wall and she watched the Viper men at the distribution table and she tried, with genuine effort, to not think about underground water.

The map was there. It sat in her head the way a tune sits after you've heard it played — not something you're choosing to think about but something that plays itself, automatically, running in the background beneath your real thoughts, and every time you notice it you try to push it away and every time you push it away it comes back, and after a while you stop pushing and it just plays.

She walked south along the harbour road. She could feel one of the four branches beneath her feet.

Not literally. She wanted to be clear about this, even to herself, especially to herself — she was not feeling water through cobblestones. Her boots touched stone and nothing else. The road was solid. The city was solid. The physical world was operating as advertised. But the map in her head was overlaid on the physical world the way a template lies over a page, and the map said: here. Below this road. Thirty feet down, maybe forty. A stone channel, four feet wide, running south-southwest, carrying water from the harbour junction toward the central point. She knew this the way she knew the timing of the garrison patrols or the distance between her alcove and the nearest alley. Knowledge that had been placed in her head by a dream that had been placed in her head by a disc that she'd taken from a dead man, and the chain of causation was exactly as insane as it sounded.

She walked past the harbour. The channel below the road was real, or the map said it was real, or she was imagining it was real, and she had no way to tell the difference because the difference was thirty feet of earth and stone and she wasn't about to dig.

She turned south toward the old city gate. Mercer Lane. The old district, narrow streets, low buildings, stone so old it had turned grey-black with centuries of salt air. She'd walked these streets a thousand times and never once thought about what was underneath them. Now she could feel it. Another branch, running south, deeper than the harbour one, cutting through bedrock that was harder and denser and darker than the limestone near the water. She could feel the direction of the water in the channel — moving, slow, purposeful, the same directional flow she'd heard in the dream. The same sound. The same certainty.

She stopped walking. She stood in the middle of Mercer Lane, on the cobblestones, with the fog thinning around her and a salt-fish vendor staring at her from his stall because she'd stopped moving in the middle of a lane and people who stopped moving in Ashport were either lost or in trouble, and she looked down at the street and she could feel the channel below her as clearly as she could feel the cobblestones under her boots.

She said, very quietly, to nobody: "This isn't real."

The disc pulsed. Once. The same single beat of warmth she'd felt that first night — a contraction, a heartbeat, a response that might have been coincidence and might not have been. The warmth spread from her ribs into her chest and faded.

She started walking again. The fish vendor looked away. The morning went on.

*          *          *

The afternoon was worse.

Not because anything happened. Nothing happened. She worked the afternoon queue — a different point, the one on Salt Lane, which was smaller and Hook-adjacent and therefore riskier but also less Viper-watched, which was the trade-off she needed today. She took two purses, both thin. She bought a half-portion of salt fish and ate it in an alley off the market square and she sat on a crate and she tried, for the third time that day, to think about something other than the map.

The map was there. It was always there. It sat behind her eyes like a second vision, transparent, overlaid on everything she looked at, so that the streets had a surface and a depth and the depth was stone channels and water and the four branches running from their access points to the central chamber and the chamber sitting in the dark below everything, round and deep and full of that density she could feel but couldn't name.

She walked past the old city gate on her way back to the bridge. The fourth branch was below her. South-running, deep, cutting through bedrock. She could feel it the way you feel a draught under a door — not on her skin, not with her hands, but with something else, something the dream had opened in her that she hadn't asked for and didn't want and couldn't close.

She thought about madness. Properly, seriously, the way she thought about risks — weighing the evidence, assessing the probability, trying to be honest with herself because self-deception in Ashport was the kind of luxury that killed you. Was she going mad? The evidence: a dream that wouldn't dissolve. A map she couldn't unsee. Sensory information — the feeling of channels below streets — that had no physical source. These were symptoms. She could list them the way a physician would list them. Hallucination. Persistent ideation. Sensory distortion. She knew what these words meant because she'd known mad people, the ones who sat in doorways and talked to the fog, and she'd watched them carefully the way she watched everything, and she'd noted the signs — the fixed stare, the repetitive speech, the confident description of things that weren't there.

Was she doing that?

She considered. She held the idea up and looked at it from several angles. Then she put it down.

No. She was not going mad. She was not going mad because madness didn't come with a map that matched a physical object she was carrying, and madness didn't pulse when you talked to it, and madness didn't arrive on the same night you fell into your first deep sleep in four days after carrying a piece of warm metal that had come from a dead man who'd been carrying it for someone else. The disc was the cause. The dream was the effect. The map was information, not pathology, and the information was either accurate or it was the most detailed, internally consistent hallucination ever produced by a nineteen-year-old brain running on thin porridge and bad sleep, and she didn't believe she was that creative.

Which left the other option. The disc was doing something to her. The disc had given her the dream. The disc had placed the map in her head, the way you place a note in someone's pocket. Deliberately. With purpose. And the map wasn't going away because it wasn't supposed to go away — it was supposed to stay, to teach her, to show her what was under her feet, and it was doing its job.

She didn't like this option either. She didn't like it because it meant the disc was not just a warm piece of metal. It meant the disc had agency, or something like agency — the ability to transmit information, to choose when and what to transmit, to reach into her sleeping mind and plant a map that her waking mind couldn't remove. That was worse than madness, in some ways. Madness was internal. Madness was yours. Whatever the disc was doing came from outside, from the metal itself, from whatever mechanism or design or intent had been built into it when it was made.

She thought about throwing it away.

The thought was immediate and practical: take the disc from the coat, walk to the harbour wall, throw. The water would take it. The grey harbour water, full of silt and debris, would close over it, and the disc would sink, and the warmth would be gone, and the map would — what? Dissolve? Stay? She didn't know.

The thought of letting go produced a specific, physical anxiety she did not like and did not examine. Her stomach tightened. Her hand went to the coat, to the place where the disc sat, and pressed against the canvas. The idea of not having it — of the empty space where the warmth was, of her ribs without the weight — sat in her body like nausea. Not in her mind. Her mind was fine with the idea. Her mind said: throw it away, go back to the queues, pay off Garrick's debt with time and coppers, live the life you were living before Pell grabbed your wrist. Her mind said: sane, safe, ordinary.

Her body said no.

She didn't examine the no. She was afraid of what she'd find — that the anxiety was not hers. That it was the disc's, projected into her, the way the map had been projected into her dream. That she was being held by an object that had decided she was important and was not going to let her disagree.

She left the alley. She walked north. The map walked with her.

*          *          *

Night came. The fog did its work. The drums started their count from the hills — three slow, then the pause, then seven fast — and the sound rolled over Ashport and settled into the stones and the silence that followed was the silence of a city holding its breath the way it held its breath every night, waiting for the drums to stop, waiting for the morning, waiting.

She lay in the alcove. The disc was warm against her ribs. The fog pressed against the bridge's underside, and through it she could hear the harbour: water on pilings, the creak of moored boats, the faint slap of tide against the seawall. And underneath those sounds — she was no longer willing to call it imagination, because imagination was a word for things that came and went and this did not come and go — the sound of water underground. Muffled. Directional. The channels from her dream, carrying water through stone, thirty feet below the street.

She closed her eyes. The map was there. She opened them. The map was still there, translucent, persistent, laid over the darkness of the alcove ceiling like a drawing on glass.

She was starting to accept — not accept, that was too clean a word. She was starting to stop fighting the understanding that whatever the disc had done to her, whatever the dream had given her, it was not going to go away on its own. Not through rest. Not through distraction. Not through the force of wanting it gone. The map was in her head the way a scar was on skin: a mark left by contact, permanent or close to it, the record of something that had happened and couldn't un-happen.

She could still throw the disc away. The option existed. She could rip the stitching open and take the dark metal from its pocket and walk to the harbour wall and let it go.

She wasn't going to.

She knew this the way she knew the map — completely, immediately, without deciding. The disc was the most important thing she'd ever held, and she knew that without being able to explain it, and the knowing was uncomfortable and wrong and entirely her own. Not the disc's influence. Not the dream's residue. Hers. The same instinct that had made her take it from Pell's hand — reflex, not decision. The recognition, below thought, that the thing in front of her mattered.

She lay under the bridge with the disc against her ribs and the map behind her eyes and the fog pressing in and the harbour sounding its patient note against the pilings. The night passed. She slept, eventually — shallow sleep, the old kind, the surface kind that kept one ear open. No dreams this time. The dream had done its work. It had delivered its message and it was finished and what it had left behind was not a memory of a dream but a permanent addition to the architecture of her knowledge, a new room built inside her head that she hadn't asked for and couldn't demolish.

In the morning the map was still there.

The disc was warm. The city was the same. The fog thinned. The drums were silent. The queues would form. The Vipers would watch. Garrick's debt would wait.

And below all of it, below the streets and the foundations and the slow-dying patience of seventeen thousand people in a city under siege, four stone channels carried water through the dark toward a chamber she could feel and couldn't name, and the disc against her ribs knew the way.
