CHAPTER TWENTY-SEVEN
Bread and Iron



Lirra's patrol map was precise.

She'd drawn it on the back of a flour sack — charcoal lines on rough canvas, the dock district rendered in clean geometry. Streets, alleys, buildings, the quay. Red marks for Viper positions: two patrol routes, annotated with times. Blue marks for the shift-change overlap. A gap, clearly labelled, at the fifteenth hour.

"One hundred and nine breaths from the fish stall," Lirra said. "I counted twice. The second count was one hundred and eleven. Call it one hundred and ten."

"I got one hundred and twelve from the harbour wall."

"The wall is higher. You're seeing the patrol earlier and losing them later. At ground level the gap starts when they turn the corner, not when they clear the sight line."

She was right. Zima took the map and studied it. The patrol routes were marked with small arrows — direction of travel, pace notes. The experienced pair (Lirra had labelled them A1 and A2) walked the alley at a steady pace, never stopping. The checking pair (B1 and B2) stopped at three points — the Harbourmaster's door, the drain cover, and the far end where the alley met the street.

"B2 checks the drain cover," Zima said.

"Every time. Three seconds. He looks down, looks around, moves on. He's not checking for anything specific — it's a habit."

"That costs us three seconds."

"It costs you nothing. B2 is outgoing. He checks the drain on his way out. By the time the gap opens, he's already past it."

Zima looked at the map again. Lirra had drawn the drain cover as a small circle with an X through it — an unintentional echo of the disc's markings that made the back of Zima's neck tighten.

"The service passage," Zima said. She traced a line on the map from the warehouse row to the alley. "There's a back route. Between the warehouse buildings. It runs the length of the row and comes out through an arch."

Lirra frowned. "I didn't map that."

"Someone else did."

"Who?"

Zima folded the map along the charcoal lines, making the creases sharp with her thumbnail. "I need to introduce you to someone. He's large. He doesn't speak. He's on our side."

Lirra's real eye went flat. "Tell me more."

"Orc deserter. Lives in the warehouse at the end of the quay. He's been there fifteen days. He's mapped the dock district better than either of us, and he knows a route to the drain cover that avoids every patrol sight line you've marked."

"An orc."

"A deserter. They cut his tongue out."

Lirra was quiet for a long time. She picked up her chicory cup, looked into it, set it back down without drinking.

"The army that's besieging us," she said. "His army."

"His former army. He cut the insignia off his vest. He's sleeping in a collapsed warehouse with a clay jug and a bedroll. He's not with them."

"You don't know that."

"I know what a person looks like when they're surviving alone. I know what it looks like when someone's been abandoned by the thing that was supposed to keep them."

The words came out harder than she'd intended. She heard herself in them — the bridge, the ledger, the debt that had been a roof and a meal and had become a leash. Lirra heard it too. Her expression shifted, a quarter-degree of recognition that she covered immediately.

"What does he want?" Lirra asked.

"Partnership. Not coin. Not food. He made the gesture — two fists together. He wants to be part of something."

"Something that involves going underground in passages that haven't been opened in two centuries, in a city under siege by his own people."

"Former people."

Lirra looked at the map on the worktable. The charcoal lines, the red marks, the blue marks, the gap at the fifteenth hour. She traced the line where Zima had drawn the service passage.

"I want to meet him," she said.

*          *          *

They went that afternoon.

The dock district was in its dead period — the quay empty, the warehouse row silent, the Hook boys absent. Lirra walked three paces behind Zima, staying close to the building walls, moving with a quiet economy that Zima hadn't seen before. In the bakery Lirra moved like a baker — steady, grounded, occupying space. On the street she moved like something else. Lighter. More aware. The way someone moved who had spent years being looked for by people she didn't want to find her.

At the warehouse gap Zima stopped. "He's inside. He'll know we're coming. Don't make any sudden movements."

"I'm a baker, not a soldier."

"You're not a baker."

"That's fair."

They stepped through the gap. The warehouse interior was lit by the afternoon sun through the broken roof, the light columns falling across the debris field in slanting parallels. Torv was standing.

He'd heard them. He was facing the entrance, the iron pipe in his right hand — not raised, but held, the way a man holds a walking stick when he's not sure if he's going to need it for something else. His amber eyes moved from Zima to Lirra and stayed there.

Lirra stopped walking. She looked up at Torv — up, because even standing straight she barely reached his chest — and her face went through three expressions in rapid succession. Zima counted them: surprise, assessment, decision.

"You're tall," Lirra said.

Torv looked at Zima. Back at Lirra. He set the pipe down, leaning it against the wall with deliberate care. He sat.

"He's trying not to loom," Zima said.

"I can see that." Lirra walked forward until she was eight feet from him. She reached into the canvas bag she carried and pulled out a loaf — fresh, still warm, the crust dark brown and split along the top where the steam had escaped. She set it on the floor between them and stepped back.

Torv looked at the bread. He looked at Lirra. His hands, resting on his knees, opened palm-upward. The gesture was universal — receiving, accepting, no threat.

"Zima says you know the dock district," Lirra said. "That you've mapped routes she hasn't found."

He nodded. One motion, slow.

"Show me."

He stood again and walked to the dust patch where he'd drawn for Zima the day before. His previous map was still there, scuffed but legible. He knelt and added to it — new lines, new marks, new routes. The service passages, the sight lines, the patrol approaches. His drawing was rough but precise, each mark placed with the spatial confidence of someone who thought in distances and angles.

Lirra knelt beside him. Not close — four feet of distance between them — but low, at his level, looking at the map from the same angle he drew it.

"Here," she said, pointing to a line. "This is the second patrol route?"

He nodded.

"And this gap here — between the warehouse and the customs house — how wide?"

He held his hands apart. Four feet.

"Wide enough for you?"

He made the sideways gesture — tilting his torso to demonstrate. Sideways. Tight, but possible.

"Not wide enough for a barrel or a crate."

He shook his head.

"Then the heavy equipment goes through the alley during the gap. You go through the service passage before the gap opens. You're in position at the drain cover first. We arrive during the gap. You open the cover. We go down."

He looked at her. Then at Zima. His expression — what she could read of it through the orc features that moved differently from human features, the brow ridge and the wide jaw and the eyes that held light like amber glass — was something she hadn't seen from him before.

Respect. He'd just been given an operational briefing by a baker, and the briefing was better than his own.

He nodded. Firm.

Lirra stood. She wiped dust from her knees. "He'll do," she said to Zima.

"That was fast."

"I don't need long. He thinks in routes and positions. He's been trained, he's disciplined, and he's been alone long enough that working with people will make him loyal." She paused. "Also, he handled that pipe like it weighed nothing. I don't know what we'll find underground. I'd like someone who can hit things if we have to."

Torv was looking between them. He couldn't follow the rapid exchange, but he could read the tone. He picked up the bread, broke it in two, and offered the larger half to Lirra.

She took it. They ate in the warehouse with the afternoon light falling through the broken roof, and nobody spoke, and the silence had the texture of an agreement.

*          *          *

Afterward, Zima walked Lirra back to the service passage exit. The dock district was still empty. The fog was coming in off the harbour, low and thick, turning the warehouse row into grey shapes.

"The scholar," Lirra said. "Eldrin. You said Dav told you about him."

"Dav said he's at the Wayfarers Hall. East side. Used to be at Ironmore University. Ancient languages."

"I know the Hall. It's a flophouse for people who used to be something. Merchants, clerks, the odd sailor. The kind of place that charges by the week and doesn't ask what happened."

"Do you know Eldrin?"

"I know the name. One of my six — the harbour clerk, Marten — mentioned him. Eldrin used to appraise objects at the harbour office. Pre-Founding artefacts, mostly. He stopped three months ago. Marten said he'd become difficult."

"Difficult how?"

"The kind of difficult that happens when a scholar finds something he wasn't supposed to find and can't stop thinking about it." Lirra tucked the remaining bread into her bag. "Be careful with him. Smart people who've lost everything are the most dangerous kind. They'll do anything to feel important again."

Zima thought about that on the walk back. The tanner's loft was a forty-minute walk from the dock district, and the route took her through the market's edge where the buffer zone was narrowest. Two Hook boys on a corner, marking faces. A Viper pair on the far side of the street, walking with the unhurried purpose of people who owned the ground. Between them: a strip of cobbles fifteen feet wide where civilians walked quickly and didn't look at either side.

She walked through the gap. The disc was warm. The patrol map was folded inside her coat on the right side, opposite the disc. The plan was taking shape — not the vague shape of an idea but the specific shape of a timetable, with routes and positions and a crew that numbered three and needed to number four.

Tomorrow. The Wayfarers Hall. The east side. A scholar named Eldrin who used to read old scripts and had stopped because he'd become difficult.

She'd been difficult her whole life. She could work with difficult.

The tanner's loft. Three coppers — the last of the queue earnings. The pallet. The dark. The woman with children wasn't there tonight. The old dockhand was. The coughing man was quiet. A new face in the corner — young, male, the hollow-cheeked look of someone who'd been eating once a day for too long.

Zima lay in the dark and ran through the plan. The drain cover. Torv first, through the service passage. Lirra and Zima during the gap. The descent. The passages. The vault.

She needed Eldrin for the script. The markings on the disc, the symbols in the passages — Maela had described them, and the dream had shown them, but dreams weren't reliable and a dead queen's description wasn't a translation. She needed someone who could read the words and tell her what they meant. Not what they felt like. What they said.

The disc pulsed slow. The loft was dark. Nine people breathing.

Three of them would go underground. She was sure of that now. Three people she'd assembled in six days — a baker who sold secrets in bread, an orc who'd lost his tongue and his army, and herself, a cutpurse with a nine-silver debt and a dead queen's key sewn into her coat.

The fourth would come tomorrow. Or he wouldn't, and she'd go underground without a translator and trust Maela's voice to guide her through the script.

She slept. The dream came. Channels of stone, water moving with purpose, the map that never faded. And at the centre, the vault, waiting the way Torv waited — patient, still, holding something it couldn't give until someone arrived to take it.
