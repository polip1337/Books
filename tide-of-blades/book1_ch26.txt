CHAPTER TWENTY-SIX
The Mute at the Gate



She went back to the warehouse the next morning with food she couldn't afford.

The fish stew cost three coppers from the Dock Lane vendor — a woman who cooked in a pot over a harbour-stone fire and served into clay bowls that were returned or charged double. Zima had lifted the coppers from the Harbour Gate queue an hour earlier, working a merchant's coat pocket while he argued with the man behind him about whether the garrison was rationing grain or hoarding it. Three coppers. The coat had held five, but she took three because three was enough and five would be noticed.

The stew was thin — more broth than fish, with grey potato pieces that had been cooked soft enough to fall apart on the spoon. But it was hot, and it smelled like food, and the half-loaf from Lirra's that she'd asked for on her way through Mercer Lane was fresh. Lirra had given it without comment and without charge. She'd looked at the two bowls of stew and the direction Zima was walking and said nothing, which was its own kind of observation.

The warehouse was quiet when she arrived. The collapsed roof let in morning light, sharper and more angular than the afternoon light had been. She stepped through the gap in the wall and stopped.

He was in the same position. Against the far wall. Knees drawn up. Back straight. The clay jug beside him. The bedroll, rolled. Nothing else had changed.

Except he was watching the gap before she came through it. He'd heard her approach — from how far she didn't know, but the amber eyes were aimed at the entrance when she stepped in, which meant he'd been tracking her from the street. Maybe from the quay. Orc hearing was supposed to be better than human hearing. She didn't know by how much.

She held up the bowls. One in each hand, the bread balanced on top of the left one.

"I said I'd come back."

He looked at the food. Then at her. Then at the food again. His expression didn't change, but something shifted in the set of his shoulders — a fraction of the tension releasing, the way a drawn-back fist relaxes when the decision not to strike is made.

She walked to the midpoint of the warehouse and set both bowls on the floor. The bread between them. She straightened and stepped back three paces.

He stood.

Standing, he was taller than she'd estimated. Seven and a half feet, with the broad-chested, long-armed proportions of the orc soldiers she'd watched from the harbour wall. His grey-green skin was darker in the morning light, mottled at the joints — elbows, knuckles, the sides of his neck — with a deeper shade that was almost black. Scars crossed his forearms and the left side of his shaved scalp. One scar ran from his temple to behind his ear, old and white, the kind that came from a blade.

He walked to the bowls. The movement was careful — not the careful of weakness but the careful of a large body in a space full of debris. He stepped over the broken timber and the masonry fragments the way a horse steps over rough ground, placing each bare foot with precision. He lowered himself to the floor beside the food, folding at the knees and hips with a control that shouldn't have been possible for someone his size, and sat cross-legged.

He picked up the bread first. Held it in both hands. Looked at it.

Then he ate. Not fast — not the frantic eating of someone starving. Slow. Methodical. Breaking the bread into pieces and eating each piece separately, chewing for a long time before swallowing. The stew he drank from the bowl, tilting it to his mouth with both hands. The potato pieces he scooped with his fingers.

Zima sat on a timber beam eight feet away and ate her own stew and watched.

He finished the bread and the stew and set the empty bowl down. He looked at her.

"My name is Zima," she said. "I'm going to tell you what I need, and you can decide."

He waited.

"I'm going underground. There are passages beneath the city — old ones, sealed for a long time. I have a way in. I have intelligence on the patrol schedules. I don't have someone who can do heavy lifting in the dark. Open iron grates, move stone, carry equipment."

She paused. His eyes hadn't moved from her face.

"It's dangerous. The passages haven't been opened in two hundred years. The city above them is under siege. The man who runs the gang I owe money to is looking for the same thing I'm looking for, and if he finds out I'm the one who has it, the debt will be the least of my problems."

The amber eyes held steady.

"I'm not offering charity. I'm offering work. A share of whatever we find. If we find nothing, you get nothing. If we find something, you get a cut."

He was quiet for a long time. The warehouse creaked — wind through the broken timbers, the old frame settling. Dust moved in the light columns.

Then he pointed at her. One finger, direct. He pointed at himself. Then he brought his two fists together, knuckles touching. The gesture was precise and deliberate, a soldier's sign language stripped to its core meaning.

Together.

"Partnership," Zima said.

He didn't nod. He held the fists together for a count of three, then lowered them. The gesture had the weight of a contract.

"I need to know your name."

He looked at the floor between them. Dusty stone, scattered with mortar fragments and the grey powder of decomposing wood. He extended one finger — the index finger of his right hand, thick as a broomstick — and drew in the dust.

T.

Then O.

R.

V.

The letters were large and square, written with the careful pressure of someone who had learned to write with a different alphabet and adapted to this one. She studied the shapes. The T was crossed with a slight upward angle. The V was wider than a human hand would make it.

"Torv."

He looked up. The amber eyes caught the light and gave it back, and for the first time she saw something in them that wasn't calculation or patience. Recognition. The recognition of someone hearing their own name from another person's mouth after a long time without it.

"Can you fight?" she asked.

He stood. The motion was fluid — knees to standing in a single movement, no hands on the ground, no hitch of effort. He walked to the wall where a section of iron pipe lay among the debris. Four feet long, two inches thick, the remains of a drainage system or a loading mechanism. He picked it up with his right hand.

He held it level, arm extended, for a count of ten. The pipe didn't waver. His arm didn't shake. The muscles in his forearm were visible under the grey-green skin, corded and distinct, working in sequence the way ropes work in a pulley system.

He set the pipe down gently, leaning it against the wall. He sat down again.

"I'll take that as yes," Zima said.

*          *          *

She told him what she knew. Not all of it — not the voice, not the dead queen — but the shape. Passages beneath the city. A vault at their centre. Access through the dock-district drain. A crew of three so far: herself, an intelligence broker, and him. A fourth person still needed — someone who could read old scripts.

He listened with his whole body. When she described the drain cover he made a lifting motion with both hands — how heavy? She spread her arms — wide. He shrugged. Wide wasn't a problem.

When she described the patrol timing — seven and a half minutes — he held up both hands, fingers spread. Ten. She frowned. He pointed at her, then made a running motion with two fingers. Fast. Then pointed at himself and walked two fingers slowly across his thigh. Not fast. His size meant he couldn't move quickly through a narrow alley. The seven and a half minutes would shrink for him.

She hadn't considered this. The patrol gap was calculated for a person of normal size moving at normal speed. Torv was neither. She'd need to add time — stagger their entry, send him first, give him the extra minutes.

"You go first," she said. "I'll follow. The others come last."

He nodded. Then he made a new gesture — hand flat, fingers together, moving in a sweeping arc from left to right. She didn't understand it. He repeated it. She shook her head.

He stood, walked to the wall, and drew in the dust again. Not letters this time. A map. A rough square for the warehouse. A line for the quay. A circle for the alley. And an arrow — curving from the warehouse to the alley along a route that didn't use the quay or the main street.

A back way. He knew a route she didn't.

She studied the map. The arrow went through what she estimated was the gap between the warehouses on the second dock row — a service passage, maybe, or a drainage channel that ran behind the buildings. She hadn't explored it. She didn't know it was there.

"Show me," she said.

He stood and walked to the gap in the warehouse wall. She followed. Outside, the morning light was full and the quay was still empty — they were in the middle of the dead period, and the world was cooperating. He turned left along the warehouse's exterior wall, past the rubble of the collapsed roof section, to a gap between the warehouse and its neighbour that was so narrow she'd have assumed it was a wall joint.

He turned sideways and edged through. She followed. The gap opened after four feet into a service passage — cobbled, overgrown with something dry and brown that crumbled under their feet, running the length of the warehouse row behind the buildings. The passage was six feet wide and roofed by the overhanging upper stories of the warehouses, creating a covered walkway that was invisible from the quay and the street.

He walked. She followed. The passage ran two hundred paces east, past the back walls of four warehouses, and emerged through a low arch into the alley behind the Harbourmaster's office.

The drain cover was thirty paces to her left.

She stared at it. Then she looked at Torv. He'd found a route from the warehouse to the drain cover that avoided every sight line she'd mapped, every patrol approach she'd timed. The route was invisible because nobody used the service passages anymore, because the warehouses were empty, because the dock district was dead ground.

"How long have you been here?" she asked.

He held up three fingers. Then closed his fist and opened it again. Three fists. Fifteen days. He'd been living in the warehouse for fifteen days. He'd spent fifteen days walking the dock district alone, mapping it the way a soldier maps terrain — exits, approaches, cover, concealment.

"You've been watching the patrols."

He nodded.

"You know the gap."

He held up both hands. Ten fingers.

"Ten minutes?"

He made a flat hand and tilted it side to side. Approximately.

Ten minutes from the service passage to the drain cover, working, and back. That was two and a half minutes more than her estimate from the harbour wall. Two and a half minutes she wouldn't have known she had.

"Torv."

He looked at her.

"Two down. One to go."

He tilted his head. She held up three fingers, then folded one down. Then another.

"Three people in the crew besides me. You're the second. I still need a scholar."

He nodded. He walked back toward the warehouse, moving through the service passage with the quiet precision of someone who owned the ground and didn't need to prove it. At the warehouse gap he stopped and turned back.

He pointed at her. Then at the warehouse. Then at the ground.

Here. Come here. When you need me.

"I will," she said.

She left through the service passage. The quay was still empty. The dock district was still dead. The morning was half gone and she had a patrol map to collect from Lirra and a scholar to find and a debt that was six weeks overdue and a disc against her ribs that pulsed with the slow, warm rhythm of something that had been waiting two hundred years and could wait a little longer.

Two down. One to go.
