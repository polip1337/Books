CHAPTER TWENTY-FOUR
The Harbour Gate Access



The first of Lirra's two days.

Zima spent it at the dock district, doing her own reconnaissance while Lirra did hers. They'd agreed to work separately — two observers in the same territory drew attention, and attention was the thing they could least afford. Lirra would take the fish stall at the alley's eastern mouth. Zima would take the harbour wall above.

The wall was good ground. It ran the length of the dock district at a height of twelve feet, wide enough to walk two abreast, with crenellations that had been built for archers and were now used by seagulls and the occasional rough-sleeper who preferred wind to company. From the wall she could see the alley, the drain cover, and three of the four approaches that a patrol might use.

She settled into a gap between two crenellations where the stone had crumbled enough to create a seat. The fog was thin today — not the thick blanket that swallowed the harbour, but a gauze that softened edges and muffled sound. Through it the blockade ships were grey shapes riding low on the water, and the hills behind the city were a darker grey behind them, and the drums went three slow, seven fast, constant as a pulse.

The first Viper patrol passed at the seventh hour. Two men — one she recognised as a dock-district regular, heavy shoulders, shaved head, short sword at his hip; the other was younger, nervous, carrying a cudgel the way someone carries an umbrella they hope not to open. They walked the alley's length without stopping, turned east at the street, and disappeared into the dock-lane traffic.

Zima marked it. Charcoal on the inside of her forearm, where the sleeve would cover it. 7:0. The zero was for the minute count — she didn't have a timepiece, so she counted in her head and estimated. Two hundred breaths between the seventh-hour bell and the patrol's appearance. Close enough.

She'd learned to count time from Garrick's training, years ago. Not clocks — Garrick didn't trust clocks. He trusted rhythm. The bells at the garrison tower rang on the hour, and between the bells you counted. A breath was roughly four seconds. Fifteen breaths to a minute. Sixty minutes between bells. If you lost count, you waited for the next bell and started over. Zima rarely lost count.

The second patrol came at the ninth hour. Different pair — a woman she didn't know and a man with a limp who walked the alley slowly, checking doorways. The limping man stopped at the drain cover, looked down at it for a count of three, and moved on. He hadn't been checking it specifically. He checked every piece of ground the same way — a soldier's habit, or a man whose injury had taught him to watch where he stepped.

9:0. Zima marked it.

Two hours between patrols. Four-hour rotation would mean the next pair was the same as the first, which would confirm the pattern or break it.

She waited. The fog thickened. The harbour traffic picked up — a fishing boat coming in from the night run, riding low with whatever it had caught or smuggled, its crew working the oars with the hunched urgency of people who wanted to be tied up and invisible before the blockade ships noticed. Two garrison soldiers on the sea wall watched the boat come in and didn't move. The boat reached the dock. Ropes thrown, made fast. The crew disappeared into the warehouses with their cargo and the soldiers went back to watching the harbour.

The third patrol came at the eleventh hour. Same pair as the first. Heavy shoulders and the nervous boy with the cudgel. Same route, same pace, same turn east at the street.

11:0. Two-hour intervals. Four-hour rotation cycle.

*          *          *

By midafternoon she had the skeleton of the schedule. Patrols every two hours, starting at the seventh hour. Two-man teams. Two teams in the rotation — the experienced pair (heavy shoulders, nervous boy) and the checking pair (the woman, the limping man). They alternated. The shift change happened at the fifteenth hour, when both pairs overlapped briefly at the eastern end of the dock lane.

The gap was at the shift change. Lirra had predicted it and Zima could see it — the outgoing pair walking east, the incoming pair walking west, and for the space between them a window where the alley was unobserved. The question was how long the window lasted.

She counted it. The outgoing pair cleared the alley's eastern mouth. The incoming pair appeared at the western approach. Between those two moments: one hundred and twelve breaths.

Seven and a half minutes. Give or take thirty seconds for the margin of error in her counting and the variation in patrol pace.

Seven and a half minutes to reach the drain cover, lift it, descend, and close it above her. The lifting would be the problem. Cast iron, three feet across, in a recessed groove. She'd need both hands, which meant the rope and any equipment would have to be pre-staged or carried on her body.

She marked the time on her forearm. 15:0 / 112b. One hundred and twelve breaths. She'd want Lirra's count from the fish stall to confirm. Two perspectives on the same gap would give her the real number.

The afternoon stretched. The fog came and went. She ate the last quarter of yesterday's bread and thought about rope.

*          *          *

The dockyard cranes were visible from the harbour wall. Four of them stood along the loading quay, their arms extended over the empty water like the limbs of dead trees. The largest was at the western end — a twenty-foot timber frame with an iron winch and cable that ran from the arm to the base through a system of pulleys. The cable was what she wanted. Hemp core wrapped in tarred canvas, thick enough to hold a man's weight, long enough to reach from the crane arm to the quayside.

The problem was access. The loading quay was Hook territory — not formally, not the way the market stalls were divided, but by usage and proximity. The Hook boarding house on Dock Street sat three hundred paces from the nearest crane, and the boys who ran errands for Farris used the quay as a gathering spot. In the mornings and evenings, the quay was populated. Anyone cutting cable would be seen, questioned, possibly beaten.

But the afternoons were empty.

She'd noticed this weeks ago, during a period when she'd been working the dock district for dropped cargo and unattended loads. Between the twelfth and fifteenth hours the docks entered a dead period — the morning boats were unloaded, the evening boats hadn't arrived, the Hook boys were running errands or sleeping, and the garrison patrols stayed on the sea wall where they could watch the blockade without walking. The loading quay emptied.

Three hours. More than enough to climb a crane, cut cable, and get out. She'd need a knife sharp enough for tarred hemp — the paring knife she carried was adequate for pockets and stitching but not for heavy rope. The fishmonger on Salt Lane sold filleting knives for two coppers. She didn't have two coppers.

She could steal one. Filleting knives hung on hooks at the stall's back, in a leather roll that the fishmonger's boy kept while the fishmonger served customers. The boy was slow and the hooks were poorly tied. She could have a knife in her pocket in under four seconds.

The calculation was automatic — risk, reward, timing, route. Garrick's training, lodged so deep it ran without conscious direction. She'd been nine years old when he'd taught her to assess a theft in three breaths: the mark, the object, the exit. Everything else was decoration.

Tomorrow. The fishmonger in the morning, the dockyard in the afternoon. Cut sixty feet of cable — more than she needed, because you always cut more than you needed. Coil it, hide it in the warehouse district, retrieve it on the night of the descent.

The plan was taking shape the way a map takes shape — one landmark at a time, each one fixing the position of the next. Lirra for intelligence. Rope from the cranes. The drain cover at the shift change. The passage beneath.

And after the passage, the vault. And after the vault — she didn't know. Maela had said the vault held everything needed to leave a city by sea. Zima didn't know what that meant in practice. Supplies. Navigation instruments. Materials. The words were categories, not quantities.

She didn't know what two hundred years did to supplies.

*          *          *

She climbed down from the harbour wall as the fifteenth-hour bell rang. The shift-change gap had confirmed — both patrol pairs visible at the eastern end, the alley empty for one hundred and twelve breaths. Tomorrow she'd check again. Lirra would check from the fish stall. Two counts, averaged, would give them the operational number.

The evening was coming in — fog thickening, light dropping, the harbour taking on the flat grey colour that meant night was an hour away. The drums in the hills changed rhythm for the first time in weeks. Not the pattern — still three slow, seven fast — but the tempo. Faster. As though the hands on the drums were agitated, or excited, or responding to something Zima couldn't see.

She filed it. Filed the new pennant on the blockade ship, the faster drums, the garrison soldiers who watched the fishing boat come in and did nothing. Small changes. The city was full of small changes, and most of them meant nothing, and the ones that meant something were indistinguishable from the ones that didn't until it was too late.

The tanner's loft. Three coppers. The pallet. The dark.

She lay on her back and counted the ceiling beams — seven, the same seven, the same dark wood with the same chemical stain — and listened to nine other people breathe. The woman with children was coughing now. A small, tight cough that she was trying to suppress. The old dockhand ground his teeth. The new man with the lung sickness was quiet tonight, which was either better or worse.

The disc pulsed slow against her ribs. Warm. Patient.

One hundred and twelve breaths. She counted them in the dark, matching the disc's rhythm, and the number held steady. Seven and a half minutes. Time enough to open a door that had been closed for two centuries, if everything went right.

If.

She slept. The dream of channels came — stone corridors, water moving with direction and purpose, four branches converging on a point she could feel like a hand on her chest. When she woke the map was still there, printed on the inside of her eyelids, and the disc was warm, and the loft was dark, and the drums in the hills went three slow, seven fast, faster than yesterday, faster than she remembered.
