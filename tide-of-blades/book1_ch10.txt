CHAPTER TEN
Warm Metal



She didn't go straight to the alcove. That was a rule she'd made for herself three months ago, one of the small operational disciplines that kept her alive: never walk a direct line to the place you sleep. The people who got caught in Ashport were the people who developed patterns — the same route at the same time, the same shortcuts, the same corners turned in the same order until someone watching could predict where you'd be at any given moment with the accuracy of a tide table. Garrick had taught her that. He'd taught her a lot of things she used against him, which was either irony or education, and she'd stopped caring which.

She went south on Harbour Lane to the quayside, then west along Dock Row past the empty cranes and the warehouses with their open mouths, then doubled back north through the alley behind the chandlery where the fog was thickest and the ground was slick with tallow runoff and nobody walked who didn't have a specific reason. She crossed Coppergate by the upper bridge — not her bridge, the merchants' crossing upstream — and went east for two streets before cutting south through the abandoned tannery grounds where the chemical vats stood half-full of things that smelled like the end of the world. The route added thirty minutes to a ten-minute walk. Thirty minutes was a reasonable price for the assurance that nobody had followed her from the market.

By the time she reached her alcove under the Coppergate bridge, the fog had thickened to the point where the harbour below was invisible. She could hear it — the slow slap of low tide against the pilings, rhythmic, mindless, the sound the water made whether anyone was listening or not. She could smell it — salt and rot and the mineral tang that Ashport's harbour produced with the persistence of a fact. The fog filled the world up to the bridge and above the bridge was nothing she could see.

She ducked under the bridge, wedged herself into the alcove where the drainpipe met the foundation wall, and sat with her back against the damp stone.

She sat for a while. Just sat. Not counting, not planning, not running the calculations that usually started the moment she reached the alcove — the day's take, the running total, the distance between what she had and what she owed. She sat and listened to the harbour and let the afternoon settle the way fog settled, slowly, everywhere, until it was part of the air.

Then she took the disc out of her pocket.

*          *          *

It sat on her palm. She looked at it for a long time before she looked at it properly, and the difference mattered. Looking at something was passive — your eyes landed on an object and reported its existence. Looking at something properly was work. You measured. You compared. You noticed the things that were wrong, because the wrong things were the things that told you what you were actually holding.

The first wrong thing was the weight.

Heavy — much heavier than its size accounted for, as if its weight belonged to something larger and had been compressed into this shape. She'd held coins her whole working life. She knew what metal weighed at every denomination — the thin coppers, the thicker silvers, the old trade tokens from before the siege that carried their weight in their edges. The disc was heavier than all of them. It sat on her palm with the settled, definite authority of something dense, something packed, as if the metal it was made from had been squeezed from a larger form into a smaller one and the original mass had come along for the compression. She shifted it from hand to hand. It was too heavy for its size the way certain stones were too heavy — the dark ones from the harbour bed that the fishermen used as sinkers, the ones that felt like they'd borrowed their weight from somewhere deeper.

The second wrong thing was the warmth.

It was constant now. About the temperature of skin, which was an unpleasant comparison and she tried not to think about it and failed. She'd taken the disc from a dead man's hand. The dead man had been cooling on the cobblestones, his body temperature dropping the way all bodies dropped once the mechanism that kept them warm stopped working. The disc should have cooled with him. Or it should have cooled in her pocket, losing the residual heat of his palm over twenty minutes of walking through the fog. It had done neither. The warmth was the same as it had been in the market — steady, even, the temperature of living skin. She pressed it between both palms and held it there and counted to sixty, slowly, and when she opened her hands the disc was exactly as warm as when she'd closed them. Her palms were warmer.

She put it down on the stone beside her.

The stone was cold. Damp, river-stone cold, the chill of masonry that hadn't seen sun in months. She placed her hand flat on the rock next to the disc and waited. After thirty seconds, the stone beneath her palm was the same temperature it had been. The stone beneath the disc was warming. She could feel the change when she moved her hand — a circle of faint heat spreading outward from the disc into the rock, as if the disc were a small fire and the stone were slowly catching.

Objects did not do this. Objects cooled. That was what objects did when they left the body of their owner — they cooled, the way rooms cool when you leave them, the way beds cool, the way food cools, the way everything that had been near a living thing went cold once the living thing was gone. She understood heat. Heat was practical. Heat came from fires and bodies and the sun and the friction of work, and when the source stopped, the heat stopped, and what was left cooled to the temperature of its surroundings and stayed there. That was the rule. The disc was breaking the rule, quietly, steadily, sitting on the cold stone under the Coppergate bridge and radiating heat into its surroundings the way a body does.

She picked it up again and turned it to the light.

*          *          *

The markings were on one face only. Fine lines cut into the dark metal with a tool sharper and steadier than anything she'd seen in Ashport's workshops — sharper than the engraver's tools at the market, steadier than the smith's stamps at the dockyard forge. Each line was the width of a hair, or less, and cut to a consistent depth that she could feel with her fingernail but barely see. The precision was inhuman, or at least inhuman by the standards of the craftsmen she knew. This had been made by someone, or something, that did not make mistakes.

Lines and curves and small symbols at four points around the edge that might have been letters in a script she didn't recognise. The lines ran from the disc's centre toward the rim in four main paths — roughly equal in their spacing, like the spokes of a wheel or the roads from a crossroads. Between the spokes, shorter lines branched and curved and met each other in patterns she couldn't parse. Some of the intersections had tiny marks beside them — dots, or dashes, or something too small to read in the alcove's thin light. The symbols at the four edge-points were different from the lines. More complex. Clustered, with the density of written language rather than geometry.

She held it up to what light came in from the street above. In the grey, fog-filtered dusk, the markings took on depth. Shadow settled into the incisions and made the pattern more visible, and she could see something she hadn't seen on her palm — the lines had a quality of territory. Routes. Junctions. The purposeful geometry of a layout that described a space rather than a decoration. They looked like they could be a map.

Maps needed a key. A legend. A system for converting marks on a surface into information about the world. Whatever key this thing used, she didn't have it. She was looking at it the way she'd look at writing in a language she'd never encountered — aware that meaning was present, unable to reach it. The meaning was there, in the lines and the symbols and the four spoke-roads and the branching paths between them, but it was locked behind a door she didn't have the tools to open.

She turned it over. The other face was smooth. Completely featureless, polished to a dull sheen, uniform from edge to edge. No markings, no scratches, no trace of the inscription that covered the front. The kind of blankness that felt deliberate. Not unfinished — finished differently. As if this face had been left empty on purpose, a surface reserved for something that hadn't been applied, or a surface that had been wiped clean so thoroughly that even the memory of what had been there was gone.

She ran her thumb across it. Smooth. The warmth was the same on both sides, radiating evenly through the metal, but there was nothing to see on the back. Just the dark surface, the dull sheen, the blankness.

She turned it to the front again, set it on her knee, and studied it the way she studied crowds before working them: section by section, looking for the detail that didn't fit the pattern. The thing that was different.

She didn't find it. The markings were consistent, precise, systematic, and entirely opaque. Whatever this disc was — a map, a tool, a token, a key — it wasn't going to explain itself to a pickpocket sitting under a bridge in the fog.

She put it on the stone beside her and looked at it. It sat there, dark and warm, radiating heat into the rock.

*          *          *

She thought about what she'd seen.

A Viper courier — she was fairly sure that's what he'd been, a runner carrying something between points in Garrick's network. The charcoal vest. The cropped hair. The youth. Vipers used young runners because young runners were fast and didn't ask questions and cost less than the older operatives, and the jobs they ran were usually straightforward: carry a message from here to there, deliver a package, pick up a payment. Simple errands for simple people, in Garrick's estimation. He valued his couriers the way he valued his coins — by their function, not their faces.

This courier had been killed. Not caught in the general violence of the market fight — targeted. The wounds said so. Two stab wounds, stomach and thigh, both placed with intention by someone who knew where to put a blade and had been close enough to put it there. Whoever had done it had been waiting for him, or had found him in the crowd and cut him off from his escort, or had used the fight itself as cover — start a brawl at the market's western end, use the chaos to get a knife into a courier who was carrying something worth killing for. The fight near the Fishmonger's Arch might not have been a territorial clash at all. It might have been an operation.

Which meant the Hooks had known. Known about the courier, known about the disc, known the route and the timing well enough to stage an attack that put the right people in the right place with the right blades at the right moment. Intelligence like that didn't come from casual observation. Someone had told the Hooks what the Vipers were moving and when.

And the Vipers had been moving it. The disc was in a Viper courier's hand, which meant Garrick had it — had it first, had been transporting it somewhere through his network. A thing that Garrick possessed and the Hooks wanted badly enough to organise a market fight to take.

Both gangs knew about it. Both gangs thought it was worth blood.

And now she had it. A nineteen-year-old pickpocket who owed nine silvers and slept under a bridge was sitting in the fog holding something that the two most dangerous organisations in Ashport considered worth killing for. She didn't know what it was. She didn't know what it did. She knew it was warm and heavy and covered in marks she couldn't read, and she knew a man had died to pass it to her, and she knew that every minute she held it was a minute the danger compounded.

She sat with that for a while.

The harbour lapped at the pilings. The fog pressed in. From the hills, faintly, the drums started — three heavy beats, slow and deliberate, each one sitting in the damp air for a moment after it should have faded. Then the silence, long enough to make her think they'd stopped. Then seven fast beats rolling together like stones tipped down a slope, and then nothing. She listened to them the way she'd listened every evening for eleven months, with the familiarity of someone who'd stopped flinching at the sound and started using it to mark the passage of time. The orcs were still there. The siege was still there. The world outside the bridge was the same world it had been this morning, except for the warm disc on the stone beside her and the dead man in the market and the blood drying on the cobblestones between two stalls.

*          *          *

She thought about the word.

The sound the courier had made with the last of himself. The deliberate, shaped syllable — the rise and the drop, the emphasis on the second part, the tonal structure of a language she had no access to. He'd used the last second of his life to grip a stranger's wrist and speak. That was a choice. A specific, final choice, made by a person who knew his time was counted in heartbeats and had decided how to spend them. You didn't waste your last moment on nothing. You didn't grab a stranger and speak if the speaking was empty.

So the word meant something. And it had been meant for someone — for her, or for whoever was there in the gap between the stalls when his clock ran out. She'd been an accident. A pickpocket moving through a fight, crouching low, stepping over things on the ground. He hadn't known her. He'd seen her face and grabbed her wrist and said a word and died, and none of it was personal. She was a delivery point. A drop. The last available hand in a chain of hands that had been passing the disc through Garrick's network until the chain broke and the disc needed somewhere to go.

Unless the word wasn't for her.

Unless it was for the disc.

She turned that idea over. A dying man, holding an object, speaking a word — was he talking to the object? Activating it? Completing some instruction that the disc required before it could be passed on? The idea was strange and she didn't like it because it implied things about the disc that she had no framework for. Things that needed to be spoken to. Things that listened. Things that responded to words with warmth.

She picked up the disc. It sat on her palm, warm, heavy, patient. Offering nothing.

She thought about her options.

Fence it. Dav at the tannery market handled strange goods and asked few questions — that was his business model, the deliberate incuriosity of a man who profited from other people's need to sell things quickly. He'd look at the disc, turn it over, make an offer. And the offer would be low, because Dav was a fence and fences paid a fraction of value and the fraction was smaller when the seller couldn't name what they were selling. She'd get coppers for something that might be worth silvers. She'd lose because Dav would know what she didn't, or he'd find someone who did, and the difference between what she was paid and what the disc was actually worth would be the distance between a fair trade and a robbery, and she'd been on the wrong end of that distance her whole life.

Sell it directly. Not through a fence — find the buyer. The Vipers had been moving it, which meant the Vipers wanted it. She could go to Garrick. Offer a trade: the disc for the debt. Nine silvers, cleared, in exchange for whatever this thing was. The mathematics were simple and the conclusion was obvious: Garrick's response to discovering that she'd taken something from one of his dead couriers would not be a negotiation. It would be the kind of conversation that happened in quiet places after dark, the kind he preferred, and at the end of that conversation she would not be holding the disc or the coins or anything else.

Throw it in the harbour. Walk to the bridge's edge. Hold her hand out over the water. Open her fingers. Twenty feet of air and then the surface and then the dark water closing over it and then done. No disc, no warmth, no word in her ear, no dead man's grip on her wrist. Clean. The safest option by every measure she had.

She held the disc over the stone. Not over the water — she hadn't stood up, hadn't gone to the edge. But she held it out, away from her body, in the open air, and she thought about letting go. The fog. The harbour below. The cold water that would take it and keep it and never tell anyone where it was.

The disc pulsed.

One beat. A single, distinct beat of warmth that radiated from the metal into her palm and up through her wrist and into her forearm — a pulse, a throb, with the unmistakable rhythm of a heartbeat. Not mechanical. Not the tick of a device. The deep, organic surge of something that was, for one moment, alive in her hand. She felt it in her bones. She felt it in her teeth. One beat, strong and slow, with a depth that went past the surface of the disc and into whatever the disc was made of, whatever was inside it, whatever it was that made this piece of deep-water metal warm and heavy and wrong.

Then stillness. The disc sat on her palm, warm and quiet, and the beat did not repeat.

She closed her hand around it.

*          *          *

She sat under the bridge in the fog and the gathering dark and she held the disc and she thought about the dead man's hand and the word she couldn't understand and the warmth that wouldn't stop, and she thought: I should get rid of this.

And she thought: not yet.

Not because she had a plan. Not because she understood what the disc was or what it meant or why it was warm or why it had pulsed against her hand like something with a heart. She kept it because the word was still in her ear — the courier's last word, the shaped sound, the rise and the drop — and because the disc had pulsed when she'd held it away from her body, and because the dead man had looked at her with clear eyes and chosen her, or chosen the moment, or chosen whatever it was that dying people chose when they spent the last of themselves on a stranger in a gap between two stalls.

She kept it because she didn't know what it was, and you didn't throw away a thing you didn't understand. That was the rule. Garrick had taught her that, though he'd meant it about debts and grudges, not about warm metal discs taken from dead men's hands. The principle held. You kept what you didn't understand until understanding came, because the thing you threw away today might be the thing you needed tomorrow, and in Ashport tomorrow was the horizon and the horizon was as far as anyone planned.

The disc sat in her hand, warm and wrong and patient, and the drums from the hills rolled their seven beats and fell silent, and somewhere behind her in the market the fight was dying down or it wasn't, and somewhere in the gap between two stalls a man she'd never met lay dead with an empty palm, and the fog filled the alcove and the alcove held her and the bridge above was solid and dark.

She put the disc in her pocket. She leaned back against the cold stone. One day left on Stel's extension. Nine silvers she didn't have. A warm disc she couldn't sell and couldn't explain and couldn't quite bring herself to throw away.

Ashport settled into another night of fog and hunger and the particular, exhausting business of not quite dying. The harbour lapped. The drums were done. The fog thickened, and the dark came on, and Zima sat under the Coppergate bridge with something in her pocket that she didn't understand, and she held the disc against her thigh and waited for morning to come and make the question easier, which it wouldn't.

She told herself that most nights. The lie was useful. The lie got you to morning, and in Ashport a lie that got you to morning was the closest thing to truth that mattered.

She slept. The disc was warm against her thigh. The dead man's word sat in her ear. And the night went on, and the fog went on, and the city went on the way it always did — slowly, grimly, one hour at a time — and nothing was resolved and nothing was safe and the disc was warm, and she slept.
