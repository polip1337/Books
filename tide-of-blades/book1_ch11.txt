CHAPTER ELEVEN
Garrick's Eyes



Morning in Ashport smelled like old fires and new trouble.

She woke with the disc in her pocket and the taste of fog in her mouth and a stiffness in her neck that said she'd slept wrong — curled too tight against the cold stone, her body instinctively shielding the pocket where the disc sat, warm and strange and still present. For a moment between sleep and waking she thought the warmth was a fever. Then her hand found the disc through the coat fabric and she felt the steady, sourceless heat and remembered what she was carrying and what it had cost the man she'd taken it from.

She sat up. Flexed her hands. Counted what she had.

The disc. The five coppers from the coin purse she'd taken during the fight. The satchel's leather pouch, which she'd crammed into her coat last night without counting because counting required light and stillness and she'd had neither. She took it out now and loosened the drawstring and poured the contents into her palm. Silver. Proper silver — not half-silvers, not clipped tokens, but minted coins with the harbour seal still legible on their faces. She counted them twice. Five silvers and six coppers.

Five silvers. In one pouch, kicked under a stall by a passing boot during a gang fight. Somebody had been carrying a decent sum before the market came apart. Merchant money, probably — the stalls near the fight's centre were run by people who dealt in goods that cost more than coppers.

She held the coins and did the arithmetic. Five silvers from the satchel, plus the five coppers from the purse, plus the eight coppers and two bent silvers she'd been carrying from the days before the fight. Total: seven silvers (two bent, which meant they'd pass at three-quarters value — call it six and a half), nineteen coppers. Against a nine-silver debt to Garrick.

Short. Better than she'd been a week ago — substantially better, the satchel had been a gift from the chaos — but still short. And the problem wasn't only the amount. The satchel's silver had been kicked loose during a gang fight. The Vipers would be inventorying their losses. A pouch of five silvers going missing from the fight zone would be noted. If she showed up at Garrick's table with that much silver the day after his people had been hit, the questions would come. Where'd you get it. That's substantial for a cutpurse. You were at the market, weren't you.

She put the coins away. Separate pockets — silvers in the belt, coppers loose, the satchel's pouch discarded in the drain. Pouches were identifiable. Coins were anonymous.

The fog was thinning. The morning arrived in the grey, reluctant way that Ashport mornings did — not sunlight, just the slow retreat of total dark, a brightening from black to grey to the colour of old dishwater that passed for dawn in a city that hadn't seen blue sky in weeks. She could see the harbour now, or the near edge of it. The water was flat. The pilings of the bridge were slick. From the hills, silence. The drums didn't come until evening.

She crawled out of the alcove, stretched the cold from her joints, and went to find out what the city knew.

*          *          *

The city was different. She felt it before she had evidence — a tightness in the air, a quality of attention in the people she passed that hadn't been there yesterday. Not panic. Ashport had weathered too many shocks for panic. Something quieter and more useful: alertness. The heightened, careful watchfulness of a population that had learned to read the signals of violence and was reading them now.

The Ashford Market was closed. She saw this from Harbour Lane — the eastern entrance was blocked by a line of garrison soldiers, four of them, standing with their backs to the stalls and their hands on their weapons with the bored hostility of men who'd been posted somewhere without being told why or for how long. Behind them, through the gaps between their shoulders, the market was a wreck. Overturned stalls. Debris on the cobblestones. The charred skeleton of at least one stall that had caught fire during the fight, the timbers blackened and still faintly smoking. The air smelled of char and the chemical sharpness of whatever the chandler's stock had contributed to the burning.

She turned south without stopping. Market closed. Garrison involved. The fight had been bad enough to threaten something the commander cared about — probably the ration distribution tables. If the violence had reached the tables, the garrison would have intervened. Not to protect people. To protect the infrastructure that kept the city from tearing itself apart faster than it was already tearing.

She bought bread from a woman selling from a basket on Dock Row. Not Lirra's bread — cheaper, harder, made with cut flour that tasted of chalk and filled the stomach without doing much else. Two coppers. She ate it walking and she listened.

Ashport's dock district had a nervous system. Information moved through it the way blood moved — in a thousand small channels, conversations between dockhands at the water point, murmured exchanges between the laundry women at the harbour shallows, the quiet gossip of people who lived close to the ground and survived by paying attention. Zima had been using this network since she was fourteen. You didn't need informants. You needed ears and patience and the ability to stand near people who were talking and look like you weren't listening.

She stood near people who were talking. She looked like she wasn't listening.

At the water point on Dock Row, two dockhands filling buckets: "— been asking since before dawn. Three of them, up near the warehouses. Vipers. Looking for something one of their couriers was carrying." "What courier?" "Pell, they said. One of the young runners. Got killed in the fight yesterday." "What was he carrying?" "Something small. They described it — dark metal, about the size of a big coin. Not a coin though. Something else."

At the harbour shallows, two of the laundry women working in the cold water: "— old Dalla said they described it twice. Small, dark, metal. Vipers want it back. Think the Hooks took it off the courier during the fight." "Dalla told them anything?" "Dalla told them nothing. Dalla's told nobody nothing since her husband died, you know that."

At the corner of Cobb Street and the tannery lane, the old men on the wall: "— found him between two stalls. Young one, runner for Garrick's lot. Pell, they called him. Stabbed twice and left. Bled out in the market while the fight was going."

Pell.

The courier's name was Pell.

She hadn't known. She'd crouched over him in the narrow gap between the stalls and watched him die and taken the disc from his hand and walked away, and she hadn't known his name, because dead people in Ashport didn't always get names. They got descriptions. A body near the arch. A stabbing in the market. Someone's courier, found between two stalls. The city processed its dead the way it processed everything else — efficiently, without sentiment, reducing people to locations and causes and the brief, flat language of a population that couldn't afford to mourn every loss or it would do nothing else.

But his name was Pell. Young. Maybe her age. Charcoal vest, cropped dark hair, narrow face gone pale. He'd grabbed her wrist and said a word she didn't understand and opened his hand and shown her a disc and died, and his name was Pell, and she was going to carry that name the way she carried the disc — close to the body, warm, uninvited, impossible to put down.

The Vipers were looking for the disc. Three men in the dock district since dawn, asking questions with the quiet courtesy that was Garrick's signature — the kind of asking that didn't raise its voice because everyone understood what happened to people who didn't co-operate. They'd described the disc: small, dark metal, like a coin but not a coin. They thought the Hooks had taken it from Pell during the fight.

Good. That meant they weren't looking for her. They were looking for a Hook, or a Hook associate, or someone connected to the other side. She was none of those things. She was a pickpocket who'd been in the wrong place at the right moment and had done what pickpockets did when something valuable appeared in an open hand.

Not yet, she thought. They're not looking for me yet.

*          *          *

She went to the tanner's loft on Cobb Street.

The loft sat above the old tannery works, abandoned since the fourth month of the siege when the last tanner exhausted his supply of hides and the chemicals in the processing vats became more valuable as trade goods than as tools. The loft was accessible by an exterior wooden staircase with three missing treads that you had to know about or you'd go through. The room above was large, drafty, and smelled permanently of the compounds that had soaked into the floorboards over decades — a sharp, organic reek that kept casual visitors away and that the regulars had stopped noticing the way you stop noticing the harbour smell after a few months.

Aud was there, sorting rags by size and condition with the methodical patience of a woman who'd found the one job that would never run out of work. She looked up when Zima came in, registered her face, went back to the rags. Aud's economics were simple: a copper bought a night, questions cost extra, and she never charged for questions because she never asked them.

The loft was empty except for Aud and a figure sleeping in the far corner, coat over the face, sex and age indeterminate. Zima took a spot near the wall where a crack in the boards admitted a finger of grey light, and she sat and took off her coat.

The disc was in her pocket. She needed it not to be.

A pickpocket's coat should be light. Heavy pockets swung, and swing drew eyes, and in Ashport eyes led to hands and hands led to the kind of attention that ended with someone going through your belongings in a quiet place. She'd been carrying the disc loose in her trouser pocket since the market. A hard, round object the size of a large coin, warm to the touch, detectable by anyone who patted her down with more than casual interest. Garrick's men searched people — not formally, not with announcement, but in the way they did everything: casually, as if the hand on your shoulder and the quick check of your pockets were just part of how friends greeted each other.

She needed to hide it.

She went to Aud's supply box near the door. Needles, thread, patches, the basic repair kit that the loft's residents used to keep their clothing in working condition. She took a needle — dull, heavy, meant for canvas — and a length of strong thread and a scrap of canvas from the rag pile. Aud watched her take them and said nothing, because nothing was what Aud said about everything.

She turned the coat inside out and laid it flat on the boards. The lining was thin fabric, worn, patched twice, the interior of a garment that had been repaired past the point where repair was kindness. She found the spot: below her left ribs, where the coat hung close to the body and the fabric doubled at the side seam. She cut a slit in the lining with her belt-knife. She placed the disc inside — flat against the seam, snug, the metal settling into the fold of fabric with the ease of a thing finding its place. She covered it with the canvas scrap and stitched the scrap down on all four sides, closing the slit, sealing the disc into a flat pocket between the lining and the outer cloth.

The work was rough. She was a pickpocket, not a tailor, and the needle was dull and the canvas was stiff and the thread caught on the lining's frayed edges with every pass. She stitched slowly, pulling each loop tight, feeling the thread bite into the canvas. The loft was quiet around her. Aud sorted rags. The sleeping figure in the corner didn't move. Through the crack in the boards, the grey light of morning held steady, neither brightening nor fading.

She finished. Turned the coat right-side out. Put it on. Stood.

The disc sat below her left ribs. She could feel it — the warmth diffused through the canvas now, spread into a low general heat along her side rather than the sharp, specific warmth of metal against skin. Like the memory of warmth. Like a hand that had been there recently and left its temperature behind. She moved. Bent sideways, reached overhead, twisted at the waist. The coat hung the same as always. The disc didn't shift, didn't swing, didn't bulge. She pressed her hand flat against the spot. Through the outer fabric she could feel a slight firmness — the canvas patch — but nothing that would register as unusual to a hand that wasn't specifically looking for it.

She left the loft with the disc against her ribs and the needle back in Aud's box and the knowledge that she was now carrying the thing the Vipers were searching for sewn into the lining of her coat, against her body, where the warmth of it and the warmth of her were indistinguishable.

*          *          *

The Harbour Gate ration queue was longer than usual. The market closure had pushed the day's commerce onto the queues — the only remaining places where Ashport's economy still functioned under something resembling structure. People who would normally buy food at the stalls were here instead, mixed in with the regulars, and the result was a line that stretched from the distribution table at the gate back along Harbour Row almost to Dock Lane. She counted heads out of habit. Eighty-three.

She took a position in the queue's middle third and started reading the crowd.

Garrick's men were at the table. Two of them, seated behind the garrison guards — Brennan on the left, heavy-set, scarred hands, the dead-eyed patience of a man who'd been doing this long enough that the work had become part of his body's rhythm. Someone younger on the right, a face she'd seen but couldn't name, marking entries on a tally sheet with a stub of charcoal. Standard protocol. Garrick logged the queues because Garrick believed in information the way other people believed in luck: persistently, superstitiously, and with the faith that accumulation would eventually produce advantage.

She watched them. Brennan was looking at the queue with his usual flat regard — the gaze of a man who'd been told to watch and was watching without investment, without focus, taking in the crowd the way a net takes in water. The younger one wrote on his sheet. Neither of them looked at her. Neither of them looked at anyone in particular.

But. She was carrying a stolen disc sewn into her coat, and Garrick's men were searching the dock district for that disc, and three of his people had been asking about it since before dawn. The question was whether the search had reached the ration tables. Whether Brennan had been given new instructions — a description, a list of people to watch for, a set of behaviours that would mark someone as worth pulling aside. Whether the normal, unfocused gaze of a man doing routine work had been narrowed by specific orders.

She couldn't tell. That was the thing about paranoia — it was indistinguishable from good judgment. The state of watching too carefully and the state of watching exactly carefully enough looked identical from the inside. She could feel the disc against her ribs, warm through the canvas, and she could feel Brennan's gaze moving across the crowd, and the two feelings together produced a specific grinding anxiety that she dealt with the way she dealt with most things: by working.

She took a purse from a dockhand who was arguing with the man in front of him about the ration cut. Five coppers, a bent token, a brass key she'd drop later. The man's attention was on his argument and his hands were in the air making points about grain distribution, and his purse was on his belt behind his right hip, unreachable while he was gesturing. She took it in the gap between one emphatic point and the next.

She took a second purse twenty minutes later, from a woman near the front of the queue who'd set her basket on the ground to adjust her headscarf. Seven coppers. Thin take, but the technique was clean and nobody looked her direction and the woman didn't check her pocket for another five minutes, by which time Zima was six places further back and facing the other way.

She passed Garrick's table. Brennan's eyes found her face — briefly, the routine sweep that he gave everyone who walked past, the quick assessment that said I see you, you're logged, move along. She met his gaze for the half-second that was normal and looked away. The younger man marked something on his sheet.

Had he marked her? Or the person behind her? Or was the mark unrelated — a tally, a count, a routine entry having nothing to do with a dark-metal disc sewn into the coat of the woman who'd just passed?

She took her ration — five ounces of grain, scooped into her bag, the reduced measure that had become the new normal since the cut — and she walked.

*          *          *

The afternoon turned grey and stayed there. She walked the dock district with her hands in her pockets and her face empty and the disc warm against her ribs, and she thought about the mathematics of her situation the way she thought about everything — in numbers, in distances, in the precise accounting of what she had against what she owed.

One day left. Stel's four-day extension expired tomorrow. Four days, he'd said, and she'd taken them because four days was better than zero and because the alternative to taking them was having the conversation about the nine silvers right then, in the Harbour Gate queue, with Garrick's quiet, polite collector standing beside her and the full weight of the ledger hanging over the exchange. She'd bought four days and she'd spent three of them and the last one was tomorrow, and when tomorrow came Stel would return, or someone would return, and they'd want the money.

Six and a half silvers, if the bent ones passed at three-quarters. Plus the coppers — twenty-six now, with today's takes. Thirty coppers to a silver. She was short. Not the vast, impossible distance between nothing and nine silvers that she'd been contemplating a week ago. A smaller distance. A distance she could almost see across, if she squinted, if she was optimistic, if optimism were something she could afford.

The disc was worth something. She was certain of that — the way she was certain about weather and crowd mood and the relative danger of different streets at different times of day. A thing that both gangs knew about, that a courier had died carrying, that Garrick's men were searching for with the quiet thoroughness of people who'd been told not to come back empty-handed — that was not a worthless object. It might be worth nine silvers. It might be worth ninety. It might be worth enough to pay the debt and buy food for a month and find a room with walls and a door that had a lock on it.

If she could sell it. If selling it were safe. If she knew what it was.

Three conditions. None met.

She kept walking. Past the warehouses on Dock Row. Past the water point where the dockhands had been talking about Pell that morning. Past the corner where she'd seen a man standing with his hands in his pockets, watching the street with the patient attention of someone who'd been told to stand there and watch. Was he Viper? Was he part of the search? Was he looking for a dark-metal disc the size of a large coin that was currently sewn into the coat lining of the woman walking past him at a measured, unremarkable pace?

She didn't know. She walked past him the way she walked past everyone — without interest, without hesitation, the gait of a person going somewhere specific who didn't have time to stop. He didn't call out. He didn't follow. He might not have been Viper at all. He might have been a dockhand waiting for a friend or a man who liked standing on corners or nobody in particular doing nothing of consequence.

Paranoia. The thing about it was that it worked in both directions. It made you see threats that weren't there, which was wasteful and exhausting, but it also made you see threats that were, which was the only reason people with paranoia survived long enough to be exhausted by it. She couldn't turn it off. She didn't want to. The disc was warm against her ribs and the Vipers were searching and one day remained on the extension and the gap between safe and caught was measured in details — a glance held too long, a pocket checked too often, a route that took her past the wrong corner at the wrong time.

She kept moving. That was what you did in Ashport. You kept moving and you watched who was watching you and you trusted nobody and you slept light and you carried your secrets in the seams of your clothing, close to the skin, where the searchers' hands would have to know exactly where to look.
