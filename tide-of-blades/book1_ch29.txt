CHAPTER TWENTY-NINE
Runes and Rust



The harbour wall east of the crane yard was empty.

That was the point. This stretch of wall had been damaged during the first month of the siege — a catapult stone had broken a twenty-foot section into a jagged slope of rubble, and the garrison had blocked it off with rope and a sign that said DANGER – NO ACCESS. The sign had faded. The rope had rotted. Nobody came here because nobody needed to, and the rubble provided a shelf of broken stone where two people could sit out of the wind and out of sight.

Eldrin was already there when she arrived. He sat on a flat stone with his knees drawn up, his coat wrapped tight, his face turned toward the harbour. The fog was thin today — she could see the blockade ships, grey shapes riding low, and beyond them the hills and the smudge of smoke from the encampment. The drums were quiet. That happened sometimes — an hour or two of silence that felt louder than the rhythm it replaced.

"You came early," Zima said.

"I didn't sleep."

She sat on the stone beside him. Not close — three feet of distance, the rubble between them. She reached into her coat lining and unpicked the stitching she'd redone that morning. The canvas scrap came away. The disc sat in her palm.

Eldrin looked at it.

His entire body changed. The hunched shoulders dropped. The jaw tightened. His grey eyes went wide and then narrow in the space of a single breath, the pupils contracting as though the disc were a bright light instead of a dark metal circle no bigger than a large coin.

He didn't reach for it. His hands stayed on his knees, the knuckles white.

"Warden's Key," he said.

The words came out flat. Not a question. A reading — he was looking at the face of the disc and translating the largest characters before his conscious mind had decided whether to participate.

"You can read it," Zima said.

"I can read the primary script. The display text. Title and designation." His voice had changed — the nervous, self-deprecating tone of yesterday was gone, replaced by something precise and focused. The scholar's voice. The one the university had heard for years before they decided they didn't want to hear it anymore. "Warden's Key. Sub-designation: Passage Authority. These are access credentials."

"For what?"

"For whatever the passages lead to." His eyes moved across the disc's face, tracking the finer lines. "The geometry isn't decorative. It's a tidal chart — a functional schematic based on water movement. The lines represent channels. The intersections represent junctions. The four marks at the compass points are access designators."

He leaned closer. His breath fogged against the disc's surface and vanished. The metal was warm enough to dissipate moisture on contact.

"Four access points," Zima said. "I know. They're under the oldest parts of the city. The docks. The Harbour Gate. The market's north edge. The old city south of Mercer Lane."

Eldrin looked up at her. "How do you know that?"

"The same way I know the disc is a key. I was told."

"By whom?"

"By the woman who made it."

His face went through the same rapid sequence she'd seen yesterday — recognition, discomfort, the darker thing at the mention of old forces. But today there was a fourth expression. Curiosity. The hunger of a scholar confronting the thing his career had been about.

"She speaks to you," he said. Not a question.

"Through the disc. Or through whatever the disc connects to. Her name is Maela."

"Queen Maela. The Founder." He looked at the disc again. "The texts reference a Warden system — a network of passage guardians appointed by the founding authority. The Wardens held keys. The keys granted access. The system was supposedly dismantled when the city's governance changed."

"Supposedly."

"Supposedly." He extended his hand — palm up, open, the scholar's request for an artefact. "May I hold it?"

Zima placed the disc in his palm. The transfer produced a reaction — the disc's warmth flared for a count of two, then settled to its baseline. Eldrin's hand flinched at the heat but didn't drop it. His fingers closed around the edge, holding it the way she'd seen him hold his pen calluses — with the careful pressure of someone who understood that what they held was both valuable and dangerous.

"Heavy," he said.

"Too heavy for its size."

"The metal isn't standard. It's not iron, bronze, copper, or any alloy I recognise." He turned the disc over, examining the smooth reverse. "The Tidal Script texts reference a material called deepsea iron — metal worked from ore found in underwater caves along the pre-Founding coastline. Nobody has confirmed its existence. Nobody has access to underwater caves that predate the city."

He held the disc at arm's length, letting the morning light fall across it. The lines on the face caught the light differently depending on the angle — at one degree the tidal chart was visible, clean and geometric; at another the secondary script appeared, smaller characters packed between the chart lines like notes in a margin.

"There's a secondary inscription," Eldrin said. "Smaller. Denser. Running between the primary lines."

"What does it say?"

He brought the disc close to his face, squinting. The grey eyes tracked the tiny characters, moving right to left, then left to right, then right to left again.

"It's an inventory." His voice had dropped. The scholar's precision was still there, but beneath it something else — an uncertainty that hadn't been present when he was reading the primary script. "A list of stored goods. Sealed, dated, with preservation notations."

He read, translating as he went, each phrase emerging halting and careful.

"Forty casks of preserved grain. Sixty jars of lamp oil. Ten crates of salt-packed spice — pepper, saffron, ginger. Navigation instruments — compass, quadrant, charts. Sailcloth, forty bolts. Rope, three hundred fathoms. Ship fittings — iron nails, copper plating, caulk. Coin — silver and gold, amount unspecified. Black powder, twenty jars. Medical supplies — bandages, sutures, distillates."

He stopped reading. His hand was shaking again — not the hunger shake from yesterday but a fine, rapid vibration that came from somewhere deeper.

"This is a departure inventory," he said. "Everything needed to equip and supply a ship for a long voyage."

"I know."

"You know." He looked at her. "She told you."

"She said the vault holds everything she set aside for the person who would need it. For leaving. Or for staying, depending on how bad things have got."

Eldrin turned the disc over again. The smooth reverse caught the light — but it wasn't entirely smooth. There were marks there too, finer than the face inscription, almost invisible unless the light hit at exactly the right angle.

"There's something on the back," he said. "I need better light. And a magnifying lens."

"Where do you get a magnifying lens in a besieged city?"

"The harbour clerk has one. Marten. He uses it for reading small-print manifests." Eldrin looked at her. "I can borrow it. I've appraised objects for the harbour office before."

"How long?"

"A day. I'll read the reverse tomorrow, same place, morning light." He held the disc out to her. The warmth had steadied — no flares, no pulses, just the constant baseline heat that she'd come to think of as the disc's resting state.

She took it back. The metal settled against her palm with the familiarity of something that had been hers for longer than the ten days since the courier's hand had opened. She sewed it back into the coat lining, sitting on the rubble in the wind, while Eldrin watched her hands work the needle.

"The stitching is rough," he said.

"The stitching is functional."

"Those aren't the same thing."

"In my world they are."

*          *          *

He talked while she stitched. Not the nervous, scattered talk of the man on Chandler's Row — this was the focused, structured talk of someone who'd been holding information alone for two years and had finally found an audience.

The Tidal Script. Pre-Founding. The oldest form of written authority in the region, predating common script by at least two centuries. The script wasn't symbolic — it was functional. The words enacted what they described. A contract in Tidal Script wasn't a promise. It was a mechanism.

"The university's position was that the functionality was mythological," Eldrin said. "That the founding-era texts exaggerated the script's properties for political purposes — to make the Founders seem more powerful, more legitimate. My position was that the texts were literal."

"Why?"

"Because I tested it." He pulled something from inside his coat — a small piece of stone, flat, the size of a playing card. On its surface: characters. Fine, angular, cut into the stone with the precision of a chisel guided by a steady hand. "This is a fragment of a boundary marker. Pre-Founding. I found it in the harbour office's miscellaneous collection."

He held it up. The characters were similar to the disc's markings — the same angular precision, the same sense of functional geometry.

"The inscription says: this stone marks the edge of the market. Not marked, present tense. Not will mark. Marks. As in: the stone is currently and perpetually performing the function of marking. I placed it on a table in my office. The table became the edge of something. Papers slid off the side nearest the inscription. A cup placed on the boundary line fell over. The stone was enforcing its own text."

Zima looked at the fragment. "That's why you were sacked."

"That's why I was sacked. A boundary stone that knocks cups off tables is an inconvenient discovery for a university that teaches Tidal Script as dead literature." He put the fragment back in his coat. "The disc in your coat is the same script, the same material, the same functional principle. But at a different scale entirely. The boundary stone marks the edge of a market stall. The disc marks the edge of something much larger."

"The vault."

"The vault, the passages, the access points. The entire system." He looked at the harbour. The fog was coming in, thickening along the water, blurring the blockade ships. "And there's something else. The secondary inscription — the inventory — it's written in active voice. The vault holds, not the vault held. Present tense. The script is asserting that those goods are there now. Currently and perpetually, the way my boundary stone asserts its boundary."

The implications of that settled into her slowly. Two hundred years. Salt-packed spice and lamp oil and rope and sailcloth and medical supplies, sealed in a vault beneath the city, described in a script that asserted their presence in perpetual present tense.

"The supplies are preserved," she said.

"If the script functions the way I believe it does — the way it demonstrably functions on my boundary stone — then yes. The vault's contents are in the condition described by the inscription. The script doesn't just record. It maintains."

She finished the stitching and tucked the needle back into her collar seam. The disc sat against her ribs, warm, steady. Present tense. The script said the vault holds, and the vault held, and the vault would hold, because the words didn't distinguish between tenses. They asserted a permanent state.

"Tomorrow," she said. "The reverse. The magnifying lens."

"Tomorrow." Eldrin stood. He was taller than he'd seemed yesterday — not physically, but in the way he carried himself. The hunched shoulders were straighter. The grey eyes had a focus they'd lacked on Chandler's Row. The scholar's voice had come back, and with it the scholar's posture.

He looked at her. "There's one more thing. The inscription on the face — the primary text. Warden's Key, Passage Authority. The word warden."

"What about it?"

"In Tidal Script, warden doesn't mean guardian. It doesn't mean keeper or protector or any of the common translations. The root word is closer to..." He searched for it. "Inheritor. The one who receives. The one who continues."

"Continues what?"

"That's what the reverse will tell us." He pulled his coat tighter. The wind off the harbour was picking up, salt and cold and the mineral smell of deep water. "I need the lens. And better light. There's at least one more line on the reverse that I couldn't read. It's smaller than the rest. Packed tighter. Like someone added it after the primary inscription was finished."

He turned and walked west along the harbour wall, back toward the city. Zima watched him go. His walk was different — still narrow, still hunched, but with a purpose in it that hadn't been there before. A man moving toward something instead of away from everything.

The disc pulsed. Once. Warm.

She sat on the rubble and looked at the harbour. The fog was coming in thick now, swallowing the ships, the water, the horizon. The drums started again — three slow, seven fast — and the city went on behind her, grey and besieged and full of people who didn't know that beneath their feet a vault sat sealed and stocked and waiting.

The disc said holds. Present tense. The vault was full.

And tomorrow, Eldrin would read the last line, and she would find out what the word warden meant in a script that didn't describe things but made them true.
