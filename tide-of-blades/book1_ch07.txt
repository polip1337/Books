CHAPTER SEVEN
The Ration Cut



The queue at the Harbour Gate was a hundred and nine people long by the time Zima got there, and it was still growing.

That was wrong. The Harbour Gate queue averaged sixty to seventy on a morning — she knew because she'd been counting it for months, the numbers a habit that lived somewhere between compulsion and survival instinct. A hundred and nine meant something had changed. Either the other ration points were closed, or the garrison had shifted the schedule again, or the news had already started moving and was pulling people toward the distribution tables the way a rumour of food pulls the hungry — not toward a destination but toward the last place the food had been seen.

She took her place at the back. A hundred and tenth. Behind her, within five minutes, fourteen more joined. The queue had the dense, compressed quality of a crowd that knew something and was pretending not to — shoulders tight, voices low, the particular hush of a sound being held behind teeth.

The cooper's widow was ahead. Sixty-third in line, roughly — Zima counted from her position and estimated, because the queue curved around the gatehouse corner and the far end was out of sight. The widow was checking her ankle. Three times in four minutes. The barometer was reading nervous, and climbing.

The fog was thin again this morning. The harbour was visible past the gatehouse wall — grey water, grey sky, the line of the breakwater, and beyond it the darker shapes of the orc warships at anchor. Five of them — from the harbour wall she could see seven in the full arc, but the gatehouse blocked the eastern end of the line. Five from here. She checked the positions against what she knew and placed the missing two behind the headland. Consistent. The blockade hadn't changed.

The queue moved. Slowly. She shifted forward with it and she listened.

The woman in front of her was talking to the man beside her in the low, clipped way people talked when they were sharing information they weren't sure they were supposed to have. Zima caught fragments. Granary. Stores. Reduction. The word temporary, spoken with the particular inflection that meant the speaker didn't believe it and didn't expect the listener to either.

She was right. The news was already moving.

*          *          *

The garrison officer arrived at the distribution table at half past the hour.

He was not one of the regular guards. He was an officer — she could tell from the coat, which was better cut than the enlisted men's and had brass buttons that still had their shine, and from the way the two regular guards at the table straightened when he appeared, the automatic deference of bodies trained to respond to rank before thought. He was carrying a sheet of paper. He unfolded it with the deliberate motions of a man performing a task he didn't enjoy and intended to complete without lingering.

He read.

By order of the garrison commander. Effective immediately. Due to lower than anticipated stores in the central granary and ongoing limitations to resupply caused by the naval blockade, the daily ration allocation per citizen is to be reduced by one-third. The reduction is temporary and will be reviewed at the end of the current supply period. Citizens are advised to supplement their rations through approved market channels where available. The garrison remains committed to the equitable distribution of all available food stocks.

He folded the paper. He did not look at the crowd. He stepped back from the table and the two regular guards took his place and the distribution resumed, and for about four seconds the queue was perfectly silent — the silence of a room after a glass breaks, before anyone decides whether to sweep it up or walk away.

Then the anger.

It started at the front, with the people who'd been close enough to hear every word and read the officer's face and understand that a short proclamation delivered quickly meant bad news the garrison wanted to be done with. A man shouted. A woman shouted over him. Several voices at once, the sound rising and merging the way separate fires merge when they're close enough — individual frustration becoming collective noise, the dense layered roar of a hundred people discovering simultaneously that the thing they'd feared had arrived.

The sound moved backward through the queue. It reached Zima fifty yards from the table, arriving not as words but as pressure — the crowd contracting, people pushing forward toward the table, toward the officer, toward the source of the information as though closing the distance could change it. Other people pushed outward, away from the compression, and for about twenty seconds the queue lost its shape entirely and became a crowd, which was a different organism with different instincts.

Zima stood where she was. She let the movement flow around her the way water flows around a post and she watched.

The anger lasted about a minute. Perhaps less. It crested the way weather crests — a peak, an intensity, and then the energy that had produced it ran out, not because the cause had gone but because the bodies producing it couldn't sustain it. You could be furious for a minute. You could not be furious forever. Not in Ashport. Not after eleven months. The anger required reserves, and the reserves had been drawn down by ten previous crises and a hundred smaller ones, and what was left after the minute was not calm — it was something flatter. Duller. The specific exhaustion of people who had been absorbing impacts for long enough that their capacity for outrage had been compressed into something too small and too dense to express.

Resignation moved through the queue like a second front. Slower than the anger, quieter. Shoulders dropped. Voices lowered. The queue re-formed — not the same shape as before, looser, with gaps where people had pushed out and not returned — but a queue again. Functional. The distribution table was open. The guards were scooping grain. The grain measure was smaller. A third less.

Everyone knew what temporary meant. Temporary was the word the garrison used when they meant permanent but hadn't worked out how to say it yet. Temporary was the word for things that started now and ended never, or ended when the siege ended, which amounted to the same thing because the siege showed no sign of ending and nobody who mattered was pretending it did.

The cooper's widow had stopped checking her ankle. She was standing still in the queue with her hands at her sides, not moving, not looking at anything Zima could identify. Past nervous. Past whatever came after nervous. Somewhere new, somewhere the barometer didn't have a reading for.

*          *          *

Zima did the mathematics while the queue inched forward.

She did this the way she did everything: systematically, without flinching, looking at the numbers rather than at what the numbers meant, because the numbers were just numbers and the meaning was where the damage lived.

One-third less food. The daily ration dropped from eight ounces of grain to roughly five. Five ounces made thin porridge for one day if you stretched it with water until the porridge was mostly water. It did not stretch to two days. Which meant the people who'd been surviving on the ration alone — the ones with no market money, no side arrangements, no connection to a gang that fed its own — would run out a day earlier than before. Which meant they'd have to find food somewhere else. Which meant the market prices would climb because the demand would increase while the supply stayed the same or dropped, and the people at the bottom — the ones already at the bottom, the ones who were below the bottom — would find out that there was further to fall than they'd thought.

The doorway people would multiply. She was certain of it with the flat certainty of someone who'd calculated enough falling to recognise the shape of it before it happened. Every ration cut produced more of them. The first cut, in month four, had been ten percent, and it had pushed maybe two hundred people below the line where they could function. The second, in month seven, was fifteen percent, and the doorway population had doubled. This was a third. The numbers would be worse. The numbers were always worse because the cuts were cumulative — you weren't cutting a third from an adequate ration. You were cutting a third from an already-cut ration, and the difference between cutting ten percent off a full plate and cutting thirty-three percent off a plate that was already half-empty was the difference between hunger and something else, something that didn't have a polite name.

More doorway people meant the gangs squeezed harder. Garrick fed his people from the ration points he controlled — that was the deal, the basic contract of the Ash Vipers: you worked for Garrick, Garrick made sure you ate. But if the ration dropped, Garrick's share dropped too, and a gang leader who couldn't feed his people was a gang leader watching his people look for someone who could. He'd compensate. He'd raise the tithe on every business in his territory. He'd push the collection schedule tighter, demand more from the people who owed him, call in debts that had been sitting in the ledger as leverage and convert them into payments.

The ledger. Her name in it. Nine silvers. Two days of the four burned.

She had two left. And the ration cut made everything harder — the queues would be longer, tighter, more compressed. More marks, which meant more opportunities. But also more desperate marks, and desperate people were harder to steal from because desperation made you watchful in a way that boredom didn't. A bored crowd forgot where its hands were. A hungry crowd knew exactly where its hands were because its hands were all it had left.

She picked up her ration at the table. The guard scooped five ounces into her bag. She could feel the difference. The bag weighed less. Not dramatically — a few ounces — but her hands knew, the way her hands knew the weight of a good purse versus a light one, because weight was information and her hands were trained to read it.

She took the bag and walked.

*          *          *

The gangs responded before noon.

Viper patrols increased. She counted three pairs in the harbour district by midday, where there was normally one. They walked tighter routes with shorter stops, the body language of men who'd been given new orders and were still calibrating what the orders meant. They weren't looking for anything specific — they were making themselves visible, reminding the population who controlled the food, who administered the tables, who stood between the grain and the people who needed it. Visibility was control. Garrick understood that. He'd understood it since before the siege, when the Vipers had been a mid-level gang running protection and loan-sharking and the occasional warehouse theft, and he understood it now, eleven months later, when the Vipers were functionally the government of half the city and the distinction between a gang and an administration was a distinction that fewer people bothered to make.

On the market's edges, the Hooks were adjusting too. A pair at the Fishmonger's Arch, two more at the southern entrance, standing with the aggressive stillness of men whose territory had just become more valuable because the food inside it had become more scarce. They'd moved their positions. Yesterday the boys at the Arch had been standing at the archway itself — today they were ten feet east, closer to the buffer zone. A small shift. Incremental. The kind of movement that happened before larger movements, the way the ground shifts before a building falls.

The buffer zone. She walked through it in the early afternoon. Three rows of independent stalls. Still there. Still operating. But the energy had changed. The Viper man near the grain seller was half a row closer to the buffer than he'd been yesterday — not enough to cross the line, not enough to provoke, but enough to signal that the line was being reconsidered. The Hook boys had moved east. The chandler was watching both sides with the sharp, measuring gaze of a woman who understood that her stall existed in a space that was being compressed and who was calculating how long the compression would take.

Zima walked through and counted and filed and kept walking.

The doorway people were already multiplying.

Three new faces in the alcove near the tannery market — a man and two women, sitting against the wall with the blank, conserving stillness of people who'd arrived at the last place they intended to go. She didn't recognise them. They weren't regulars. A week ago they'd been somewhere else — an apartment, a shared room, one of the boarding houses on the south side that charged two coppers a night. The ration cut had been announced five hours ago and they were already here, which meant they'd been close to the edge before the cut and the cut had been the fraction that tipped them.

On Harbour Row, a family. A woman sitting against the wall with a child under each arm. Two children, both under ten. The woman had a canvas bag beside her and nothing else. Her face had the quality Zima recognised from the doorway people she'd been watching for months — the look of someone performing a calculation they didn't want to finish. How long on what's left. How many days. What happens when the days run out.

She walked past them. She'd learned to walk past. Stopping meant engaging, and engaging meant feeling, and feeling was a resource she'd rationed more carefully than the garrison rationed grain. She walked past the family and counted the children — two — and filed the image in the place where she kept the things she didn't look at directly, alongside the doorway on Wick Street and the nine silvers in Garrick's ledger and the way Stel's fingers had adjusted his cuffs when he told her, politely, that she had four days.

*          *          *

She worked the afternoon queues and the late-market crowd and the take was thin.

The queues were too compressed, the bodies packed too tight, the attention too focused. She tried the Harbour Gate queue again in the afternoon and managed one purse — four coppers and a tin button, from a woman whose coat had an outside pocket and whose eyes were fixed on the distribution table with an intensity that bordered on devotion. In the market she picked up two coppers from a man who'd set them on a stall counter while he examined a piece of cloth, and three more from a pouch that someone had dropped or kicked near the grain stall. Nine coppers for the day.

She sat under the Coppergate bridge at dusk and she counted.

The coins spread across the stone in the dim light. She sorted them by type, the way she always did — coppers in one row, silvers in another, the half-silvers in between. The total was the total. She'd been adding to it for three days and the adding was not fast enough and the total was not large enough and the gap between what she had and what she owed was a gap that three days of pickpocketing had not closed and one remaining day was not going to close either.

Three silvers and a pile of coppers that didn't add up to a fourth. That was what she had. Nine silvers was what she owed. Six short, roughly, and tomorrow was the last day, and six silvers in one day was not a sum she could steal from ration queues and market crowds, not in this city, not with these takes, not with the queues tightening and the marks getting harder and the whole economic machinery of Ashport contracting around her like a fist.

She was going to default. The understanding arrived without drama, without the moment of crisis she might have expected. It was just arithmetic. She'd been running the numbers for three days and the numbers had been telling her the same thing each night and she'd been looking past them each morning, waiting for the numbers to say something different, and the numbers had said the same thing every night and tomorrow Garrick's machinery would start.

The fog came in. The drums started. Three slow beats from the hills, each one heavy enough to bruise the damp air, patient and deliberate, as though the orc drummers understood what the sound did to the city and chose to do it anyway. Then the silence. Then the seven fast ones, rolling, tumbling, a sound that had been Ashport's evening for eleven months and had become as much a part of the city as the fog and the salt and the hunger.

She listened, and she thought about the ration cut, and the doorway people, and the family on Harbour Row, and the Vipers tightening their routes, and the Hooks shifting east, and the buffer zone in the market — three rows, still holding, still there, for now.

The city had taken the blow. That was the word for it. Not a catastrophe, not a collapse. A blow. The kind of impact a body absorbs standing up — you take it, you stay on your feet, you breathe, and for a moment you think you're all right because you're still vertical and your legs still work and the pain hasn't arrived yet.

But the pain arrives later. It always does. A blow to the body doesn't break you at the moment of contact. It breaks you in the hours after, when the bruise deepens and the swelling starts and the tissue underneath begins to fail in ways you can't see from the outside. The ration cut was the blow. The real damage — the structural damage, the kind that changed what the city was and what its people could endure — hadn't started yet. It was propagating. Moving through the population the way a crack moves through ice: slow, directional, following the existing weaknesses, the places where the structure was already thin.

She put the coins back in her pockets. She lay down in the alcove with her coat pulled tight and her back against the cold stone and she listened to the harbour and she didn't sleep for a long time.

Tomorrow the market. The last day. The last chance to add to the count before the count stopped mattering and the ledger took over.

The cats came. Two of them, the lean harbour toms, expecting food. She didn't have anything for them tonight. She lay there and they sat at the edge of the alcove and waited with the patient, uncomprehending expectation of animals who'd been fed before and didn't understand why tonight was different, and she looked at them and thought about the woman on Harbour Row with a child under each arm and a canvas bag and nothing else, and she thought about the doorway on Wick Street getting smaller every time she passed it, and she thought about the nine silvers she didn't have and the one day she had left and the machinery that was going to start tomorrow when the day ran out.

She'd figure something out. She always figured something out.

The problem with always figuring something out was that the somethings were getting smaller and the problems were getting larger, and the distance between the two was a distance she could measure now — could feel it in the weight of her pockets and the thinness of her rations and the count of doorway people increasing every day — and the measurement said: you are running out of room.

The fog thickened. The cats left. The drums from the hills were silent. Ashport settled into a night of reduced rations and reduced patience, and the blow it had taken that morning worked its way deeper into the city's body, finding the cracks, following the weaknesses, doing the slow invisible work of damage that wouldn't show on the surface until it was too late to repair it.

Under the Coppergate bridge, in the alcove that smelled of low tide and old mortar, Zima lay awake with three silvers in her pockets and a debt that couldn't be paid and a deadline that couldn't be moved, and she stared at the underside of the bridge and she waited for morning the way the city waited for the siege to end — without expectation, without plan, with nothing but the habit of endurance and the fading suspicion that endurance, by itself, was enough.
