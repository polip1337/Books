CHAPTER TWENTY-THREE
Twelve Percent



The back door was open. Lirra was already sitting on the worktable, boots crossed, two cups poured. The same arrangement as yesterday. A pattern, Zima realised. Lirra used patterns the way Zima used counting — to impose order on a world that didn't offer any.

"You expected me," Zima said.

"You need something. People who need things come back." Lirra pushed the cup forward. "Sit."

Zima sat. The chicory was the same bitter temperature. She drank it and let the silence work. In the queue she'd learned that silence made people uncomfortable, and uncomfortable people talked to fill the space. But Lirra wasn't a mark, and Lirra didn't fill silence. She occupied it.

"I need patrol intelligence," Zima said. "Viper patrols, dock district, specifically the alley behind the Harbourmaster's office. Two-man routes, rotation times, the gap at the shift change. I need it mapped, timed, and confirmed over two consecutive days."

"That's specific."

"I'm a specific person."

"And in exchange?"

"What do you want?"

Lirra's real eye narrowed. The glass eye stayed wide and amber and slightly wrong. "I want to know what you're doing. Not what you told me yesterday — what you're actually doing. The drain cover, the patrols, the thing in your coat. All of it."

Zima had known this was coming. She'd rehearsed versions of the truth on the walk over — edited versions, truncated versions, versions that left out the dead queen and the voice inside her skull. None of them had been convincing. Lirra read people for a living. Half a truth would insult her.

She set the cup down.

"There are passages under the city," Zima said. "Old ones. Built before the walls. They run from four access points to a central vault. The vault has been sealed for two hundred years. The disc in my coat is the key."

Lirra didn't move.

"The drain cover in the dock-district alley sits directly above one of the access points. If I can get through the cover during the patrol gap, I can reach the passage network and follow it to the vault."

"What's in the vault?"

"I don't know exactly. Supplies. Materials. Something that was put aside a long time ago for someone who would need it."

"Put aside by whom?"

Zima considered lying. Considered saying she'd read it on the disc, or that Dav had told her, or that it was speculation. She looked at Lirra's hands — still, flour-free today, resting on her thighs with the patient weight of someone who would wait as long as it took.

"By the woman who built the passages. Two centuries ago. Her name was Maela."

"The queen."

"Yes."

"The dead queen."

"Yes."

Lirra's expression didn't change. She processed information the way her oven processed dough — taking it in, applying heat, producing something transformed. The transformation was internal and invisible, but Zima could feel it in the quality of the silence.

"How do you know this?" Lirra asked. "Not from the disc. The disc has markings. Markings don't tell you a queen's name or her intentions."

This was the part Zima had no way to edit.

"She told me."

"Maela."

"Yes."

"The dead queen spoke to you."

"In my head. Not through the air. Through the disc, I think. Or through whatever the disc connects to." Zima kept her voice flat. This was either going to end the conversation or change it, and decorating the words wouldn't help either outcome. "She sounds impatient. She's been waiting a long time."

Lirra got off the worktable. She walked to the oven, opened the iron door, and looked inside at nothing — the oven was cold, no fire, no bread. She closed the door. She stood with her back to Zima for a count of nine.

"You're either lying," Lirra said to the oven, "or the world is different than I thought."

"Both of those things can be true at the same time."

Lirra turned around. "They can't, actually." But the corner of her mouth moved, and Zima recognised it as the closest Lirra came to conceding a point.

She walked back to the worktable and leaned against it instead of sitting. The posture was different — standing put distance between them, a negotiator's distance instead of a collaborator's.

"I'll map the patrols," Lirra said. "Two days, as you asked. Two-man routes, rotation times, the shift-change gap. I'll time it myself from two positions — the fish stall and the warehouse overhang."

"What do you want for it?"

"Twelve percent."

Zima blinked. "Of what?"

"Of whatever's in the vault."

"You don't know what's in the vault."

"Neither do you. Twelve percent of an unknown quantity is a fair price for intelligence that keeps you alive long enough to find out." Lirra folded her arms. The flour dust on her forearms was yesterday's — she hadn't baked this morning. She'd been waiting. "And I want something else."

"What?"

"Garrick has been researching founding-era documents at the harbour records building. Three visits. Three weeks. He's been drawing on the plans — connecting passage fragments, mapping something he doesn't quite understand." Lirra's voice was the same even tone she used for everything, but her real eye was harder than before. "He knew about the passages before you did. He had the disc before you did. He sent a courier to deliver it to someone who could read it, and the courier died, and you picked it up."

"I know all this."

"Then you know that whatever you're planning, you're planning it against the clock. Garrick doesn't need the disc to find the access points. He needs time and the harbour records and someone clever enough to read old maps. He has all three."

The room was quiet. The oven ticked — contraction now, metal cooling against brick. From the street beyond the front room came the distant sound of a handcart on cobbles.

"Two days," Zima said. "You map the patrols. Twelve percent."

"Twelve percent and a full briefing before we go underground."

"Before we go underground."

Lirra held her gaze. "You don't think I'm going to map the route and then wave goodbye from the surface."

Zima hadn't thought about it. She'd been thinking of Lirra as a source — someone who provided intelligence and received payment. The idea that Lirra would come underground hadn't occurred to her, and the failure to consider it felt like a miscalculation of the kind that cost more than it should.

"The passages could be dangerous," Zima said.

"The surface is dangerous. At least underground nobody's looking for me." Lirra unfolded her arms and picked up her chicory. "Twelve percent, a briefing, and I'm on the crew. Those are my terms."

Zima studied her. The glass eye, the cropped hair, the baker's hands that were also an intelligence broker's hands. Lirra had survived four years running a coded-bread network under the noses of two gangs and a corrupt garrison. She'd identified Garrick's research from a single client's reports. She'd noticed the disc's warmth from four feet away.

"Done," Zima said.

Lirra drank her chicory. "Two days. I'll have the map when you come back. And Zima?"

"What?"

"Whatever you're planning, plan it fast. Garrick doesn't research things for fun."

*          *          *

Outside, the fog had lifted enough to show the harbour in hard grey light. The blockade ships sat in their positions. The drums went three slow, seven fast. Zima walked toward the dock district with the disc warm against her ribs and Lirra's words settling into the framework she was building in her head.

Twelve percent. It was fair. More than fair, given what Lirra was offering — not just patrol schedules but an intelligence network that knew what Garrick was doing and how close he was to finding the passages on his own.

She ran the numbers. If the vault held what Maela had described — supplies, materials, everything needed to leave a city by sea — then twelve percent was a significant share. But twelve percent of survival was still survival, and zero percent of nothing was the alternative.

The dock district was quiet. Midmorning on a siege day — the fishing boats that ran the blockade at night were tied up and sleeping, the dockhands who crewed them the same. The Harbourmaster's office was closed, its windows boarded, the brass nameplate green with salt corrosion. The alley behind it was narrow and shadowed and smelled of old fish and harbour water.

The drain cover sat at the alley's midpoint. Cast iron, three feet across, with a lifting ring recessed into a groove. She'd seen it before — walked past it dozens of times on her way through the dock district — but she'd never looked at it as a door. It was a drain cover. Every city had them. They were part of the ground the way cobbles were part of the ground, invisible until you had a reason to see them.

She had a reason now.

She didn't touch it. Not today, not with the patrol patterns unmapped and the shift-change gap unconfirmed. She walked past it at the same pace she'd walk past anything, noting the position relative to the alley walls (centred, slightly east), the condition of the iron (rusted but intact), the lifting ring (functional, not seized), and the shadows (full cover from the west, partial from the east, none from directly above at midday).

At the alley's mouth she stopped and counted. Forty-three paces from the drain cover to the street. A patrol coming from the east would have line of sight for twenty of those paces. From the west, fifteen. The alley had no side exits. Once you were in it, you committed.

She needed the timing. She needed the two-day map. She needed Lirra.

And she needed rope. Fifty feet of strong rope, which meant the dockyard cranes, which meant the afternoon gap when the district emptied.

Tomorrow.

She walked back toward the harbour wall and ate the second quarter of Lirra's bread and watched the orc ships and planned. The disc pulsed slow against her ribs. Maela's voice didn't come. The silence was its own kind of answer — approval, or patience, or the conservation of energy for what was coming.

Three things on her list. Patrol map. Rope. Someone who could read old scripts.

Two of those she had a plan for. The third — the scholar at the Wayfarers Hall, the name Dav had given her, Eldrin — was a shape without detail. She knew the name. She knew he'd been at Ironmore University. She knew he'd been sacked. She didn't know what he looked like or where exactly in the Hall he slept or whether he'd help or run or sell her to the nearest buyer.

But that was a problem for after the rope. One thing at a time. Count the steps, work the list, keep the disc warm against her ribs and the debt ticking over in the ledger and the clock running down on every plan she'd ever made.

She finished the bread. She folded the cloth and put it in her pocket. She walked home — home being the tanner's loft, home being three coppers and a pallet that smelled of chemicals, home being the space between one day and the next where she lay in the dark and counted the ceiling beams and listened to nine other people breathe.

Twelve percent. The number sat in her head like a coin on a table. She could work with twelve percent.
