CHAPTER TWENTY-ONE
Bread and Silence



She counted the streets between the tanner's loft and Mercer Lane. Nine, if she took the route through Chandler's Alley. Eleven if she went by the harbour wall where the Viper patrols were thinnest. She took eleven.

The morning fog sat low and grey against the rooftops, pressing the city flat. Somewhere in the Thornback Hills the drums were going — three slow, seven fast, the same rhythm they'd kept for eleven months. Zima had stopped hearing them as sound. They were weather now, a pressure in the skull that meant the siege was still the siege and nothing had changed except the ration portions and the number of people sleeping in doorways.

She'd slept badly. The tanner's loft had been full — nine bodies on pallets, the woman with children in the far corner, the dockhand who ground his teeth, a new arrival she didn't recognise who spent the night coughing into his sleeve with the wet, barking sound that meant the lung sickness was in another building. Three coppers a night for a pallet and a roof. The pallets smelled of tanning chemicals and old sweat and something sharper underneath that she'd stopped trying to identify.

The disc sat against her ribs where she'd sewn it, warm through the canvas. She touched it without thinking, a gesture that had become habitual in the five days since the courier's hand had opened and the dark metal had dropped into her palm like it belonged there. She caught herself and moved her hand to her pocket. The pocket was empty. She'd spent the last copper on the pallet.

Mercer Lane was one of the older streets — cobbled instead of packed earth, buildings that leaned but hadn't fallen, a gutter that still drained. It had survived the compression better than most because it sat between territories rather than inside one. The Vipers didn't patrol it. The Hooks didn't mark it. It was a gap, and gaps were where Zima lived.

She smelled the bakery before she saw it. Wheat and woodsmoke, faint at first, then solid enough to walk into. Twenty paces. She'd measured it once, during a week when she'd been watching Lirra's delivery routes and trying to understand why the woman baked more loaves than she sold.

The sign above the door read B_KE_Y. The missing letters had blown off during the autumn storms and nobody had replaced them. Lirra had told the chandler's boy it was because she couldn't afford a sign-painter. Zima had watched her pay for a week's worth of flour in silver and doubted the explanation.

She went around to the back.

The door was unlocked — it was always unlocked during working hours. Lirra had told her once that a locked door attracted more attention than an open one, and that the kind of people she worried about wouldn't be stopped by a lock anyway. At the time Zima had taken it for the ordinary caution of someone who worked alone. Now she heard it differently.

Inside: heat, flour dust, the heavy sweet smell of dough in its second rise. The back room was narrow — a worktable that ran the length of the wall, shelves above it stacked with bowls and proving baskets, a brick oven in the far corner with its iron door closed and heat bleeding through the brick. Sacks of flour along the opposite wall. A ledger on a stool. Two cups, both empty.

Lirra stood at the worktable with her back to the door. She was working a piece of dough the size of a man's head — punch, fold, turn, portion. The rhythm was mechanical and exact, the kind of precision that came from years of repetition. Her sleeves were rolled to the elbow. Flour to the wrist. Cropped dark hair, cut close enough that Zima could see the shape of her skull.

"Sit down," Lirra said without turning. "Four minutes."

Zima sat on the stool by the ledger. The ledger was closed. She didn't touch it.

She watched Lirra's hands instead. The punching motion came from the shoulder, not the wrist — baker's hands were stronger than they looked. The folding was precise, each turn exactly a quarter rotation. The portioning was done by feel, but the portions were identical. Zima counted six. Six loaves from a single batch at this hour meant Lirra had been working since before dawn. The oven temperature and the proving time meant the first batch had gone in while the streets were still dark.

Six loaves for a shop that sold to three regular customers and whatever foot traffic came through. Mercer Lane didn't get much foot traffic anymore.

Lirra finished the sixth portion and set it in a proving basket. She wiped her hands on the cloth that hung from her belt, turned around, and looked at Zima for the first time.

The glass eye was on the left side. It was close to the colour of the real one — a dark amber — but deliberately a shade off, too green, as though Lirra had chosen the mismatch on purpose. The real eye was the one doing the looking. It moved across Zima the way Zima moved across a crowd: quick, systematic, cataloguing.

"You've been sleeping rough," Lirra said. "Different rough than usual. You changed your spot."

Zima said nothing.

"And there's something in your coat that wasn't there before." Lirra's gaze dropped to Zima's left side. "Left side. Below the ribs. You keep touching it."

The flour on Lirra's hands caught the light from the oven grate. Her expression was the same one she wore when she was reading dough — assessing, not yet deciding.

"I didn't come here for bread," Zima said.

"I know."

"I need information about Viper patrol patterns. Specifically the dock district, from the Harbour Gate east to the fourth crane."

Lirra didn't answer immediately. She turned back to the worktable, picked up a cloth, and began wiping down the surface with the same methodical precision she'd used on the dough. The oven ticked — metal expanding against brick.

"That's a specific stretch of ground," Lirra said. "Why?"

"Because I need to get into the drain cover in the alley behind the Harbourmaster's office without being seen."

Lirra stopped wiping. She didn't turn around, but the stillness in her shoulders was its own kind of response.

"The Vipers run two-man patrols through there," she said. "Four-hour rotation. The shift change leaves a gap, but I'd want to measure it before I trusted it."

Zima felt something settle in her chest. Not surprise — she'd suspected Lirra tracked patrol patterns. The coded bread, the delivery variations, the extra loaves. But hearing it confirmed was different from suspecting it.

"You know their schedules."

"I know a lot of things." Lirra turned around again. The glass eye and the real one both aimed at Zima, and the effect was like being read by two different people at once. "I know Garrick's men have been asking about a dead courier named Pell and something he was carrying. I know the Hooks hit three Viper positions during the market fight and the Vipers pulled back to the ration-table strongholds. I know the neutral ground has halved in the last two weeks and the garrison doesn't care."

She paused.

"And I know you've been carrying something under your coat that you didn't have a week ago, and it's making you nervous enough to walk eleven streets to a bakery when there are shorter routes."

Zima counted the seconds it took her to decide. Three.

"I didn't come here just for patrol schedules."

"No." Lirra folded the cloth, set it on the table, and pulled the second stool out from under the shelf. She sat down across from Zima. The distance between them was four feet — close enough to talk quietly, far enough that neither could reach the other without standing. "You came because you need someone, and you've been watching me long enough to know I'm not what I look like."

"What do you look like?"

"A baker." The real eye held steady. "What do I look like to you?"

Zima considered lying. She considered deflection, misdirection, the tools she used on marks in the ration queue. She looked at Lirra's hands — the flour, the calluses, the small burn scars on the right forearm — and at the glass eye that watched without watching, and at the proving baskets where six identical portions waited to become bread that would carry messages to addresses that didn't match the delivery route.

"You look like someone who sells information," Zima said. "And I need to buy some."

Lirra's mouth moved — not a smile, something shorter. An acknowledgment.

"Then you'd better tell me what you're not telling me," Lirra said. "Because the thing in your coat is warm. I can see the heat from here."

Zima's hand went to her left side before she could stop it. The disc was pulsing — slow, rhythmic, like a heartbeat that belonged to something much larger than her. Through the canvas and the coat lining she could feel the warmth radiating outward, and she realised with a dropping sensation that Lirra was right. The heat was visible. Not as light, but as the faintest distortion in the air above the fabric, the way a road shimmers in summer.

She hadn't noticed. She'd been carrying it against her body and she hadn't noticed.

"Sit down properly," Lirra said. "And start at the beginning."

*          *          *

She didn't start at the beginning. She started at the middle, which was the part she could verify and the part that didn't require her to explain that a dead woman's voice had spoken inside her skull.

The market fight. The courier. The disc in his hand. The warmth that shouldn't exist. The map-like markings. The weight that was wrong for the size. The Vipers searching.

She left out the dream. She left out Maela. She left out the voice that had said pay attention, girl with the impatience of someone who'd been waiting two centuries for a conversation.

Lirra listened the way she kneaded — with her whole body, leaned slightly forward, hands still on her thighs. She didn't interrupt. She didn't nod. When Zima finished, Lirra sat quiet for a count of eight.

"Show me."

Zima unpicked the stitching at her coat lining. The canvas scrap came away and the disc sat in her palm, dark metal catching the oven-light. The warmth was immediate and obvious — Lirra held her own palm six inches above it and pulled back.

"That's not residual heat," Lirra said. "That's generating."

"I know."

"Objects don't do that."

"I know."

Lirra studied the disc without touching it. Her real eye tracked the markings — the map-like geometry on the face, the smooth reverse. She tilted her head to read the finer lines, and the glass eye caught the oven-light and threw it back amber.

"The Vipers want this," she said.

"Garrick wants this."

"There's a difference?"

"Garrick wants it because he knows what it is. The Vipers want it because he told them to find it." Zima closed her hand over the disc. The warmth pulsed once against her palm. "I think he's been looking for it — or something like it — for weeks. Before the courier. Before the market fight."

"What makes you think that?"

"Because the courier was carrying it somewhere specific. You don't give a runner a route unless you know the destination."

Lirra was quiet for a long time. The oven ticked. Somewhere in the front room, something shifted — a proving basket settling, dough expanding against wicker.

"I need to check something," Lirra said. "Come back tomorrow. Same door."

She stood, took the cloth from the table, and went back to her dough as though the conversation had been about flour prices and delivery schedules.

Zima sewed the disc back into her coat lining in the alley behind the bakery, using a needle she kept in her collar seam. The stitches were rough — her hands were unsteady. The disc was warmer than before, warmer than when she'd shown it. As though it had recognised something in the room and responded.

She walked the eleven streets back through the fog, and the drums in the hills went three slow, seven fast, and the city went on being the city, and nobody looked at her twice.
