CHAPTER SIXTEEN
What Maela Says



Two days passed before the disc warmed again.

She worked. She ate. She carried the map in her head and the disc against her ribs and she went about the ordinary business of surviving in Ashport, which was the only business anybody had. The morning queue at the Harbour Gate. The afternoon queue on Salt Lane, the smaller one, Hook-adjacent, where the crowds were thinner and the takes were thinner and the risk was slightly different in character — more exposure, fewer bodies to disappear into, but also fewer Viper eyes. She took three purses over two days. The total was unremarkable: seven coppers and a half-silver from the first day, nine coppers from the second. She counted them under the bridge each night and ran the total and she was still four silvers and four coppers short of Garrick's debt, and the debt was a stone in her pocket that she carried everywhere and put down nowhere.

She waited for Maela. She didn't think of it in those terms — she thought of it as waiting for the disc's temperature to change, which was a physical observation, not a supernatural one, and the distinction mattered to her even though she knew it was thin. The disc was warm during the day. Its resting heat. The warmth she'd calibrated over a week of wearing it — low, steady, almost comfortable, the body heat of an object that was not alive but was also not entirely inert. At night the warmth dropped slightly. During the two days after the voice, it stayed at the daytime level — no climb, no build, no sign of whatever charge Maela had said needed accumulating.

She thought about what she'd heard. She thought about it the way she thought about a mark's behaviour before a take — from several angles, checking for inconsistencies, looking for the thing that didn't fit. The voice had said: there are passages beneath this city. The dream had shown her passages. The two sources matched. Either both were real or both were parts of the same deception, and if she was being deceived by a voice inside her own head, the deception was so complete that she had no instruments to detect it, which meant trusting or not trusting amounted to the same practical outcome: she needed more information.

She didn't go to Lirra. The plan was still Lirra first, Eldrin second, but the plan had been made before the voice and the voice had changed the order of operations. The disc was not just a valuable object anymore. It was a connection to something — someone — who had specific information about what was under the city. Intelligence about Viper movements could wait a day. Two days. Long enough to hear what Maela had to say.

On the second evening, the disc started warming after dark.

*          *          *

She was in the alcove. The fog was thin tonight — a high, wispy kind that let her see thirty or forty feet beyond the bridge, the rooftops visible against the grey sky, the harbour water catching the last of the light. She could see the fishing boats at their moorings, five of them, rocking gently. She could see the shape of the breakwater. She could not see the orc warships beyond it but she could feel them, or she imagined she could, the way she'd always imagined she could — by the quality of the silence past the breakwater, the particular emptiness of water that was being watched.

The harbour bell had rung nine. Night was full. The drums had done their count an hour ago — three and seven — and the silence after was the silence she'd been living in for eleven months, the held breath of a city under siege, and she was used to it and she was not used to it and she suspected she would never be fully used to it.

The warmth started at half past nine. She knew because she'd been counting the quarter-bells, the way she counted everything. The disc's temperature climbed — not the step-change of the voice night, but a steady build, a gradual ascent that she tracked through the canvas the way you track the rising of a tide mark on a wall. Slow. Deliberate. The disc gathering whatever it gathered. Building its charge.

She sat up. She put her back to the wall. She did not draw her knife.

The warmth climbed further. The pressure came — the thickness in the air, the density she'd felt before, the sense of space being occupied by something she couldn't see. She recognised it now. The first time it had been terrifying. The second time — this time — it was familiar, which was its own kind of unsettling, the realisation that she was getting used to the approach of a dead woman's voice the way she'd gotten used to the drums.

Then:

You're calmer this time. Good.

The voice was stronger than before. Clearer, more present, the words fully formed and the personality behind them fully arrived — the low, roughened authority, the impatience held in check, the intelligence assessing her from the other side of whatever bridge the disc maintained. Two days of rest had made a difference. The voice sounded like a person now, not a signal.

"You said you'd come back," Zima said. She kept her voice low. Not because anyone would hear — the bridge was her territory, the silence-coin kept it empty — but because talking to the air was a thing she hadn't reconciled herself to and might never.

I did. And I don't have as much time as I'd like, so I'm going to talk and you're going to listen, and then you can ask your questions and I'll answer the ones that matter.

"Who decides which ones matter?"

I do. The faintest amusement. It should have been terrifying — a dead woman, amused — and it wasn't. It was the amusement of someone who was used to running the conversation and was rediscovering the rhythm of it after a long silence. There are passages beneath this city. I built them.

Zima said nothing. She'd heard this start before, the night the connection broke. She waited.

Before I built the walls. Before I built the harbour. Before anything that stands on the surface today. The passages came first. A pause — not a break in the connection but a breath, a gathering, the kind of pause a speaker made when the next part was important. They follow the old water channels. Natural channels, carved by the river that ran through this valley before the city existed. I found the channels when I was surveying the site for the harbour foundations. They were old then — ancient, cut into the bedrock by centuries of water. I expanded them. Dressed the stone, widened the routes, sealed the junctions. Built a network.

"How extensive?"

Four main branches. Each runs from an access point on the surface to a central vault. The longest branch is about three hundred yards. They're under the oldest parts of the city, the parts built on bedrock. The newer construction — the districts east of the market, the warehouses along the quay — those are on softer ground. The passages don't extend there.

"The dream," Zima said. "The map. That was from the disc."

Yes. Not from me — from the disc. The disc carries the map. It's part of what the disc is. When you took it, it read you. It assessed you. It decided you needed to know the layout of the network, so it showed you.

"It decided."

It's designed to. I built it that way. The disc finds the person who needs to carry it, and when it finds them, it starts teaching them. The map was the first lesson.

The word lesson again. Zima noted it the way she noted prices and distances and the positions of Viper patrols — automatically, filing it for later examination. A lesson implied a curriculum. A curriculum implied more lessons.

"What's the second lesson?"

Me. The voice was flat. Factual. I'm the second lesson. The disc taught you the map because you need to know where the passages are. It connected you to me because you need to know what's in them. The disc is a tool. I'm the context.

Zima sat with that. She let the silence hold for a moment, the harbour sounds filling the gap — water on pilings, the creak of rope, the muffled bell of a fishing boat shifting at its mooring.

"The vault," she said. "What's in it?"

Everything I could put aside over twenty years of planning. The voice was faster now, more certain, moving on ground she'd covered before — in her own mind, two centuries ago, and countless times since, in the long dark of whatever existence the disc maintained. Supplies. Equipment. Preserved goods — food stores, sealed against spoilage in ways that should still be holding. Coin, a significant quantity. Navigation equipment. Tools. Rope, canvas, fittings. Everything a person would need if the city reached a point where the options were leave or hold.

"Leave or hold?"

For leaving. Or for staying, depending on how bad things have got. The sentence landed with a finality that was not emotional but practical — the weight of a woman who had planned for catastrophe the way Zima planned for a take, with precision and foresight and the cold understanding that the worst case was not hypothetical. I built this city knowing it would face a crisis. Every city does. The question is never whether the crisis comes — it's whether you've put something aside for the people who have to live through it.

"And you put aside a vault full of supplies."

I put aside everything I could. The voice was quiet but not thin — present, controlled, the deliberate quiet of someone choosing their words. I didn't know what the crisis would be. Siege, famine, fire, flood, war — I didn't know. So I planned for all of them. The vault holds what you'd need for any of them. The disc is the key. The only key.

"Why the only key?"

Because I couldn't trust anyone else to decide when to open it. The sharpness was back. I could have given keys to the garrison, to the council, to whoever governed the city after me. I didn't. People use things. People find reasons to open locked doors before the doors need opening. They'd have emptied the vault in the first decade — oh, we need coin for the new quay, we need rope for the fishing fleet, we need supplies for the festival. Little withdrawals. Reasonable withdrawals. And in fifty years the vault would be empty and the crisis would come and there'd be nothing left.

She paused. The disc pulsed — a slow, steady beat of warmth.

So I built a lock that nobody could pick. The disc. It circulates. It moves through the city, hand to hand, year to year. It assesses everyone who touches it. And when the crisis comes — when the city actually needs what I stored — the disc finds the right person and gives them the map and gives them me.

"It's been circulating for two hundred years."

Yes. The flatness was back. I know. The disc has passed through I don't know how many hands. Lost in a drawer for decades. Used as a paperweight. Sold to a merchant who kept it on a shelf. Stolen, dropped, found, stolen again. Every person who held it was tested and every person failed, and the disc moved on, and the vault waited, and I — She stopped.

Zima waited.

I waited. The voice was quieter. Not thinner — the connection was strong, stronger than it had been last time. But the volume was down, the way a person's volume goes down when they're saying something they've been carrying for a long time and the carrying has worn a groove in it. I don't know what I am, exactly. I know I'm in the disc. I know I'm aware — of the disc, of whoever holds it, of the city. Dimly. Shapes and pressures. I can feel the weight of people on the streets. I can feel the walls. I can feel the damage — the harbour blockade, the gaps in the northern fortification, the army in the hills. I built this place, girl. It speaks to me even when it's broken.

The disc was warm. Steady. The connection held. Outside, the fog pressed against the alcove's mouth, and the harbour sounded its patient rhythm, and the night was quiet in the way that Ashport nights were quiet — not silence but reduced, the city's volume turned down, the living pushed indoors and the streets left to the fog and the cats and the people who had nowhere indoors to go.

"How bad is it?" Zima said. "The city. From what you can feel."

Bad enough. The answer was immediate and unsurprised, as if she'd been waiting for the question. I can feel the hunger. It has a weight — not physical, not something I could measure, but a pressure. Thousands of people underfed. I can feel the walls — the damage from the catapult strikes, the places where the stone has been patched and the patches are weaker than the original. I can feel the army. Fifteen thousand, roughly. They're patient. They haven't attacked because they don't need to. They're waiting for the city to do their work for them.

"That's my theory."

It's a good theory. The impatience had eased into something closer to professional acknowledgement — one pragmatist recognising another. The siege will end in one of two ways. Either the city opens the gates, or the city stops being a city. Both happen when the food runs out. When the rations hit zero, the garrison will negotiate or the population will riot, and either way the gates open and the army walks in. That's the timeline you're on. That's what the vault was built for.

"How long?"

I don't know. I can feel pressures, not inventories. But the hunger is heavier now than it was when you first touched the disc. That was — A pause. The disc pulsed. Six days ago?

"About that."

Six days and the pressure has increased. That's not good. That means the rations are being cut, or the supply is being disrupted, or both. The timeline is weeks, not months. Maybe less.

*          *          *

Zima was quiet. She let the information settle the way she let a crowd settle before she worked it — observing the pattern, the movement, the shape of what had been said and what it meant.

Weeks. Not months. The city had weeks of food left, or weeks of governance, or weeks of both, and the difference between the three was academic because all three ended the same way.

"The four access points," she said. "Where?"

The voice described them. One near the old docks, in the harbour district's oldest section, where the waterfront predated the quay construction. Zima matched the description to the harbour she knew: the crumbling stone jetty south of the main pier, the section that nobody used because the stonework was unstable. One near the northern wall, in the foundation — below the wall itself, accessed through a passage that Maela described as running behind the drainage channel at the wall's base. Zima matched this to the Harbour Gate section, the oldest part of the fortification. One in the market district, near the northern edge — below a building that had been a storehouse in Maela's time, and which Zima suspected was the grain store on Salt Lane or one of its neighbours. And the fourth in the old city south of Mercer Lane, in the district where the streets were narrowest and the buildings were oldest and the bedrock was closest to the surface.

She memorised the descriptions the way she memorised everything — precisely, checking each detail against the map in her head. The four endpoints the voice described matched the four branches of the dream. The map and the explanation were the same geography, different angles. The dream was the picture. Maela's words were the address.

"The vault is at the centre," she said. "Below everything."

Below everything. The satisfaction in the voice was professional — the pride of a builder describing what she'd built. Fifty feet below the current street level, roughly. The branches converge on it. The vault door responds to the disc and only the disc. You hold the disc to the door and the door opens. Nobody has been inside that chamber since I put the last crate in and sealed it and walked away.

"And then you died."

A week later. The voice was matter-of-fact. I had the wasting sickness. I'd had it for two years by then. I knew how long I had. Long enough to fill the vault. Long enough to build the disc. Not long enough to do anything else.

The disc pulsed. The warmth was high but steady — the connection holding, the charge sufficient. Stronger than last time. Maela had been right: the disc built its capacity with use. The bridge was widening.

"Why me," Zima said. She'd asked this question before, two nights ago, and the voice had broken before it could answer. She asked it now because it was still there, sitting in the queue of things she needed to know, and because she didn't believe the disc's assessment was infallible, no matter what a dead woman said about her own engineering.

I told you. The disc chose you. I don't control —

"You said the disc chose me. That it tests people and I passed." She paused. "That describes a lot of people in Ashport."

The disc was quiet. Not the silence of a broken connection — the silence of someone considering her response.

Then: No. It describes the people who know the city. It doesn't describe the people the city knows back.

"I don't understand that."

You do. You just don't want to. The impatience, but softened — a teacher with a student who's being stubborn. You've been feeling the channels for days. The map in your head isn't a picture — it's a connection. You can feel the passages because the passages can feel you. The disc is a bridge, and bridges run both directions. The city's infrastructure — the old infrastructure, the original channels, the things I built — those things are aware of you the way I'm aware of you. Not consciously. Not with thought. But the way a lock is aware of a key. You fit. Most people don't.

She didn't like that. She didn't like the idea that the city — the stone and the water and the channels below the streets — was aware of her, even in the limited, mechanical way Maela described. A lock aware of a key. It was a metaphor, probably. It was also, she suspected, not entirely a metaphor.

"What do you want from me," she said. The question that mattered. The question underneath every other question. In Ashport, everyone wanted something. Garrick wanted his nine silvers. The Vipers wanted the disc. The Hooks wanted territory. The garrison wanted to survive until the siege ended. Everyone wanted, and everyone's wanting had a shape, and if you could see the shape you could predict the behaviour. "What do you actually want?"

Go to the vault. See what I left. Use it. The simplicity of the answer was disarming — no conditions, no contract, no list of obligations or supernatural bargains. The voice of a woman who'd filled a room with useful things and wanted someone to open the door.

"That can't be all."

The disc cooled by a fraction. When the voice came back, it was different. The authority was still there, the roughness, the intelligence. But what was underneath the command voice was different now — not softer, exactly. Exposed. A layer below the command voice, below the builder's pragmatism, below the dead queen's two centuries of waiting.

I want to be remembered.

Zima waited.

I built this city. I planned the walls and I dug the channels and I dressed the stone and I filled the vault and I made the disc and I died. And the city forgot me. The voice was quiet. Controlled. Not trembling — Maela's voice was not a voice that trembled — but stripped down, the words carrying nothing extra, no rhetoric, no performance. Two hundred years and they can't read my name on the seawall. The script I wrote in — Tidal Script, the old notation, the writing I used for everything — nobody reads it. The stories about me are gone. The founding histories are gone. I'm a word they can't pronounce on a stone they walk past without looking.

The disc was warm. Steady. The harbour sounded its note against the pilings.

I don't need statues. I don't need festivals. I need someone to know that I built Ashport and that I filled those vaults and that I planned for this day, two hundred years ago, because I knew it would come. I need that to be known. I need one person to carry that knowledge the way you carry the disc — against your body, as part of your life, something you don't put down. That's what I want. That's the price for the vault and the passages and everything I left.

The silence after was long. Zima sat with it. She sat with the specific, surprising pain of it — not her pain, Maela's, but transmitted through the disc with the same clarity as the words, the ache of a woman who had built something enormous and been erased from it. She'd known forgotten people. She was a forgotten person — nobody outside the ledger and the alcove knew her name or would notice if she disappeared. But she'd been forgotten for nineteen years. Maela had been forgotten for two hundred.

"That's not a high price," she said.

It's the only one I have.

*          *          *

The voice continued. Thinner now — the charge dropping, the bridge narrowing, but not broken. Not yet.

There are things you need to know about the access points. The one near the harbour — the old dock section — is the easiest to reach but it's in territory that's being watched. The one at the northern wall is the most difficult physically — you'll need to get behind the drainage stonework, which means working in the wall's foundation, which means the garrison might see you. The market access point is the most dangerous because it's in the densest part of the city and the most people will be around. The Mercer Lane access is the safest. It's in the old district. Quiet. The entrance is in a cellar that hasn't been used in decades.

"Mercer Lane," Zima said. "That's where I'd start."

That's where I'd start too. The approval was grudging, the way all Maela's approvals seemed to be — given reluctantly, as if praising someone for good sense cost her something. But don't go alone. The passages are intact but they're dark and they're old and the footing is uneven and if you fall and break something there's nobody down there to help you. You'll need light. Rope. Someone to watch the entrance while you go in.

"I know."

Then plan. Don't rush. The vault has been waiting two hundred years. It can wait another week. A pause. The disc cooled again — a small dip, a flutter. The charge is dropping. I don't have much more tonight.

"One more question."

Make it count.

A dozen questions sat in the queue — about the vault's contents in detail, about the passage construction, about the disc's mechanisms, about how Maela existed inside the metal and what that existence was like. She picked the question that mattered most.

"Will you be there? When I go down. Will you be able to talk to me in the passages?"

A silence. Longer than the others. The disc pulsed — slow, fading.

I don't know. The honesty again, the kind that cost. The disc works best when you're still. When you're moving, the connection is harder to maintain. In the passages, close to the infrastructure I built — maybe. The old stone, the channels, the vault itself — those are the things I'm most connected to. The closer you are to them, the stronger the bridge. But I can't promise. The warmth stuttered. I can't promise anything except that I'll try.

"That's enough."

Is it? The old sharpness, for a moment. Then gone, and the voice was thin, the words losing their edges, becoming shapes rather than language.

Go. Plan. Be careful. Don't show the disc to anyone who doesn't need to see it. And don't —

The voice cut out. Not a fade this time — a break. An abrupt severing, the connection snapping like a cord under too much tension. The disc's warmth collapsed. The heat dropped from active to resting to below-resting in seconds, and the pressure in the air vanished, and the density lifted, and the alcove was just an alcove again — stone and damp and fog and the sound of the harbour and the absence of everything that had been there a moment before.

Zima sat in the quiet. The disc was cool against her ribs. Cooling further. The ghost of the last word hung in her mind — don't — followed by nothing, the sentence abandoned, the warning unfinished. Don't show it. Don't trust someone. Don't go somewhere. She'd never know. The bridge had closed.

*          *          *

She didn't sleep the rest of that night. She sat in the alcove and she let the conversation settle the way silt settles in still water — slowly, layer by layer, the information drifting down through her thinking and arranging itself on the bottom.

Passages. Vault. Four access points. The disc as the only key. Supplies, equipment, coin. Enough for leaving or staying. Weeks, not months, before the city reached its breaking point. And a dead woman's only price: remember me.

The vault. She tried to imagine it — a sealed room, fifty feet below the street, filled with crates and chests and everything Maela had put aside over twenty years. She thought about the access points and matched them to the city she knew. The old docks: Viper-adjacent, risky. The northern wall: garrison territory, physically difficult. The market: too many people. Mercer Lane: quiet, old, a cellar nobody used.

What she'd need. Light — a lantern, or candles, enough for hours underground. Rope — fifty feet, at least, strong enough to hold her weight if the footing was as bad as Maela said. Someone she trusted, to watch the entrance, to come after her if she didn't come back.

Someone she trusted. She ran the list of people she trusted in Ashport and the list was very short and the people on it were all people she trusted for specific, limited things — Dav for fair prices, the silence-coin people for keeping quiet about the alcove. Specific trust. Transactional trust. The kind of trust that held as long as the terms held and collapsed the moment the terms changed.

She needed something different. She needed someone who would stand at the mouth of a hole in the ground and wait while she went down into the dark, and who wouldn't ask too many questions, and who wouldn't sell the information, and who wouldn't panic if she came back carrying things that didn't make sense. She needed Lirra. She needed the baker on Mercer Lane who ran messages through bread and who had, by all accounts, kept three years of secrets without a single one leaking.

And she needed Eldrin. The scholar who could read Tidal Script. Because the disc had markings, and the passages would have markings, and the vault would have markings, and those markings were in a language that one person in Ashport could read, and that person was sitting at a corner table in the Wayfarers Hall with his papers and his hunger and his expertise that nobody else wanted.

Lirra for intelligence. Eldrin for the script. Herself for the rest.

The plan was forming. Not complete — plans were never complete in Ashport, because the city changed too fast and the things you planned around had a habit of moving — but the shape was there. The sequence was there. The steps, laid out in order, one after the next, each one riskier than the last.

She lay on her side in the alcove. The stone was cold. The disc was cool against her ribs, resting, spent, the vessel empty and beginning the slow process of refilling. Above her, the bridge. Beyond the bridge, the fog. Beyond the fog, the city — the walls, the queues, the gangs, the army in the hills, the seventeen thousand people who were living on the surface of a place that had a secret underneath it that only she could reach.

Maela. Not the voice — the woman. A person who'd built a city from bedrock up, who'd dug channels and dressed stone and laid foundations and planned walls and filled a vault and made a disc and set it loose and died, and who'd spent two hundred years in the dark inside a piece of metal, waiting for someone to find it and listen and remember.

Two hundred years. She tried again to hold the number and couldn't. She could hold a week. She could hold a month. She could hold the eleven months of siege, the slow subtraction of everything she'd known. But two hundred years was a chasm, not a number. Two hundred years was ten lifetimes of waiting, or twenty lifetimes, depending on how you counted, and Maela had been aware through all of it — dimly, she'd said, shapes and pressures — but aware. Knowing the vault was sealed. Knowing the disc was circulating. Knowing that hand after hand after hand was failing the test, and the crisis hadn't come yet, and the city was forgetting her one decade at a time.

She closed her eyes. The map was there. Four branches, four access points, the vault at the centre. She knew where to go. She knew how to get there. She knew what she'd find, or she knew what Maela said she'd find, and she had no reason to doubt Maela and one good reason to trust her, which was that the dead queen's only request had been the simplest and saddest thing a person could ask for.

Remember me.

The fog thickened. The night deepened. The harbour moved. The disc was cool and quiet, carrying its dead woman and its map and its patience, resting against the ribs of a pickpocket who slept under a bridge and owed nine silvers and had just been given a key to something that might, if any of this was real, change everything.

She slept. She did not dream. And in the morning, the map was still there, and the disc was warm, and the plan was waiting, and she got up and she went to work because that's what you did in Ashport — you worked, you survived, you carried what you carried, and you waited for the moment when the carrying turned into something else.

The moment was close. She could feel it the way she felt the channels — not with her hands, not with her eyes, but with the sense the disc had opened in her, the sense that had no name, the one that told her what was under her feet and what was coming and how little time she had.

She pulled her coat tight. She walked into the fog. The city waited.
