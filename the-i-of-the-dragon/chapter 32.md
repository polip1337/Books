Chapter 32: The New Transfer

Monday morning the third floor smelled less like coffee and more like the particular ozone sweetness of too many screens running too long in a room without windows.

Zack had spent Thursday afternoon pretending a desk in the middle of the floor belonged to him. Fujita had arrived at five with a password on a sticky note and the expression of a man who had fixed the same problem four hundred times. By Friday Zack could log into the internal dashboard, which mostly meant maps he didn't fully understand yet and incident feeds that used words like *probable Class 2* the way weather reports used *partly cloudy* — accurate enough to plan around, useless if you wanted certainty.

He still didn't have a window desk.

Shiro had not forgotten. Shiro had sent an email that said WINDOW STATUS: PENDING FACILITIES, which was either bureaucracy or Shiro's sense of humor. Zack wasn't sure which hurt more.

The Monday briefing started at eight.

People drifted in with cups and tablets and the loose-jointed tiredness of humans who had either been up all night or were still carrying last night in their shoulders. Zack took a chair near the aisle because Nyx liked to be able to see the door. She sat on his shoulder with her tail curled, warm weight, claws light through his shirt.

Shiro stood at the front beside a screen that currently displayed nothing useful — a map of Tokyo with several colored dots that meant something to someone who wasn't Zack yet.

"Quick item before we go through the overnight," Shiro said. His voice didn't raise. It didn't need to. The room quieted anyway. "New transfer from the New York chapter. He'll be on the Tokyo Bay assessment team with Tanaka's group and — " a glance at Zack, " — Gottjaeger, cooperative status, as schedules allow."

The door opened.

The man who came in wasn't trying to look impressive, which made the impression worse. He was Zack's height within an inch, built lean, dark hair cut short enough to be practical and long enough to suggest someone had once cared about it and then stopped. He wore the same kind of civilian-dark clothing everyone in the room wore when they weren't pretending to be insurance analysts. No uniform. No swagger.

What he had instead was placement. The way he moved to the side of the room without brushing anyone, without hesitating, like he'd already measured distances and decided where he belonged.

Shiro nodded at him. "Marcus Cole. Brand rank. Kinetic affinity. Questions about his schedule go through Tanaka."

Marcus's eyes swept the room once. Efficient. Not unfriendly. Assessing.

They landed on Zack.

Something happened in Zack's head — not a name, not yet, more like hearing a chord from a song you used to know and being unable to place it. Familiar in the way a face in a crowd could be familiar without being *known*.

Marcus's expression didn't change.

That should have been a clue. It didn't land as one. It landed as nothing, the way most things landed before coffee.

Shiro moved on to the overnight feed. Zack tried to listen. Half his attention stayed on the wrong thing, the way your tongue finds a cut you didn't know you had.

When the briefing broke for tasking, people stood and talked in low voices. Zack stayed seated a second longer than he needed to, sorting his notes — he didn't have notes, he had a blank document titled MONDAY — and then stood because sitting there looked stupid.

Marcus was already talking to Tanaka, a Forge-rank woman with a scar at her hairline who Zack had seen twice and still couldn't reliably pick out of a lineup unless she turned her head. She nodded at something Marcus said. Marcus nodded back. The exchange looked practiced on his side and interested on hers.

Zack walked toward the coffee machine because coffee was a reason.

He was halfway there when Marcus stepped into his path — not blocking, exactly, just occupying space in a way that required acknowledgment.

"Zack," Marcus said.

His voice matched the rest of him. Controlled. American in a flat Midwestern way that didn't waste syllables.

"Hey," Zack said. He almost added *welcome* and didn't. Welcome sounded fake in his mouth.

Marcus looked at him another beat longer than the situation needed.

"You look familiar," Zack said, because his mouth sometimes arrived before his brain and because it was true in the useless, blurry way it was true.

Something flickered in Marcus's eyes. Not warmth. Not anger. A quick shutter, like someone closing a file.

"We haven't met," Marcus said.

"Okay," Zack said.

Marcus nodded once, precise, and moved past him toward Tanaka again.

Nyx's claws tightened slightly on Zack's shoulder.

That was rude, she said.

"He said we haven't met."

He said it like you insulted him, she said. By existing.

Zack looked at Marcus's back. "I don't know what that means."

Nyx didn't answer, which meant she didn't know either and hated not knowing.

---

The familiar introduction happened because the Guard's building had rules about familiars in common spaces and Marcus apparently cared about rules in the way people who had lived inside institutions cared about them — not because they loved institutions, but because knowing where the lines were made it easier to decide when to cross them.

They used the third-floor side room where Hashimoto sometimes did instrument calibrations. Smaller. A table. A window that looked at another window.

Marcus stood near the door. On his shoulder sat something that shouldn't have been able to stay on a shoulder.

Kira was the shape of a manta ray if a manta ray had been lifted out of water and told to behave. Flat. Silent. A charcoal-gray expanse with edges that didn't quite resolve when you looked directly at them, like smoke held in form by intention. No eyes in the way Nyx had eyes — more a sense of attention, focused, cold.

Nyx went very still.

Kira didn't move either.

The room didn't fill with sound. It filled with absence. The feeling of two weather systems recognizing each other across a distance too short to be safe.

Marcus's gaze shifted once to Nyx. Once to Zack. Back to whatever internal checklist he was running.

"She doesn't bite," Marcus said.

"She bites," Zack said at the same time Nyx said, I bite.

Marcus's mouth twitched. Not quite humor. "Kira doesn't."

Kira didn't confirm or deny. She simply continued to be there, occupying space without weight, her attention distributed across the room in a way that felt less like looking and more like recording.

Nyx's tail lifted a fraction.

We should leave, she said.

Zack didn't move yet. "Nice to meet you," he said to Marcus, because someone had to say something normal.

Marcus nodded. "Yeah."

They left.

In the hallway Nyx's claws dug in once, hard enough to sting through cloth.

I don't like the manta ray, she said.

Zack waited until they were past a cluster of Forge-ranks arguing quietly about a route assignment. "Why."

Because she's watching everything, Nyx said. Because she's storing it. Because she looks at you the way something looks at a problem it hasn't decided how to solve yet.

"That's dramatic."

I'm dramatic, Nyx said. She's worse. She's quiet drama. The kind that shows up later.

Zack almost laughed. He didn't. The hallway was still the Guard's hallway, and laughing felt like the wrong currency.

---

He didn't see Marcus again until late afternoon, when Zack went looking for Shiro to ask a pointless question about dashboard permissions and found Shiro near the stairwell with Marcus standing too close for it to be accidental.

Not a fight. Not even tension visible from a distance. Just two men in a building full of secrets having a conversation shaped like a private one.

Zack slowed without meaning to.

Shiro saw him. Of course Shiro saw him. Shiro saw everything in a radius unless he decided not to.

"Gottjaeger," Shiro said. "I'll find you in ten."

"Sure," Zack said, because what else was he supposed to say — *sorry, continue your mysterious sidebar*?

He went around the corner and stood there like an idiot, shoulder pressed to cool concrete, Nyx muttering insults about eavesdropping that didn't stop her from listening harder.

Voices carried. Not words. Tone. Shiro's steady. Marcus's flat.

Then Marcus asked something that had the shape of a question you didn't ask in a hallway unless you needed an answer you couldn't get from a file.

Shiro answered.

The answer went on long enough to be a real explanation, not a brush-off.

Marcus didn't interrupt.

When Shiro finished, silence.

Zack waited for another voice. For Marcus to say *alright* or *that's insane* or anything human.

Nothing.

Footsteps. Marcus walking away — not fast, not slow, the pace of someone who had gotten what he came for and didn't need to pretend otherwise.

Shiro appeared at the corner a moment later, coffee in hand, expression neutral in the specific way Shiro's neutral meant *work in progress*.

"Dashboard permissions," Shiro said, as if Zack had been standing there for a legitimate reason.

"I wanted to know if I can see the bay instrument feed," Zack said, which was true enough to count.

"You can," Shiro said. "I'll unlock it tonight."

He walked past. He didn't look back.

Nyx shifted on Zack's shoulder, fur — scales — whatever — prickling in a way that had nothing to do with temperature.

He's not angry, she said quietly. The new one. He's something else.

Zack watched the empty hallway where Marcus had gone.

"What," he said.

I don't know yet, Nyx said. But I don't like it.

"You don't like Kira."

I don't like the whole thing, she said. The way it fits together.

Zack breathed out. His hands were steadier than they should have been. That wasn't comfort. That was the body doing what bodies did when the mind hadn't caught up.

He went back to his desk in the middle of the floor, no window, login screen glowing, Tokyo doing whatever Tokyo did beyond walls he couldn't see through.

On the screen, a dot pulsed over the bay — slow, steady, like a heartbeat translated into data.

Waiting, the dashboard said.

Zack stared at it until the word stopped meaning anything.

Then he closed the laptop and went to find something to eat that wasn't Guard coffee, because some problems didn't have answers yet, and pretending otherwise would have been a different kind of lie.
