Chapter 23: Angles of Approach
They found a room two streets back from the chamber and put a wall between themselves and the glow.
It had been a home. The walls were intact enough to block the wind that moved through the upper level in slow, directionless currents, carrying the ice dust with it. Zack sat on the floor with his back against the far wall, his legs stretched out, and looked at the ceiling for a while without thinking about anything in particular.
Nyx sat beside him rather than on him, her wings folded, her tail curled around her feet. She wasn't watching the door. She wasn't watching him. She was just there, her faint warmth radiating against his arm, her breathing slow and even.
Outside, the crystal pulsed. Three beats. The long silence. Two more.
After a while Zack said, "She was waiting for someone who knew what to do."
Nyx didn't answer immediately. When she did, her voice was quieter than usual. She was waiting for someone to know she'd waited. Those aren't the same thing.
Zack thought about that. "No," he said. "They're not."
Another silence. The dust moved across the floor in slow patterns, following air currents too faint to feel. He watched it trace a spiral near the doorway and then lose itself against the wall.
He thought about Rei. Not about the crystal or the plan or the corruption — just Rei, the specific reality of her: her laugh, her complete inability to be subtle about anything, the way she'd looked when she pulled him and Kaito into her orbit like it was the most natural thing in the world and dared them not to enjoy it. She was lying in a bed in an inn somewhere above the portal he'd come through, and she was running out of time, and the thing that could save her was forty meters away behind a wall of corrupted light and drifting Voidstalkers.
He'd carried the weight of that since the moment Shiro sent him here. He was still carrying it. The difference was that somewhere between the shaft and the antechamber it had stopped feeling like a clock and started feeling like a reason.
He pushed off the floor and stood.
Nyx looked up at him.
"Alright," he said. "Let's figure this out."

He started with what he knew.
The chamber had one confirmed entrance — the collapsed section of the outer wall. He'd seen three Voidstalkers from the alley, but the room was large enough that there could be more. They weren't patrolling. They weren't reacting to him. Whatever the crystal was doing to them, it was keeping them close and keeping them passive, at least until something changed that.
"If I go in loud, everything in that room wakes up," he said, thinking aloud. Nyx had taken up her usual position on his shoulder, her attention sharp now, the stillness of the last few minutes set aside. "I've fought them in worse numbers, but not in a confined space with a corrupted crystal in the middle that we don't understand yet. One wrong move and—"
And we don't know what it does, Nyx finished. The ones near it were moving slower. Slower could mean weaker. Or it could mean they're more connected to it.
"Connected how?"
I don't know. But when something is feeding, you don't want to startle it.
Zack hadn't thought of it that way. He turned it over. The Voidstalkers below had been aggressive, reactive, territorial. The ones in the chamber were none of those things. The crystal was doing something to them — keeping them docile, keeping them close, keeping them generating more of whatever it needed. Startling that system could go badly in ways he couldn't predict.
"So we can't just fight through," he said.
Not straight in. No.
He thought about the layout. The collapsed wall meant the approach was obvious — there was only one way in that he'd seen. That meant anything inside with functioning senses would register movement from that direction. The Voidstalkers hadn't reacted to him watching from the alley, but he'd been still and at distance. Moving through the gap was different.
"The gap in the wall is about four meters wide," he said. "I couldn't go around it without crossing open ground and I'd be visible from inside the whole time." He paused. "But I don't need to go fast. The ones in there weren't tracking. They were drifting."
Nyx's tail flicked. You want to drift.
"I want to move like I'm not a threat. Slow. No aura, no fire. Nothing that changes the temperature of the room." He looked at her. "How much does your shield change the local temperature when you summon it?"
Barely anything. It's not fire.
"And your fire blast?"
That I'd feel. She considered. I can summon shields without you feeling it much. The blast is different.
"Then shields only, and only if we have no choice." He worked through it. "I go in slow. I stay near the wall, not the center. I don't activate anything unless contact is unavoidable."
Nyx was quiet for a moment. And if contact is unavoidable?
"Then we deal with it fast and quiet and hope the ones near the crystal are too absorbed to notice."
That's the optimistic version.
"I know."
Okay. She shifted on his shoulder. What about the crystal itself? We get to it, then what?
This was the part he'd been circling. The soldier's words. The corruption is in the crystal, not of it. The frost on the glass. Nyx hearing something underneath the wrongness — the original frequency, still present, still the thing the Ice Men had built this tower to protect.
"I don't know yet," he said honestly. "We get close enough to look at it. We see what we're actually dealing with before we decide anything. Taking it in its current state—"
Is not an option, Nyx said. I told you that.
"I know. I'm not planning to." He met her eyes. "I trust your read on it. If you say we can't touch it yet, we don't touch it yet."
She held his gaze for a moment. Something shifted in her expression — not softened exactly, more like something that had been slightly braced became slightly less so. Good, she said. Just that.
He looked toward the doorway, the violet-black glow visible at the far end of the street, pulsing against the ceiling. Three beats. The long silence.
"One more thing," he said. "The approach from the alley — there's about twenty meters of open ground between the last piece of cover and the gap in the wall. I'll be visible the whole time."
I'll go first, Nyx said. Low, slow, along the ceiling. I can get above the gap and tell you what's directly inside before you move.
"You'll be inside the room."
Briefly. The ceiling's high enough. Her wings twitched. I'm small and dark and I won't be using anything. I'm less interesting than you are.
He wanted to argue with that and couldn't. She was right. A small black shape moving along a dark ceiling was categorically less alarming than a human-shaped heat source crossing open ground. "Alright," he said. "You scout the inside, signal me when it's clear, and I move."
And if it's not clear?
"Then you get out and we find another way."
Nyx snorted. There is no other way.
"Then we'll find out when we get there."
She was quiet for a moment. Then, with the tone of someone conceding a point they've already decided to concede: It's a reasonable plan.
Coming from her, that was something close to high praise. Zack didn't say so.
He checked his hands out of habit — no flame visible, aura pulled inward and held there, everything quiet. The ember at his core was steady. Present, available, banked. He'd gotten better at holding it that way without losing track of it, keeping the fire ready without letting it announce itself. He couldn't have done this three weeks ago. He wasn't sure exactly when he'd learned it.
"Let's move," he said.
They left the room. The dust shifted in the doorway as they passed through, the fine powder catching the violet-black light from the end of the street and glinting faintly, briefly, before settling again.
The crystal pulsed. Three beats. The pause. Two beats.
Zack moved toward it, Nyx low against his shoulder, both of them quiet.
