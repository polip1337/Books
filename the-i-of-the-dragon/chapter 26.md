Chapter 26: What They Were
Nyx talked for a long time.
She described the crystal's frequency the way someone describes a sound they've been hearing so long they'd stopped noticing it until the room got quiet enough to make it impossible to ignore. The corruption on the surface — loud, chaotic, the wrong notes played at increasing volume. Underneath that, the original thing, which she kept struggling to put into words that translated across the gap between what she sensed and what language was built to carry.
It's not a sound exactly, she said. It's more like... the feeling that a sound left behind. After it stops. The shape of it still in the air.
Zack listened and didn't interrupt.
The corruption is reactive. It responds to things — to heat, to movement, to the Voidstalkers it was generating. The original frequency doesn't respond to any of that. It just continues. It's been continuing this whole time regardless of everything happening around it.
"Like it doesn't know," Zack said.
Like it doesn't need to know. It's not aware the way you and I are aware. It's more fundamental than that. She paused. The Icemen built their whole civilization around it. I think I understand why. It's very — steady. Whatever else is happening, it keeps going.
Zack thought about the dream. The spire in the plaza, the Icemen touching it briefly as they passed, closing their eyes. The deep hum of it spreading outward through the ice. Not worship exactly. Something more practical. An anchor.
He looked up at the crystal, still rotating, the corruption still churning through it.
"Can it be reached," he said. "The original frequency. Can something get to it through all of that."
Nyx was quiet for long enough that he looked at her.
I think so, she said finally. I think that's actually what it's waiting for. Something to reach it. Her tail curled against the platform edge. But not tonight. I need to understand the shape of it better before I try anything. And you need to rest.
He wanted to argue and didn't. His arms were still aching from the fight. His mana reserves felt like a wrung-out cloth. If anything else came through either entrance in the next hour he'd be dealing with it at significantly reduced capacity, which was not how he wanted to deal with things in a room that had just demonstrated what it could generate.
"Alright," he said. "We find somewhere to sleep. Then we come back."

He found them a room further from the chamber than the one they'd used before — deeper into the residential district, a place the crystal's pulse reached as a faint pressure rather than the bone-deep hammering of the chamber itself. The walls were intact. No secondary entrances. He checked it twice before settling.
He was almost asleep when he realized he'd checked it at all.
It wasn't something he'd thought to do in the lower city, at least not automatically. Down there he'd been reactive — finding cover when he needed it, resting when exhaustion forced him. Up here he'd scouted the room, assessed the sight lines, identified the single entrance and positioned himself relative to it. The difference between someone surviving and someone operating.
He didn't know when that had changed.
He closed his eyes. Sleep came faster than he expected.

He woke to Nyx watching him from across the room.
Not from his shoulder or from a perch near the door. She'd positioned herself in the center of the floor, her wings folded, her tail still, her amber eyes fixed on him with an expression he couldn't immediately read.
"What," he said.
Nothing. She looked away. You were still. I was checking.
He sat up, working the stiffness out of his shoulders. The crystal's pulse reached him faintly through the walls, the new pattern Nyx had described — faster at the surface, steady underneath. "How long did I sleep."
Long enough. She stretched, her wings flaring briefly before folding again. I've been listening to it.
"And?"
I think I have the shape of it now. She crossed the room and took her usual position on his shoulder. Her warmth was immediate and familiar, the small weight of her settling him in some way he'd stopped questioning. The original frequency runs in a specific pattern. The corruption is layered on top of it but doesn't change the pattern itself — it just buries it. Like I said. Frost on glass.
"So if the frost comes off—"
The pattern is still there. Still intact. Still doing what it was built to do.
Zack thought about Rei. About what Shiro had said, that the crystal could save her, that it had the capacity to stabilize what the fox demon had done to her spirit energy. If the corruption came off and the crystal was what it had been, that was still true. The original thing was still the thing they'd come for.
"How do we get the frost off," he said.
Nyx was quiet for a moment. I need to try something. And I need you to let me do it without explaining everything first, because I don't fully have the words for it and trying to explain it might make it harder.
He looked at her.
She met his eyes steadily.
"Alright," he said.

They went back to the chamber.
It was quieter than he'd left it. The drifters hadn't returned — whether they'd dissolved entirely or simply dispersed into the tower's lower levels he couldn't tell, but the room was empty except for the crystal and the platform and the corruption still cycling through both. The secondary entrance showed no movement. Both entrances showed no movement.
Zack climbed onto the platform and stood below the crystal. Nyx left his shoulder.
She flew up to the crystal's level and hovered there, her wings beating in a rhythm that was slower than it needed to be for flight, more deliberate. She oriented toward the crystal without touching it, her horns catching the violet-black light and throwing it back in amber and red.
She closed her eyes.
Zack watched.
For a while nothing visible happened. The corruption churned at its usual pace. The crystal rotated. The pulse continued its altered rhythm. Nyx hovered, eyes closed, wings moving in that slow deliberate beat.
Then the pulse changed.
Not dramatically — a fraction, a shift in the interval between beats that Zack felt more than heard. The original frequency underneath, which he'd only been able to perceive as a color through the corruption's surface, strengthened slightly. Not enough to see clearly. Enough to feel the difference.
Nyx's eyes opened.
She came back down to his shoulder without speaking. He waited.
There's a way, she said finally. But I can't do it alone. The corruption is reactive — when I try to reach the original frequency it responds to me, moves to block me. I need something it won't respond to the same way.
"Me," Zack said.
Your fire. She paused. Not as a weapon. The corruption knows fire as a weapon, it's learned to absorb and redirect it. I need it as a frequency. A carrier. She seemed to be choosing words carefully, working through something she'd figured out in the hover but hadn't fully translated yet. When we fight together, our signals overlap. You've felt that.
He had. The moments in combat when her shield timing matched his movement without communication, when his positioning anticipated her approach. The bond doing something beneath the level of thought.
"I've felt it," he said.
If you channel through me — not fire as fire, but the base of it, the resonance underneath — I can use it to reach the original frequency. The corruption is looking for heat as a weapon. It won't read what I'm describing the same way.
Zack turned that over. Channeling through her meant giving her something he'd never given anything — full access to the root of his power, unfiltered, no boundaries maintained on his end. Every instinct he had about controlling his fire was an instinct about exactly that boundary.
"What does that mean for you," he said. "If it goes wrong."
I don't know, she said. Honest, without softening it. But I know what happens if we touch that crystal in its current state. We've been circling that answer since we got here and neither of us wants to say it out loud.
She was right. He'd known it since the dying priest's warning. Since the soldier's frost on glass. Taking the crystal corrupted wasn't an option — not because it would fail to save Rei, though it might, but because carrying that corruption out of the tower would be carrying whatever had destroyed this civilization into everything beyond it.
He looked at the crystal. The original frequency underneath, still patient, still intact.
"Tell me what you need me to do," he said.
Nyx told him.
It was simpler than he expected and harder. She needed him to stop managing his fire — not to release it outward, not to let it flare, but to stop the constant low-level maintenance that kept it organized and directed and shaped into tools. The way you'd stop holding a breath you'd been holding so long you'd forgotten you were holding it.
He'd never done that. He didn't know if he could.
"I'll try," he said.
Don't try, Nyx said. That's the wrong word for it. Just — stop.
He stood on the platform below the crystal and thought about what stopping meant. He thought about the first time Aiko had shown him how to bring the fire to the surface, the breathing exercise in the forest, the warmth moving from his core down his arms. Every technique he'd learned since then was a version of the same thing — organizing that warmth, directing it, giving it shape and purpose.
He let go of the shape.
It was like stepping off a ledge and finding the ground closer than expected. The fire didn't surge or flare. It simply spread, filling in spaces he'd been unconsciously keeping it out of, warm and sourceless and undirected, present without purpose.
Nyx made a sound he'd never heard from her.
Then she flew back up to the crystal, and he felt her working through the bond — not a sensation he could describe in terms of temperature or pressure but something more like being listened to at a level below words, his fire used not as fire but as a tuning fork, something the original frequency could recognize and respond to.
The corruption on the crystal's surface convulsed.
Not gradually. All at once, a violent reactive contraction that made the violet-black currents reverse direction for a moment before slamming back into their pattern — faster now, chaotic, the rhythm that had been sustained for so long suddenly unstable. The crystal's rotation stuttered.
Hold, Nyx said.
He held.
The corruption fought. He could feel it through Nyx, through the bond — reactive and dense and deeply embedded, the frost that had been on the glass long enough to become part of the glass, resisting removal. Nyx pushed against it with something that didn't feel like force, more like a key in a lock, the right shape turning.
The crystal flared.
White-blue light, the original frequency, flooding the chamber from the inside out — not destroying the corruption but displacing it, filling the space it had occupied, the violet-black retreating toward the crystal's surface and then beyond it, dispersing into the chamber's air in thin threads that caught the light briefly and then were gone.
The rotation steadied.
The pulse settled — clean and even, no longer the altered rhythm of something contaminated. Three beats. The silence. Two beats. But different now. The beats themselves were different, fuller, the silence between them not empty but resonant, the shape of sound still in the air.
Nyx descended slowly. She landed on his shoulder and her weight against him felt the same as always — the small familiar pressure, the warmth, the tail settling against his neck. He reached up and put a hand near her without quite touching her, the way you'd hold a hand near a flame to check without committing.
"Are you alright," he said.
Yes, she said. Then, after a moment: That was very loud.
"The corruption?"
The original frequency. When it came back. A pause. It's — a lot. I understand why they built a whole city around it.
Zack looked up at the crystal.
It was clean. The double-pyramid form rotating slowly in the chamber's light, the glow it cast now blue-white rather than violet-black, steady and deep. The platform below it caught the light and threw it back without distortion. The chamber walls, still ice, still pale, looked different in this light — not bruised, not pressured. Just ice.
He reached up toward the crystal.
It came off the platform's air without resistance, settling into his palm, warm and solid, lighter than it looked. The pulse moved through his hand and up his arm and into his chest where it met his own fire and simply coexisted, two different things in the same space without conflict.
He closed his fingers around it.
"Rei," he said. Not to Nyx. Not to anyone in particular. Just the word, standing in the cleaned-out chamber of a tower that had held on long enough for someone to arrive.
Nyx said nothing. She rested her head briefly against the side of his neck — a small, deliberate contact, there and then gone.
He put the crystal in his pack and turned toward the exit.
They had a long way down.
