Chapter 28: What Shiro Knows
He slept for fourteen hours.
He knew this because Kaito told him, with the specific satisfaction of someone who had been awake and tracking the time and felt that this information should be shared. Zack accepted it, accepted also the bowl of rice and miso that Rei's father set in front of him without comment, and ate at the table in the inn's main room while the morning light came through the window at a low angle and lay across the wooden floor in long pale strips.
Nyx ate something small that Rei's father had also provided without being asked, which said something about Rei's father that Zack filed away.
The inn was quiet. Kaito sat across from him with his hands around a cup of tea, his glasses clean, his posture carrying the particular quality of someone who had come through a sustained period of stress and hadn't yet recalibrated to its absence. Aiko was somewhere upstairs. Shiro was somewhere unspecified, which was his preferred location.
Rei was still in bed, awake, improving.
Shiro had administered the crystal the previous night while Zack slept — had taken it from the bedside table and done whatever the Dragon Guard's process involved, and by morning Rei's color was better and the pallor was retreating and she'd eaten breakfast and complained about the quality of the tea, which Kaito reported as the most encouraging sign yet.
Zack finished his rice and set the bowl down and looked at the window.
"How long was I actually gone," he said.
Kaito pushed his glasses up. "Four days. Roughly. Shiro said time in the Void isn't consistent so it's hard to — "
"Four days."
"Give or take."
Zack sat with that. Four days in the tower had felt like two, possibly three, the rhythm of it compressed by the sustained focus required to stay alive in it. Four days on the outside. Rei stable but barely, the crystal still unrecovered, Kaito sitting on the step in the dark waiting.
"You stayed out there," Zack said. "In the courtyard."
Kaito looked at his tea. "Not the whole time."
"But last night."
A pause. "Aiko said you'd come back through the portal. That Shiro had set the coordinates. But I didn't — " He stopped. Started again with more precision, the way he did when he wanted to be accurate rather than diplomatic. "I wanted to be there when you came through. In case."
Zack didn't say anything. Some things didn't need a response so much as acknowledgment, and acknowledgment didn't always require words.
After a moment Kaito said, "Nyx is smaller than I thought a dragon would be."
Tell him size is a matter of perspective, Nyx said from the windowsill, where she'd positioned herself in the morning sun with the focused appreciation of a creature that had spent four days in a frozen tower.
Zack relayed it.
Kaito looked at her. Then, with the careful seriousness he brought to things he found genuinely interesting: "Can she understand everything we say?"
Yes, Nyx said.
"She says yes," Zack said.
Kaito turned this over. "Does she find it strange. Listening to conversations where people don't know she's following."
Nyx tilted her head. Tell him I find it educational.
Kaito received this and nodded slowly, as if it confirmed something he'd already suspected.

Shiro found Zack an hour later, in the small room off the main hall where Rei's father stored equipment. Zack was sitting on a crate checking the state of his jacket — shredded in three places, the sleeve nearly gone on the left arm — when the door opened and Shiro came in and shut it behind him.
He didn't sit. He stood with his back against the door and his arms loose at his sides, the long coat settled around him, his mismatched eyes on Zack with the expression that wasn't performance.
Zack set the jacket down.
"You want to ask me something," he said.
"Several things," Shiro said. "I'll start with the one that matters most." He paused, not for effect — genuinely organizing. "The crystal you brought back. When you found it, what state was it in."
Zack looked at him. "You know what state it was in. That's why you sent me instead of going yourself."
Something shifted in Shiro's expression, too brief to read. "Humor me."
"It was corrupted. Deeply. It had been for a long time — long enough that it was generating Voidstalkers rather than just attracting them. The tower's entire upper section was affected." Zack kept his voice even. "The Ice Men were still fighting on the lower levels. They didn't know. Or they knew and there was nothing left to do about it."
Shiro was quiet.
"I cleansed it," Zack said. "Before I took it. It's clean."
"I know," Shiro said. "I could tell when I used it on Rei. The frequency was intact." Another pause. "What I want to know is how."
Zack looked at him for a moment. Then he looked at the door behind Shiro's shoulder, not because anything was there but because it was easier than looking at Shiro while he decided how much to say.
The Dragon Guard had resources. Knowledge accumulated over centuries of exactly this kind of work. Whatever Shiro knew about Essence Crystals and their corruption — and he clearly knew more than he'd shared before sending Zack through the portal — was information that would have been useful before the tower. The decision to withhold it had been Shiro's.
"Nyx," Zack said.
Shiro's eyebrows moved fractionally. "Your familiar cleansed an Essence Crystal."
"We did it together. The familiar bond — the resonance overlap. She could hear the original frequency underneath the corruption. We used that to reach it."
Shiro said nothing for long enough that Zack counted the silence. Eight seconds. For Shiro, who filled silences as a default behavior, eight seconds was significant.
"That's not something we knew was possible," Shiro said finally.
"I gathered."
"The Guard has encountered corrupted crystals before. The standard protocol is containment and disposal. We've never successfully cleansed one." He said it without inflection, which was its own kind of inflection. "The ones we've contained — there are three in secured storage — have been there for decades."
Zack thought about what that meant. Three crystals, contained, uncleansed. Three civilizations' worth of what he'd seen in the tower — the dust on the floor, the market with its empty stalls, the soldier in the antechamber waiting until waiting was the only thing left.
"They can be cleansed," Zack said. "If you have a dragon with the right sensitivity and a fire-type with enough control to channel without directing."
Shiro looked at him with something that might have been respect and might have been calculation and was probably both. "You'd be willing to share how."
"I'm telling you now."
"In more detail. With the Guard."
Zack leaned back against the wall and crossed his arms and looked at Shiro steadily. "That depends."
"On what."
"On what the Guard does with it." He held Shiro's gaze. "Those Ice Men in the tower — the uncorrupted ones — they were still fighting. They've probably been fighting since before I was born. If there's a way to help them, and the Guard's answer is to log it in a file somewhere—"
"That's not the answer," Shiro said.
"How do I know that."
Shiro was quiet again. Shorter this time. "You don't," he said. "Not for certain. I could make you a number of reassuring promises and you'd have no way to verify them until well after the moment where verification would be useful." He said it flatly, without apology. "What I can tell you is that what you've just described changes the calculus on those three contained crystals significantly. That's not nothing."
It wasn't nothing. Zack had already decided that before Shiro said it, which was annoying.
"The formal offer's coming," Zack said. It wasn't a question.
"This evening," Shiro said. "When Rei is well enough to be present. She'll want to hear it."
"She's not part of this."
"She is now," Shiro said, without particular weight. Just fact. "She saw what happened at the inn. The ice dagger Aiko conjured. The Dragon Guard arriving. She was unconscious for most of it but she was there for the parts that mattered, and she's been asking questions since she woke up." He paused. "She's going to be difficult to contain."
"Don't try to contain her," Zack said. "It doesn't work and she'll hold it against you."
Something at the corner of Shiro's mouth. "Noted."
He moved away from the door, which in the small room meant he moved toward the center of it, which made the space feel considerably more occupied. Up close his mismatched eyes were more apparent — the gold one and the dark gray one, both watching Zack with the same focused attention.
"The Voidstalker activity in Tokyo has increased," he said. "While you were in the tower. Not dramatically — no single incident large enough to compromise concealment — but the frequency has gone up by roughly thirty percent over four days."
Zack felt the number land. "That's fast."
"Yes." Shiro's voice was careful. "The current hypothesis within the Guard is escalation — that what we've been seeing in Tokyo is the leading edge of something larger. The Void is thinning in more places than it was a year ago. The crystals in storage are showing increased resonance activity, even contained." He paused. "And there are reports from other cities. Jakarta. São Paulo. Nairobi. Different types, different behaviors. But more of them."
Zack thought about Aiko's training in the forest. The wooden dummies, the breathing exercises, the spar that had felt enormous at the time and now felt like the first rung of a very long shaft. He thought about the bull in Shibuya and the raptor at the university and the fox at the inn, each one different, each one a variation on the same basic wrongness.
He thought about what the soldier in the antechamber had called the corruption. The thing that had reached the crystal from below. The thing she hadn't had a name for, had only described with a gesture that Nyx had translated as the thing that was hungry before anything else existed.
Zack, Nyx said.
She was on the windowsill in the next room but the bond carried over walls. He felt what she was feeling before she gave it words — the specific stillness of something listening that has just heard what it was waiting for.
I can feel something, she said. Below. Very far below. Deep under the city.
He kept his face still and kept his eyes on Shiro.
"How far does your detection range?" he asked.
Shiro's expression shifted slightly. "Depends on the type. Short-range within a district, mid-range with the tablet. Why."
"Nyx is sensing something," Zack said. "Now. Beneath Tokyo."
Shiro reached into his coat and pulled out the tablet — not the consumer device it resembled but the Dragon Guard version, its screen alive with readings Zack didn't know how to parse. He watched Shiro's face instead. Watched the careful practiced neutrality fracture at the edges as he read the screen.
"That's not possible," Shiro said quietly. To himself, not to Zack.
"What isn't."
Shiro looked up. The calculation in his expression had shifted into something that was not quite alarm — too composed for that — but adjacent to it. "The readings under the bay have been stable for six months. We check them every week. Last check was two days ago." He looked at the tablet again. "This is — significantly not stable."
It moved, Nyx said. Whatever it is. It was somewhere else and now it's here. I felt it arrive.
Zack relayed it.
Shiro stared at him. Something passed through his expression that Zack couldn't name — not fear, not surprise exactly, more like the look of someone who has been building a model of a situation for years and has just received data that the model was missing a variable.
He pocketed the tablet.
"I need to make some calls," he said.
"Go," Zack said.
Shiro moved to the door and opened it, then paused with his hand on the frame. He didn't turn around. "You asked earlier how you could know what the Guard would do with the information you brought back." A pause. "You're going to keep asking that question. It's the right question to keep asking." Another pause. "I can't tell you the answer. But the fact that you're asking it is why I think this works."
He left without elaborating. The door swung closed behind him.
Zack sat in the small room with the shredded jacket on his knees and the morning light coming through the gap under the door and Nyx's attention threading through the bond from the next room, still fixed on whatever she'd felt moving beneath the city.
He thought about the soldier's words. The thing that was hungry before anything else existed. The Void thinning in Jakarta and São Paulo and Nairobi. Thirty percent increase in four days.
He thought about a shaft going up through the dark and what it meant to find the next rung.
He put on the shredded jacket and went to check on Rei.
