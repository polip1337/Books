Chapter 27: The Long Way Down
The shaft was worse going down.
He hadn't thought about that on the way up — the climb had consumed enough attention that the return trip hadn't existed yet as a problem. Now it did. The rungs were the same ancient ice brackets jutting from the wall, the gaps in the sequence the same gaps he'd navigated on the way up, but descending changed the geometry of everything. Going up, a missed grip meant falling back to the last rung. Going down, a missed grip meant falling through all the ones below.
His arms were also significantly worse than they'd been twelve hours ago.
Nyx flew in slow circles below him, her wings beating in the shaft's cold air, her eyes tracking his position. She wasn't commenting. He appreciated that.
He found his rhythm and kept it. Hand, check, foot, check, lower, repeat. The aura-made handholds he'd burned into the wall on the ascent were still there — shallow depressions in the ice, shaped to his grip, slightly discolored from the heat. He used them again on the sections where rungs were missing, the familiarity of his own handprints steadying.
His shoulders burned by the halfway mark.
Rest, Nyx said, when he paused on a section of intact scaffolding to catch his breath.
"I'm fine."
You're shaking.
He looked at his hands. She wasn't wrong. The tremor was fine and constant, the kind that comes not from a single effort but from accumulated hours of effort with insufficient recovery between them. He sat on the scaffolding's narrow platform and let his arms hang.
The crystal's weight in his pack was real and present. He was aware of it the way you're aware of something fragile you're carrying — not paranoid, just conscious, the knowledge of it shaping how he moved. The pulse came through the pack's material faintly, the clean frequency Nyx had restored, three beats and the resonant silence. It didn't feel like the tower anymore. It felt like something that had been retrieved.
He thought about the Ice Men.
Not the ones he'd fought, not the corrupted ones, not the ones whose faces he'd seen in the moment before his fire hit them. The ones from the dream. The ones in the plaza touching the spire and closing their eyes, the craftsman whose tools still sat on the table in the hut below, the soldier who had waited in her sealed room until waiting became the only thing left to do.
He'd taken the thing they'd built their civilization around. He'd cleaned it and he'd taken it and he was carrying it out of the tower in a backpack and there wasn't a version of that which felt uncomplicated.
He didn't try to make it uncomplicated. He sat with it for the duration of the rest and then stood up and kept climbing down.

The temple level was quiet.
He came through the shaft's base and into the hollow column and from there into the rear of the hall, and it was the same as he'd left it — the altar, the pillars, the ice dust in its slow currents across the floor, the double doors still ajar at the far end. No new bodies. No movement.
The dying priest's alcove was empty. Of course it was. The fine powder of what had been the soldier lay in a settled drift near the antechamber entrance two levels up. He didn't think about the math of how many drifts of powder he'd walked through on the way down.
He crossed the temple floor and pushed through the double doors into the second-level city.
The market plaza was as they'd left it. The stalls, the basins, the small carved bone vertebrae in their row. He didn't stop. He moved through it at pace, through the narrowing streets toward the first-level gate, Nyx silent on his shoulder.
He heard the fighting before he saw it.
The same sounds from before — the crack of ice, the keening of Voidstalkers, the deep resonant reports of Ice Men fighting in concert. It was coming from the direction of the main gate, the entrance they'd originally come through. He slowed and moved to the edge of the nearest building, checked the street beyond.
A group of Ice Men held a barricade of stacked ice slabs across the widest street on this level. Eight of them, their fissures pulsing with the pale corrupted blue he now recognized as distinct from the clean glow of unaffected ones. Six Voidstalkers pressed against them from the other side, tendrils lashing, the jellyfish-forms flickering in and out of coherence as the Ice Men's ice-spear barrages hit and hit again.
The Voidstalkers were losing. Slowly, but losing.
He watched for a moment. The Ice Men's movements were precise, coordinated in the way he'd seen them coordinate before — the dream showing him what that had looked like at full strength, this showing him what it looked like diminished and corrupted, the original unity still present underneath the damage done to it. Still functional. Still holding.
He could go around. The streets on the level's far edge would take longer but avoid the fight entirely.
Go around, Nyx said.
He was already moving.

The first-level streets felt lower-ceilinged than he remembered, though nothing had changed. The knowledge of what was above him — the temple, the residential district, the chamber, the empty platform where the crystal had been — made the distance between this floor and the tower's heart feel more substantial. He'd been up there. He was coming down. The tower's geography had become personal in a way it hadn't been when he was climbing into the unknown.
He found the inner gate and went through it into the kill box.
The Jellyfish corpses were still there. Hundreds of them, strewn across the ice-spiked interior in various states of dissolution. The Ice Men had held here. He crossed the field of them without looking too closely and reached the outer gate, the ruined entrance where he'd first come through, the twisted metal frames and the pale blue light spilling through.
He stopped at the threshold.
The landscape of the Void beyond was unchanged — the frozen rain, the suspended droplets, the distance. Somewhere ahead, the portal Shiro had sent him through. Somewhere on the other side of that portal, an inn on a Japanese mountainside, and Rei, and Kaito and Aiko, and a situation that had been running without him for however long he'd been in the tower.
He didn't know how long that was. Time in the Void moved oddly and he'd stopped tracking it somewhere around the second rest in the craftsman's hut.
Long enough, Nyx said, reading the shape of the thought if not the words. But not too long. She'll still be there.
He didn't ask how she knew. She probably didn't. It was the right thing to say and she'd said it, which was a different kind of knowing.
He stepped through the gate into the frozen rain.

The walk back was nothing like the walk in.
On the way in he'd been alone and disoriented and the landscape had been a series of threats to navigate. Now he had Nyx, he had the crystal pulsing steadily in his pack, and he had a rough sense of the terrain from having already crossed it. The Jellyfish field, the crumbling Void-edge, the cave where Nyx had hatched, the regions of denser rain that marked the island's center.
He moved with direction and the distance felt shorter.
The frozen rain slowed him — it always had, the physical resistance of pushing through suspended droplets, the care required near the field where the dormant creatures lay half-buried. Nyx guided him through the same path she'd found before, her movements confident now, her sense of the terrain established.
He came through the field's far edge and found the portal where Shiro had opened it, a faint shimmer in the air above a section of flat ground, the runes still faintly active at its edges.
He looked at it for a moment.
Then he put his hand through and tore it open.
The portal resisted briefly — it had been sitting half-closed, the mechanism Shiro used decaying at the edges without maintenance — and then gave, the shimmer expanding into the doorway shape he recognized. Through it: dim light, wooden floors, the smell of wood smoke and cold mountain air.
He stepped through.

He came out in the inn's courtyard.
Not the room — the courtyard, the open space between the main building and the tree line, the same ground he'd crossed with the others what felt like a different lifetime ago. The cold was Japanese mountain cold, which was cold in an ordinary way, which after the Void felt almost warm.
Kaito was sitting on the courtyard's wooden step.
He had his knees pulled up to his chest and his glasses slightly fogged from his own breath, and he was staring at the middle distance with the expression of someone who has been sitting in the same spot for long enough that sitting has become its own kind of activity. When Zack came through the portal he startled, his glasses nearly going, and then he was on his feet.
He didn't say anything for a moment. He just looked at Zack.
Then: "You look terrible."
"Thanks."
"How long—" Kaito stopped. Started again. "We weren't sure when. Shiro said the Void runs differently. Aiko's been—" He stopped again. "Are you hurt?"
"Not seriously."
Kaito's eyes went to his shoulder, where Nyx sat watching the exchange with her tail curled neatly. Something shifted in Kaito's face — relief, and something else underneath it. He'd known about the egg. He'd been there for the whole of it. Seeing the dragon that had come out of it, small and black and watching him with amber eyes, clearly meant something.
"She hatched," he said.
"She did," Zack said.
He's smaller than I expected, Nyx said, only to Zack.
Be nice, Zack said back.
I'm always nice.
Kaito pushed his glasses up. "Rei's awake," he said.
Zack went still.
"Since yesterday. She's — not recovered, not fully, but she's awake. She's been asking about you." Kaito's voice was steady in the way of someone who had practiced steadiness through a period that had required a lot of it. "Aiko's with her. Shiro comes and goes."
Zack felt something in his chest unknot that had been knotted since the moment Shiro had sent him through the portal. Not resolve — that had never wavered — but something held against the possibility that he'd arrive too late. It released now, quietly, in the cold of a Japanese mountain courtyard with Kaito standing in front of him and Nyx warm on his shoulder.
"Take me to her," he said.

The room was small and warm from a portable heater someone had brought in. Rei was propped against the headboard with her arm in a proper sling — someone had found medical supplies — and she looked diminished in the specific way of people who have been very ill and are now on the other side of it. Her blue hair was loose, her face still carrying some of the pallor from the coma, her eyes tracking the door as it opened.
When she saw him she didn't say anything immediately. She looked at him the way you look at someone you'd started to make peace with not seeing again.
"Hey," Zack said.
"You look terrible," Rei said.
"Kaito said that already."
"Kaito's right." She shifted against the headboard, wincing slightly. "Is that a dragon on your shoulder."
"Her name is Nyx."
Rei looked at Nyx. Nyx looked at Rei with the particular evaluating attention she gave to everything she hadn't categorized yet.
I like her, Nyx said, after a moment.
Zack relayed it.
Rei's expression did something complicated and then resolved into something that was mostly her usual face, which was the best indicator of genuine feeling she had. "Tell her I'd like her better if she came with a less dramatic origin story." She looked at Zack. "Sit down before you fall down."
He sat in the chair beside the bed. The pack went on the floor, the crystal's pulse faint through the material, three beats and the resonant silence.
Aiko was in the corner, standing rather than sitting, her silver hair loose. She looked at him for a long moment and then looked at the pack on the floor. He nodded once. She closed her eyes briefly.
From the doorway, Shiro watched.
His expression was the one Zack was beginning to understand was his actual expression — not the performative amusement he deployed like a tool, but something more considered underneath it. His mismatched eyes moved between Zack and the pack and back to Zack. He said nothing.
Zack reached into the pack and set the crystal on the bedside table.
It sat there in the room's warm light and pulsed — clean, steady, the frequency Nyx had restored, three beats and the resonant silence. The light it cast was blue-white and it fell across Rei's face and the headboard and the wall beyond without distortion.
Rei looked at it. "That's what you went to get."
"That's what I went to get."
She looked at it for another moment. Then she looked at him. "Was it bad."
He thought about the honest answer. About the Ice Men and the dust and the chamber and the large Voidstalker and Nyx hovering with her eyes closed reaching for something she didn't have words for. About the soldier in the antechamber who had stopped expecting anyone to come.
"Yes," he said.
Rei nodded. She didn't push for details. She had the good sense to know when details could wait.
"Okay," she said. "Thank you."
Zack leaned back in the chair and felt the accumulated weight of the tower settle into his bones now that there was nothing left to carry it toward. Nyx shifted on his shoulder and curved her tail against his neck, the small familiar warmth of her, and he let his eyes close for just a moment.
Just a moment.
He was asleep before Rei could tell him he still looked terrible.
