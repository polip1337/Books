Chapter 31: Ember
The Dragon Guard's Japan Chapter operated out of a building in Shinjuku that looked, from the outside, like an insurance company.
This was apparently intentional. Zack stood on the pavement looking up at it — eight floors, glass and grey concrete, a logo by the entrance that said HINOMARU RISK CONSULTING in the kind of font that communicated reliability without trying too hard — and thought that if you were going to hide a secret organization that fought monsters, an insurance company was probably the right cover. Both dealt in catastrophe. Both kept meticulous records of things going wrong.
It smells like paper, Nyx said from his shoulder. And old coffee.
"Most offices do."
I don't like it.
"You've never been in an office."
That's how I know I don't like it.
Shiro had told him to come on Thursday. It was Thursday. Zack pushed through the glass door.
The ground floor was a functional insurance office — receptionist, waiting area, a rack of brochures about flood coverage and business liability. The receptionist looked up when he came in, looked at Nyx on his shoulder, and picked up a phone without changing expression.
"Mr. Gottjaeger for registration," she said, and buzzed him through a door that looked like it led to a supply closet.
It led to a stairwell. The stairwell led to the third floor, which was where the insurance company stopped and something else started. The walls here were the same grey concrete but covered in screens showing feeds Zack didn't have context for — map overlays, data columns, a handful of people in civilian clothes moving between desks with the focused quiet of people who had too much to do. Nobody looked up when he came through.
Shiro was waiting by the far wall, holding two cups of coffee, looking like a man who had been awake since before it was reasonable to be awake and had made his peace with it.
He handed Zack one of the cups.
"Registration takes about two hours," Shiro said. "Most of it is paperwork. Some of it is not paperwork. Try to answer the assessors' questions accurately."
"What happens if I don't."
"The assessment comes back inconclusive and we do it again next week." He paused. "Nobody wants to do it again next week."

The paperwork portion took forty minutes and covered, in order: personal details, next of kin — Zack wrote his parents' names and felt briefly strange about it — medical history, a twelve-page document about the Guard's confidentiality requirements that a man named Fujita walked him through with the energy of someone who had explained it several hundred times and had stopped finding it interesting around the two hundredth. Zack signed where indicated. Nyx read over his shoulder and occasionally said things like that clause is quite broad in a tone that suggested she had opinions about contract law.
He didn't know where she'd learned about contract law. He'd stopped asking where she learned things.
The combat assessment was in the basement, which required another stairwell and a badge that Fujita clipped to Zack's jacket with the practiced efficiency of a man who clipped badges to jackets for a living. The basement was a long room with reinforced walls and the smell of scorched concrete and a drain in the center of the floor that Zack tried not to think too hard about.
Two assessors were waiting. Both Forge-rank, according to their badges, which was the first time Zack had seen the ranking system displayed on anything physical. One was a woman in her thirties named Oda who carried a tablet and had the manner of a doctor conducting a physical — professionally interested, personally uninvested. The other was a younger man named Hashimoto who kept looking at Nyx with barely concealed excitement and who Oda had already told to focus twice before Zack had been in the room ninety seconds.
"We'll start with output measurement," Oda said. "Standard aura display, please. Both active and passive if you have them."
Zack called up his Scorching Aura. The temperature in the room shifted, the heat rolling outward in a steady wave. Oda's tablet registered something. Her expression didn't change.
"And the active skill."
He brought up Armor of Flame. The fire wrapped his forearms, the blades forming at his knuckles, the familiar weight of it settling into his hands.
Hashimoto made a sound.
"Hashimoto," Oda said.
"Sorry," Hashimoto said. He didn't look sorry. He looked like a person watching something he'd read about and had not quite believed until now.
Oda made notes. Zack held the skills and waited.
"You can release," Oda said. She turned to Hashimoto. "Familiar assessment."
Hashimoto approached Nyx with the focused enthusiasm of someone who had been waiting for this specific task. He held up a small instrument — a flat disc, glass and metal, that Zack didn't recognize — and said, "May I?"
Nyx looked at the instrument. Then at Hashimoto. Then back at the instrument.
He's very earnest, she said to Zack.
Be nice, Zack said.
I'm always nice, she said, and held still while Hashimoto passed the disc slowly along her wingspan.
The disc made a sound. Hashimoto looked at it. He made a note. He passed it along her wingspan again. The disc made a different sound. He looked at Oda.
"It's doing the thing again," he said.
"Note it as anomalous," Oda said, without looking up from her tablet.
"I noted it anomalous last time."
"Note it anomalous again."
Hashimoto noted it anomalous. Nyx watched this process with her amber eyes and the expression she used when she found something genuinely interesting and was deciding whether to admit it.
The live-fire scenario came last. A contained Class 1 Voidstalker, released from a holding cell at the room's far end — smaller than the ones Zack had fought in the tower, its movements disorganized, the gray eyes tracking without the intelligence the larger types had. It saw him and came forward in the way they always came forward, the instinct toward spirit energy overriding everything else.
Zack put it down in eleven seconds.
He knew it was eleven seconds because Oda said, afterward, "Eleven seconds," in the tone of someone reading a measurement they hadn't expected.
"Is that good?" Zack asked.
"It's the fastest recorded scenario completion in this facility," Oda said. She made a note. "You used three distinct techniques in sequence without a recovery interval between the second and third."
"The second one left it off-balance. The third was easier because of it."
"Yes," she said. "That's observable." She made another note. "Mr. Gottjaeger, when the scenario began, did you assess the target's movement pattern before engaging?"
"Briefly."
"How briefly."
"A second. Maybe less."
"And your engagement sequencing — was that planned before the scenario started or determined in the moment?"
Zack looked at her. "In the moment."
"Entirely."
"Yes."
Oda made a note that took longer than the previous ones. Hashimoto, standing slightly behind her, was looking at Nyx again. Nyx was looking back at him with the patient attention she gave to things she'd decided were harmless.
"Thank you," Oda said. "We'll have your assessment result within the hour."

The result took forty minutes. Zack spent them in a waiting area on the third floor with bad coffee and a view of the operations screens and Nyx on the arm of his chair, systematically investigating the structural integrity of the chair's upholstery with one claw.
Stop that, he said.
I'm bored, she said, but stopped.
Shiro appeared at the forty-minute mark with a lanyard and a small laminated card. He held it out. Zack took it.
The card had his photo — taken by Fujita during the paperwork stage, badly lit, Zack looking slightly to the left of the camera — his name, a series of numbers he didn't yet know how to parse, and a single word printed in red beneath his name.
EMBER
He looked at it for a moment. Shiro watched him look at it.
"Ember," Zack said.
"Ember," Shiro confirmed.
"That's the lowest one."
"It is."
Zack turned the card over. The back had the Guard's logo and a QR code and nothing else. He turned it back over. EMBER was still there, printed in red, beneath his name and above a headshot of himself looking at the wrong part of the camera.
"The assessment noted your output as significantly above Ember-level," Shiro said. "It also noted that your engagement methodology showed no evidence of protocol adherence, structured communication, or chain-of-command awareness during the live-fire scenario."
"There was no chain of command. It was just me."
"The scenario parameters include an implicit chain of command."
"The implicit chain of command didn't help me put it down in eleven seconds."
"No," Shiro said. "It didn't." He paused. "The ranking system measures demonstrated operational discipline alongside raw capability. Your raw capability is not what's holding you at Ember."
Zack looked at the card again. He thought about the tower — four days in a collapsing Void-island fighting things well above anything this facility had in its holding cells, cleansing a corrupted Essence Crystal that the Guard's best had considered uncleansable, carrying the result back through a portal in his backpack.
He thought about Tanaka, whose name he didn't yet know, three weeks in the future, and how the answer to that particular problem was also going to be complicated. He didn't know about Tanaka yet. He was just standing in a Guard facility in Shinjuku with an Ember ID and a bad coffee.
He put the card in his pocket.
"Alright," he said.
Shiro looked at him. The look was assessing in the way Shiro's looks were always assessing, measuring something Zack couldn't fully see. "That's it?"
"What else is there." Zack picked up the coffee. It was genuinely bad. "I know what I can do. The card says something different. One of those things will change." He drank the coffee. "Where's the Brand assessment?"
Something at the corner of Shiro's mouth. Not quite a smile. "You have to be registered for thirty days before you can attempt Brand."
"Why."
"Because thirty days ago you didn't exist in our system."
Zack accepted this. It was the kind of rule that existed for reasons that made sense from a certain angle and were simply the rule from every other angle. "Fine. Thirty days."
Three hundred and sixty hours, Nyx said helpfully.
"Thank you, Nyx."
Twenty-one thousand six hundred minutes.
"I know how time works."
I'm just saying it's a long time to be an Ember.
"I know it's a long time."
You'll have to put Ember on forms.
"Nyx."
Every form. Ember. Right there in the field where it asks your rank.
"Nyx."
She curled her tail around his shoulder and said nothing more, which was its own form of commentary.
Shiro watched this exchange with the expression he used when he was choosing not to react to something. "There's a desk assigned to you on the fourth floor," he said. "Operations briefings are Monday and Thursday at eight. Bay situation update is Tuesday at nine, attendance requested."
"Requested."
"You're cooperative status, not staff. Requested." He paused. "But I'd strongly suggest attending."
Zack nodded. He looked around the third floor — the screens, the quiet movement of people between desks, the machinery of an organization that had been doing this for longer than he'd been alive and had its own ways of doing it and its own sense of what the words effective and correct meant.
He was the most dangerous Ember in the Guard's recorded history, apparently.
He had thirty days before he could take the next assessment.
"Is the desk near a window?" he asked.
Shiro looked at him.
"The fourth floor desk," Zack said. "Is it near a window."
"I have no idea," Shiro said. "Why does that matter."
"Nyx likes to watch things."
I do, Nyx confirmed.
Shiro looked at Nyx. Nyx looked at Shiro with the direct amber-eyed attention she gave to things she'd decided merited evaluation.
"I'll see what I can arrange," Shiro said, and went back to whatever it was Shiro did when he wasn't managing other people's assessments.
Zack took the stairs to the fourth floor. The desk wasn't near a window. It was in the middle of the room, between a support pillar and a shelf of binders, facing a wall.
He sat down in the chair. It was a reasonable chair. The desk had a lamp that worked and a keyboard connected to a monitor showing a login screen he didn't have credentials for yet. On the surface, someone had left a sticky note that said WELCOME / IT will sort your access Thursday PM / — Fujita.
Nyx examined the desk surface thoroughly and found nothing worth investigating. She settled on the lamp instead, which was warm.
Ember, she said, not unkindly.
"Ember," he agreed.
He looked at the login screen. Outside, through the window that someone else's desk was near, Tokyo was doing what Tokyo did. Twenty-one thousand six hundred minutes until he could try for Brand.
He found a pen in the desk drawer and put it in his pocket, because it seemed useful to have a pen, and waited for IT to sort his access.
