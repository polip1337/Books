Chapter 22: What the Ice Remembers
The antechamber door hadn't been forced.
That was the first thing Zack noticed. Every other sealed space he'd found in the tower had shown some sign of violence — gates bent inward, walls cracked from impact, barricades smashed and abandoned. This door was intact. The ice around its frame was unbroken, the surface smooth except for a single groove worn into the wall beside it, at roughly shoulder height for an Iceman. The kind of groove that forms when something rests its hand in the same spot, repeatedly, over a very long time.
It had been locked from the inside.
Nyx landed on the frame above it, studying the seal. Deliberate, she said. Not hiding. Waiting.
Zack pressed his palm flat against the door. The ice was colder than the surrounding walls — denser, as if it had been reinforced from within. He pushed.
It held for a moment, then gave, sliding inward with a sound like a held breath releasing.
The room beyond was small. Four walls, a low ceiling, no furniture. An alcove cut into the far wall, the same kind he'd seen in the craftsman's hut — made for an Iceman to sit in, to rest, to think. The Iceman in it wasn't moving.
Zack stood in the doorway and looked at it for a moment before stepping inside.
It was smaller than the others he'd encountered. Not a guard — the proportions were wrong, the build lighter, the crystalline plates of its body arranged differently, layered in a way that suggested function other than combat. A robe of sorts covered most of it, the same silk-like material he'd seen on the dying priest in the temple below. An oval of polished stone clasped it at the shoulder, identical to the one from the craftsman's market.
No corrupted veins. No violet-black threading through its fissures. The glow from its cracks was the palest blue Zack had seen in anything still living, barely a suggestion of light, like the last coals of a fire that had been burning for too long.
He crossed the room and crouched in front of the alcove.
The Iceman didn't move.
Its head was bowed, arms resting across its knees, posture folded inward. Not collapsed — deliberate. It had arranged itself this way. Whatever it had been doing in here, it had been doing it for long enough that the position had become the position, the only one that made sense anymore.
Zack waited.
Nyx settled onto his shoulder without a word.
After a while — long enough that Zack had started to wonder if it was already gone, if the faint glow was just residual — the Iceman's head shifted. Not quickly. The movement was so slow it took him a moment to register it was happening at all, the head turning by degrees, the featureless face orienting toward him the way a plant orients toward light it had stopped expecting.
The fissures pulsed. Once. The tinkling sound came — softer than any he'd heard, the chiming of something worn almost to nothing — and Zack understood it as words without knowing how.
You came.
Not a question. Not quite a statement either. Something between the two, something that hadn't decided yet what it was.
Zack didn't answer immediately. He stayed crouched, keeping himself at the Iceman's level. "We're looking for the crystal," he said finally. "We came up through the column. From the temple."
The fissures pulsed again, slow and irregular. The tinkling took longer to resolve into meaning this time.
The temple. A pause. The temple still stands.
"It does."
Another long silence. The Iceman's head turned slightly further, as if taking him in more fully. Its gaze — if it could be called that, the featureless face giving nothing away — seemed to settle on Nyx.
Small-wing, it said. The same word the dying priest had used. Something in the tone was different though. Softer.
Nyx's tail tightened around Zack's neck, just slightly. She said nothing.
The Iceman was quiet for so long after that that Zack thought the exchange might be finished. He was still trying to decide whether to speak again when the tinkling came back, fragmentary, effortful.
We thought... someone would come. In the beginning. We thought... the lower gates would hold. That help would come from outside. The light in its fissures wavered, dipped, steadied. Then we thought... perhaps not from outside. Perhaps from within. Someone who reached the crystal first... who could stop it before— It paused. We kept thinking this. For a long time we kept thinking this.
Zack felt the weight of that land in his chest and stay there. He didn't try to respond to it.
Then we stopped thinking it, the Iceman said. It is strange. When you stop expecting a thing, you do not notice the moment it happens. You simply... continue. And then one day the door opens and you have to remember what expecting felt like.
Nyx made a small sound. Not words. Just sound.
The Iceman's fissures pulsed again, the light strengthening slightly, as if the conversation itself was sustaining it. You are warm, it said. Flesh-warm. Fire within. Its head tilted toward Zack. You fought your way up.
"Yes."
How many of mine did you kill.
The question was flat. Not accusatory — it had moved past accusation a long time ago. It simply wanted to know.
Zack held its gaze. "A lot."
They would not have known what they were doing, the Iceman said. The corruption takes the ones with the weakest connection to the crystal first. The outer guards. The patrols. It moves inward. Another pause. They were not themselves.
"I know," Zack said. He did know. He'd known it since the dream, since standing in the plaza watching the city as it had been, since the first Ice Man he'd brought down after understanding what this place was. Knowing hadn't made it easier. It had made it harder and he'd done it anyway and he was still doing it and at some point you had to decide what that meant about you and he hadn't finished deciding yet.
He didn't say any of that. It didn't seem like the right time.
The crystal, the Iceman said eventually. You need it.
"Someone I care about is dying. Without it, she doesn't survive."
The fissures pulsed in a long, slow rhythm. It is not what it was, the Iceman said. You know this already. You spoke with the one in the temple.
"He warned me. He didn't tell me what to do about it."
No. Something in the tinkling shifted — not quite sadness, not quite irony. He would not have known. We did not know, at the end. We thought if we could hold long enough... if we could keep the crystal safe long enough... the corruption would stop spreading on its own. That something would change. A pause. Nothing changed. Things that are rotting do not stop rotting because you guard them carefully.
Zack leaned forward slightly. "Is there a way to fix it?"
The Iceman was quiet for a long time.
We did not find one, it said at last. But we were many, and tired, and dying. You are few. And you are here, which means you are either very capable or very fortunate. Its head tilted again. The small-wing hears the crystal.
It wasn't a question. Zack glanced at Nyx.
She was very still on his shoulder, her amber eyes fixed on the Iceman. "I can hear something wrong with it," she said. "And something underneath the wrong. Something that's still the original thing."
Yes. The fissures brightened, fractionally. That is the shape of it. The corruption is in the crystal, not of it. Like frost on glass — it changes what you see, not what the glass is. If the frost could be removed—
It stopped.
The light in its fissures dipped sharply. Zack watched the pulse slow, irregular now, the intervals between beats lengthening.
"Stay with me," he said.
The Iceman's head lowered slightly. Not in acknowledgment — in the involuntary way of something that was running out of the thing keeping it upright. We waited, it said, very quietly. I am glad... the door opened.
The tinkling faded. The fissures dimmed to almost nothing, a last filament of blue light threading through the cracks in its form.
Then it was gone. The same way the priest had gone, the same way all of them went — not violently, not with sound. The form simply came apart, slowly, dissolving into the fine ice dust that coated everything on this level, and Zack understood with a certainty that settled into his bones that the dust wasn't just dust.
He stayed crouched in front of the empty alcove for a moment.
Nyx didn't say anything. She didn't make a joke or redirect him or tell him to get moving. She just stayed on his shoulder, her warmth a small steady thing against the cold, and let the silence be what it was.
After a while, Zack stood.
The frost on the glass, Nyx said quietly.
"Yeah."
It's not nothing. Her tail shifted against his neck. It's not an answer. But it's not nothing.
Zack looked at the fine powder settling in the alcove. Then he turned and walked back toward the door, ducking through it into the corridor beyond. The violet-black glow pulsed at the far end of the level, three beats, the long silence, two more.
He knew what was in that chamber. He knew what it had done to this place and the people who'd built it. He knew it was still spreading, still calling, still humming the wrong notes so long it had forgotten the right ones.
And somewhere underneath all of that, according to Nyx, the original thing was still there.
Frost on glass.
He started walking toward the glow.
