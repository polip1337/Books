---

The *Graceful Ruin* was exactly where the contract said it would be.

That was the first thing that felt wrong.

The K-302 outer system was the kind of place that inspired the word *desolate* without requiring any exaggeration. The red dwarf at its center threw light like a dying fire — ruddy, thin, traveling a long way to accomplish very little. A sparse asteroid field occupied the middle orbital band, the remnants of something that hadn't quite managed to become a planet a few billion years ago and had been quietly embarrassed about it ever since. Beyond that: nothing. No stations, no traffic lanes, no navigational beacons except the one the *Graceful Ruin* was still broadcasting from its parking orbit in the outer dark.

A navigation failure, according to the contract. Ship goes dark, crew evacuates or doesn't, vessel sits undisturbed for eight months in an empty system.

Except that wasn't quite what my sensors were showing.

"Zara," I said.

"I see it." She was already leaning forward in the pilot's chair, both hands on the controls, her eyes moving between the sensor display and the optical feed. Her voice had the quality it got when she was several steps ahead of the conversation. "Two ships. Small. They're running tight against the *Ruin's* shadow."

I'd caught the contacts at the edge of my sensor range — two drive signatures, cold and minimal, the heat output of ships running on standby rather than anything that would read as active on a passive scan. They were parked in the *Graceful Ruin's* radar shadow, which was a deliberate choice. The *Ruin* herself was three hundred meters of mid-class hauler, boxy and utilitarian, designed to move cargo efficiently rather than elegantly. Her shadow, from our approach vector, was wide enough to hide two small ships if those ships wanted to be hidden.

"They've been there a while," I said, analyzing the heat differential between the ships' hulls and the ambient temperature. "Six hours minimum. They're cold-running — just enough power to maintain life support and passive sensors."

"Waiting," Zara said.

"For what?"

"For someone like us." She sat back, but her hands stayed on the controls. "The advance survey that the contract posted — it was done by an automated probe. A probe can't board. It photographs the hull, registers the beacon, logs the findings. It can't tell you if the *Ruin* has been occupied since the survey was filed." A pause. "The contract posting has been public for two months. Anyone looking at it sees what we saw: intact hull, accessible outer system, no weapons sign. And now two somebodies are sitting in the shadow of that ship waiting for the salvage crew to arrive and do all the hard work."

"And then take the salvage."

"And then take the salvage. And the ship, if it's seaworthy. And us, if we're worth ransoming, or quiet, if we're not." She said the last part without emphasis. Just information. "Pirates, scavengers, doesn't matter what you call them. Same logic."

I ran a quick calculation on the two contact signatures. Both were smaller than us — one approximately twenty meters, one closer to fifteen. Fast attack craft, probably, or repurposed light shuttles. Nothing that would read as a serious military threat individually, but we had a training rig on our dorsal mount and whatever their weapons were were almost certainly real.

*Tier 2 railgun,* my registry said, cheerfully.

*Solid rod,* my actual dorsal mount contained.

This is fine, I thought. This is completely fine and not at all a problem.

"Options," I said.

"We leave," Zara said immediately. "Turn around, log the contact with the contracting party as evidence of third-party interference, collect the abort fee — there's usually an abort fee in a contract like this." She kept her eyes on the sensor display. "That's the smart option."

"And?"

"And we came out here for the salvage, not the abort fee. The *Ruin* is intact. Those two ships are small. And you—" she glanced at the camera "—are not exactly a standard salvage vessel."

I turned that over. It was not, strictly speaking, a flattering assessment. It was accurate, though, the way Zara's assessments always were — stripped of sentiment, oriented toward utility. I was not a standard salvage vessel. I was a Core, with one hundred and five units of Aether in reserve and a set of abilities that didn't appear in any ship catalog, and the two ships currently hiding in the *Ruin's* shadow did not know that.

What they knew was what our transponder told them: *Meridian Cross,* armed civilian freighter, one dorsal mount, registered.

What they didn't know was that the mount was theater.

"If we leave," I said slowly, thinking it through, "we lose the contract payout and whatever the *Ruin's* systems would have given us in compatible tech."

"Yes."

"If we don't leave, we have a functional dorsal training rig and the element of surprise."

"Functionally, yes."

"That's a terrible weapons loadout."

"It is," Zara agreed. "But consider: they don't know that." She leaned forward again, studying the geometry of the contacts' positions. "They're waiting for us to approach the *Ruin* and start work before they move. Standard ambush logic — let the prey commit to a position, then close in. But if we change the geometry before they're ready—" She pulled up the navigational display and started sketching vectors with her finger. "If we approach the *Ruin* from a high angle instead of the standard flat approach, we put ourselves between their position and the star. They'd be trying to track us against the thermal noise of K-302. Their passive sensors would have a harder time getting a clean lock."

"That makes their fire solution worse."

"Significantly worse. And if we come in fast enough—"

"We're inside their optimal engagement range before they can react." I looked at the vectors she'd drawn. The math worked. Barely, and with the caveat that everything had to go correctly, and nothing about this situation suggested everything was going to go correctly, but the math worked. "Zara. What happens if I hit one of them with the Aether conversion beam?"

She went still. "You can do that?"

"I've been hitting asteroids with it for weeks. It's a directed energy conversion at range. The question is whether the conversion is fast enough, and what the yield is, and whether I can aim precisely enough not to hit the *Ruin* or us." I ran the calculations quickly. "A ship that size — fifteen to twenty meters, probably composite alloy hull — the conversion at Tier 1 range should work. The yield would be—" I paused. The yield would be significant. Not because I wanted to hurt anyone in particular, but because converting a small ship into Aether was exactly what the system was designed to do and a small ship contained considerably more matter than any asteroid I'd processed. "We'd gain Aether. Probably a lot."

"Alex." Zara's voice was careful. "There might be people on those ships."

"I know."

We were both quiet for a moment.

"So," she said, at last. "Modified plan. The conversion beam is the last resort. We try to make them decide it's not worth it first." She turned to the navigation console. "Bring us in on the high vector, fast. Paint them with active radar the moment we're within range — let them know we see them. The training rig tracks, so point it at the larger one. And talk. If they think we're a real threat, most of the time they'd rather find easier prey."

"And if talking doesn't work?"

"Then we find out what a Core can actually do in a fight." She started calculating the approach. "Strap in. Metaphorically."

---

The approach took eleven minutes.

Eleven minutes during which I ran the high-angle vector Zara had sketched, pushing my drive hard enough that the thermal plume would be visible on any passive sensor in the system — not hiding our approach but advertising it — something that had chosen a heading rather than drifted into one. Eleven minutes during which I watched the two contact signatures slowly resolve from heat-blurs into actual hull profiles on my sensors.

The larger one was a converted mining shuttle, roughly twenty-two meters, with what read as a pair of forward-mounted kinetic launchers bolted onto a frame that hadn't been designed for them. Whoever had done the work had done it competently if not elegantly; the mounting brackets were solid and the power coupling ran to what looked like a functional charging system. Those were real weapons.

The smaller one was something I didn't have a catalog entry for — fifteen meters, angular, designed with the kind of aesthetic that prioritized making the other person feel they were looking at something unpleasant. Point-defense spines along the dorsal surface. Fast-looking, in the way that some things were fast-looking even sitting still.

*Don't think about the conversion beam yet,* I told myself. *That's the last resort.*

At eight thousand kilometers, I activated the active radar.

The pulse went out, painted both contacts with the cheerful indiscriminate thoroughness of a system saying *I know you're there and I have chosen to know it loudly,* and I watched both ships' power signatures spike as their crews responded to being lit up.

"They're powering weapons," Zara said.

"Yes. And their drives are coming up." I opened the general hailing frequency on short-range radio. "This is the *Meridian Cross* on commercial salvage contract K-302-7. We have sensor lock on two unregistered vessels in the shadow of our contracted target. Identify yourselves or vacate the area."

Silence.

The converted mining shuttle's kinetic launchers were tracking us now. I felt the geometry of it the way I felt everything — not abstractly, but as a physical fact, the way you feel a cold draft before you know what direction it's coming from. Two barrels pointed approximately at me. The training rig on my dorsal mount swiveled to point approximately at the shuttle, which was the correct instinct and also a piece of theater I desperately hoped nobody called.

"Meridian Cross." A voice, finally, from the shuttle. Rough, slightly processed through what sounded like old comm hardware. "You're in contested space. Turn around and no one has a problem."

Contested space. The kind of language that meant *we were here first and we've decided that constitutes ownership*, which was not a legal framework that appeared in the Galactic Accord but was nonetheless the operative framework in K-302.

"This is a filed contract," I said. "The *Graceful Ruin* is contracted salvage. Your presence here isn't registered anywhere I can see, which suggests you're either planning to dispute that contract or you're hoping I don't know it's filed. I know it's filed."

Another pause. Longer this time.

"Meridian Cross," the voice came back. Different tone. Slightly more considered. "You've got one mount and we've got two ships. Leave the contract and move on, and we all spend today not being dead."

I looked at my sensor data. The angular smaller ship had moved — not aggressively, but repositioning, putting itself between me and K-302's asteroid field. Boxing us slightly. They'd done this before.

Zara said nothing. Her hands were on the controls. Her biometric feed showed sixty-eight heartbeats per minute, which for Zara was elevated. Not panic. Focus.

*They think I won't fight,* I thought. *They're probably right, except they don't know what I'm made of and they've miscounted my assets by at least one.*

I thought about the Aether conversion beam. About what it could do. About the people possibly inside those ships.

I also thought about something Zara had said back in the cockpit, about the wormhole defense of systems. *Even smaller powers can hold out against the heavy hitters. Or at least make it not worth it.*

Make it not worth it.

"Last warning," the voice said.

I made a decision.

---

The Aether conversion beam was designed for asteroids.

What I aimed it at was not an asteroid. It was the mining shuttle's forward kinetic launcher — specifically the mounting bracket on the port-side barrel, where my active sensor sweep had told me the weld was the thinnest and the power coupling ran closest to the surface. Not the ship. Not the hull. A component. A component that, if converted, would make the launcher non-functional and make a very clear statement about what I could do.

The beam hit it at thirty-kilometer range.

The bracket dissolved in a spray of golden particles that were genuinely beautiful against the dark of the outer system and I felt the Aether flood back into my reserves in a sharp, warm rush — not much, a component rather than a ship, but clean and immediate.

// AETHER RESERVES: 105 → 109 //

The launcher didn't explode. Nothing dramatic. It sagged, the barrel assembly losing its mounting support and drooping at an angle that meant it was no longer pointing at anything in particular. The shuttle's hull was untouched. Everyone inside it was fine.

The silence on the radio lasted four full seconds.

"What the—" the voice started.

"That was a component," I said. "I have excellent targeting resolution and a lot of patience. Vacate the area or we have a longer conversation about what I do to ships that don't."

Another silence. Shorter.

The mining shuttle's drive flared to life — not the controlled ignition of a tactical repositioning but the full burn of something that had decided very quickly to be somewhere else. The angular ship hesitated for three seconds, which was three seconds of the most uncomfortable sensor contact I'd experienced, and then it followed.

I watched them go. Their drives burned until they were past the edge of my sensor range. They didn't come back.

"Okay," Zara said.

"Okay," I agreed.

"That was—" She stopped.

"I know."

"The beam took out a *weapon mounting.*"

"I had a clean line to the bracket. The weld quality was poor."

She turned to the camera. Her expression was doing several things simultaneously, which I was still learning to read when they happened all at once. There was something that was probably relief, and something that was clearly recalculating the threat assessment she had assigned to me, and something else underneath both of those that I couldn't quite name. "When you said the conversion worked at range, I thought you meant on asteroids."

"It works on anything with mass," I said. "I just— hadn't aimed it at anything that mattered before."

*Anything that mattered.* She heard the weight in it, I think. She didn't press.

"Right," she said, turning back to the console. "We'll talk about that later. Right now the *Ruin* is sitting there and we have a contract to complete."

---

The *Graceful Ruin* had been a workhorse.

You could tell from the outside — the hull plating was the functional flat grey of a ship that had never had any opinions about aesthetics, the external fittings were all standard-grade, no custom work, no personality. A vessel built to carry things from one place to another as efficiently as possible and to continue doing so for as long as it was asked. The kind of ship that accumulated minor repairs over years until the repairs *were* the ship, the original manufacturer's hull a framework inside someone else's maintenance history.

She was approximately a hundred and forty meters — bigger than me by a comfortable margin — and she was intact. Not just visually intact, the way the advance survey had noted: my sensors read the hull integrity at eighty-three percent, which was remarkable for eight months drifting untended. Life support was offline, power core in standby, but the structural members were sound.

"She's in good shape," I said.

"Better than she should be for a navigation failure." Zara was suited up in the new acquisition from Port Perilous — the well-maintained mid-weight with the emergency redundancy oxygen port — and running through her pre-EVA checklist in the airlock. "Navigation failures are violent. You lose control of a ship that size, momentum does its own thing, you hit something or you don't. She looks like she stopped."

"She's in a stable orbit."

"Ships in navigation failure don't park themselves in stable orbits. Someone flew her here." The suit camera came online — I felt the feed integrate, Zara's perspective stitching into my awareness alongside everything else. "Or someone flew her here and then left. Or someone flew her here and is still aboard."

"Life signs?"

"Run it."

I swept the *Ruin* with every sensor I had. No heat signatures consistent with biological activity. No atmospheric pressure readings suggesting anyone was breathing inside. No movement. "Nothing active. But—" I ran the sweep again, focusing on the engineering section. "There's a residual heat signature in the aft compartments. Very faint. Too diffuse to be a living thing but too specific to be ambient."

"Something was running back there recently." She sealed her helmet. "Jury's still out on what."

"I can try to board from the standard airlock," I said. "Match velocity, extend the docking arm—"

"No." Firm, immediate. "We're not docking with an unknown. If there's something wrong in there and we're locked to her hull, we can't move fast." She checked the thruster pack on the suit — not the light maneuvering jets built into the environment layer but a separate unit, clip-on, designed for controlled traversal rather than emergency use. "I go across on the pack. You hold position forty meters off and keep the sensors on everything. If the two friends come back, you tell me instantly."

"And if something goes wrong inside?"

"Then you come get me." She said it like it was simple.

I thought about the conversion beam. About what I'd just demonstrated I could do. About the fact that there was a fairly wide spectrum of *something going wrong* between *fixable* and *I point a conversion beam at the problem,* and most of the spectrum was occupied by situations where pointing a conversion beam at the problem would make things considerably worse.

"Be careful," I said.

"Stars don't reward careful," she said, which I'd started to recognize as one of her sayings, the ones that sounded like philosophy and functioned as deflection. "They reward fast and prepared."

She cycled the airlock and stepped into the dark.

---

The traverse took about four minutes — the *Ruin* was forty meters out, and the thruster pack covered that distance in a controlled arc that Zara ran with the relaxed precision of someone who had done EVA traversal enough times that it had stopped feeling like falling and started feeling like walking. I watched through the suit camera and through my external opticals simultaneously, the two perspectives giving me a slightly disorienting stereo view of the same crossing.

The *Ruin's* outer airlock accepted manual override — the emergency release, standard-issue, the same on every commercial vessel. Zara had the panel off and the release engaged in under two minutes, which was faster than it should theoretically take and suggested she'd done this on vessels she was less authorized to enter than this one. I did not ask about that.

The inner airlock led to a short pressurization corridor, currently vacuum. Then the main crew corridor, running fore-aft through the ship's center. Zara's helmet light cut a white cone through the perfect dark of a ship that had been without power long enough to cool to ambient.

"Three crew complement on the manifest," she said quietly. "Where are they?"

"Crew quarters are midship. I'd try there first."

She moved forward. The suit camera caught details in passing: a coffee mug — the concept was apparently universal, or at least widespread enough to be common — magnetically adhered to a work surface in the narrow galley alcove, a half-finished repair log on a wall-mounted screen that had gone dark. The *Graceful Ruin* had the texture of a place that had been left mid-sentence rather than evacuated deliberately.

"They didn't plan to leave," I said.

"No." She found the crew quarters hatch. It opened on manual.

Three emergency stasis pods. Each occupied.

I ran the biosigns through the suit's hazmat sensors, cross-referenced with what little I knew about biostasis readings. Alive. Three faint, slow, cold signatures of people who had climbed into emergency stasis and either hadn't come out or couldn't.

"They're in stasis," I said. "All three. Alive."

Zara stood in the doorway and looked at the pods for a moment. "So they didn't abandon ship," she said. "They hid."

"From what?"

She turned slowly, her helmet light sweeping the corridor outside. "From whatever made them want to leave and also convinced them they couldn't."

We found it in engineering.

---

The *Graceful Ruin's* engineering section was a standard mid-class layout — main drive assembly, power core housing, secondary systems arrayed around the central corridor. The residual heat signature I'd detected came from here, from a console near the secondary power bus that had been recently active and recently — deliberately — burned out.

"Someone wiped it," Zara said, examining the blackened console with the scanner. "This isn't damage from power failure. The circuitry was overloaded on purpose. Someone ran maximum current through it and held it there until the board fused." She pulled back a panel below it. The data storage unit inside had been physically removed, the bracket empty, the connections cleanly cut. "They took the logs."

"Why?"

"Because the logs showed where they'd been or what they'd carried." She stood up. "Or what was done to them." She ran her scanner over the wider engineering space. "The drive is intact. Power core is in standby — genuine standby, not failure. Someone brought this ship here and parked it. Properly. And then climbed into a pod and waited." She looked at the camera on her shoulder, which was the closest thing to looking at me directly when she was inside another ship. "This wasn't a navigation failure, Alex. Someone wanted this ship to look like a navigation failure."

"The contracting party," I said.

"Maybe. Or the crew staged it themselves, and the contracting party knows something happened but not what." She moved back toward the crew quarters, thinking out loud in the clipped, flat way she did when she was assembling pieces rather than presenting conclusions. "If the crew knew what they'd been carrying — or what had been done to their ship — and they wanted to disappear without anyone coming to ask questions, you'd stage a navigation failure. Park somewhere empty, wipe the logs, climb into stasis, wait for a salvage crew. Salvage crew opens the pods, crew wakes up, tells whatever story they've agreed on."

"And the salvage crew in the original plan," I said, following the logic, "wasn't two pirate ships waiting in ambush. It was someone who could be trusted."

"Or paid." She was back at the pods. "Which means either someone in the contracting chain is involved, or the crew had their own contact who knew where to find them and the contract was a backup." She looked at the pods. "Either way, we've got three people in stasis and a ship with wiped logs and a cargo hold we haven't checked yet."

"What do we do with the people?"

Zara was quiet for a moment. This was a different kind of quiet than the calculation-running quiet — something slower in it, something that was deciding rather than calculating. "We wake them up," she said finally. "Carefully. And we see what they say."

---

Waking someone from emergency stasis was not a fast process, and it was not my area of expertise, but the *Graceful Ruin's* medical systems were still on standby power and Zara knew what she was doing. She ran the revival sequence on the nearest pod, talking me through it with the patient precision of a mechanic explaining a process to someone who would need to replicate it.

The person who came out of the pod was a woman, somewhere in the middle decades of her life, with the kind of face that had spent a lot of time in low-gravity environments and the disorientation of someone whose last conscious moment was eight months ago and who was now looking at a stranger in an unfamiliar suit telling her she was fine.

"You're fine," Zara said. "Stasis recovery. Take a breath."

The woman took a breath. Then several more. Then she looked at Zara with eyes that went through confusion and fear and arrived, fairly quickly, at assessment. "Who are you?"

"Salvage contractor. Your ship was on a public contract listing." Zara kept her voice level. "You're in K-302. You've been in stasis for approximately eight months. Do you know where you are?"

"Yes." The woman's voice was rough from disuse. "Where's—" She looked at the other two pods.

"Still under. I wanted to talk to you first." Zara sat on the edge of a supply crate and looked at her steadily. "What happened to your ship?"

A long pause. The woman was deciding something. I could see it through the suit camera — the micro-expressions of someone running internal negotiations, weighing what to say against what they could get away with not saying, the calculation of someone who had planned for this conversation and was now executing the plan under the additional stress of eight months' cognitive fog.

"Navigation failure," she said.

"Your drive is intact," Zara said. "Your core is in standby, not failure. Your logs were wiped and the storage physically removed. You parked this ship deliberately in a stable orbit." She let that sit. "Navigation failure is the story you filed. What actually happened?"

Another pause. Longer.

"What do you want?" the woman asked.

"The contract payout, which I'm entitled to for locating the vessel. The Aether yield from your compatible systems, which is mine by salvage right." Zara tilted her head slightly. "And an explanation, which costs you nothing if what you were carrying wasn't something I have a problem with."

The woman looked at her for a long moment. Then at the suit camera, which meant she was looking, in some sense, at me, though she didn't know that. Her expression shifted — something that might have been relief, or at least its first cousin. "You're not with them," she said.

"Not with who?"

"The people who were supposed to be here." She closed her eyes briefly. "The people who told us to park here and wait."

"No," Zara said. "We're not with them. We ran two ships off your shadow on the approach, if it helps."

The woman opened her eyes. "Small ships? A converted shuttle and an angular—"

"Yes."

"Those weren't ours." Something shifted in her expression — harder. "Those were someone else's problem. Which means—" She stopped. Reached some internal conclusion. "Alright. I'll tell you what I can. But wake the others first. I don't talk about this without my crew."

---

The three of them sat in the *Ruin's* galley, wrapped in emergency thermal blankets, holding drinks from Zara's suit pack while I listened through the suit camera and ran slow, quiet sensor sweeps of the surrounding system.

What they'd been carrying was cargo manifested as industrial machinery parts. What the machinery parts had actually contained — nested inside, shielded from standard scan — was data cores. Military-grade encrypted storage, the kind that the woman described with the careful vagueness of someone who knew exactly what it was and was choosing not to say. They'd been paid to carry it from one station to a contact they'd never met in person. The contact had, at the last moment, gone silent. And then someone else had started asking questions about the *Graceful Ruin's* route.

So they'd parked. Wiped the logs. Gone under. Waited.

"The data cores," Zara said. "Still aboard?"

"Hidden. Yes." The woman — her name was Tersali, she'd said, and she'd offered it with the slightly suspicious generosity of someone who knows they're asking for something — looked at Zara with the frank assessment of someone who dealt in the same grey spaces. "They're not yours."

"I know they're not mine." Zara set down her drink. "But whoever wanted them knows this ship. And now two ships I've never seen before were parked in your shadow, which means either they found you through the public contract listing or they've been looking and got lucky." She paused. "Either way, staying here doesn't get better. You need to move."

"We need the drive calibrated. Liang—" she nodded at one of the others, a man who was still working on full consciousness, moving slowly, "—Liang's our engineer. He'll need an hour."

"You have an hour," Zara said. "We'll hold position."

She stood up, and the suit camera tilted as she looked around the galley — at the cold coffee mug still magnetically stuck to the work surface, at the repair log on the dark screen, at the particular preserved quality of a space that had been mid-sentence for eight months. "When you leave," she said, "leave in a different direction than we came from. And file a new navigation log from whatever system you dock in. Different ship name if you can manage it."

Tersali looked at her. "You're not going to ask for more?"

"I'm going to ask for the cargo manifest of the compatible tech in your hold," Zara said. "And the contract payout. That's what I came for."

She walked back through the *Ruin's* corridor toward the airlock, and I watched the cold ship pass in the suit camera, and I thought about three people in stasis pods waiting for a contact who'd gone silent, and about data cores nested inside industrial machinery, and about how much more sat underneath this than the surface showed.

"Zara," I said, in her earpiece.

"I know," she said.

"The data cores—"

"Not our problem."

"Someone thought they were worth parking three people in stasis for eight months."

"A lot of things are worth a lot to someone." She reached the airlock. "Our job was the salvage. We did the salvage." The outer door cycled. "Some things you leave where they are."

I thought: *Yes.* I thought: *But we know they're there now, which is a different thing from them not existing.*

I kept both thoughts to myself.

---

The cargo hold had enough compatible tech to run the numbers and feel good about them.

Zara spent forty minutes in there while the *Ruin's* engineer worked on the drive calibration, moving through with her scanner and flagging components with the practiced efficiency of someone who had been doing this since before I existed. Drive housing components. Power bus relays. A secondary sensor array that was a different model than mine but close enough in architecture for the Integration module to recognize it. Environmental control units, structural repair systems, navigational processing boards.

// COMPATIBLE COMPONENTS IDENTIFIED: 14 //
// ESTIMATED AETHER COST REDUCTIONS: SIGNIFICANT //

She hauled what she could carry in two trips back to the *Unsinkable II.* I started the repair cycles the moment she was aboard, running the compatible components through Minor Repair the way we'd learned to do it back in the carrier — systematic, efficient, each piece reducing the cost of the next system on the list.

The sensor array integration first. Then the power bus relays into the grid. Then the structural components into the hull sections that were still at less than full efficiency.

I felt myself improve, incrementally, the way I'd felt it in those early weeks in the belt — the genuine satisfaction of systems coming online, the warmth of repair threading through damage, the ship becoming more itself. More capable.

**HULL INTEGRITY: 91 → 95 → 99/100**
**AETHER RESERVES: 105 → 82 → 74/200**

And then something else happened.

I was running the last of the component integrations — the navigational processing boards from the *Ruin,* which my Integration module had identified as compatible with my sensor array in a way I didn't fully understand until the Aether started flowing — and the system did something I hadn't prompted.

// CORE THRESHOLD REACHED //
// AETHER INVESTMENT SINCE TIER 1: SUFFICIENT //
// CORE TIER ADVANCEMENT AVAILABLE //
// TIER 2: COST 30 AETHER //
// TIER 2 UNLOCKS: AETHER SENSE //

I stopped what I was doing.

Aether Sense. It had been greyed out since I first found it in the Core Functions menu, locked behind a Tier threshold I hadn't reached yet. I'd looked at it enough times in quiet moments to have the description memorized.

// AETHER SENSE //
// PASSIVE ABILITY //
// EFFECT: DETECT AETHER SIGNATURES AT RANGE. IDENTIFY AETHER-BEARING TARGETS, CONCENTRATION LEVELS, AND SOURCE TYPES WITHOUT ACTIVE SCAN. RANGE AND RESOLUTION SCALE WITH CORE TIER. //

Thirty units. I had seventy-four.

"Zara," I said.

She was stowing the second load of components in the cargo hold. "Yeah."

"I'm at the Tier 2 threshold."

A pause. Then footsteps — quick ones — back toward the cockpit. She came through the hatch and sat down in the pilot's chair and looked at the main display where I'd put the upgrade menu. Her eyes moved across it. "Aether Sense," she said.
