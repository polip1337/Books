# Chapter 1: The Drift

The fluctuation on the power readout was small, a ghost in the machine, but I knew it was everything. I leaned closer to the humming device, a miniaturized fusion reactor no larger than a suitcase. The OmniCorp brass called it the "Eternity Cell." Free, sustainable power. My pet project.

And my tombstone, if I can't fix this, I thought, rubbing my tired eyes.

The problem was efficiency. The reactor was stable, but it wasn't producing the power output the models promised. It was like an engine that idled perfectly, but refused to accelerate. My theory was that the magnetic containment field was too conservative, stifling the plasma reaction. The safety protocols were bleeding away potential energy.

I'd spent the last six hours coding a workaround. A subtle rewrite of the field regulation software, allowing the magnetic pinch to loosen by point-zero-five percent. It was a risk, but those were acceptable with funding running out.

"Final calibration," I muttered to the empty lab, my voice swallowed by the reactor's low thrum. I input the last sequence and initiated the test.

For a glorious second, it worked. The power output spiked, climbing past the predicted threshold. The reactor glowed with a healthy, bright blue light. A grin started to form on my face.

Then the fluctuation returned. But it wasn't small anymore.

The power level oscillated, then surged again, higher this time. The thrum deepened into a drone. A warning flashed on my screen: CONTAINMENT FIELD OSCILLATION.

"Abort," I said, hitting the shutdown command.

The system accepted it, but it didn't make a difference. The reactor was cycling its own increased output back into the containment field generators, creating a self-reinforcing loop. I'd created a perfect, closed circuit of escalating energy.

CATASTROPHIC CASCADE IMMINENT. CONTAINMENT BREACH IN 30 SECONDS.

Cold dread washed over me. 29… 28…

I slammed the physical master cutoff. It was dead. The reactor was powering itself now, independent of the grid.

25… 24…

I dove for the safe room yanking the red lever. The heavy door began to grind shut.

Thank god for fucking OSHA regulations

20… 19…

The drone became a shriek. Tools flew from my bench, clattering against the reactor's casing, pulled by the increased magnetic field. The light from the core turned from blue to a violent, blinding white.

15… 14…

My eyes opened wider. I slammed the red lever again — nothing. Still closing, still too slow. I turned, looked back. The reactor was the heart of a newborn star.

10… 9…

I saw my reflection in the polished steel — wide-eyed, frozen. I threw my shoulder against the door.

5… 4…

A wave of force picked me up and threw me across the lab like a discarded toy. The heavy blast door flew by me and slammed into the wall.
 
Well, shit, my mind supplied, a strangely calm counterpoint to the chaos. 

I didn't feel the impact with the far wall, not really. There was a distant, crunching thud, and then the sensation of sliding to the floor. 

My life didn't flash before my eyes. There was no highlights reel, no great achievements. Instead, a single, bitter thought crystallized in the fading light of my consciousness: This is it? All those dreams of building something that mattered, of getting out there and seeing the stars… and I end up as a stain on the floor of a corporate lab.

I'd spent my mid-thirties chasing promotions and paychecks, my passion for engineering slowly suffocated by endless performance reviews and soul-crushing bureaucracy. I'd traded the stars for a corner office that never materialized.

The light from the burning lab dissolved into a velvety, infinite blackness.

So, this is death, I mused, my thoughts becoming diffuse, unmoored. Kind of underwhelming. No light, no relatives, just… nothing. A letdown.

I expected oblivion. Instead, after a span of time that could have been a second or a millennium, a point of light ignited in the darkness. It wasn't a warm, welcoming light. It was a cold, blue-white pinprick.

Then another appeared. And another.

Soon, the void was no longer empty. It was filled with a sprawling, brilliant tapestry of stars, nebulae, and the distant, swirling arms of a galaxy I had never seen before. It was the most beautiful thing I had ever seen — and the most terrifying.

Okay, I think awe is the word I'm looking for, I thought. This is breathtaking.

I realized I was being more literal than I meant to be. I tried to take a breath, to feel my chest rise and fall. Nothing. I tried to move a hand, to touch my face. I had no hand to move, no face to touch. Panic, cold and sharp, lanced through my disembodied awareness.

What the hell? Where am I? Where is my body?

As if in answer to my silent, frantic questions, a cool, rectangular interface materialized in my perception. It was a translucent blue, etched with clean lines and alien glyphs that somehow resolved into understandable text. It looked like something straight out of the sci-fi games I used to play to unwind.

// SYSTEM BOOT-UP INITIATED… //
// SCANNING CONSCIOUSNESS… //
// ENTITY DESIGNATION: ALEX RIVERA. CONFIRMED. //
// SOUL-SPARK INTEGRATION… COMPLETE. //
// WELCOME, CORE. //

"Core?" I tried to say, but the word had no sound. It was just a thought, echoing in the confines of my new, limited existence. What's a Core? What is this?

The system ignored me, continuing its methodical, impersonal procession.

// HOST VESSEL DESIGNATION: SS NOVA DRIFT. //
// STATUS: DERELICT. //
// INTEGRITY CRITICAL. //
// ASSIGNING DIRECT CONTROL. //

The starfield before me snapped into a new context. It wasn't a view, it was a sensor feed. The pinpricks of light were distant suns, and the dark shapes occluding them were jagged, mountain-sized asteroids. I was in the middle of a belt, adrift.

With a lurch of perception that had no physical component, I felt the rest of my new "body." Or rather, the lack of it. I could sense the skeletal frame of a small starship — the SS Nova Drift. I felt the brittle, barely-there state of the hull, the cold vacuum in compartments that should have been warm. I felt the stillness of dead engines and the silence of empty corridors.

I was the ship. The ship was me.

Oh, you have got to be kidding me, I thought, a hysterical edge to my internal voice. I got isekai'd. I actually got reincarnated. But not as a hero, or a prince, or even a goddamn goblin. I'm a… a flying junkyard.

My "eyes" were external cameras, most of them dark or cracked. Through the visual sensor on the port side, I could see a section of my own hull — pitted and scarred by micrometeorites, painted in the faded, flaking paint in black and yellow. A long, jagged tear ran along the side, exposing twisted support beams and some kind of tank.

A new set of data streams began to trickle into my awareness, a constant, low-level hum of information that was as intrinsic to me now as my own heartbeat had once been.

<< STATUS OVERLAY >>
HULL INTEGRITY: 14/100
POWER CORE: 3% (STANDBY)
PROPULSION: OFFLINE
AETHER RESERVES: 0/50
WEAPONS SYSTEMS: OFFLINE
SHIELDS: OFFLINE
CREW COMPLEMENT: 0
CORE TIER: 0

The numbers were a punch to a gut I no longer possessed. It was even worse than I thought. No propulsion or weapons. I was about to fall apart on my own, a wreck, a thinking corpse in a metal coffin. 

Okay, Rivera. Don't panic. You're an engineer. Think. The thought of a lifetime. You're alive, in a manner of speaking. You have a problem — several dozen, in fact. So, start with what you know.

I focused on the concept of "diagnostics," and a menu flickered into existence, listing a dozen broken or dormant systems. At random, I tried "internal comms." A burst of static screeched through my consciousness, making my awareness flicker.

Right. Broken.

I tried something different: I turned my attention — my will — to the most pressing issue. Power. The reactor was at 3%, barely enough to keep my core consciousness and the most basic control online. If that failed, I'd go dark. Permanently.

I focused on the "Power Core" readout, trying to find a way to route emergency energy, to bypass whatever was failing. As I concentrated, a new prompt appeared.

// CORE ABILITY: AETHER CONVERSION. //
// DESCRIPTION: CONVERT MATTER/ENERGY INTO AETHER, THE FUNDAMENTAL RESOURCE FOR CORE DEVELOPMENT AND VESSEL MAINTENANCE. //
// PROXIMITY SCAN INITIATED… //

A wave of energy, invisible and subtle, pulsed out from my core — a module deep within the heart of the ship. It washed over the immediate vicinity, painting my perception with new data. Most of the space around me was an empty void. But then, my sensors tagged a small, nickel-iron asteroid drifting a few hundred meters away. It was highlighted in a soft, golden glow.

POWER CORE: 2.9% 

TARGET ACQUIRED: ASTEROID (NICKEL-IRON)
AETHER POTENTIAL: LOW
ESTIMATED YIELD: 2 AETHER UNITS
INITIATE CONVERSION? Y/N

Hope, a feeling I hadn't felt since before the explosion, sparked within me. Aether. This is the stuff. The XP, the mana, the currency. This was how I survived.

I didn't have a hand to press a button, so I formed the intention, a clear, decisive [Y].

Damn, that made me sound smart, communicating in single letters.

For a moment, nothing happened. Then, a thin, barely visible beam of silver light lanced out from a dormant emitter array on my hull. It struck the asteroid. There was no sound, but I felt a faint vibration through my frame. The rock didn't explode or vanish, it… unraveled. It faded from solid matter into a shimmering cloud of golden particles that hung in space for a second before streaming towards me, flowing through my damaged hull and into my core.

The sensation was incredible. It was like drinking ice-cold water after a lifetime in the desert. A wave of pure, revitalizing energy coursed through my systems.

AETHER RESERVES: 2/50
POWER CORE: 2.4%

Okay, I got the stuff. Now what stuff can l do with my stuff?

A quick exploration of the System interface revealed a sub-menu I'd missed: << CORE FUNCTIONS >>. Inside, I found two options greyed out: AETHER SENSE and MINOR REPAIR. Aether Sense required Tier 1, but Minor Repair was available for a cost.

FUNCTION: MINOR REPAIR
TIER: 0
COST: 5 AETHER
EFFECT: RESTORE 5-10 HULL INTEGRITY POINTS TO A TARGETED SUBSYSTEM.

There was a blinking message attached. I "clicked" it with my mind.

AETHER BASIC TUTORIAL
AETHER CAN BE USED TO ACTIVATE CORE ABILITIES. IT CAN ALSO BE DIRECTLY USED TO REPAIR THE SHIP IN EMERGENCIES OR CONVERTED INTO POWER

The stuff of wonders. Let's try it.

I willed the aether to fix the rend in the port hull. Through a flickering internal sensor, I watched as twisted support beams groaned and slowly straightened. A section of hull plating, paper-thin and brittle, thickened and sealed over, the metal knitting together with a faint, shimmering light.

<< STATUS UPDATE >>
HULL INTEGRITY: 14/100 -> 15/100

Holy hell, I thought, a slow grin spreading across a face that no longer existed. It works.

If this is not nanotech, I'll eat my hat. Do ships wear hats? ... Back to work Rivera!

Emboldened, I scanned again. This time, I found a smaller, faster-moving piece of debris — a chunk of twisted metal that might have been part of another ship long ago. It was highlighted in the same golden glow.

TARGET ACQUIRED: SPACE DEBRIS (COMPOSITE ALLOYS)
AETHER POTENTIAL: VERY LOW
ESTIMATED YIELD: 1 AETHER UNIT
INITIATE CONVERSION? Y/N

I confirmed, and the silver beam lashed out again. The debris dissolved into a wisp of golden light. The energy was less this time, a mere trickle, but it was enough.
POWER CORE: 1.8%
AETHER RESERVES: 2/50

I now had a grand total of two Aether. 
Five Aether, I mused. I'm two or three chunks of space junk away from a real repair. Let's go shopping.

I began scanning in earnest, my awareness pulsing out in waves. I found another small asteroid, yielding another 2 Aether. Fortunately, still in my range. The conversion felt smoother this time, the energy integrating into my systems with less resistance. I was learning. I immediately put both points into power.

POWER CORE: 3.2%

Then, my scan pinged on a cluster of frozen gases clinging to a dark, carbonaceous rock. The yield was a paltry 3 Aether, and I took it.

AETHER RESERVES: 5/50

Alright, let's see what this repair function can do.

I focused on the MINOR REPAIR function. The 5 Aether cost was deducted instantly.

AETHER RESERVES: 0/50

A warm, tingling sensation spread from my core module outwards, following the pathways of my internal structure. It was like feeling blood flow into a numb limb.

<< STATUS UPDATE >>
HULL INTEGRITY: 15/100 -> 22/100
SYSTEM ONLINE: INFRARED TELESCOPE

Okay, I thought, a wave of fierce satisfaction washing over me. That's more like it. I can fix this.

A new, independent thought entered my mind. The ship's designation, "SS Nova Drift," felt impersonal. It was a name given by someone else, for a ship that had died. This was a new entity. Me. And I was, against all odds, still here.

I pushed my awareness into the ship's registry, into the core identification beacon. With a surge of will, I erased "SS Nova Drift." I needed a name. I'd sunk on Earth, my life and body destroyed. But here I was, risen from those cosmic ashes.

I input the new name. The ship's systems accepted the change with a soft chime.

// VESSEL REGISTRY UPDATED. //
// NEW DESIGNATION: UNSINKABLE II. //

Unsinkable II, I thought, the name resonating with a dark, defiant humor. Because the first version of me absolutely sank. Let's see if this one has better luck.

And let them wonder what happened to Unsinkable I

My moment of christening was shattered by a new alert, this one flashing a gentle but insistent amber.

// PROXIMITY ALERT. //
// INFRARED TELESCOPE DETECTED AN OBJECT ON COLLISION COURSE. //
// ANALYSIS: ASTEROID, 50 METERS DIAMETER. //
// TIME TO IMPACT: 4 MINUTES, 32 SECONDS. //

I focused my lone working camera, panning it slowly. There it was. A hulking, irregular mass of rock and ice, tumbling end over end. Its path was clear, and my own drifting course was just predictable enough to put them both in the same place at the same time. An impact like that would shatter me into a thousand pieces.

The fear returned, cold and sharp. But like before my first instinct was to act. 

No. Not again. I didn't come back from one explosion just to die in another...

What can I use?

No engines. No thrusters. Only one tool.

The Aether beam converted matter. What if I used the conversion process not to absorb, but to push? To create a reaction.

I targeted the asteroid and initiated conversion. The strain was immediate. My power dipped to 3%. The beam was a whisper against a mountain.

Not enough.

The memory of the feedback loop surfaced. A cycle of power building on itself.

I broke a small part of the rock down. As the Aether streamed back, I fed it directly back into the emitter, overloading the circuit. Creating a runaway cycle.

ENERGY FEEDBACK DETECTED. EMITTER INTEGRITY FAILING.

The beam brightened, thickening into a chaotic lance of energy. It drained my power supply at a prodigious pace. The lance hit what looked like a cluster of sensors.

It exploded.

PORT SENSOR ARRAY OFFLINE.

Which would hopefully knock me off course.

I cut the beam. The feedback loop collapsed. I was at 0% power. 

I felt the asteroid pass, a gravitational sigh against my hull. 

Then, it was gone.

I had nearly killed myself to avoid being killed. I was alive, but I might still die. 

EMERGENCY SHUTDOWN 
The Unsinkable II drifted on in darkness and silence.
