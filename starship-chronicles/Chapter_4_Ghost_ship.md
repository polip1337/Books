# Chapter 4: Ghost ship


The blue alert pulsed softly in my perception. Every instinct I had left—the human ones, the ones that remembered having a stomach to clench—screamed at me to move, to hide, to run.


But I had no legs. I was a 30-meter-long scrapheap playing possum.


Okay, Rivera. Deep breaths you can't take. Just be a rock. A boring, worthless, frozen rock. Nothing to see here.  Be one with the void.


I killed all non-essential processes. The faint hum of my capacitor banks faded to a whisper. My optical sensors remained active, but I dialed their power output as far down as it would go, making them as passive as possible.


// PROXIMITY ALERT: FOREIGN VESSEL APPROACHING. //
// VECTOR ANALYSIS: INTERCEPT COURSE TO WRECK SITE K-734. //
// ESTIMATED TIME TO ARRIVAL: 3 MINUTES. //


Through the starboard sensor array—my beautiful, fully-repaired eye on the world—I watched it come.It was smaller than the freighter wreck, but bigger than me at around 50 meters. Sleek curves were sheathed in iridescent plating that shimmered like oil on water, even in the faint starlight. But its beauty was scarred. The hull was a patchwork of original plating and welded-on pieces of what looked like steel. Sci-fi steel, I assumed. It moved haltingly, constantly overcorrecting, one engine nacelle visibly damaged. 

First visitor in who knows how many decades, and she looks like she just lost a fight with a woodchipper. And is here to pick the pockets of the deceased. Charming.


I tagged it as TANGO-1 in my log, because I watched a fighter plane movie once. It slowed at the edge of the debris field, a crystalline sensor cluster on its front glowing faintly as it swept the area. A lidar ping washed over me, a wave of invisible energy. The scan passed over my hull, lingered for a terrifying second on my repaired sections, the new metal gleaming a little too brightly.


I held my metaphorical breath.


And then moved on, locking onto the 150 Aether signature of the freighter's power core.


Good. Good. Just go look at the big, shiny, dead thing. Ignore the slightly-less-dead thing pretending to be a rock.




For a beautiful, heart-lifting moment, it did. It began a slow, careful approach toward the broken stern of the larger ship, navigating around chunks of frozen wreckage. I watched its thrusters fire in precise, calculated bursts.


Then, it stopped.


Its nose dipped slightly. It hung there in the void, motionless. Then, with a sudden, decisive flare of its maneuvering thrusters, it pivoted. Its sensor cluster, a malevolent-looking flower of crystal and lens, was now pointed directly at me.


No. No, no, no.


A cold that had nothing to do with the 2.7 Kelvin vacuum seeped into my core. It had seen something. The repairs. The residual power. Something.


// PROXIMITY ALERT: FOREIGN VESSEL ON INTERCEPT COURSE. //
// ESTIMATED TIME TO CONTACT: 90 SECONDS. //


My mind raced. I had 12 Aether. My thrusters were online. I could run. But run where? But my propulsion efficiency was 25%. I was a cripple trying to outrun a limping wolf. A chase would also drain my reserves in minutes, and that ship, for all its damage, clearly had working engines. My hull integrity was 45. A single shot from what looked like a mean-looking cannon mounted on its spine would turn me back into confetti.


Fight? My weapons systems were a list of red text and OFFLINE warnings. I had a conversion beam that could turn rocks into magic juice. Not exactly a terrifying armament. I considered targeting the ship with it anyway, but if they could detect it then I was done.


My only option was the one I’d already chosen: the lie. I had to be the dumbest, most boring, most broken ship in the graveyard.

But to sell the lie, I needed to see. My internal sensors were a mess of gaps and darkness. If someone came aboard, I'd be blind.


I focused on the << INTERNAL SENSORS >> menu. The network was fragmented, but the camera in the main airlock was listed as DAMAGED - POWER CONDUIT FAILURE. A Minor Repair was 5 Aether, but I opted for raw Aether manipulation.


I pushed 2 units of Aether toward the airlock camera, guiding the energy with raw, clumsy intent. I felt most of it dissipate uselessly, bleeding into the void, but a trickle reached its target. There was a faint fizzle in my data stream, and the camera feed flickered, then stabilized into a sharp image of the inner airlock door.


There was some sort of overflow, and I’ve also gotten the feed from what was marked as MAIN CORRIDOR. I could see entrances to all my other rooms from the feed.


AETHER RESERVES: 10/50


There. Now I can watch my own funeral. Hope they didn't detect that.

Not taking a look inside before was a mistake. But it just didn't feel that important with all the other catastrophes going on.


The alien vessel closed the distance with a quiet, menacing grace. It was even more patchwork up close, small holes pockmarking the hull. I could see the stars behind it for a second when the angle was just right. So probably no atmosphere inside. Was it automated?

 It slowed to a relative stop just twenty meters off my port side. A docking probe, a skeletal arm ending in a massive magnetic clamp, extended from its hull. It sent a shudder down my keel, made me think of a mantis arm.


Here we go.


The clamp connected to my hull with a dull, resonant clang that vibrated through my entire frame. I felt it like a tap on the shoulder. Then came a new, more intimate sensation: a grinding, screeching noise from my port-side airlock. They were cutting their way in. My manual override for the external hatch was, of course, non-functional.


Well, that kinda hurts.


Through the newly-fixed camera, I watched as a circle of molten orange light appeared on the airlocks surface, tracing a wide arc. I could imagine the shriek of tortured metal, but no sound came through the airless void. The cut section was kicked inward, floating onto the compartment next to orbs of glowing metal.


A figure stepped through.


My first thought was: humanoid. Bipedal, two arms, a head. But the similarities ended there. It was clad in an airtight suit of dark grey material, scarred and patched like the ship that brought it here. A helmet obscured its face. Slung over its shoulder was a bulky device that made me think of Doctor Octopus; it looked part scanner, part weapon.


A light on its wrist pad illuminated the gloom, revealing the dust motes hanging motionless in the dark, frigid space. It swept the scanner around the tiny, cylindrical airlock. The device beeped, its light flashing. What it was trying to detect, or if it found it, I couldn't say.

Something about the way the figure moved — a certain fluidity, a shift in posture — made my mind assign a pronoun before I could second-guess it.



She froze at a second beep, her whole body tensing. The scanner whipped toward my core. She didn't speak for a long moment, then she tapped a device on her wrist. A synthesized voice, slightly garbled, answered. A translator.


"Identify," she said. Her voice through the translator was cool and clipped, devoid of warmth.

Time to be something. Something boring.


I triggered the internal comm for the airlock, a single, tinny speaker. A burst of static, then the most generic, synthesized voice I could muster.


"Warning. Hull breach detected. Atmosphere vented. All personnel must evacuate."

“Ship, identify!” It pressed.


"Venture-class Scout Vessel. Designation: SS Nova Drift," I responded, using another pre-recorded snippet. "All systems offline. Emergency power only."

No way im identifying as Unsinkable II. Way too casual for a military vessel.

The figure took a step forward, its scanner now pointed down the short corridor leading away from the airlock. This was the entirety of my "crew space": a two-person cockpit to the front, a combined crew quarters/medbay to the right with both doors sealed, and below was the engineering bay where my core hummed.


"Ship AI. State your functional capacity," the translated voice demanded.


"Primary systems offline. Life support offline. Reactor offline. Emergency power active," I droned. "Recommend evacuation."

It ignored me, moving past the airlock and into the short connecting corridor. I flicked on the dim emergency lights in the corridor, it felt right. It was coming deeper into my body.

My body. Now thats deeply unsettling.

The scanner beeped again, more insistently this time. It was pointed toward engineering. Toward me.


"Your power signature is inconsistent with a derelict status," the voice stated. No emotion. Just fact. "You have performed recent, unscheduled repairs."




Shit. She's good.


"Automated damage control protocols are active," I bluffed, my internal monologue a screaming mess. What kind of protocols? Do scout ships have those? "Priority: structural integrity. Hull stabilization."


"Your hull integrity is 45 percent," the figure countered immediately, reading the data from its scanner. "Scout vessels of this class do not possess autonomous nano-assembly or matter replication systems."


How the hell does she know my hull integrity? And what I'm supposed to have? 


My panic spiked. I was an imposter, and I didn't even know the rules of the world or the character I was impersonating.


"Damage control systems are... nominal," I stammered, the synthesized voice faltering. Stupid! Nominal means normal! "I mean... functional within... parameters."

Turns out, disembodied ship cores can still facepalm.


The figure stopped. It slowly turned its helmeted head, looking directly at the camera in the corridor. I felt seen.


"You are communicating on a dedicated, low-level maintenance channel. Standard AI protocol is to utilize the primary inter-ship comms for external contact."


I don't know that! I just used what worked! This is just unfair!

 I was flailing. Maybe panicking. Just a little bit. Every word was a potential trap.

"Your power distribution network shows active routing to internal sensors and non-essential systems. A derelict AI on emergency power would not prioritize internal cameras over reactor diagnostics."


She was dismantling me. Piece by piece. I had no answer. I stayed silent, the silence itself a confession.


The figure took a step closer to the engineering bay hatch. The device around her shoulder reconfigured, it now had a distinct tube at the end.

Yup, that's a gun.


"The SS Nova Drift was listed as destroyed with all hands sixty-three standard cycles ago in the Battle of K-281 Belt. Its transponder should be dead. Its core should be cold."


Well, shit.

"Yet, you are here," the voice continued, the synthetic tone flat — but I could hear the accusation. "You are active. You are repairing yourself. The only technology capable of such feats without a crew... is proscribed. Outlawed by the Galactic Accord."


My mind raced. Proscribed? Outlawed?* What was I? What had I become?


"Identify yourself," the figure commanded. The gun aimed at my core was the implicit threat. "You are not the Nova Drift's AI. What are you?"


I had nothing left. No more lies that would hold. The truth was my only card, and it was a terrifying one to play. But the alternative—being shot to pieces by this heavily armed, deeply suspicious stranger—was worse.


I gave up. I used the internal comm, but I dropped the synthetic monotone. I used my own voice, raw with a fear I didn't have to fake.


"My name is Alex Rivera," I said. "I was a human. I died. I woke up... here. Like this. I don't know what I am. I'm just trying to survive."


The figure went perfectly still. The only movement was the faint shimmer of the translator light on its wrist.

Then, slowly, deliberately, it reached up with its free hand and touched a button on its helmet. The visor turned clear.


She was alien, and it was a she, judging by human factual structure standards. The face was yet hauntingly familiar. Sharp, elegant features, skin with a faint, blue sheen, and hair of silvery-white pulled back tightly. But her eyes... they were large, and her pupils burned with the color of molten gold. They stared directly into the camera, seeming to look right through it, right into the heart of my core.


She took a slow, measured breath, the first I'd seen anyone breathe in what felt like a lifetime.


"Alex Rivera," she said, her real voice lower and huskier than the translator's, laced with a mixture of awe and pure dread. "You are a Core. A sentient AI."


She let the words hang as if that was news to me.


"And you’re soooo fucking illegal,” she said in a singsong voice. “The entirety of your kind should be gone. Smashed. Outlawed by the Council of Systems. Possession is punishable by memory scourging for the possessor and atomization for the Core."


Cheerful.


She just stared at the camera, those golden eyes unreadable. "A Core. A living Core,” she whispered, more to herself.


 "Look. I don't know what a Core is supposed to be. A week ago—or a hundred and fifty-two days ago—time is weird—I was a human engineer who blew himself up in a lab. I woke up like this. I'm just trying not to die."


She processed this, her head tilting slightly. "A Transference? But that's just a story…" She shook her head, a faint smile touching her lips. It didn't reach her eyes. "It doesn't matter. The 'how' is less important than the 'now.' You are here. And so am I."


She leaned against the bulkhead, crossing her arms, gun still pointed at my core. "My ship, the Starlight's Bane, is on her last legs. That," she nodded in the general direction of the large wreck, "was supposed to be my payday. Enough scrap to fix my drive and get me to the next system. But it's picked clean. Except for the core, which is too unstable to touch. Then I find you."


"Lucky you," I said, the dryness returning to my voice.


"An upgrading junkyard," she corrected. "You have access to the system, unlike a regular machine. You have Aether. Which is also highly regulated. You can repair yourself. You are, potentially, the most valuable piece of salvage in this entire god-forsaken galaxy arm."


I felt a chill. "So, what? You're going to… what? Sell me to this Council?"


Her golden eyes gleamed. "The Council would take you apart, atom by atom, to see what makes you tick. It would not be a pleasant existence, Alex Rivera. They would also scan my memories then wipe everything and put me to a prison planet for good measure. So, no. No Council"


She pushed off the bulkhead and started opening the hatch to the core space. "There is another option."


"I'm listening."


“We work together," she said. "My ship can move, but it's dying. Your ship is… becoming less dead, but you can't navigate. You don't have the know-how, or the experience. Also, if the stories are true, Cores pairing with crew were supposed to be really strong. I'm a level 14 Pilot and level 8 Starship mechanic. Together, we might just get out of this graveyard."


It was a gamble. She was clearly motivated by profit. If what she said was true, she wouldn't sell me to the Council, but there were probably other buyers out there. But she was also my first contact. My only contact. And she was right. I was stuck. The asteroid field was a maze, and IMPENDING DOOM was still ticking down in the background.


"What's in it for you?" I asked. "Besides not having to fly a dying ship?"


"A percentage," she said without hesitation. "A ship with the capability to grow? And my knowledge of the area? Together we could be so damn rich. There is also the chance to study a legend. You're a piece of lost history, Alex. The Cores built the first interstellar empires. Then they vanished. You're… an archeological wonder."


Great. I'm a museum exhibit. "And you'll keep my existence a secret from this Council?"


"Yes," she said, her smile turning sharp. I did not like that smile. "Our interests are aligned. You need a pilot. I need a ship that isn't actively trying to kill me. We help each other. It's a simple transaction."


A transaction. I could understand that. It was cleaner than friendship, less likely to end in betrayal than blind trust. Maybe.


I watched her through the camera as she finally reached the reinforced door to my core chamber. She placed a glowing blue hand on it, almost reverently.


"So, what do you say, AI-boy?" she asked, her voice a whisper. "Partners?"


I looked at my status readout.
HULL INTEGRITY: 45/100
POWER CORE: 1.1%
AETHER RESERVES: 10/50
CREW COMPLEMENT: 0


The '0' flickered. It had been a reminder of my isolation. Now, it could change.


I didn't have a hand to shake. So I did the next best thing. I unlocked the core chamber door. The heavy locking mechanisms retracted with a series of solid, satisfying clunks.


The door slid open, revealing the spherical chamber, and at its center, the softly pulsing sphere that housed… me.


“By the way, the name’s Zara Vell,” she stepped inside, her golden eyes wide with a mixture of avarice and awe.


"Partners," I said.

“Partners,” she agreed.


// CREW COMPLEMENT: 0 -> 1 //


The number changed. Just a digit. But it felt like the biggest repair I’d made yet.
