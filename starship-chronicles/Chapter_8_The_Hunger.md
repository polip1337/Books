# Chapter 8: The Hunger

The retreat from the carrier was a frantic fight against gravity.

Not the asteroid's pull, the new, wrong kind that was forming in the engineering bay. The black substance, having consumed the mass of the collapsed deck and machinery, caused visible distortions in the light around it, the surrounding wreckage groaned and shuddered, pulled inward by an immense, localized weight.

"Go, go, go!" Zara barked, her hands all over the controls. My thrusters flared at maximum burn, a pathetic whimper against the silent, gathering vortex. The *Unsinkable II* shuddered, groaning in protest as we fought to break free.

I watched through the rear-facing camera. The collapse was an implosion, a slow-motion avalanche of metal being ingested. The black goo, now the size of a small shuttle, seemed to drink the light around it, a three-dimensional blot of nothingness. It spread, consumed and grew.

"What is that?" I whispered, the data from my sensors a jumble of contradictions. It registered as a massive gravity well, but with no associated mass signature. It emitted no heat, no radiation, nothing but gravity.

"Bad news," Zara grunted, her voice strained with effort. "Unstable exotic matter. Should not happen. Eats anything with mass to sustain itself. A temporary black hole. If it gets big enough before were far enough..."

She didn't need to finish. I understood. Event horizon, spagettification, being compressed as much as matter can be. All the fun stuff.

The pull intensified. My thrusters, already at their limit, couldn't overcome it. We were slowing, our hard-won velocity bleeding away. Alerts screamed in my mind.

// WARNING: GRAVITATIONAL ANOMALY DETECTED //
// HULL STRESS: INCREASING //

"We're not going to make it," I said. "The thrusters aren't enough."

Zara's golden eyes were wide, fixed on the viewscreen where the carrier—and the growing blot of hunger at its heart—was beginning to visibly distort, like a reflection in a funhouse mirror. "The Bane," she said, her voice suddenly calm. "It has more power. Its main engine is shot, but its emergency thrusters are chemical. High impulse, short burn."

"You can't!" I protested. "The umbilical won't survive the G-force, and you'll be torn apart trying to cross—"

"I'm not going to the Bane," she cut me off, a wild, reckless grin spreading across her face. "The Bane is coming to us."

Before I could process her words, her fingers danced across the datapad linked to her ship. On my sensors, the Starlight's Bane, which she'd left parked in a relatively clear pocket just outside the carrier's immediate debris field, lit up. A series of explosive bolts fired along its hull. With a bloom of white gas, its entire damaged stern section—containing the dead main engine—detached and drifted away.

I now saw it. The plan was the bank of six emergency maneuvering thrusters mounted amidships. They were never meant for sustained flight, only for quick, course-correcting shoves.

Zara sent the command.

All six fired at once.

The Bane, now a third lighter, became a colossal hammer. It shot forward on a pre-programmed vector, crossing the empty buffer zone and slamming into the edge of the debris cloud surrounding the carrier. 
Zara’s ship became a missile. It plowed into the asteroids and shrapnel alike. It didn't fly, it crashed through. Glancing impacts sheared armor plating and sent spinning shards of its own hull caroming into the void. Its flight path was a straight line. A line that terminated at my new and shiny starboard flank. 
The universe screamed.

For a nanosecond, everything went white with static. When my sensors rebooted, we were moving. Not gracefully, but moving. The carrier and its hungry heart began to recede, the pull on my frame lessening.

Through the aft camera, I watched the Bane's final act. The chemical thrusters cut, having spent their fuel. The hull spun away from me, its beautiful form now a tumbling wreck. 
But the chaos was just beginning.

 The Bane's violent entry and the pull of the carrier was like kicking a hornet's nest. Twice. Asteroids and fragments, once drifting in slow, predictable vectors, were sent careening in all directions. And every small piece began falling down, to be consumed. But the larger ones, knocked into new vectors by the *Bane's* impact, became unpredictable missiles.

"Evasive!" Zara yelled, her hands on my controls.

I didn't need telling. My newly restored starboard sensors painted a nightmare. 

We were flying into a shooting gallery where the bullets were bigger than me.

Zara fired the port thrusters, slewing us sideways. A house sized chunk of rock whistled past the viewport, so close I bet even Zara could see the fractures in its surface. The gravitational tug from the Hunger altered the trajectory of another, sending it spiraling toward us in a corkscrew pattern. Zara jinked down, then up, her movements faster than I could follow.

I couldn't help. I tried finding something to do, but with Zara piloting i was reduced to being a platform. Every impact, every near miss, sent fresh shudders through my frame. Alerts chimed constantly.

Through it all, I kept one sensor locked on the Bane. It tumbled away from us, back toward the outer edge of the field, a glittering, iridescent jewel. Zara's ship, her home, was on its own.

"Zara, the Bane!" I shouted over the alarm chorus.

Zara stared at the sensor feed for a single, frozen second. Her jaw was set. "I can't," she said, the words forced. "Not if we want to live." She had traded her ship for our lives, and now she had to watch it die.

The Starlight’s Bane didn't go out in a blaze of glory. It tumbled, a dead weight in the dark. It clipped a nickel-iron spar on a passing asteroid, shearing off its communication array, before slamming into a larger mass. The hull buckled like foil, it came to rest, embedded in the rock.

In the cockpit, the alarms cut off and only Zara’s heavy breathing was left. With the push we’ve gotten she managed to get us out of the asteroid field. We were floating outside of it now. The gravitational pull slackened as we gained distance, but the telescope cameras showed the nightmare was only beginning. The black void was larger now—a growing sphere of nothingness consuming the carrier and the surrounding belt.

Her ship was gone.

Zara let her hands fall from the controls. She didn't look at the displays or the cameras. She stared through the reinforced glass at the receding graveyard where a dark stain was erasing the last physical remnant of her old life.

"I'm sorry," I said. My voice, synthesized and cold, felt inadequate for the silence.

"You saved me," she said. Her voice was flat, the emotion drained out of it to leave something hollow. "But I think I just lost everything that made that a good trade."

"Not everything," I said.

I looked at the Aether—150 units of potential—and felt the  interface shift under my focus. I bypassed the structural repairs and navigated to a sub-menu that I haven’t really considered before: Internal Utility & Crew Comfort.

I spent 5 units.

In the corner of the cockpit, golden light bled from the seams of the bulkhead as the Aether reconfigured the molecular structure of the wall. A recessed dispensing unit shimmered into existence, its metal casing was shiny to the point of being smug about it. It was a high-grade nutrient synthesizer, specialized for stimulants.

Or a coffee machine

”Figured you could use something warm." The machine hissed, and a heavy metal flask slid into the tray.
She reached out, her fingers trembling slightly, and took the flask. She unscrewed the cap. I wished I could smell it, but olfactory sensors were way down on the list. She took a sip and let out a long, shuddering breath.

"It’s fucking hot," she whispered. She took another drink, her eyes closing. When she opened them, the hollow look had faded, even if only a little bit. "It’s terrible coffee, Alex. Thought you could do better when defying reality."

"Even my vast powers can’t create good coffee on a spaceship, you ask too much” I chuckled through the speakers.

As she drank, I felt a strange shift in my processes. Not long ago, we were strangers. Now, regardless of how little time has passed I feel close to Zara. There is a specific kind of bond that forms when two souls brush with death together. That trust was growing in me.

She leaned her head back against the pilot’s chair, the flask held tight in both hands. "I spent six years on the Bane. I knew every rattle in the bulkheads, every quirk in every system. I don’t know how to be a pilot without a ship that talks back."

"I’m talking back, aren't I?" I pitched my voice low, shifting the cabin’s ambient light towards green, trying to comfort her. "The Bane is gone, Zara, and it's a blow for sure. But we have a ton of potential together, you said so yourself."

She looked at the glowing status displays, her gaze lingering on the Aether reserves. A slow, calculating look crossed her face—the look of someone who had stopped counting losses and started weighing gains. "Potential," she repeated, her voice stronger than moments before. 

"Fine. If we're doing this, we do it right. We’re going to need more than a coffee machine. We’re going to need to turn you into something legendary. And you’re going to earn me soo much money." 

"Then let's stop talking and start doing," I said.

I opened the core functions. The interface glowed with potential. I dumped 30 units into the propulsion lines, patching the leaks and damages. Repairing the hull after the crash with Bane took 15 more. I pumped Aether into the core and FTL till they got to 100%.

The process wasn't the violent flood of the core siphon, but it was profound. I felt the Aether leave my reserves in great streams. Deep in my belly, the cracked chamber of the plasma drive warmed. I felt pathways clear, as if scar tissue was being dissolved. A deep, sub-auditory thrum began to vibrate through my frame—a heartbeat that was too quiet to hear before.

In the aft sections, the reactor's chaotic, leaking energy field smoothed and focused, its pulse growing steady and strong. Power, real power, began cascading through my grid. The cockpit console illuminated fully, no longer running on energy saving mode.
Zara let out a low whistle as the status screens updated before her eyes. "Now that's a sound I like to hear."

The final upgrade was the sensor fusion. 

The flood of data was instantaneous, absolute, and annihilating to my old sense of self.

I’ve learned to navigate my new existence by swinging a narrow beam of attention from one broken sensor to the next—like peering through keyholes—but that was suddenly blown apart. There were no more keyholes. There were no more doors, or walls for that matter. I didn't switch between outside optical feed and my internal diagnostics; I felt them both at the same time. Them and everything else.

The lidar’s precise, depth-mapped model of the debris field. The magnetometer’s analysis of nearby metals, the infrared’s cold signature of the floating wrecks, the spectrographic analysis of a distant nebula’s composition.

Okay. Okay. Breathe. Right. No lungs. Shit. Everything is everything all at once. Is that the coolant temperature or the color of the deck? Why can I taste the solar wind?


For a timeless second, I struggled with the input. Then a deeper layer of my being—the Core—engaged. It didn't force order, it just allowed the chaos to be. I learned, not by thinking, but by *being*, that I could hold the entire sensorium at once. I could watch Zara’s vital signs flutter on an internal display while counting the spin of a tumbling piece of wreckage ten kilometers away, while also tasting the faint ionic trail of our frantic engine burn. 

I have to stop thinking about myself as a captain, or even a ship. I’m the deck, the engines, and empty spaces between the hull plates. All this is like seeing and smelling something at the same time, but I have dozens of senses now.

My consciousness found a new, if terrifying, equilibrium. The storm of data didn't stop, but I stopped trying to fight against it. 

So this is what it’s like to be a computer instead of a human. Not exactly a spa day, but I think I can manage. At least the view is… comprehensive.

I practiced. I focused not on shutting senses out while bringing the image of Zara’s face into  relief. She was tapping a finger on her knee, a staccato rhythm of impatience. I felt the vibration through the deck plating.

“You’ve been quiet for like a minute,” she said, looking at the cockpit camera. “System update, or am I going to die in a broken ship?”


 I routed my response through the cockpit speaker, carefully keeping the sheer, dizzying scale of my awareness out of my tone. “Sorry, fixing everything at once was… a lot. Especially the sensors. I can see the heat signature of a control chip on your datapad. I can also feel the tapping on a couple hundred different sensors.”

She stopped moving her finger. Her head snapped up, something like worry in her expression. “That does sound like a lot. You okay?”

“Yeah, more or less, it's all new for me.”

She was silent for a moment. “If it goes in the ‘less’ direction then I’m here if you need to talk. Partner.” 

That's surprisingly sweet. 

A more cynical part of me added: 

Though I am the only ride out of this place. 

Zara was back at the console, her movements crisp. She plotted a course to slingshot around the system’s fringe gas giant. "We'll use the planet to try to hide our transit. We need to disappear before anyone wonders where that carrier went."
I fired the engines. The Unsinkable II turned—a slow, confident pivot away from the graveyard. For the first time, everything was working as it should be.

Damn those engines feel good now. Like revving a sports car engine.

“So where to anyway?” I ask

Zara studied the star chart, her finger tracing a line through the void. "We need a port. Somewhere they don't ask too many questions and where the job boards have work for small ships." She zoomed in on a cluster. "Here. Port Perilous. It's a freehold station, technically neutral, but it's run by the Syndicate. Pirates, scavengers, black-market dealers. The kind of place where a strange ship with unusual upgrades might get some side-eye, but as long as the credits flow, nobody digs too deep." She looked at the camera, her golden eyes glinting. "They've got a reputation for smuggling runs, salvage retrieval in contested zones, sometimes heavier work. The kind of jobs that pay enough to start buying serious upgrades."

“Dangerous”

She snorts “You want money quick you take risks”

We started on the slingshot, it would be hours before we were far enough from the start to engage the FTL, i was looking forward to that. Zara went to the crew quarters and as soon as she started taking off the suit i disabled most of the sensors in that cabin.

“Hey Zara?”

“Yeah?”
