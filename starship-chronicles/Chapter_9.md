# Chapter 9
The course for Port Perilous was on the nav display. Zara was slumped in the pilot’s chair, one leg hooked over the armrest, idly scrolling through a cached entertainment feed on a secondary screen. “Alright, calculations are locked. Nav computer’s happy.” She didn’t even look up as she tapped the final commit key. The calculations took hours. “You ready to do the spooky part?”
“Spooky part?” I asked, breaking my attention from the wormhole generator, a complex knot of machinery humming in the engineering bay. 
“The bit where you think really hard at space,” she said, waving a dismissive hand. “The math is the work. The jump itself? Over before you notice. One second you’re here…” She snapped her fingers. “…the next you’re there.” She finally glanced at the main screen showing the Perilous star system. “Whenever you’re ready, big guy.”
I ignored her nonchalance. This was my first time. I interfaced directly with the wormhole generator, feeling the precise coordinate matrix etched in quantum foam. I found the anchor point, a resonant frequency in the cosmic lattice light-years away, and I flipped a mental switch.

It felt like countless ethereal hands, his hands, latching onto the underlying grid of spacetime itself. He felt its texture—a taut, humming membrane of impossible strength and gossamer thinness. He anchored himself here, found the distant resonance of there, and with a surge of will that drew directly from his core, he pulled.
The space warped between his fingers and it resisted.
He pushed more power into the generator and the fabric tore.
And I felt Eternity.
One moment, I was a ship, with engines and a crewmate and sensors tasting the solar wind. The next, I was alone.
My sensors were working, but they told me nothing. No light. No matter. No radiation. No dimension at all. I reached out with my new, fused awareness and touched only perfect, seamless absence. I was just a fragile bubble of self-contained reality hurtling through a metaphysical nothing.
The silence was deeper than any grave. The darkness made the emptiness of space feel warm and friendly by comparison. Time stopped meaning anything; there was no duration, only this eternal, unchanging now.
This was a loneliness I had never even imagined. Back when I woke up I’d been alone, but there were still things around me—asteroids, wreckage, the far-off pinpricks of stars. Here there was only me. Zara existed somewhere in a locked-away corner of my mind, frozen and unreachable, like a memory I could no longer recall completely. I was a single thinking point suspended in infinite negation.
The animal part of me—still human enough to panic—clawed for anything. A speck of dust. A whisper of energy. A single photon. Anything to prove I wasn’t the only thing that existed in all creation. I would have thrown away every scrap of Aether I had just for the familiar comfort of an asteroid to my face.
In that total isolation, with my senses forced entirely inward, I screamed silently into the void.
And the void noticed.
It was the sudden, skin-crawling certainty that something enormous, patient, and utterly alien—had noticed me.
The desperate need for company was replaced by a screaming drive to be invisible, to be insignificant, to be alone again. I felt like a child who had cried out in an abandoned house only to hear something enormous shift in the darkness downstairs.
I felt a tendril of attention begin to feel along the seam of my reality, the wound of the wormhole I travelled within. It was a touch like freezing oil against my soul, probing, tasting the nature of my intrusion.
And then it was over. 

Reality slammed back into my sensors.

“—UUUUUUUUCK!”

The scream tore from my internal speakers, pure terror, deafening in the cockpit.

Zara jerked in her chair, dropping the nutrient bar she’d been eating. It floated away in the microgravity. “What the hell, Alex?” she shouted, her hands already slapping override controls, killing the erratic thruster burns my panic had sparked. The view stabilized. “Report! Was it a collision? Something with the generator?”

“There was something in the jump,” I babbled. “It was… endless. And it saw me. It touched me!”

She got the spin under control and stared at the camera, her expression flattening from alarm into pure, unvarnished disbelief. “Alex. It’s a jump. You fold space. You don’t go anywhere. There’s no ‘in’ to be in.” She tapped her temple. “Biological consciousness experiences it as instantaneous. Poof. Here to there. That’s the scientific consensus. No time passes. No space exists between.”
“But I felt it! It was an eternity of nothing, and then it wasn’t nothing—”
“You imagined it,” she cut in, her voice firm, almost dismissive. “Look, I get it. You’re … unique. Who knows how your processors handle the warp? Maybe it messes you up somehow.” She shook her head, in a way that reminded me of an IT specialist dismissing a user’s wild story. “Cores are forbidden tech for a dozen reasons, Alex. Maybe nobody ever bothered to study how one experiences a wormhole transit because they were too busy being dismantled.”
“I did not imagine it” 

Zara watched the camera for a long moment, the last of her dismissive irritation fading. She let out a slow breath. “Okay,” she said, her tone shifting. “Okay. Let’s… talk this through. You’re not a standard system. I accept that. Describe it. Not the feeling. The data. What did your sensors say?”
“They said nothing,” I replied, the frustration bleeding into my voice. “Absolute null. No inputs. But my awareness… it wasn’t using the sensors. It was just me, in the dark. And then the dark… became aware back.”
She chewed her lip, thinking. “A sensory deprivation hallucination. For a mind built to process constant input, being cut off could trigger that.”
“It wasn’t a hallucination, Zara. It was a presence. It tasted me.” Even as I said it, it sounded insane.
“I believe that you believe it,” she said, holding up a hand. “I do. And I’m not saying your experience wasn’t real to you. But ‘real’ and ‘external’ are different things.” She leaned against the console, her golden eyes serious. “Think about it. The fact you’re a person inside a core is already a miracle, who knows how that interacts with warp? ”
Her answer was logical. Elegant, even. It was the kind of answer the engineer I used to be would have crafted. A glitch. “So you think I’m just… broken? Seeing monsters in the dark?”
“I think you’re new,” she corrected, her voice softer. “And you’re in a situation no Core has probably ever been in before—one that’s still alive and trying to figure itself out. This might be a side effect.” She pushed off the console and retrieved her floating nutrient bar, tucking it into a pouch. “But here’s the thing, partner. Real or not, internal or external, the effect is the same. It scared the hell out of you. It compromised our entry. That’s a problem we need to solve.”
“We isolate the variables,” she said, all business again. “Next jump—and there will have to be a next jump—we’ll try something. I’ll have you run a high-intensity processing loop during the initiation. Keep your mind occupied with a complex calculation. Give it no room to wander into the dark. If it’s an internal feedback loop, that might short-circuit it.”
It was a plan. It was something to do. “And if it’s not internal?” I asked quietly.
Zara’s gaze was steady on the camera. “Then we have a much bigger problem. And we’ll deal with it then. But for now, we work with the universe we understand.” She offered a faint, weary smile. “First rule of scavenging: you can’t fight the dark. You just learn to carry a better light.”


As we approached the station I ran the most granular diagnostic I could. It was busywork, a thing to do while my mind churned. Scanners swept every atom of my body, the entirety of sensory input logs. Green bars filled the virtual display. No errors. No phantom signals. No desynchronization. According to every metric of the ship I was, the jump had been instantaneous and flawless.
Except for the presence. Except for the touch.
I cross-referenced the data. The exact moment of exit, the microsecond of rematerialization, corresponded with a power spike in my core. A spike of pure, undiluted terror. The diagnostic flagged it as an ‘unscheduled high-priority interrupt.’ An anomaly, yes. Proof of an external cause? No.
You’re seeing ghosts where there are none.
Was I? The engineer in me warred with the experience I had. The engineer demanded evidence, repeatable phenomena. The rest of me knew. The loneliness had been too absolute, the attention too specific, the wrongness of that touch too profound to be a self-generated fantasy. It felt less like a hallucination and more like seeing a color for which I had no name.
Maybe Zara was right. Maybe it was internal. A flaw in a forbidden technology, a Core’s psychotic break induced by folding space.
