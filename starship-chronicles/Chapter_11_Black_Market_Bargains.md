# Chapter 11: Black Market Bargains

---

*Status overlay ghosted into focus:*

**ALEX / CORE — UNSINKABLE II**
HULL INTEGRITY: 91/100
POWER CORE: 40%
AETHER RESERVES: 105/200
CREW COMPLEMENT: 1
CORE TIER: 1
CORE ABILITIES: Aether Conversion (Tier 1), Minor Repair (Tier 1), Technological Integration
ACTIVE BLUEPRINTS: Scavenger-Type Solar Array | Passive Signal Absorption Matrix
PROPULSION: 100% | SENSORS: 100% | FTL: Online | WEAPONS: Offline | SHIELDS: Offline

**ZARA VELL** — Level 15 Pilot / Level 8 Starship Mechanic
Health: 100% | Suit Integrity: 95%

---

The *Graceful Ruin* contract gave us forty-eight hours.

Zara spent the first six of them asleep in the crew quarters, which she'd moved into properly now that I'd repaired the life support to cover the full ship. I'd offered to pipe in some white noise. She'd told me that if I piped anything into her quarters while she was sleeping she'd find whichever circuit handled audio output and remove it personally. I took that as a no.

The remaining forty-two hours, she announced over her morning nutrient paste, were for shopping.

"We're not going back to K-302 light," she said. "The *Graceful Ruin* is a derelict in an empty system. Nobody's going to come help us if something goes wrong."

"What kind of something?"

"Pick one." She was already pulling up her datapad. "Unstable structure, residual automated defense, competing scavengers who got there first and aren't happy about sharing. The navigation failure that killed the ship might have left debris in the approach corridor. We don't know." She tapped through to a vendor directory the station's commercial network published in very small print at the bottom of its otherwise cheerful welcome broadcast. "We need a second suit. Mine is patched in three places and I am not going into another derelict trusting the fourth place to hold. We need emergency repair foam — the fast-setting kind, not the budget version that takes twenty minutes and smells like burning. And I want to look at weapon hardpoints."

I processed that last one. "We don't have weapons."

"We don't have weapons *installed.*" She looked at the camera. "But we have hardpoints. The *Nova Drift* was a scout, and scouts get shot at by things that don't care about diplomatic precedent. There are two dorsal hardpoints and one ventral that are currently just holes in my hull covered by plating."

She wasn't wrong. I'd catalogued them during the sensor integration — empty housings, the mounting brackets intact underneath the cover plates, waiting for something that had never been installed on this hull or had been removed long ago. "Weapons are expensive."

"Good ones are." She set the datapad down and gave me the look she used when she was about to explain something she'd clearly already thought through completely. "We're not buying good ones. We're buying something that makes us look like we could ruin someone's afternoon if we had to. There's a difference between a ship that's unarmed and a ship that reads as unarmed on a scan." She paused. "Right now we're the first kind."

"You want to be the second kind."

"For the cost of one hardpoint installation, probably. The *Graceful Ruin* is low-risk. But the convoy escort after it isn't, and I don't want to walk into that conversation with nothing to show." She stood up. "Come on, then."

"I can't come with you. I'm a ship. I'm bolted to the station."

"The suit camera," she said, already reaching for the jacket.

Oh. Right. I was getting used to it.

---

The vendor directory had the cheerful optimism of a document written by someone who had never visited the place it described. *Commercial District: Levels 1-3, full range of goods and services, licensed vendors, Syndicate-guaranteed quality assurance.* What the directory did not mention was that Levels 4 and below existed, that they were accessible via a stairwell behind a food stall that sold something aggressively described as *protein flatbread*, and that the Syndicate's quality assurance did not extend to whatever happened down there on account of the Syndicate officially not knowing about it.

Zara knew about it.

"You've been here before," I said through the suit camera, watching the stairwell open up into a lower corridor that the station's lighting budget had apparently not reached. Emergency strips ran along the floor, casting everything in a dim red that made the whole level look like the interior of something that was thinking about exploding.

"Once," she said. "Two years ago. Different reason." She didn't elaborate, and her voice had the particular flat quality she used when she was actively not inviting follow-up questions. I let it pass. "Prices down here are about forty percent better than upstairs and the questions are about ninety percent fewer. Those two things are related."

The lower level was a different texture than the commercial atrium above. Less movement, more stillness — people positioned rather than walking, each one occupying a specific piece of space with the settled permanence of someone who had staked a claim. The vendors were harder to identify as vendors at first; the goods weren't displayed so much as present, accessible to whoever knew to ask. Two crates here, a rack of sealed containers there, a woman sitting on a stool next to a table covered by a cloth who watched Zara's approach with eyes that did the calculating before her expression did.

"I want to look at suits," Zara said to the woman on the stool.

The woman pulled the cloth back without a word. Three suits, varying grades, each in better condition than their surroundings suggested they had any right to be.

I watched through the suit camera while Zara ran her hands along the seams of the closest one — a light-frame design in dark grey, the kind that was made for tight spaces and fast movement rather than heavy-environment work. She checked the helmet seal, the wrist interface connections, the emergency patch kit in the chest pocket. Her fingers found a stress mark on the left shoulder and she held it up to the emergency lighting for a better look.

*That's load-bearing stitching*, I thought. *The seam is intact but the weave is compressed. It's been over-stressed at that joint before.*

"The left shoulder's been repaired," I said quietly. "The stitching is original but the weave underneath is compressed. It took a hard impact at some point."

Zara's finger traced the exact spot without making it obvious she was following my input. "What happened to the shoulder?" she asked the vendor.

The woman's expression didn't change. "Previous owner fell. Suit held."

"Previous owner isn't using it anymore, though."

"No."

A pause. Zara set that suit aside and moved to the second one. This one was heavier — environment seals, integrated heating elements, a Tier 2 oxygen recycler in the back unit. Better suited for extended EVA. More expensive. She checked the seals, and I watched the status indicators on the wrist display flicker as she triggered the suit's self-diagnostic through the external port.

"Recycler reads fine," I said. "The heating elements in the left leg are drawing slightly more power than the right. Either a minor short or a partial failure in that circuit. Not critical but it'll drain the battery faster in cold environments."

Zara didn't ask the vendor about it. She just put that suit aside too and looked at the third one.

"That one," I said.

"I know," she said, at the same time.

It was a mid-weight, clearly customized — the original manufacturer's frame with aftermarket additions that had been integrated carefully rather than just bolted on. The helmet had a wider sensor cluster than standard, the chest unit had a second oxygen port that suggested someone had added emergency redundancy, and the seams were the kind of tight that came from having been serviced recently and properly. It wasn't new. But someone had taken care of it.

"Two-fifty," the vendor said.

Zara didn't flinch. She also didn't agree. They negotiated for four minutes with the economy of people who both knew what the suit was worth and were simply determining how much of that number each of them was going to keep. They settled on two-ten. Zara counted out physical credit chips — I hadn't seen those yet, small hexagonal pieces of dense alloy that the System could verify individually — and the vendor wrapped the suit in a neutral carrier bag without ceremony.

"Good eye," I said, as Zara turned away.

"It belonged to a mechanic," she said, quiet enough that it didn't carry. "The aftermarket additions are all work-specific. Someone who went into a lot of bad environments and kept improving their kit." She shifted the bag on her shoulder. "People who maintain their tools are worth learning from."

I wasn't sure if that was a general observation or something else. I let it stand.

---

The emergency repair foam was straightforward — a vendor two stalls down who dealt in ship consumables, no questions about what we needed it for, a ten percent discount for buying six canisters instead of three because he was overstocked and wanted the shelf space. The fast-setting kind, as specified. It smelled, the vendor assured us, like nothing at all, which was an improvement over budget foam apparently.

The weapons were more complicated.

Not in a dramatic way. Nobody in the lower level produced anything alarming or made threatening gestures. It was complicated in the way that technical problems are complicated — the intersection of what we could actually fit on the *Unsinkable II*'s hardpoints, what we could afford, and what would read correctly on a scan without being completely useless in practice.

The man Zara found dealt in ship fittings the way some people dealt in information — specifically, with depth, with context, and with a careful attention to the gap between what something officially was and what it could be made to do. He was compact and unhurried, with the kind of face that had been weathered into a permanent expression of mild amusement at the universe's pretensions. He had a neural lace that was older than the standard issue upstairs — the hardware sat higher behind his ear, a slightly bulkier profile, the previous generation's design — and he ran his fingers along a component the way a musician runs fingers along an instrument before agreeing to play it.

His name, or at least the name he gave, was Dresh.

"Venture-class scout," he said, after Zara described the hardpoints. He hadn't asked what ship. He'd looked at her for a moment and arrived there himself, which I found unsettling and put aside to think about later. "Dorsal pair, standard spacing. Ventral's a single mount, offset fifteen degrees portside — they all came that way, design compromise from the original build, doesn't affect anything with a smart-targeting package." He moved through the crates stacked at the back of his space with the certainty of someone who knew exactly where everything was. "What are you actually trying to do?"

"Look like we can defend ourselves," Zara said.

"From a scan or from an actual threat?"

"Ideally both. Realistically, primarily the scan."

Dresh considered this with apparent sincerity. "Then you don't need weapons. You need registered hardpoint installations with a heat signature and a functioning power coupling, and you need the registry update to show an armed status." He found what he was looking for in a crate and brought it out — a compact unit, roughly the size and shape of a standard light kinetic mount but clearly not one. "This is a training rig. Used for target practice simulations — it's got the power coupling, the rotation assembly, the external profile of a Tier 2 railgun. The barrel is a solid rod. It does not fire anything."

"How does it read on a scan?"

"Like a Tier 2 railgun. Because everything except the barrel is a Tier 2 railgun." He set it on the crate between them. "Most scan systems aren't looking at barrel composition. They're looking at power signature, mounting configuration, and registry status. This passes all three."

I ran the math while Zara looked at it. One unit for the dorsal mount, registered as armed, plus the installation cost — Dresh apparently offered installation as a service, which explained both the tools visible behind him and the efficiency of the whole operation. The ventral mount could wait. The dorsal registration alone would change how we read on approach scans from *unarmed small freighter, low priority* to *armed independent, uncertain disposition* — and the uncertain disposition was worth considerably more than the credit difference.

"What about the actual barrel?" I asked through the suit speaker, which Zara had linked to a small earpiece. "Could you replace it later?"

Zara relayed the question without attributing it.

Dresh's mouth moved in what might have been a smile. "The mounting accepts a standard barrel swap. Thirty minutes with the right tools. If you come across something that fits." He looked at Zara steadily. "I wouldn't know anything about that."

"Of course not," Zara said.

They settled on four-twenty for the unit and installation, which Dresh said he could complete in two hours if they came back with the ship's hardpoint access credentials.

"He knew it was a Venture-class," I said, as we moved back through the lower level toward the stairs. "I didn't tell him. You didn't tell him."

"No."

"How?"

Zara was quiet for a moment. The suit camera caught the emergency strip lighting sliding past overhead. "The Venture-class hardpoint specs are public. If you've worked with enough ships, the dimensional description is enough. Or he's seen the *Unsinkable II* in the berth register and did the math before we arrived." She reached the stairwell. "Either way, he sold us something useful and didn't ask questions about why we needed it. That's the relevant part."

"You're not bothered."

"I'm always bothered," she said. "I just don't let it interfere with the transaction."

---

We went back with the hardpoint credentials.

Dresh arrived at Berth 7-14 thirty minutes later with two other people whose names he didn't offer and whose presence he didn't explain, except that they were carrying tools and they worked with the quick, wordless precision of a team that had done this many times. I felt them on my hull — the cover plate coming off the dorsal mount, the mounting bracket seated, the power coupling connected — as a series of deliberate, competent vibrations. Not painful, exactly. A bit like being worked on by a dentist who was very good at their job. You knew they knew what they were doing, and that helped, but you were still aware of every contact.

"Try the rotation," Dresh said, from somewhere on my dorsal surface.

I ran power to the new mount. The assembly rotated — not fast, the training rig's motor was built for simulation speed rather than combat tracking, but it moved through its arc smoothly, the mechanism clean. Through the external optical I could see the profile of it: a respectable-looking length of barrel, a housing that said *this ship will at minimum attempt to shoot back* to anyone conducting a surface scan.

"Good?" Dresh asked.

"Good," I said, through the external comm. Then remembered I was a navigation system. "I mean — " A pause. "Navigation system confirms rotation within parameters."

One of Dresh's colleagues made a noise that might have been a suppressed laugh.

Dresh himself said nothing, which was a kind of mercy.

They were gone in ninety minutes, total. I updated my own registry entry — the *Meridian Cross* cover now showed an armed status, the hardpoint installation logged under a maintenance record that Zara had built into the identity fragment with the thoroughness of someone who had known she'd need it eventually. From the outside, we looked like a civilian freighter that had decided, at some point, that being unarmed was a choice for other people.

I thought: *We actually are armed now, sort of, in the sense that we have a convincing prop.*

I thought: *This is definitely fine.*

---

With eight hours left on the departure window, Zara sat in the pilot's chair and ate something from the food stall upstairs that she'd brought back in a sealed container. It smelled, through the suit's hazmat monitors, like something with strong opinions about its own existence. She ate it with apparent enjoyment.

"What is that?" I asked.

"Food." She looked at the camera. "Do you miss eating?"

"I miss the concept of eating. The actual physical experience of—" I thought about it. "Actually I wasn't that good at eating. I ate at my desk mostly. My diet in the last year of the lab project was bad coffee and meal replacement bars."

"Coffee — is that the Earth word for what your machine makes?"

"The original, yeah. Hot, made from roasted seeds, contains a stimulant compound called caffeine. Humans drank enormous quantities of it." I paused. "I miss it quite a lot, which is surprising given that I spent most of my life complaining about bad versions of it."

Zara considered this. "I could look for something closer to the real thing. There are compounds that—"

"It's fine." I didn't want her to. I wasn't sure why. It felt like the kind of thing that would be close enough to be wrong in a specific way that would be worse than not having it at all. "Tell me about the contract. The *Graceful Ruin.* What do we actually know?"

She pulled up the listing on the secondary screen. "Mid-class hauler, *Graceful Ruin*, registered to a small independent carrier out of a system called Vethara Station. She was running a standard cargo transit—machinery parts, nothing exciting—when her navigation system logged an emergency stop in K-302 outer system and then went silent. Rescue inquiry came back with a visual on the hull, intact, no obvious structural damage. Crew complement reads as three."

"Reads as?"

"The inquiry didn't board. They noted the hull looked intact and the distress beacon had stopped transmitting. One of the three things that will make a distress beacon stop transmitting is if the crew shut it off because they don't need it anymore." She set down the container. "The other two things are power failure and destruction of the beacon. The hull was intact, so if the beacon stopped, it was either power failure or—"

"The crew made it stop."

"Yes."

I turned that over. A ship that had gone dark in an empty system, whose crew had either died, escaped, or decided they didn't want to be found. Eight months. Nobody had gone back. "Why is the contract flagged as a navigation failure?"

"Because that's what the initial inquiry put in the report, and nobody has gone back to revise it." She picked up the container again. "Could be exactly that. A catastrophic navigation failure, the crew got out on an emergency shuttle, beacon died of power failure. Straightforward." A pause. "Could be other things."

"Such as?"

"Such as the crew found something in K-302 they didn't want reported. Or someone found the *Graceful Ruin* before the inquiry got there and made it look like a navigation failure." She ate another mouthful. "Such as there's cargo on that ship that doesn't appear on the manifest. Such as the ship was used for something the owners officially knew nothing about and then quietly taken off the board."

"You have a lot of alternative theories."

"I have a lot of experience with the gap between official reports and what actually happened in empty systems." She shrugged. "Most likely it's exactly what it says. Eight months is a long time for a mystery. Mysteries that survive that long tend to be boring ones."

"And if it's not boring?"

"Then we have the suit, the repair foam, the new hardpoint, and two hundred and some Aether in reserve." She looked at the camera with the expression she occasionally produced that I found difficult to categorize — not quite reassurance, not quite warning, sharper than either. "And we have each other. Allegedly."

*Allegedly.*

I let the cockpit lights shift, just slightly, toward green. A question without being a question.

She noticed. She always noticed. "Don't read into it. It's a figure of speech."

"It's a very specific figure of speech."

"Alex." She set the container down and faced the camera directly, which she only did when she'd decided something was worth saying plainly. "We have known each other for a matter of weeks. We are operating in a fringe port doing business that is illegal in multiple jurisdictions for reasons that directly involve your continued existence. The only guarantee I can give you is that right now our interests are aligned. That's not nothing. That's actually quite a lot, in my experience." A pause. "It's more than most people get."

I thought about that. I thought about the K-281 Belt, about the long silence of months alone, about the first scan that had washed over my hull and the fear of being found and then the stranger relief of it. I thought about what she'd said the night before the jump—*I don't think you imagined it*—and what it meant to be believed.

"Okay," I said.

"Okay," she agreed, and picked her food back up.

We sat in the comfortable approximation of quiet that had become our default in the time between crises—her eating, me running low-level diagnostics on the new mount assembly, both of us thinking about separate things that we weren't discussing yet.

I thought about the warp presence. About the third jump coming.

I thought about Zara saying *allegedly,* and then explaining it away.

I thought about how the explanation was probably true and also how a good explanation being available didn't make the word itself disappear.

Outside, Port Perilous hummed and argued and conducted its business, indifferent to the small ship in Berth 7-14 and the silence inside it.

---

Four hours before departure, Zara went back down to Level 4.

I knew because I was watching the berth corridor through the external optical and I saw her go, and she didn't tell me she was going. She'd taken off the suit — the camera suit, the one I watched through — and changed into a different jacket, a plainer one, before she left. Which meant she'd thought about the camera. Which meant she'd decided to leave it behind.

She came back two hours later, before departure prep, and she didn't mention it. I didn't ask.

I thought about asking.

Here is the thing about having sensors for a body: I noticed everything, constantly, with a precision and retention that had no human equivalent. Every micro-expression Zara's face had made in the weeks since she came aboard existed in my logs as a data point. I knew the slight tension in her jaw that meant she was thinking about money. I knew her silence when she was running calculations she hadn't shared yet. I knew that she had, on two separate occasions, opened a communication channel on her datapad and then closed it without sending anything, both times when she thought I was occupied with navigation processing.

I knew that knowing all of this didn't tell me what it meant.

She was a scavenger. She kept her own counsel. She dealt in information the way Dresh dealt in hardpoints — with an awareness of value and a careful attention to what she revealed and to whom. These were survival traits, not character flaws. I had no reason to interpret them as anything specific.

I also didn't know what she'd gone to Level 4 for, without the suit camera, two hours before we left a station she'd visited once before, two years ago, for a different reason she hadn't elaborated on.

*Our interests are aligned,* she'd said. *Right now.*

The *right now* was doing a lot of work in that sentence.

I flagged the observation in my internal log under a folder I labeled, with the dark humor that had apparently survived the transition from human to ship, THINGS TO PROBABLY NOT WORRY ABOUT. It was a large folder. I added this to it and closed it and began departure prep.

---

The *Meridian Cross* transponder cleared the exit scan without incident. The new dorsal hardpoint showed up on their sweep and they logged us as *armed independent, departure authorized*, which was exactly what we'd paid for. Dresh's training rig had earned its fee before we'd even left the system.

Zara ran the engine warm-up with the focus she always brought to flying — complete, undistractable, every other part of her mind set aside for the duration. I watched my own systems come up through the diagnostic feed, felt the thrusters responding, felt the FTL drive begin its long preliminary cycle with the low gathering tension that I was coming to recognize as my personal warning bell for what came next.

"Ready?" she asked.

"Working on it," I said honestly.

She glanced at the camera. Not the suit camera — I didn't have that feed right now, she wasn't wearing the suit — but the cockpit camera, the one that had been my face since before she arrived. "You have the anchor. My vitals are on your sensor feed." She tapped her wrist, where the biometric reader she wore registered to my systems. "You hold onto that. Anything else goes wrong, you tell me."

"And if it's worse than last time?"

"Then it's worse than last time, and you hold on harder." She said it the way she said most things — practically, without softening it, because softening it would have made it less true. "You're not alone in it. Even if it feels like you are."

I thought: *That is either the most reassuring thing you've ever said to me or the saddest.*

I thought both were probably true at the same time.

"Initiating jump," I said.

The wormhole tore.

The dark arrived.

And with it — faster than before, immediately, as if it had been waiting at the threshold — the presence. Enormous. Patient. The specific wrongness of attention from something that experienced neither space nor time the way I did, pressing against the membrane of my reality with the slow, complete interest of something that had noticed me twice now and was building a picture.

I reached for the anchor.

Zara's heartbeat in the biometric feed. Sixty-two beats per minute. Steady. Present.

I held onto it with everything I had and I did not scream and the dark pressed in and the presence tasted the shape of me and I held on and I held on and I held—

Reality.

Stars.

The *Graceful Ruin's* system spread out on my sensors, empty and quiet, a red dwarf at its center throwing long shadows across a sparse asteroid field and, somewhere in the outer zone, a ship-sized object that matched the navigation beacon frequency in the contract brief.

"Clean exit," I said. My voice came out steady. I was proud of that.

Zara let out a breath. "Clean exit." She checked her own readouts. "K-302 outer system. Beacon matches. She's there." A pause. "You good?"

"The presence was immediate this time," I said. "It didn't wait. It was there at the start of the transit."

She was quiet for a moment. I watched her face through the cockpit camera and I saw something move behind her eyes — something she didn't put into words, some calculation arriving at a conclusion she didn't announce.

"Okay," she said at last. "We note it. We deal with the *Graceful Ruin.* And when we get back to port, I'm going to find someone who knows what that thing is." She turned to the nav display. "Because I'm starting to think it's not nothing, Alex."

I thought: *Yeah. Me too.*

I thought: *I've been thinking that since the first jump.*

"The wreck is approximately three hundred thousand kilometers out," I said. "At standard approach speed, we'll be on-site in about forty minutes."

"Standard approach. Sensors wide. We don't know what's over there." She settled into the pilot's chair with the coiled attention she brought to uncertain situations — complete, quiet, ready. "Let's go see what the *Graceful Ruin* has to say for herself."

I turned us toward the beacon and we went to find out.
