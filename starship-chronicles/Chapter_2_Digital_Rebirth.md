# Chapter 2: Digital Rebirth

A single percentage point of power reignited my consciousness.

It wasn't a smooth awakening. It was a stuttering cascade, like someone was flipping breakers in a dark house, each one illuminating a new, unfamiliar part of my mind. One second I didn't know I had pressure sensors, the next I knew the exact pressure inside my hull — the same as the void's, as it happened. The temperature was 2.7 Kelvin. Hull stress was minimal.

What…?

I was back. Alex Rivera was operational.

Memory data loaded in a raw, unedited stream. The feedback loop. The damaged port emitter. The passing asteroid. Then, the shutdown warning.

Damn. I blew my own face off to survive. Didn't think a ship could feel pain. At least I didn't die. Again.

But where did the power to turn me back on come from?

My focus went to the power readout. POWER CORE: 1%. I dove deeper, into the << POWER SYSTEMS >> sub-menu.

- MAIN REACTOR: OFFLINE
- SECONDARY GENERATORS: OFFLINE
- CAPACITOR BANKS: 1% CHARGED
- EMERGENCY AUXILIARY POWER: ACTIVE

I selected the emergency auxiliary power log.

// SYSTEM LOG: STANDBY PERIOD //
// DURATION: 152 DAYS, 4 HOURS, 17 MINUTES //
// POWER SOURCE: PASSIVE THERMOELECTRIC GENERATORS (HULL-MOUNTED) //
// OUTPUT: 0.0003% PER STANDARD DAY //
// PROJECTED TIME TO 1% CAPACITOR CHARGE: 152 DAYS //

Five months

Five months.

The numbers hit with the force of a physical blow. I had been offline (Dead?) for one hundred and fifty-two days. The energy for my reboot had been scraped together over that time, harvested by hull plates converting the faint, fading heat differential between my own residual warmth and the void.

A lucky break. Something inside was still warm enough to matter.

The log displayed another estimate: forty years to reach full charge. It was a number so large it lost meaning. I operated on a different scale now — ship time, not human time. A thought surfaced. If I kept running, repairing, and replacing damaged systems, could I keep going forever?

Okay. Don't… think about that now. Leave the implications for later. Engineer. Problem. Solve.

I cross-referenced my current location with my drift projection. My course had shifted. The asteroid designated AS-734 was on my path. The estimated time until that proximity event was at one hundred and eighty-two days.

I immediately changed the designation to IMPENDING DOOM. 

That's more like it. So I have time to power up to a whopping 2.1% before I become a permanent feature on that rock's surface.

Aether was the variable. To get Aether, I needed power for the conversion beam. I had 1% power.

Time to spend it wisely

I reviewed the << CORE FUNCTIONS >>. The MINOR REPAIR option still required 5 Aether. I remembered my initial, clumsy attempt at a hull fix — I could manipulate the Aether raw, but the process was inefficient. The system also confirmed a one-way Aether-to-Power exchange at a flat rate of 1 Aether for 1% power.

My starboard sensors were online. I triggered an Aether Conversion scan, pouring a fraction of my precious 1% into the pulse. It was a weak, anemic thing, barely reaching a kilometer out. It returned two pings. A small, metallic asteroid with a potential of 2 Aether, and a diffuse cloud of frozen gases worth 1.

Three Aether. It would cost me most of my power to get it.

The choice was simple. Spend power to get Aether, or conserve power and wait. Waiting meant accepting the timeline of months and years. I chose action.

I targeted the metallic asteroid. The beam was a thin line of light. Power dropped. 0.8%. 0.5%. The asteroid dissolved into particles. 0.3%. The Aether streamed into my reserves.

AETHER RESERVES: 2/50
POWER CORE: 0.2%

I was near shutdown. I immediately converted 1 aether to power. I targeted the gas cloud.

AETHER RESERVES: 2/50
POWER CORE: 1.2%

I had two units of Aether. Pure gain.

My decision was what to do with the Aether. Convert it to power, temporarily improving my situation. Or use it for a permanent change.

I examined the << POWER SYSTEMS >> again. The Main Reactor was a network of critical failures. The Secondary Generators included a bank of solar collectors. Their status was FULLY OCCLUDED BY DEBRIS. EFFICIENCY: 0%.

I focused on the solar collector system. I pushed the two units of Aether toward it, not using the formal repair skill, but guiding the energy with raw intent to clear the debris. It was an imprecise, wasteful process. I felt energy dissipate uselessly into the void, the sensation made me grind my nonexistent teeth. But then, a fragment of hull plating broke free. One collector panel unlocked and began to track slowly.

<< STATUS UPDATE >>
SECONDARY GENERATORS: EFFICIENCY 0% -> 12%
POWER CORE RECHARGE RATE: 0% / DAY -> 0.06% / DAY

The change was significant. It was also permanent. 

If I overspend, I'll lose only 2 weeks. Now that there is some breathing room, it's time to take a look under my own hood

I began a methodical survey of my own systems. The registry listed me as a Venture-class scout vessel, previously the SS Nova Drift. The logs were corrupted. A final distress signal was a garble of static. My only hint to a past life was the black and yellow paint scheme I'd seen on my hull.

I mapped the damage. The hull was a patchwork of points of failure. The port sensor array was gone. Life support was offline. As was navigation, medbay, water reclamation... The list was long and depressing.

Let's try from the other end. List all operational systems, or the ones that are functional but powered down.


<< SYSTEMS STATUS: POWER-SAVING MODE >>

// CORE SYSTEMS //
- AI CORE: OPERATIONAL (POWER CONSERVATION MODE)
- MAIN COMPUTER: OPERATIONAL (MINIMAL PROCESSES)

// POWER SYSTEMS //

...Solar panels and passive electricity — blah blah...

// PROPULSION //
- ATTITUDE THRUSTERS: 8/8 OFFLINE 

// COMMUNICATIONS //
- SHORT-RANGE RADIO: OPERATIONAL (NO TARGETS)
- DISTRESS BEACON: CYCLING (INSUFFICIENT POWER)

// SENSOR ARRAYS //
-  OPTICAL: OPERATIONAL (REDUCED COVERAGE)
- SHORT-RANGE RADAR: OPERATIONAL (LIMITED RANGE)
- MAGNETOMETER: OPERATIONAL (MINIMAL SCANNING)
- SURVEY PROBES: 2/4 AVAILABLE (FULLY FUNCTIONAL)

// INTERNAL SYSTEMS //

Yeah, yeah...wait.

I scrolled back up mentally. 

Jackpot! God damn probes!

They were reconnaissance models. They could operate beyond my sensor range. The primary launch mechanism was a magnetic rail that required a power spike of 0.5%. But there was another way. After some menu-surfing and ignoring a prodigious number of warnings, I managed to load one probe into the launch tube. I manually released the clamps and gave it a minimal nudge with its own onboard engines

The view from my only working external optical sensor was the most beautiful thing I had ever seen. The silver dome of the probe eased out of the launch tube like new life being born, its sensor petals unfolding silently in the vacuum. Diagnostics confirmed it was fully functional.

I established a low-bandwidth laser link. The data feed was slow, but it was a new set of eyes. Better ones.

The probe's sensors began painting a broader, more detailed picture of the asteroid field. It confirmed my drift trajectory toward IMPENDING DOOM with greater precision. It also identified several larger Aether signatures I'd been unable to detect with my weakened main scanner — chunks of ore with 5 and 7 potential, too far for me to reach with just the range of the aether beam.

Then the probe scanned a new sector. A debris field. The remains of a larger ship, its structure broken open like a carcass, surrounded by a cloud of fragments. The probe's scanner lit up with multiple Aether signatures. 3, 5, and one powerful signal from the mangled engineering section: 150 Aether.

The debris was dense and it could easily damage my hull.

It was a treasure trove. It was also a deathtrap.

I directed the probe to orbit the wreckage, mapping its contours.

I turned my attention to other systems. I needed a complete understanding of my capabilities and constraints.

The << PROPULSION SYSTEMS >> menu was a list of failures. The primary plasma drive was offline, its manifold cracked, its reaction chamber cold. The thruster array was just as dead, fuel lines frozen, ignition sequencers broken. Propulsion Efficiency was flipping between 2% and 0% because the system wasn't entirely deceased — just utterly unresponsive. I was a leaf on the cosmic wind.

The << SENSOR SYSTEMS >> menu was more detailed, but no less grim. The port array was gone. The starboard array was operational, but limited. I had a standard radar for detecting solid objects. A lidar for finer detail. A magnetometer for detecting metal and power signatures. Most of these systems were running at low power, their range reduced. The probe had a better sensor suite than I did currently.

The << COMMUNICATIONS SYSTEMS >> were a ghost town. I found the main subspace transmitter, or what was left of it. According to the sensors, it looked perfectly fine, if one ignored a 12 centimeters hole right through the middle of it. 

Looks like some high velocity shot went through here. I'd be happier that railguns exist if I wasn't currently suffering the consequences of being shot with one. But there is something even more interesting here.

Subspace. The word was familiar from a hundred sci-fi stories, but the reality was sitting shot in my hull. My old life at OmniCorp had been about squeezing fractional efficiencies from fusion reactors. The idea of manipulating the fabric of the universe itself was so far beyond my pay grade it was laughable. Old Man Henderson in Theoretical would have sold his soul for a glance at this. I could almost smell the stale coffee and whiteboard markers from the R&D bullpen.

I focused my intent on the term "Subspace Transmitter" and queried the system.

The interface flickered, text scrolling in its cool, impersonal font.

// SYSTEM RESPONSE: GENERAL OVERVIEW //
// TECHNOLOGY: SUBSPACE TACHYONIC FIELD PROJECTOR //
// FUNCTION: NON-LOCAL INSTANTANEOUS COMMUNICATION VIA QUANTUM-ENTANGLED TACHYON BUNDLES. //
// RANGE: THEORETICALLY UNLIMITED. PRACTICAL RANGE DETERMINED BY PROJECTOR POWER AND FIELD COHERENCE. //
// PRIMARY LIMITATION: REQUIRES A PRE-ESTABLISHED SUBSPACE BEACON NETWORK FOR TARGETED COMMUNICATION. BROADCASTS INTO UNMAPPED SPACE ARE POSSIBLE BUT RECEPTION IS STATISTICAL. //

I digested this, my education trying to wrestle the magic tech. Tachyons. Theoretical particles that always move faster than light. Of course. They don't break causality; they just ignore it. I wondered how much power it would take to send an email to a different spiral arm.

But it's not just power. Even if I could fix this thing, who's listening? The "pre-established network" is probably run by whoever built me, and judging from the amount of micrometeorite pockmarks on the hull, I've been here for a while. 

This one goes to the 'later or if desperate' file

The short-range radio worked, but there was nothing to talk to. The system log showed repeated, automated attempts to send a distress signal during my long sleep. All had failed, lacking the power to punch a signal through the chaotic mass of the asteroid field, let alone power the ruin of the main array. 

The old logs were intermittent, suggesting memory loss, so I couldn't say how long I'd been here. The oldest log was dated 20 years back by shipboard time.

So not only a junkyard, but an old junkyard. Maybe I'll have some worth as a fossil. I sighed mentally. Back to cataloguing

I checked the << CREW SUPPORT SYSTEMS >>. Life support was a glaring red icon. It didn't matter. I was the only crew that counted now.

The << COMPUTER CORE >> was myself. I saw my own architecture. Processing nodes. Memory banks. The core housing my consciousness was a spherical chamber deep within the ship's center. It was intact.

The << DAMAGE CONTROL SYSTEMS >> showed a network of internal sensors with gaps where wiring was severed, empty fire suppression tanks, and several emergency bulkheads sealed, isolating breached sections.

The << AUXILIARY SYSTEMS >> included things like internal lighting, environmental sound, and non-essential monitors. Most were deactivated to conserve power. I left them that way.

This thorough review took time. My power level crept upward. 1.2% to 1.3%. 

I turned back to the Aether problem. The 150 Aether unit was the target. But my current Aether Conversion beam was weak. I examined the AETHER CONVERSION function in the core menu. It was a Tier 0 ability. Its range, efficiency, and speed were all at 1, whatever that meant. Tier 1 would supposedly improve it. So would finding a way to put more power into the beam without causing a failure.

I considered the debris field itself. It was a hazard. A wrong move could cause more damage. My hull was at 22 integrity. An impact with a sharp piece of wreckage could lower that number quickly. I needed a way to navigate it.

The probe data continued to come in. It mapped the outer perimeter of the field. It identified a potential entry vector, a relatively clear path that led toward the wreck's broken stern. The path was narrow. It would require precise movement.

Precision required thrusters. Thrusters required repair.

I looked at the MINOR REPAIR function again. Cost: 5 Aether. I had 0. I needed to gather 5 Aether from smaller, safer targets before attempting the wreck.

My power reached 1.4%. I could attempt another scan and conversion. The risk of shutdown was still present.

I decided to wait. I used the time to analyze the composition of the nearby asteroids from the probe's data. I identified those with higher metal content, as they tended to yield more Aether. I created a priority list of targets within a one-kilometer radius.

My power reached 1.6%. I could launch the second probe but decided to save it for later. Instead I decided to use the power for Aether gathering.

I targeted a priority-one asteroid, a chunk of iron-nickel with 2 Aether potential. The beam fired. Power dropped to 1.3%. The Aether was absorbed.

AETHER RESERVES: 2/50

I waited again. The cycle was repetitive. Scan. Wait. Target. Fire. Wait. It was the work of a machine. I was a machine.

The thought surfaced, cold and clear, and for a long moment I just watched it, a passive observer in my own mind. 

I was a machine — and yet I was more. I still felt the familiar itch of impatience that had plagued me in the lab when one experiment or another was taking too long. I remembered the tapping fingers, the sighing, of getting up to pace. I never noticed the navigational lights were blinking to the pace of my mental tapping.

A different memory of frustration came. The third year at OmniCorp. The quarterly performance review. My supervisor, a man whose name I'd forgotten, but whose bland, placid face was etched into my memory, had looked over my project timeline.

"You're not a solo artist anymore, Alex. You're part of an orchestra. You need to learn the rhythm of the corporation. The tempo."

The "tempo" had meant pointless meetings, redundant approval layers, and sanding down any innovative edge until it was smooth and safe and utterly mediocre. I hated that tempo. I'd fought it, raged against it in my lab.

Humph...and that rashness killed me in the end.

I gathered two more units of Aether from a chunk of carbonaceous rock. The process was slow. My power regenerated to 1.5%, then was spent again to get the Aether.

AETHER RESERVES: 4/50

I was one unit away from a Minor Repair. I needed to decide what to repair. The thrusters were the obvious choice for navigating the wreck. But my power core was still critically low. A functioning thruster would consume power I did not have.

I checked the thruster system specifications. The attitude control thrusters were low-power devices.
A brief burst to change orientation would use a negligible amount of energy. The main problem was their current state of disrepair. They were not simply offline; their fuel valves were seized and their nozzles were clogged.

A Minor Repair could fix one thruster assembly. It would be a start.

I found another target: a small piece of debris with 1 Aether potential. I used my last bit of power to convert it.

AETHER RESERVES: 5/50
POWER CORE: 0.1%

I had the required Aether. I activated the MINOR REPAIR function and focused it on the port-bow thruster assembly. The structured energy flow was more efficient than my raw manipulation. I felt the system purge itself of blockages. A valve cycled. A nozzle was cleared.

<< STATUS UPDATE >>
PORT-BOW THRUSTER ASSEMBLY: ONLINE
PROPULSION EFFICIENCY: 2% -> 12.5%

It was a small change. But it was a tool. I could now, in theory, make a small adjustment to my vector. 

It's like balancing a pen on a finger with just a single thruster

I tested it. I commanded a short burst from the port-bow thruster. A vibration ran through my hull. My perspective, the view from my external cameras, shifted infinitesimally. Since I was already tumbling, the starfield moved a fraction faster. The power cost was barely noticeable.

It worked. I had a degree of control.

I now had a plan. Gather more Aether. Use Minor Repairs to bring more thrusters online. Increase my maneuverability. Then, attempt to navigate the debris field and reach the 150 Aether unit.

The timeline was one hundred and seventy-eight days until my close pass with IMPENDING DOOM.
