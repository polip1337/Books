# Chapter 5: Partners

The word "Partners" hung in the air between us heavier than a neutron star. Zara, my accomplice to a cosmic crime, stood in my core chamber, her golden eyes tracing the lines of my housing sphere with a look that was a bit too calculating for my liking.

"Alright, partner," she said, her voice losing some of its wary edge and taking on a brisk, business-like tone. "First order of business — we need a room that doesn't open to the void. The Bane's living quarters lost pressure three weeks ago. Sleeping in a vac-suit gets old. And you... well, you might not breathe, but having a pressurized compartment is useful for running diagnostics without a helmet — and you know, living.

She had a point. My entire ship was a freezer. "The cockpit," I suggested. "It's the smallest enclosed space. Biggest hull breach is on the starboard side, but the interior door seals might still hold."

"Let's find out," she said, all business. "I'll get my kit. You... do whatever it is you do. Be ready to power up work lights."

The next few hours were a crash course in interspecies cooperation. Zara was nothing if not efficient. Guess she had to be, to be a solo scavenger. She retreated to her ship, the *Starlight's Bane*, and returned with a portable welding torch and a diagnostic kit that made my internal sensors look like stone tools. I, in turn, became a glorified toolbox and light source, using my control over internal systems to power up work lights where she needed them and rerouting what little energy I had to the cockpit's environmental systems for testing.

It was strange, having a physical presence inside me. Every footstep, every clang of a tool against my bulkhead, sent minute vibrations through my frame, a constant reminder that I was no longer alone. It felt good, even if I was a piece of floating machinery.

I watched through the cockpit camera as she worked. She sealed a hairline crack in the viewport frame with quick, precise bursts of plasma, the actinic light flaring in the camera feed. 
"Your turn," she said, not looking up as she tapped the bulkhead next to the interior door. "Run a deep integrity check on this door seal. My scanner's reading the polymer as brittle. I don't fancy being blown into the corridor because of a faulty gasket."

"Uhm... how do I do that? I don't have any sensors inside."

"Typical Venture-class. Built cheap, died young." She sighed, unpacking a spool of sealant gel from her kit. "Don't flicker the lights."

As she worked, I couldn't hold back my curiosity any longer. "Zara. Back there... you said I wasn't allowed to exist. That I'm a 'Core'. What did you mean? And you mentioned a 'System' too."

She didn't look up from her work, applying the gel with practiced motions. "The System is just... how things are. It's the universe keeping score. For biologics like me, it's an interface. It gives us readouts on our health, our skills, the tech we're using." She tapped her temple. "I see my oxygen levels, my suit integrity, the Tier rating of this torch in my hand. Everyone has one. Over a certain threshold."

"Threshold?"

"Yeah, non-sentients, like animals, have it too. Though a different, more instinctual version. The jury is out over whether bacteria or viruses have one."

*A power leveled malaria? Now that's a scary thought*

"You mentioned tech levels?"

"It's the System's way of grading technology. A brand-new, top-of-the-line radar might be Tier 5. After a few years, when something better comes out, it might get downgraded to Tier 4. It's not permanent. It's a reflection of where a piece of tech stands in the current galactic pecking order." She finished the seal and looked at the camera. "Your little trick, the Aether conversion... that's Core tech. Forbidden tech. The System handles it differently. I don't know the details—nobody does, since you were all supposed to be scrap—but it's clearly giving you menus, costs, abilities. Right?"

"Right," I admitted, feeling exposed. My game-like interface was a topic that felt private. Dangerous to talk about, though I couldn't really skip this one if we were to work together.

"Yeah,” I said,” I have two core skills, one for gathering and one called minor repair."

 "Can you use it on my ship?"

The question sent a thrill through me. I focused my awareness, pushing it beyond my own hull, through the docking arm clamped to my side. I found a damaged sensor array on her ship, highlighted it in my mind, and willed the **MINOR REPAIR** function to activate.

Nothing happened. A system message flickered, red and definitive.

**// ERROR: TARGET VESSEL CORE IS ACTIVE. //**
**// AETHER-BASED MANIPULATION IS INCOMPATIBLE WITH ACTIVE BIOLOGICAL/ TECHNOLOGICAL INTERFACES. //**
**// UNAUTHORIZED CORE INTERFACING HAS BEEN LOGGED/**

I relayed the message to Zara, a wave of frustration washing over me. "It's blocked. Says your ship's interface is 'active' and it's a violation. Also, what does the logging of the message mean?"

Her brow creased. "No idea, but doesn't sound good. We don't need anyone coming here. Don't worry about my ship — she's a tough old bird. She'll hold. Let's just get this cockpit sealed."

The welding and manual patching were done. It was time for the test. Zara had brought over a compressed air tank from the *Bane*. "Okay. I'm going to pressurize. If the door blows, I’m not paying for the damages"

"I will watch it like a hawk." I disabled all my other views and focused on the door.

"Like a what?"

"Uhm, a hawk? A bird with notoriously good eyesight?"

She shrugged. "You're weird." Then she opened the valve. With a loud, hissing roar that was shockingly alien after the perpetual silence, air flooded into the small compartment. I monitored the pressure climb. 

10 kPa... 50 kPa... 80... 

The seal held. We reached a standard breathable pressure.

"Seal is holding," I reported, a strange sense of accomplishment blooming in my core. We had made a bubble of life in this metal tomb.

"Good. Now, let's see if the life support system can scrub the CO2 I'm about to breathe into it." She unsealed her helmet, taking a cautious breath. "Damn you smell stale" She looked around the dusty, frozen cockpit. "You’re the messiest ship I know”


“Not like I have hands to dust the place. But you do. I'm sure there's a duster somewhere around. And a mop.”

“Okay, okay, no need to get all prissy. I’m just saying” She waved her hand, dismissively which made her tumble in the air. She didn't seem concerned. “Whats the status on the scrubbers?”


I pulled up the **<< LIFE SUPPORT >>** sub-menu. The atmospheric processor was listed as **OFFLINE - MULTIPLE COMPONENT FAILURES**. It was our highest priority now that the room was sealed, but the aether cost made me wince.

"I need to repair it," I told her. "It's half my Aether."

Zara whistled. "Steep. Why can’t something be easy for once?" She kicked the empty air tank. It slowly flew through the compartment. "Wish we had the spare parts. I could jerry rig stuff, but my ship is from a completely different design school, it's going to take ages to interface it."

“Hmph,” I added eloquently. I was deep in thought. Was it worth it to spend the aether now, or maybe there were better ways to do it? Like setting Zara on the scrubbers while I gathered more.

As I thought, I watched the tank slide through the air enjoying its weightless grace, awarded to such objects only by the lack of gravity. It made me wonder about all the detritus that has to be floating though the crew quarters, I haven't even taken a peek inside.

Meanwhile, the air tank bumped against the atmospheric processor's grate.

My system window was still focused on the **MINOR REPAIR** function, targeting the offline LIFE SUPPORT systems. But the message changed.

**// COMPATIBLE COMPONENT DETECTED: COMPRESSED GAS TANK (LIFE SUPPORT). //**
**// INTEGRATING RESOURCE... //**
**// AETHER COST REDUCED. //**
**// INITIATE REPAIR? Y/N //**

The cost had changed from 5 Aether to 4.

"It worked!" I exclaimed, the excitement clear in my voice. "The cost dropped! It's integrating the tank!"

"Do it," Zara commanded, her own eyes wide with interest.

I confirmed. The now-familiar warmth of the repair function flowed through the circuitry around the processor. But this time, it felt... smoother. More directed. I felt through internal sensors as wires re-knitted themselves with purpose, and the air tank dissolved into the aether gold, integrating it directly into the system. Its bulk got used to patch my internal gas tanks. A soft hum filled the cockpit as the atmospheric processor flickered to life.

**<< STATUS UPDATE >>**
**LIFE SUPPORT (COCKPIT): ONLINE**
**HULL INTEGRITY: 45/100 -> 50/100**
**AETHER RESERVES: 10/50 -> 6/50**

Zara checked her wrist scanner. "Air quality is improving. CO2 levels dropping. You just turned an incompatible part into particles and fixed your system. That's... so damn unfair!”

"It's the Aether," I said, realizing. "It's not just repairing putty. It used to create mass to repair the hull, it seems like even if the part is vaguely compatible, it uses its mass and reduces the costs."

“It's not how anything is supposed to work." She slowly grinned showing way too much teeth. "So we don't actually need fitting, or working parts. We just need... junk. Wait, I got an idea."

She jumped off and moved through the corridor using the handholds to her ship and came back with what looked like a small metal plate. She put it up to the broken piloting console.

“Does it lower the cost?” 

I checked, now understanding what she was going for.

//**MINOR REPAIR**//
//COST: 5 Aether //
//**PROCEED? [Y]//

“No, it doesn't recognize it as a valid part. Seems like we need the right kind of junk. Let’s try to find the threshold.”

We spent the next few hours in a frenzy of experimentation, our earlier tension forgotten in the thrill of discovery. Zara became a scavenger and a mule, raiding her own damaged ship for parts.

She brought over a damaged power relay from a non-essential system on the *Bane*. I used it with a **MINOR REPAIR** on my faltering internal power grid. Cost reduced from 5 Aether to 3.

She salvaged a cracked but working console from an escape pod. I used it to fix the helm. The Aether cost was 2, and the repair was flawless, the new device looking perfectly new.

It even has that new electronics shine. Ridiculous.

**<< STATUS UPDATE >>**
**HULL INTEGRITY: 50/100 -> 56/100**
**AETHER RESERVES: 10/50 -> 0/50**

It was a symbiosis. She provided the raw, physical components and the practical knowledge of what went where. I provided the miraculous, reality-breaking glue that made it all work.

By the time we were done, the cockpit was not just pressurized; it was functional. The lights were bright and steady, the air was clean, and my internal power grid was stable. My hull integrity had climbed to 56 — but I spent all my aether.

Zara stood in the center of the room, her helmet off, looking at the now-pristine control panels. "Not bad, Alex. Not bad at all." She turned to the main viewport, staring out at the colossal, broken form of the carrier wreck we had originally been targeting. Its 150 Aether core was still a siren's call, but it was no longer an impossible dream.

She tapped the viewport with a blue finger. "The carrier out there… its old. Valiant class from the Empire, like you. Top of the line at its time but three tiers out of date now. The class is still around, the joke being that you have to be really valiant to crew one of those. It's a fighter deployment and maintenance platform with just enough engines to not lag too badly behind the rest of the fleet. Minimal offense and defense capability.” Her eyes went unfocused and it sounded like she was reading from a wikipedia page. “Its parts will be more compatible, probably a better discount."

“How do you know so much about it? You know every ship class?”


She shook her head absently. “Nah, I just studied up on the way to this battlefield. Never know what piece of info might come in handy and let you survive. Besides, some of the Valiant class got repurposed as mobile pirate bases. I bet someone eyed this one too, but everyone deemed it too dangerous. I wonder why the Empire didn't scuttle it.”

“They probably didn't have Aether. The scavengers, I mean, no idea about the Empire.”

A silence stretched for what felt like eternity, but she was not saying anything more.

“Uhm, what Empire are we talking about? Is it evil?”

Zara’s boisterous laugh, sharp and sudden, cut through the quiet of the cockpit, causing me to flinch in surprise. She laughed so hard she actually curled up into a ball and rolled in the air. 
“Evil? What are you? Five?”

“Hey! It was a valid assumption!” Was that defensiveness in my tone? No way.

She kept on laughing, unable to stop. Finally, blessedly, she stopped and wiped a tear from her golden eye. “They are no more capital ‘E’ Evil than any next space power. They jostle for power, influence, and money. The usual stuff. Actual battles like this are quite rare.”

“Could you elaborate a bit?” 

I do feel like a child not knowing the first thing about this place. I don’t even know if she's telling the truth. The Empire might be killing puppies right now and I have no one to crosscheck it with.

She stretched in the air, as if laying on a comfortable couch.

“Sit down, or whatever you do, and listen then my to my wise words, ill try to use simple words but i dont have crayons”

I turned the lights off. The hum of life support stopped.

“Hey! Not funny!” She tried to glare at where she knew my camera was but she was off by a couple of degrees in the darkness.

“Partners, remember?”

“Can’t a girl have a little fun? Okay, okay, I’m sorry.” Then added under her voice. “Prickly little ship”

I considered turning the lights to the max, but decided she was sorry enough. And she had a gun. I set everything back to default. Air started flowing again.

“So, you mentioned that battles are rare?”


“Yeah, it has to do with how easy it is to defend a system. Wormhole tunnels connecting them are easy to detect. Wait, you know what a wormhole tunnel is?” 

Instinctively I hued the light in the cabin a bit greener.

I'm developing a body language. A ship language?

“Ill take that as a yes. So easy to detect right?”She was animated, I guessed being a scavenger was lonely work. ”And each one has a very specific vector you can exit on, which makes all kinds of fun stuff possible. Mines, kinetic rounds, prearmed single fire weapons.”

“Couldnt you send dummy ships, or some kind of mine sweepers, or even stones with engines through?”

“Of course there are ways to counter the defences, but due to the price of wormhole engines, it's prohibitively expensive. Even smaller powers can hold out against the heavy hitters. Or, at least, make it not worth it. There are even some pirate held systems that everyone knows about, but they are just too hard to dislodge.”

She looked outside at the looming carrier. “But first we need to get out of here.”

I followed her gaze. The wreck was more than a source of Aether. It was a hardware store. A treasure trove of "vaguely compatible parts."

"You're thinking what I'm thinking, partner?" Zara asked, her voice a low, eager murmur.

"I'm thinking," I replied, the phantom sensation of a grin spreading across my non-existent face, "that it's time we went shopping."
