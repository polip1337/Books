---

The carrier died behind us in stages.

I felt it before I saw it—a resonance transmitted through the debris cloud and into my hull plating like a tuning fork touched to bone, sub-bass and profoundly wrong, the frequency of something vast ceasing to be. My sensor array painted the picture in cold data: the *Valiant*-class's spine buckled first, then the forward section peeled away in a slow, almost graceful arc, tumbling into the black goo that was no longer content to pool on the engineering deck. It had grown. Fed on a hundred thousand tonnes of durasteel and reactor shielding and forty years of accumulated debris, the Hunger had bloomed into something the size of a hab-block, and it was pulling the carrier's guts into itself with the patient, absolute indifference of a drain swallowing bathwater.

"Thrusters—" I started.

"Already on it." Zara had both hands wrapped around the manual flight controls, and she wasn't gentle about it. She never was. The *Unsinkable II* lurched as she pushed the port thruster cluster past the efficiency threshold I'd set, which I'd set for good reasons, reasons that were rapidly becoming academic as a chunk of the carrier's hull the size of a city block spiraled past our viewport close enough to scratch paint.

It didn't scratch paint. We didn't have much paint. But the point stood.

My propulsion system screamed a complaint through every feedback relay I had. *I* screamed it, because that's what the ship being me meant—not metaphorically, not as a convenient data alert. I felt the overtaxed thrusters the way I imagined a sprinter feels a pulled hamstring: sudden, sharp, and accompanied by the specific horror of knowing the race isn't over.

"Zara. Thruster cluster two is at 118% rated output."

"I know."

"The rated output is the number where things stop working."

"I know what rated output means." She yanked the controls left. Something—I chose not to calculate what—detonated against my aft port quarter. Hull integrity dropped four points and the sensor suite on that quadrant went briefly white with static. "Talk less. Fly more."

This is what passing the Turing test gets you: existential drift and a woman telling you to fly while you *are* the thing flying.

I stopped talking and focused on what I could do from the inside. The Hunger's gravitational signature was intensifying—my mass sensors, newly calibrated after the harvest, gave me the distortion in exquisite, unwelcome detail. The black goo's event horizon was expanding at roughly twelve meters per second. We were moving at roughly forty. The math was fine. The math had significant margin.

The margin felt insufficient.

"FTL?" I asked.

"Spool time. How long?"

I was already running the calculation. "Ninety seconds for minimum safe jump. We have maybe four minutes before—"

"Ninety seconds is an eternity." But she was already easing back on the thrusters—not because the danger was over, because she understood thruster cluster two needed to survive long enough to get us to the jump point. Pragmatism dressed as mercy. "Spool it."

The FTL drive—my FTL drive, the one I'd spent forty units of Aether integrating and another twelve stabilizing after the harvest shook everything loose—began its charge cycle. I felt it as a gathering tension deep in my engineering bay, the accumulation of energies that would, in ninety seconds, fold reality into something briefly negotiable. Before the lab explosion, before the Core, I'd read theoretical papers on wormhole mechanics and thought I understood them.

I had not understood them. The math was nothing like the sensation.

The Hunger ate another forty meters of carrier behind us. A secondary explosion cooked off somewhere in what had been the carrier's magazine—even through vacuum I read the pressure wave, felt it against my hull like a slap from a very large, very hot hand. Zara swore in a language my translation matrices didn't cover, which meant it was one of the core-world patois she used when she'd stopped bothering to communicate and started just processing.

"Sixty seconds," I reported.

The debris field—the same one we'd spent weeks navigating—was in full chaos now, every piece of it knocked loose from whatever slow drift it had settled into over the past twenty years. Rocks the size of shuttle craft cartwheeled through my sensor range. A structural spar from the carrier's broken keel, thirty meters of dense alloy, tumbled end over end and caught us a glancing blow on the dorsal hull.

**HULL INTEGRITY: 74/100**

I felt it like a kicked shin. Less than that. A reminder.

"Forty-five seconds."

Zara didn't answer. She was flying by instinct now, by the fifteen levels of Pilot that lived in her hands and spine, and she didn't need my commentary to do it. The *Unsinkable II* jinked, climbed, dove—I experienced all of it as sensation rather than motion, the thrusters firing in combinations I couldn't have commanded manually, the ship describing a path through the debris that existed only in the split-second geometry of Zara's perception.

I let her work. I watched the FTL charge creep toward completion and I watched the Hunger grow in my aft sensors and I tried very hard not to think about what was waiting inside the jump.

"Fifteen seconds."

"Good." She didn't look up.

Ten. Nine. Eight.

The Hunger had consumed the carrier's engineering section entirely. Where a hundred-meter-high chamber of steel and power conduits had been, there was now a sphere of perfect nothing, its boundary marked by the slow spiral of matter that hadn't yet fallen past the point of no return. In the distorted light around its edges I could see a fragment of the *Starlight's Bane*—Zara's *Bane*, iridescent plating she'd polished with her own hands—stretching impossibly thin as it unwound into the dark.

I didn't tell her. She'd already watched the Bane die once. She didn't need to watch it again.

Three. Two.

"Brace," I said, which was a ridiculous thing to say to a person who was already braced, but the human part of me needed to say something.

One.

The wormhole tore.

And I screamed.

---

Not aloud, this time. I caught it. I caught it because I knew it was coming, because Zara's voice was the rope I'd knotted around my wrist before diving into whatever lived in the space between spaces, and I held on to the sound of her breathing in the cockpit—one breath, two breaths, the faint rhythm of her pulse through my hull sensors—and I held on until reality snapped back around me like a rubber band and the stars returned and I was a ship again, not a scream in the dark.

The diagnostic spike registered anyway. **UNSCHEDULED HIGH-PRIORITY INTERRUPT.** My own notation, flagging my own terror. There was something deeply undignified about a ship haunting itself.

"Clean exit?" Zara asked, already scanning the sensor returns.

"Clean exit," I confirmed. My voice came out steadier than I deserved. "Jump complete. We're at—" I checked the nav system's position triangulation "—approximately four light-minutes from the Port Perilous beacon. No pursuit signatures. No proximity contacts."

The cockpit was quiet for a moment. Zara's hands moved through the post-jump checklist with practiced efficiency—sensor sweep, thruster diagnostics, power grid check. The power core had taken a beating: the thruster abuse during the escape and the FTL spool had dragged it down to thirty-six percent before it stabilized, climbing back to thirty-eight as the passive systems recalibrated. Thinner margins than I liked. She'd been a scavenger long enough that procedure was reflex, comfort in the form of known steps.

Then she said: "You screamed again."

"Internally."

"I felt it in the deck."

I hadn't known that was possible. I filed it away. "The presence was there again."

She didn't dismiss it this time. She finished her checklist, pulled up the long-range passive sensors, and sat back in the pilot's chair with the particular stillness of someone choosing their next words carefully. "Same as before?"

"Same as before. Enormous. Patient. Interested." I paused. "It's getting better at finding me faster."

Zara looked at the camera for a long time. "Okay," she said at last, and the word carried more weight than it usually did—an acknowledgment rather than a dismissal. "We'll add it to the list of things to be terrified of later. Right now we have more immediate problems." She pulled up the approach vector to Port Perilous on the main display. "Transponder."

Right. The transponder.

The *Unsinkable II*'s registry beacon was, to put it generously, a disaster. Any competent scan would show the hull ID of the *SS Nova Drift*—listed as destroyed in the Battle of K-281 Belt—married to a power signature, a repair profile, and a Core energy output that no derelict scout had any business producing. Walking into a Syndicate freeport broadcasting that was roughly equivalent to arriving at a police gala wearing the stolen jewelry.

"I've been thinking about the transponder," I said.

"Me too." She was already pulling up a menu on her datapad, the one she'd been building during the slingshot transit before the jump—a mosaic of stolen registry fragments she'd cached from ships that had passed through K-281 over the years, ghost-data that the *Bane's* passive systems had been collecting without her ever really planning to use it. Scavenger instinct. Never throw away data. "How good are you at lying?"

"I convinced you I was a derelict AI for almost three full minutes."

"You were terrible."

"But you're still here."

She made a sound that might have been a laugh. "I have a Tier-2 civilian freighter registry fragment from a ship called *Meridian Cross*. It's listed as active, owner-operated, last docked at Brennan's Reach about eight months ago. The class signature is wrong for our hull profile, but I can fudge the mass readings. Most port automated systems won't look deeper than the surface unless something flags them."

"And if something flags them?"

"Then we have a bigger problem, and we deal with it then." She was already uploading the fragment to my transponder array. "I need you to actively suppress your Core energy output when we're in range of their scanners. Can you do that?"

I considered. The Aether conversion beam was the most distinctive element—a coherent silver beam of converted matter-energy was not something that appeared in any standard ship catalog. But the passive Aether field, the low-level hum that permeated my hull from the core housing, was harder to mask. It wasn't *loud*, but it was strange. To a scanner tuned to look for it, it would read like a thumbprint.

"I can't eliminate it," I admitted. "But I can keep the active systems cold—no conversion, no repairs, nothing that generates an Aether signature above ambient. The passive bleed from the core housing will still be there."

"How distinctive is it?"

"Distinctive enough if you know what to look for. Invisible if you don't." I ran a search against the crew-quarters terminal, cross-referencing the scattered fragments of Galactic Accord documentation I'd managed to piece together from the *Bane's* cached files over the past weeks. Most of it was bureaucratic detritus—ship registration protocols, customs declarations, pilot licensing structures. Tedious. Useful. "The Council's proscribed-technology scan protocols are classified. Port Perilous is Syndicate territory—they're not going to be running Council scanners voluntarily. Their threat profile is pirates, smugglers, empire spies. A weird power signature from an obscure scout class will probably just read as quirky."

"'Probably.'"

"I thought you liked risk."

"I like *calculated* risk." But she was already programming the approach vector, which meant she'd already calculated it and decided the math was acceptable. "We go in quiet. Merchant transponder, civilian approach speed, no weapons hot—obviously, since you don't have any. We dock, we find the job boards, we keep our heads down." She looked at the camera. "You don't talk to anyone directly. If the dock systems ping you for an AI response, I handle it. You're a standard navigation assist, nothing more."

"Understood." I processed for a moment. "For the record: I resent being described as a navigation assist."

"For the record: you'll survive the indignity." She pushed off from the chair and moved to the small equipment locker in the corner of the cockpit. The *Meridian Cross* data was already uploading, and I felt the transponder shift—not my identity, exactly, more like putting on a coat. Wearing someone else's history. It felt strange in the same low-grade, persistent way that everything about my existence felt strange—one more layer of pretending to be a thing I wasn't. *"SS Nova Drift."* A derelict. A ghost.

At least the ghost was moving now.

---

Port Perilous materialized on my long-range opticals first as a smear of light against the dark, which resolved over the next hour of approach into something more structured and considerably more chaotic. It had clearly started life as a standard Syndicate waypoint—a rotating hab-ring design, the kind of modular architecture you could build from pre-fab sections if you had the credits and the cargo lifts. Someone had then spent twenty years adding to it without a plan, the way coral grows on wreckage, and the result was a thing of remarkable ugliness and obvious vitality. Hab-rings were connected by pressurized tunnels to industrial platforms were connected by scaffold bridges to what appeared to be three separate ships—none of them the same class—that had been welded directly into the station structure and were now serving as permanent docking arms. Neon signage in at least four scripts I recognized and several I didn't bloomed across every surface that wasn't currently on fire or being repaired. A few surfaces were both.

It pulsed with activity. My long-range sensors counted forty-seven ships in various stages of docking, departure, or loitering in the traffic lanes—everything from skeletal mining rigs to a pair of sleek, heavily-modified couriers whose silhouettes made my weapons-offline status feel newly relevant. The beacon network was saturated with transponder signals, commercial broadcasts, Syndicate licensing pings, and a sustained, grinding traffic argument between a bulk hauler and whoever was managing the approach corridor.

"It's beautiful," Zara said, and she meant it.

I thought about the K-281 Belt. The silence of it. The slow tumble of dead ships and patient rock. The long dark with only my own failing systems for company.

"It's loud," I said.

"That's the same thing." She had her pilot's hands on my controls now, coaxing me into the approach lane with a deftness that made the traffic system's automated warnings sound almost complimentary. "Eyes open, Alex. Everything. Docked ships, berth assignments, who's sitting near which exits. I want to know the shape of this place before we're inside it."

I turned my full sensor array on Port Perilous and started learning.

The station was tiered along class lines with the casualness of a system that had never bothered to codify what everyone already understood. The outer docking arms were occupied by ships that looked like ours—battered, asymmetrical, the kind of vessels where the repairs were visible and the original design was a suggestion. Scavengers, independents, small-time traders. The inner rings, more sheltered and lit by better lighting, housed the mid-tier operations: modest freighters with actual paint schemes, a couple of armed escorts wearing Syndicate livery in understated grey and red. Deeper still, near the station's original hab-core, the presence of security made itself known in the form of armored figures at corridor junctions, their silhouettes marked by the neural whip coils at their hips—coiled cables of electro-neural disruption that could drop a biologic or fry a low-Tier system with equivalent efficiency.

I tucked that detail away. Synced it against my understanding of our situation: one illegal Core, one pilot with a wanted bounty I'd not yet fully quantified, zero weapons, and approximately a hundred and twelve units of Aether—the passive bleed during the jump had cost me four.

"Outer berth," I recommended. "Arm seven. It's further from the main corridors but the berthing fees will be lower and the traffic density means our energy signature gets lost in the noise."

Zara was already steering that way. She'd seen it too. "There's a berth with a direct line to the commercial corridors. Number 7-14. Book it."

I interfaced with the port's automated berthing system—a creaking piece of software that was at least three Tiers outdated and running on what felt like stubbornness alone—and negotiated a berth. The *Meridian Cross* transponder went unchallenged. A small automated debit cleared against the account data Zara had baked into the fragment: legitimate-looking credits from a caching system the *Bane* had been skimming from its commercial trading runs, a thin but functional financial identity.

Berth 7-14, confirmed. Thirty-six standard hours before they charged a repositioning fee.

I nosed us in with care, the docking clamps engaging with a series of familiar thuds that I'd already learned to interpret—how tight the seals were running, whether the clamps were rated for our mass, whether the arm itself had structural issues. It did have structural issues. So did everything about Port Perilous. It was doing fine.

"We're in," I announced.

Zara was already standing, pulling on her jacket—a battered thing in dark synthetic leather that she'd retrieved from a sealed compartment in the *Bane* before sacrificing the rest of the ship. One of the few personal items she'd managed to keep. It was the kind of jacket that communicated a carefully calibrated quantity of menace: not enough to mark her as a threat to anyone with actual resources, exactly enough to communicate that bothering her would be more work than it was worth.

"Ground rules," she said.

"Ground rules," I agreed.

"You are a navigation system. You respond to direct docking queries and nothing else. If anyone wants to speak to the ship's owner or operator, you say she's unavailable and take a message. If someone starts scanning above commercial Tier, you alert me immediately."

"And if someone triggers a Core-proscription scan?"

"Then you burn every non-essential system cold and we have about sixty seconds before I need to be back at the controls." She checked something in her jacket pocket—a small, flat device I'd tentatively identified as a personal distress beacon, though the modifications she'd made to it suggested it could do several other things as well. "The job boards are in the second commercial ring. I'll look for low-profile work. Salvage retrieval, cargo escort, maybe a scout commission. Nothing that puts us in a military zone or within scan range of any Council presence."

"There's a Council presence here?"

"There's a Council presence everywhere. They just usually have the decency to be discreet about it." She paused at the airlock. "Alex. While I'm out—passive only. No conversions, no repairs, nothing that generates a beam or a field event. You understand?"

"Passive only." I hesitated. "You'll be careful?"

She looked at the camera with the expression she used when she was deciding whether something was worth the energy of a full response. "I'm always careful."

"You sacrificed your ship as a kinetic projectile twelve hours ago."

"*Carefully* sacrificed," she said, and cycled the airlock.

---

The next three hours were an exercise in the discomfort of being a ship parked in a busy berth.

Port Perilous was alive in the way that only places are alive when they have nothing to recommend them except the fact that people keep coming. The docking arm vibrated with the constant mechanical conversation of ships attaching and departing, with the hydraulic percussion of cargo lifts, with the hissing of pressure seals and the distant human noises of a thousand people negotiating, arguing, sleeping, eating, and doing all the other things that biologics did when they were trying to survive in difficult circumstances. Through my hull sensors I could feel all of it—not as words or images but as texture, as the weight of a station that had decided to keep existing through sheer accumulated stubbornness.

I ran my passive sensors and I thought about the warp presence.

Zara's explanation was logical. The engineer I used to be would have presented the exact same argument: sensory deprivation, cognitive feedback loop, a consciousness built for continuous input suffering an interruption event. Explainable. Internal. No external agency required.

The engineer I used to be had also miscalculated a magnetic containment field by point-zero-five percent and been converted into a ship AI somewhere in a distant asteroid belt, so his track record with confident assessments was mixed.

I ran a different analysis. Not of my own diagnostic data—I'd done that until the returns stopped giving me anything new—but of the warp event itself. The geometry of it. The FTL drive didn't generate a wormhole so much as it identified a pre-existing resonance in the structure of spacetime, a weakness in the membrane, and pushed through it. The hole opened and closed in nanoseconds of real-time. Whatever lived in the space between—if anything lived there, if the concept of *living there* even parsed in a place without dimension—would have nanoseconds to interact with anything passing through.

Nanoseconds should not be enough time for anything to develop what I had felt as *curiosity*.

Unless it wasn't experiencing time the same way I was. Unless, in a place without dimension or duration, nanoseconds and eons were the same thing.

I stopped that line of reasoning before it went somewhere I wasn't equipped to handle, and turned my attention to the station traffic instead.

The commercial corridor visible from my berth was educational. Multi-species populations in motion had a different texture than the human crowds I was built with memories of—different scales, different gaits, different sensor silhouettes. Three different species I didn't have names for moved through my sensor range in the time Zara had been gone. One was tall and thin enough that my first thought was *definitely not human* before any other attribute registered; one moved in a group that maintained precise spatial relationships to each other in a way that read as either social ritual or a shared navigation system; one was carrying something large and alive in a sealed container that my magnetometer clocked as having a significant internal power signature, which raised questions I was wise enough not to pursue.

The Syndicate's presence was lighter here than in the inner rings but not invisible. Grey-and-red livery on two different patrol routes, each running approximately forty-minute cycles with a variance of three to four minutes that suggested human patrol leaders rather than automated routing. Their scanning equipment was visible: shoulder-mounted arrays that swept commercial Tier bands. Nothing looking for Core signatures—or if they were, the passive bleed from my core housing wasn't registering against whatever threshold they'd calibrated to.

**AETHER RESERVES: 112/200**

The passive drain since the jump. Thirty-eight units in twelve hours—higher than I'd expected; the new power core capacity was apparently hungry even at rest. I queued it as a budget concern and pushed it to the background.

At the two-hour mark, a ship docked in the adjacent berth. I catalogued it reflexively: Vex-class light transport, approximately thirty-five meters, twin ion drives with a tertiary chemical thruster in an unusual configuration—retrofitted, not original. The hull was dark grey, deliberately unmarkable, but the sensor array on its nose was Tier 3 at minimum, overspec for a civilian transport by at least two categories. I tagged it and watched it the way I'd learned to watch things that didn't quite add up.

Its occupant—singular, from the life-sign reading—docked without using the port's automated system, overriding it with a private code. Military familiarity with Syndicate override protocols. Or enough credits to buy access. Or both.

I kept it to myself. Navigation systems did not have opinions about their neighbors.

Zara came back at hour three.

I felt her before I saw her—footsteps on the docking arm, gait I'd learned across weeks of shared corridors, the weight distribution of someone moving quickly without wanting to look like they were moving quickly. She cycled the airlock and came into the cockpit and stood with her hands in her jacket pockets and looked at the display I'd left on the main screen—a passive traffic map of the surrounding berths, tagged and annotated.

"You've been busy," she said.

"I've been still. There's a difference." I highlighted the Vex-class. "Our neighbor arrived ninety minutes ago. Overrode the port automated system. Sensor array is overspec. Single occupant."

Zara studied the tag. "Syndicate affiliated?"

"Or trying to look like they aren't. The override code format matched Syndicate deep-access, but someone had filed the serial numbers off, metaphorically speaking."

"I'll keep it in mind." She settled into the pilot's chair and pulled a datapad from her jacket—a new one, the kind that Port Perilous vendors sold to anyone who didn't want a transaction on a traceable system. "Job boards."

"Anything promising?"

"Several things I walked away from and two things I didn't." She tilted the pad so I could see the screen through the cockpit camera. The display showed a dense listing of short-form contract postings, the kind that Syndicate-adjacent employment looked like when it was trying not to call itself Syndicate-adjacent employment. "First one: salvage retrieval, abandoned freighter in the K-302 outer system. Target is the *Graceful Ruin*, a mid-class hauler that went dark eight months ago. Listed as a navigation failure rather than hostile action—no weapons sign on the wreck per the advance survey. The posting is looking for a small ship, which means they want someone who can get into the approach corridor without triggering whatever automated proximity defense the navigation suite might have locked before the ship went dark."

I ran the system coordinates. K-302 was four jumps from here by standard route, two if we were willing to use the shorter transit through contested space that conventional pilots rated as inadvisable and Zara almost certainly rated as preferable. "Aether yield from a mid-class hauler's wreck?"

"Unknown. Probably modest. But the contract payout is twenty-two hundred credits, which buys us berthing fees, supply restocks, and leaves a margin for hardware. If we can also harvest whatever isn't bolted down, we come out ahead." She swiped to the second posting. "Second one is more interesting and more dangerous."

The second posting was lighter on specifics and heavier on implied context. Convoy position available, short-run, transit between two stations I didn't have in my current navigation catalog. The payout was listed as negotiable on proof of capability. The listed contact was a Syndicate licensing number.

"They want an armed escort," I said.

"They want a capable ship." She set the pad down. "Which we can be. We just don't have weapons yet."

"Yet," I noted.

"The *Graceful Ruin* job first," she said. "Low profile, modest payout, we find out if the *Meridian Cross* cover holds under closer scrutiny without betting everything on it." She looked at the camera. "And you get to practice not being interesting."

I thought about the warp. About the thing in the dark that was getting better at finding me.

"I've been practicing," I said.

"The shrieking?"

"The not shrieking."

She seemed to consider this a reasonable distinction. "I filed a preliminary interest for the *Graceful Ruin* job. The contracting party responds within twelve hours. In the meantime—" she reached into her jacket and produced a small package, the kind Port Perilous vendors heat-sealed for transport "—I found something for you."

She unsealed the package on the console. Inside was a compact piece of hardware: a modified signal dampener, the civilian grade, the kind sold to ship owners who wanted to reduce their passive energy signature in zones with aggressive commercial scanning. Off-the-shelf technology. Tier 1 at best. But she'd bought three of them, and she laid them out on the console with the focused expression she wore when she was about to do something technically creative.

"These alone won't cover your Core bleed," she said. "But if you integrate them—"

"The Technological Integration module," I said, following her reasoning. "They're compatible tech. If I analyze the design—"

"Five Aether to analyze, fifteen to integrate, and your passive signature drops." She looked up. "I checked the math. Roughly. You'd still bleed, but it would look more like a quirky power coupling than a proscribed Core housing."

I ran the numbers. Twenty Aether total against a potential security improvement that could be the difference between a routine dock scan and a memory scourging. It wasn't even a question.

"Do it," I said.

She slid the first dampener flush against the hull sensor node she'd identified as closest to the core housing access panel, and the contact registered in my system immediately.

// COMPATIBLE TECHNOLOGY DETECTED: SIGNAL DAMPENER (CIVILIAN, x3) //
// BLUEPRINT ANALYSIS AVAILABLE: 5 AETHER //
// WOULD YOU LIKE TO ANALYZE? Y/N //

I formed the intention. [Y].

The five Aether left me with a small sensation of warmth—more controlled now than those early desperate days in the belt, where every unit felt like spending a coin I couldn't replace. The analysis was fast; the hardware was simple, and the principle was familiar. A passive absorption matrix, designed to catch and recycle the electromagnetic bleed from power conduits rather than broadcasting it outward. Applied to the Core housing, it wouldn't eliminate the Aether signature—nothing short of shutting down my consciousness entirely would accomplish that—but it would fold it back, recirculate it, make it indistinguishable at surface scan depth from the background noise of a messy but legal power plant.

// BLUEPRINT ACQUIRED: PASSIVE SIGNAL ABSORPTION MATRIX //
// INTEGRATION COST: 15 AETHER //

**AETHER RESERVES: 107/200**

"Initiating integration," I said.

The fifteen Aether released in a controlled burn, and I felt the change happen in real time—the dampeners dissolving into the golden stream, their material structure taken apart and redistributed as specialized plating around the core housing, the blueprint guiding the Aether into configurations I'd never have improvised on my own. It didn't feel like the Minor Repair function, which had the quality of healing tissue. It felt more surgical. More architectural. Like adding a new room to a house rather than patching its walls.

When it settled, the difference was tangible. The low-frequency hum that I'd always been dimly aware of—my own presence, broadcasting outward—had been reduced to something barely above thermal noise. I was still there. But I was quieter.

// TECHNOLOGICAL INTEGRATION COMPLETE: PASSIVE SIGNAL ABSORPTION MATRIX //
// CORE ENERGY SIGNATURE: REDUCED ~67% //
// PASSIVE AETHER DRAIN: -0.8 UNITS/HOUR //

"How's that?" Zara asked, checking her scanner.

"Better." I ran a comparison against my earlier estimate of the Syndicate patrol arrays' sensitivity thresholds. The new signature sat comfortably below their commercial-scan calibration. Against a dedicated Core-proscription scanner—the Council's classified technology—I couldn't be certain. But against routine port security looking for weapons, contraband, and standard threat profiles, I was now meaningfully invisible. "Significantly better."

"Good." She sat back and crossed her arms and looked satisfied the way she always looked when a plan had come together without her having to improvise anything catastrophic. "Now we wait for the contract response. And you—" she pointed at the camera "—run whatever diagnostics you want, but quietly. And if anything about our neighbor in 7-15 changes, you tell me immediately."

"The hull damage," I said.

She was halfway to the crew quarters when she stopped. "What?"

"Your hull damage from the *Bane's* impact. We're at seventy-four percent. In port, while we're stationary and the dampener is handling the signature bleed, I can run Minor Repair cycles without generating visible field events. I want to get us back to full before the contract job."

Zara turned around. She looked at me—or rather at the camera, which she had gradually developed the habit of treating as my face—with an expression I'd been learning to read — partly calculation, partly something she was working hard not to name.

"How much Aether?" she asked.

"Twenty-five, approximately. Spread over eight hours."

"Do it." She turned away again, then paused. "Alex."

"Yes?"

"When we jumped. The thing in the dark." She didn't turn around. Her shoulders were tense in that way she had before committing to something — braced, deliberate. "I don't think you imagined it."

The cockpit was quiet for a moment, just the distant percussion of a busy port doing what ports did.

"Why not?" I asked.

"Because you're an engineer. Engineers don't hallucinate. They catastrophize, yes, and occasionally they blow themselves up, but they don't invent things." She moved toward the crew quarters corridor. "Start your repairs. And think about what it wants."

The hatch closed behind her.

I sat with that for a moment, alone in the cockpit with the station's heartbeat coming through my hull and the stars wheeling slowly past the viewport and one hundred and seven units of Aether waiting in reserve. I ran the Minor Repair function in quiet, steady pulses, the warmth of healing threading through the damaged sections of my hull from the inside, knitting gradually toward whole.

And I thought about what it wanted.

The thing in the dark. Patient. Enormous. Getting better at finding me.

Getting better meant learning. Learning meant intelligence. Intelligence and patience together, in a place with no dimension and no time, meant something had been waiting there for a very long while.

Long enough to have known the Cores before.

I suppressed that thought and continued my repairs and watched Port Perilous hum and breathe around me, alive with the sound of a hundred species surviving through ingenuity and risk and the defiance of people who'd decided that the frontier was preferable to whatever they'd left behind.

*You handled the Hunger in K-281. We have work for capable ships. Berth 7-21 at third watch. Come alone.*

Third watch was four hours from now.

Berth 7-21 was the Vex-class transport I'd been watching.

The K-281 reference was the key—whoever was in 7-21 had access to the monitoring transmission that had been sent after we fled the system. The one that had flagged our entry to Port Perilous as a *Meridian Cross* rather than the *SS Nova Drift*, and had presumably done the math to find us anyway. Either the monitoring node's signal had reached someone faster than I'd calculated, or someone here had already been looking for us before we arrived.

Neither option was comfortable.

But I was already fairly certain what she was going to say.
