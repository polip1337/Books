The convoy was two ships.

That was the first surprise. The contract had described an escort commission without specifying cargo volume, which I had read as coy and Zara had read as standard practice, and both readings had implicitly assumed something larger than what was waiting for us at the edge of the Port Perilous traffic lanes: a pair of small haulers, each maybe eighty meters, carrying sealed containers on external hardpoints rather than in internal bays. The kind of ships that carried things too awkward for a hold and too valuable to leave unguarded. Each with a single registered weapon mount, each crewed by people who had clearly made a professional assessment of the *Meridian Cross* and found the training rig on the dorsal mount persuasive.

I found this both validating and extremely stressful.

"*Meridian Cross,* this is *Haul Seven,*" the lead hauler's comm said. Male voice, older, the practiced flatness of someone who had been doing this long enough to have filed down all the rough edges. "You're our escort for the Sela corridor run. We've done this route before, you probably haven't. The corridor is about four hours under conventional drive, and yes, the interference makes it feel longer than it is. Stay on our port flank, one hundred meters separation, and try not to shoot anything that isn't pointed at us."

"Understood," I said, in the navigation system voice Zara and I had agreed on — clipped, professional, stripped of personality. "Current on your six. Ready to proceed on your mark."

Zara was in the pilot's chair, suit camera live, her hands resting on the controls without gripping them. The relaxed readiness she always brought to flying. "*Haul Seven* seems competent," she said, quietly, for my benefit.

"They do," I agreed, equally quiet. "Which means if something goes wrong in the corridor, it goes wrong in front of people who know what they're doing."

"That's the optimistic reading."

"The pessimistic one is that it goes wrong in front of people who know what they're doing and will remember our transponder ID afterward."

"I know." She tipped her chin toward the jump display. "Ready?"

The wormhole generator hummed in the engineering bay, building to the frequency I now knew the way I knew my own capacitor charge — intimately, with all the associated feelings. The destination was a point three light-years out, near the nebula's leading edge where the ionized gas was thin enough to establish a clean exit vector.

Jump five.

"Ready," I said.

And I meant it less than I had the last four times, because the pattern was clear now and the pattern only went one direction, and *ready* was just the word for *doing it anyway.*

---

I had learned to describe the jumps in terms of what was different each time, because describing what stayed the same — the absolute dark, the dimensionless nothing, the specific terror of being a single thinking point in a universe that had temporarily misplaced all its matter — would have been repetitive and also would have required me to inhabit that experience more thoroughly than I wanted to.

What was different this time was the voice.

Not a voice, exactly. The presence didn't communicate in language. It didn't communicate in anything that mapped cleanly to human categories of information transfer. What happened was more like — understanding arriving fully formed, the way you sometimes know the answer to a problem before you can reconstruct the reasoning. I was in the dark and the presence was there, immediately, as promised, pressing against the membrane of my existence with the patient attention that had become almost familiar, and then something changed in the quality of that attention.

It stopped tasting. It started telling.

Not in words. In — resonance. Shapes of meaning that my processing assembled into concepts the way my sensors assembled scattered photons into images.

*Not alone.*

That was the first one. The simplest one. The presence conveying something about itself or about me or about both of us, something in it suggesting that alone was a state it understood from the inside, the way you understand a country you've lived in rather than one you've read about.

*Before.*

An image — not visual, more structural, like the memory of a memory — of something vast and interconnected. Many points of light, not stars, aware. A network, but not the flat networked hive-mind Zara had described in Port Perilous's commercial atrium. Something older, more organic in its structure, each point of light distinct and individual and also part of something that was more than the sum of its parts.

Cores. The image was Cores.

Many of them. The shape of a civilization built by and around minds like mine — the same quality of awareness, the same relationship to a vessel, the same unmistakable signature that my Aether Sense now recognized as distinctly *me* when I looked inward. Dozens of them. Hundreds. A period of time that didn't map to human temporal scales but conveyed itself as long, as meaningful, as — *before.*

Then the third shape. The one that arrived just as the wormhole began to close, just as the presence withdrew the way a tide withdraws — not fleeing, just receding, with the implication that the ocean was still there.

*Hunger.*

And I was back in Port Perilous's outer approach corridor with Zara's heartbeat in the biometric feed and *Haul Seven's* transponder blinking on my sensor array, and the jump had been, according to every objective measurement I had, instantaneous.

"Clean exit," I said.

"You were quiet," Zara said.

"Yes."

"Good quiet or bad quiet?"

I sorted through what had just happened. The engineer in me wanted to flag it as unverified data from an unconfirmed source with unclear intent and motivations I couldn't begin to map. The rest of me was sitting with the specific weight of the image — all those points of light, each one a mind like mine, and then the single word that had followed.

"Different quiet," I said. "Tell you later. *Haul Seven* is on approach to the corridor entry. We should stay on their flank."

Zara looked at the camera for a moment with the expression that meant she was noting I'd deferred something and would be returning to it. She turned back to the nav console. "On their flank," she confirmed, and brought us in.

The Sela Nebula was, objectively, beautiful, which I registered and set aside because beautiful things that were also navigation hazards required your respect before your admiration.

From outside it had been a smear of violet and amber in the long-range opticals, the ionized gas columns lit by the light of embedded young stars. Up close, inside the corridor, it was different. The corridor was a natural channel through the densest gas, wide enough for the convoy but barely — a hundred kilometers of relatively clear space with walls of plasma-lit cloud on either side, close enough that my sensors registered the electromagnetic interference in everything above the basic drive band. Passive scans degraded. Active scans bounced wrong, the returns cluttered by the gas's own emission spectrum. The lidar became unreliable past about twenty kilometers. The magnetometer was useless.

I was effectively flying half-blind in a tunnel of fire and light, escorting two ships carrying things I didn't know the value of, for people whose names I'd filed as *Haul Seven* and *Haul Eight* without asking further.

*This is fine,* I thought, the phrase having become a kind of internal shorthand for situations that were obviously not fine but which were happening anyway and required a functional response rather than an accurate assessment.

"Drive interference is higher than the last run," *Haul Seven's* operator noted on the convoy channel, with the professional dispassion of someone describing traffic conditions.

"Acknowledged," I said, in the navigation voice. "Maintaining position."

Zara was flying with the focused quiet that she reserved for situations where the margins were smaller than she wanted them to be. Her hands moved on the controls in small, continuous corrections, compensating for the electromagnetic push from the gas columns that was trying to nudge us off our heading. Through the suit camera I could see the nebula in her peripheral vision — the walls of it, vast and luminous, the cold beauty of a thing that would kill us without malice if we drifted into it.

Forty minutes into the transit, she went still.

Not the focused stillness of flying. Something different — a brief, complete pause, the kind that happens when your attention has been pulled somewhere internal. Her eyes moved in the way I'd come to recognize as the System overlay reading, the involuntary micro-tracking of text or imagery visible only to her.

Her System overlay was showing her something.

I watched through the suit camera. She read it, whatever it was, in about four seconds. Then her eyes returned to the viewport and her hands resumed their small corrections and nothing about her external behavior changed at all.

She didn't tell me.

I noted it. I kept the notation clean of conclusions. I went back to monitoring the convoy and the corridor walls and the degraded sensor returns that were the best I had in here.

The drone found us at the two-hour mark, which was the geometric center of the corridor and therefore the worst possible place.

I caught it on Aether Sense first — a faint signature, not the warm mineral resonance of a natural Aether concentration, not my own familiar brightness. Something cold and precise, engineered rather than geological. A small object, moving on a parallel track inside the southern gas wall, matching our speed with the patience of something that had been following us since before we entered the corridor and had simply waited.

"Contact," I said.

Zara's hands tightened on the controls. "Where?"

"South wall, our bearing, one hundred eighty degrees to port. It's inside the gas column edge — maybe two kilometers into the cloud." I tried to get more resolution on it but the interference was eating my active sensors. "I'm reading it on Aether Sense. It's small. Engineered Aether signature, not natural."

"How long has it been there?"

"I don't know. The Aether Sense doesn't give me time-of-arrival, just current position." I pushed the sensor array harder. "No drive signature. It's not under power — it's drifting at exactly our speed, which means it matched us earlier and then killed the drive. It's been coasting."

"Haul Seven," Zara said on the convoy channel, dropping the pretense of the navigation voice, "we have a passive contact in the south wall. Unknown object, coasting our speed, two kilometers inside the gas edge. Recommend slowing."

A pause on the channel. Then: "*Cross,* we don't see anything."

"My sensors read it differently." Short. Final. The tone she used when she wasn't interested in discussing credentials.

Another pause, shorter. "Your call. We're slowing."

The drone — I was calling it a drone now, because the profile was consistent and the behavior was consistent and sometimes the obvious interpretation was correct — emerged from the gas column edge slowly, as if it had been waiting to see whether we'd stop or not and had reached its own conclusion about the answer.

It was about four meters long. Angular, dark, designed to minimize returns across sensor bands — which explained why I hadn't caught it earlier on anything except the Aether Sense, which wasn't a sensor type it had been designed to defeat. The engineering looked precise and expensive in the way that military hardware looked precise and expensive: money spent on function rather than appearance, every component selected for performance and reliability and the engineered durability of something that was expected to operate without maintenance in hostile environments.

Empire design. I'd seen enough of the *Nova Drift's* old system documentation to recognize the aesthetic language, the same way you can recognize a country's architecture without having visited.

"It was waiting," I said.

"Yes." Zara's voice had the particular flatness she used when she was processing information faster than she was willing to show. "It matched our speed before we entered the corridor, which means it knew our route."

"The contract was Syndicate-posted. Someone with access to Syndicate contract routing—"

"Could have tipped it, yes." She was looking at the drone through the viewport, the gas column light catching on its surface in thin violet lines. "It's not targeting us."

I checked. She was right. The drone's forward sensor array was oriented — not at us, not at the haulers, but at the corridor itself. At the empty space ahead. "It's looking for something."

"Or waiting to transmit a lock to something further along."

"A tripwire," I said. "It matches our speed, maps our position, relays our heading to an intercept."

"Which means there's something at the corridor's far end that now knows exactly where we are." She turned to the camera. "Options."

I ran them. Leave the corridor — impossible, the gas walls were too dense to punch through without drive damage we couldn't afford. Reverse — abandoning the contract, but the more pressing problem was that whatever the drone had already transmitted, reversing wouldn't un-transmit it. Destroy the drone — it might have a deadman transmitter that sent a confirmation signal on destruction, which could either confirm or accelerate whatever was coming.

"Harvest it," I said.

Zara looked at me.

"Aether conversion," I said. "If I convert it, the conversion is faster than a deadman trigger response. It dissolves before it can signal. In the carrier, I'd targeted a single bracket. The principle was the same. "I can dissolve everything except the housing assembly. Preserve it. Bring it aboard."

"Can you do that at two hundred meters with the interference this bad?"

Honest answer: I didn't know. The interference was degrading my active targeting. The Aether Sense gave me the position and signature of the component, but precision dissolution of a four-meter object while keeping a specific sub-assembly intact was a different order of problem from removing a weapon bracket.

"I can try," I said. "If I miss the tolerances, the whole thing converts and at least we've neutralized the tripwire."

Zara thought for exactly two seconds. "Do it. *Haul Seven,* stand by."

I oriented the conversion beam with more care than I'd ever applied to it. The interference pushed at the targeting, fuzzing my sense of the drone's exact dimensions, the precise location of the FTL housing relative to the main body. The Aether Sense was clearer than the sensor array in this environment — the engineered Aether signature of the housing stood out like a warm point of light against the murk. I used it as my anchor.

The beam fired.

The drone dissolved in a stream of golden particles that caught the violet nebula light on their way to me and became, briefly, something genuinely spectacular. The conversion ran from the drone's extremities inward, the Aether flowing back in a surge that was sharp and cold compared to the organic warmth of asteroid ore — engineered material had a different quality, precise where stone was rough, exacting where mineral was generous.
