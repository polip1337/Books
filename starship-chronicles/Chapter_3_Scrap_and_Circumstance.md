# Chapter 3: Scrap and Circumstance

This was a grind. I fell into a rhythm that was part meditation, part menial labor. Scan. Wait for power to tick up. Fire the Aether beam at a pre-selected chunk of rock or metal. Absorb the trickle of energy. Wait again.

The highlight was spending the odd point of aether to power up a bit faster.

My world had shrunk to percentages and lists. The grand tapestry of stars was just a backdrop for my resource spreadsheet. I was a cosmic accountant, and business was slow.

I created a graph of when to spend the aether to get energy to be most economical given the incoming debris. I did it for fun.

Oh how the definition of fun changes

AETHER RESERVES: 4/50
POWER CORE: 0.7%

Four. Only one more to go. At this rate, I'll be done sometime before the heat death of the universe.

Just one more for another MINOR REPAIR. I was still torn between another thruster and more power generation. The numbers I ran were inconclusive. It all came down to how many rocks were in my path and that was hard to estimate. I had to do everything manually, there was no super AI helping me. 

Or rather I'm the AI. And damn the calculations for course corrections are hard. Especially that my center of mass shifts around with all the unbolted stuff inside. Gotta clean it up sometime

Later. Definitely later.

 This was worse than crunch time at OmniCorp. At least then I'd had bad coffee and actual work to break the boredom. Here, my only refreshment was the faint hum of my own capacitor banks charging.

Right. Focus. Next target.

My dutiful little scout had identified a cluster of metallic asteroids a few kilometers away, but they were out of my beam's effective range. Too out of the way to risk spending the power on maneuvering. 

I was stuck mining the gravel I stumbled across. I targeted a lump of iron silicate, a real bottom-feeder of the cosmos, worth a single Aether unit.

The beam lanced out. Power dropped to 0.4%. The rock dissolved into a wisp of gold.

AETHER RESERVES: 5/50

Hmm... Now that I actually have the cash to spend, I wonder… What if there was a different way to reach that stuff?

I navigated back to my core functions, my attention drifting over the MINOR REPAIR option. Next to it was my primary tool: AETHER CONVERSION. I'd been using it on instinct, but I'd never really examined it. I focused my awareness on it — not to activate it, but to query it, to pull up its spec sheet.

// CORE ABILITY: AETHER CONVERSION //
// TIER: 0 //
// RANGE: 15 KM //
// EFFICIENCY: 50% //
// PROJECTION SPEED: SLOW //
// UPGRADE TO TIER 1 AVAILABLE //
// COST: 10 AETHER //
// TIER 1 EFFECTS: //
// - RANGE INCREASED TO 30 KM //
// - EFFICIENCY INCREASED TO 55% //
// - PROJECTION SPEED: SLOW //

I quickly checked how far the herd of high quality rock was pasturing. At closest I'd be passing within 28KM of them.

Was there enough aether to gather before I got to that point? 

For once it turned out that I should easily be able to collect the Aether. It would just be extremely boring.

Unsinkable II tumbled towards its destination.

I let the passive systems work, my awareness drifting through the ship's silent bones. I found myself habitually checking the internal temperature of compartments I knew were open to vacuum, or the oxygen levels in rooms that hadn't held a breath in decades. The human part of me was still looking for a body to maintain.

A new alert chimed, soft and happy somehow.

// SECONDARY GENERATOR EFFICIENCY INCREASED: 12% -> 13% //
// POWER CORE RECHARGE RATE: 0.06% / DAY -> 0.065% / DAY //

One of my jury-rigged solar panels had been struck by a micrometeorite and shaken off another fragment of debris. The gain was microscopic, but it was a gain. 

See? Progress. Slow, grinding, soul-eroding progress.

I remembered a project manager at OmniCorp — a woman who loved Gantt charts more than life itself — telling me, "Rivera, you have to learn to celebrate the incremental victories." I'd wanted to throw my keyboard at her. Now, floating as a sentient scrapheap, I finally understood what she meant. When you're at rock bottom, any movement upward feels like a miracle.

My power crawled to 0.8%. I scanned again. The ping was weak, but it was there. A fragment of what might have been a ship's hull panel, tumbling slowly. Aether Potential: 2.

I took it.

AETHER RESERVES: 7/50

Three to go.

The wait was longer this time. My mind, no longer needing to sleep, began to wander through my own architecture. I found the ship's old navigational database. Most of it was corrupted or locked behind security protocols I no longer had the codes for, but I found a fragment of a starchart. The local star was designated K-281, a red dwarf. My asteroid field was listed as "K-281-Belt, Class-D (Resource Depleted)."

Resource Depleted. Great. I'm trying to rebuild myself with the spare change others left in the couch cushions.

POWER CORE: 1.1%

Time to get back to work. I targeted another piece of debris. This one was different. It was a flat, rectangular sheet of some dark, crystalline material, one edge fused and blackened as if torn from a larger array. My scan identified it as a photovoltaic panel, heavily damaged. Aether Potential: 1. But as I focused on it, a new line of text appeared beneath the standard readout.

// COMPATIBLE TECHNOLOGY DETECTED: PHOTOVOLTAIC PANEL //
// BLUEPRINT ANALYSIS AVAILABLE: 5 AETHER //
// WOULD YOU LIKE TO ANALYZE? Y/N //

I paused. A blueprint? I'd been thinking of Aether as a kind of universal repair putty, but this was different. 

Analyzing scrap for blueprints? Well it already feels like a game so what's a bit of Aether reverse-engineering?

I had 7 Aether. Spending 5 was a huge gamble. But the potential payoff…

I formed the intention. [Y].

Five Aether units vanished from my reserves. The damaged panel dissolved into the usual stream of golden particles, but this time, the light seemed to coalesce in my data banks, forming schematics, material stress diagrams, and efficiency tables. It was a torrent of information, neatly categorized and indexed by my system. It still made my head hurt.

// BLUEPRINT ACQUIRED: 'SCAVENGER-TYPE' SOLAR ARRAY //
// NEW CORE FUNCTION AVAILABLE: TECHNOLOGICAL INTEGRATION //

A new menu branch unfolded in my consciousness. I focused on it.

<< TECHNOLOGICAL INTEGRATION >>
- SCAVENGER-TYPE SOLAR ARRAY
- COST: 15 AETHER
**- DESCRIPTION: PREFERRED BY THE SCAVENGER CLANS IT IS OPTIMIZED FOR WORK AT THE EDGE OF STAR SYSTEMS WHERE THEY USUALLY ROAM.
- EFFECT: REPLACES STANDARD SOLAR COLLECTORS. INCREASES POWER RECHARGE RATE  IN LOW LIGHT ENVIRONMENTS BY 500%.
- DOWNSIDE: INCREASES SHIP'S OVERALL ENERGY SIGNATURE. REDUCES MAXIMUM OUTPUT BY 60%.REDUCES HULL INTEGRITY BY 5 DUE TO STRUCTURAL MODIFICATIONS.

I stared at the description. How the hell did the description work? I got that there existed some sort of scavengers in this universe but how did the system know that? If a new organisation was formed would its name be also in the descriptions of some parts?

Is it omniscient?

I shook my head mentally, nothing I can do about it now, I needed more information. So I turned my attention to the numbers. A five-fold increase in power regeneration? It was a game-changer. But the cost was steep. Fifteen Aether was a fortune. The reduced hull integrity was worrying — dropping from 22 to 17 would make me dangerously fragile. And a bigger energy signature meant I'd be easier to detect. In a universe where ships got shot full of holes, that was a serious concern.

So that's the trade-off. Power for stealth and durability. Do I feel lucky?

I didn't. Not yet. But the option was there, a tantalizing future upgrade. For now, I was back to square one, with only 2 Aether to my name.

Well, that was an expensive lesson. Don't blow your wad on speculative research when you're living paycheck to paycheck.

I needed to get back to basics. My original plan had been to upgrade my Aether Conversion beam. That was a sure thing. I needed nine more Aether for that.

The grind began again, more tedious than before because I knew what I was missing. Scan. Wait. Fire.

AETHER RESERVES: 3/50

POWER CORE: 1.3%

I targeted another piece of debris. Aether Potential: 1.

The beam fired. The debris vanished.

AETHER RESERVES: 4/50

The cycle repeated. Scan. Wait. Fire.

AETHER RESERVES: 5/50

I was halfway to my beam upgrade. The monotony was absolute. I decided to dig into the main reactor error messages, maybe I could repair something myself to decrease the aether cost.
 My dive into the main reactor error messages revealed a tangle of disasters.The power core was riddled with faults—leaky conduits, misaligned magnetic containment fields, and a coolant system that was more theoretical than functional. Fixing these issues manually was tempting, but the Aether cost for even minor repairs was steep, and the system's diagnostics were maddeningly vague, given that some of my sensors were shot. The literal kind.
The deeper I dug, the more I realized that after the battle damage and decades of neglect attempting a manual fix risked destabilizing the entire system. Potentially blowing the whole setup up. I couldn't afford to gamble on a partial repair that might brick the reactor entirely. Instead, I resigned myself to the slow grind, knowing the upgraded Aether Conversion beam was my best bet to gather enough resources to tackle the reactor's issues later.
Maybe when I get the REPAIR or even MAJOR REPAIR, or whatever the actual name is.
POWER CORE: 1.6%

I scanned again. This time, my pulse picked up a smaller, faster-moving piece of debris—a chunk of twisted metal from the same wreck as the solar panel. Aether Potential: 1. As I locked on, the same message appeared.

// COMPATIBLE TECHNOLOGY DETECTED: SENSOR RELAY NODE //
// BLUEPRINT ANALYSIS AVAILABLE: 5 AETHER //
// WOULD YOU LIKE TO ANALYZE? Y/N //

Another one? The temptation was there, a siren call. But I'd been burned once.

No. Discipline, Rivera. Stick to the plan.

I converted it normally, adding the single unit to my reserves.

AETHER RESERVES: 6/50

Four to go.

I continued the slow harvest, my patience fraying. I was an engineer, damn it, not a farmhand. I was built for solving complex problems, not for this repetitive scraping. I thought of the endless, mind-numbing data-entry tasks I'd been given as a junior engineer, the corporate belief that grinding down passion was a form of team-building.

Finally, after what felt like an aeon, I gathered the last unit I needed.

AETHER RESERVES: 10/50
POWER CORE: 0.9%

Finally, Okay, no more distractions.

I focused my will on the Aether Conversion ability and pushed all ten units of Aether into the upgrade.

The effect was immediate. It wasn't a physical sensation so much as a cognitive one. A fundamental piece of my operating software just... expanded. The parameters governing the conversion beam widened. What was once a complex calculation became simpler. Like muscle memory, I didn't have to focus on specifics, I just did it.

// CORE ABILITY UPGRADED: AETHER CONVERSION TIER 0 -> TIER 1 //
// AETHER RESERVES: 0/50 //

And there, arriving just on time, was the cluster of metallic asteroids my probe had found. The closest one, a jagged shard of iron and cobalt, glowed with a steady, promising light.

TARGET ACQUIRED: ASTEROID (IRON-COBALT)
AETHER POTENTIAL: 5
ESTIMATED YIELD: 5 AETHER UNITS
INITIATE CONVERSION? Y/N

A five-unit rock. One conversion, and I'd be back in business.

Now we're talking.

My power was at 0.9%. I could do this. I targeted the asteroid and fired. The beam that shot out was noticeably thicker, a solid bar of silver light. The conversion was faster, too. The asteroid faded into a stream of golden particles.

The sensation as the Aether flooded in was richer, more potent.

AETHER RESERVES: 5/50
POWER CORE: 0.6%

Five Aether, just like that. The upgrade was already paying for itself. I immediately activated MINOR REPAIR and focused on the starboard-bow thruster assembly. The now-familiar tingling warmth spread through that section of my hull. I felt valves unstick and conduits clear.

<< STATUS UPDATE >>
STARBOARD-BOW THRUSTER ASSEMBLY: ONLINE
PROPULSION EFFICIENCY: 12.5% -> 25%
HULL INTEGRITY: 22/100->29/100

With two thrusters operational, my control improved dramatically. I could now arrest my tumble and hold a stable orientation. I was no longer a drifting derelict; I was a poorly equipped, but purposeful, starship.

I gathered the rest of the cluster of asteroids, for a total of 22 Aether after spending some to get more energy. 

Minor repair. Go!

<< STATUS UPDATE >>
STARBOARD-SENSOR ARRAY: ONLINE
EFFICIENCY: 0% -> 50%
HULL INTEGRITY: 29/100->35/100

I channeled my inner Thor.

Another! 

<< STATUS UPDATE >>
STARBOARD-SENSOR ARRAY: ONLINE
EFFICIENCY: 50% -> 100%
HULL INTEGRITY: 35/100->45/100

AETHER RESERVES: 12/50

With the starboard-sensor array fully restored to 100% efficiency, I felt a rush akin to opening my eyes after keeping my right one closed for weeks. The sensation was almost human — like regaining sight in a long-blinded eye — filling me with a quiet thrill that briefly drowned out the monotony of my cosmic grind.

With two sensor arrays, I also regained some abilities. I could now track the subtle drift of debris, anticipate collisions with greater precision. The feeling of control and taking small steps to improve my situation was intoxicating.

I went for the sensor array, since I wanted to be able to see into all directions in the debris cloud. I took a look through my newly fixed array. 

So this is how it works

Where once a gaping gash had marred the starboard side, it was now mostly closed, the hull's surface knitted together like a healing tissue. The MINOR REPAIR operations, which had pushed my HULL INTEGRITY to 45/100, had clearly worked their magic, smoothing out the damage and reinforcing the ship's outer shell. 
This means that HULL INTEGRITY is separate from the state of the inner systems. Maybe I'll be able to repair them faster once no aether is wasted on repairing the hull. Good to know.


I turned my attention to the main event: the wreck and its 150 Aether prize.

Using the probe's detailed map, I plotted a course. The path was a winding route through the densest part of the debris field, a slalom of jagged metal and frozen gas. It would require careful, continuous adjustments.

I started slowly, firing short bursts from my thrusters to alter my drift. I wasn't flying so much as nudging my trajectory. It was a patient, painstaking process. The wreck grew slowly larger in my optical sensor.

I gathered more Aether along the way, using my upgraded beam to siphon 3 units from a passing carbonaceous chondrite. I now had 15 Aether in reserve.

As I drew closer, the scale of the wreck became apparent. It was a carrier, its back broken, the stern section twisted away from the main hull. The black and yellow paint scheme was familiar, a twin to my own, though this one was scarred with the pockmarks of high-energy weapons fire.

It wasn't a match I'd noticed before, but now, with the wreck this close, the resemblance was obvious. Not just paint, but layout — plating pattern, structural spine, engines. The ship in front of me had been built from the same design language as mine.

So whoever built me built this too.

My beam swept wider. More wreckage. Half-melted hulls, shattered turrets, broken keels. And not just from one side.

Some of the ships bore the yellow-and-black scheme. Others had a different look: angular designs, bulkier armor, green markings painted in flowing lines. 

It was a battlefield.

So — I was dropped into someone else's war grave.

Whatever this place had been, it was quiet now. Just dead ships and the long silence that followed.

Someone picked this place clean, that's why there is so little useful scrap. Maybe those Scavengers the description mentioned?

I pushed the thought aside. One crisis at a time.

I was now at the edge of the debris field. The clear path my probe had identified was a narrow channel between two large, rotating pieces of the freighter's superstructure.

Okay, easy does it. No sudden moves.

I fired a sustained burst from my port thrusters, aligning myself with the entrance. I drifted forward, my sensors tracking the slow rotation of the metal giants on either side.

I was halfway through when a new alert flashed.

// PROXIMITY ALERT: DEBRIS DRIFT. //
// OBJECT ON COLLISION COURSE. //
// ANALYSIS: HULL FRAGMENT (2 METERS). //
// TIME TO IMPACT: 45 SECONDS. //

A jagged spear of metal was on a direct path to my starboard side.

Damn it.

I fired my starboard thrusters, pushing myself portside. The fragment slid past, so close I could have counted the rivets. The maneuver cost me power and put me off-course. I corrected, firing a quick burst to straighten out.

I was through the channel. The broken stern of the freighter lay before me. And there, nestled within the wreckage, was the source of the 150 Aether signal: the ship's mangled power core.

Almost there. Just a little closer.

I began my final approach, my thrusters firing in tiny, precise puffs to kill my relative velocity. I was so focused on the core that I almost missed the new alert.

It wasn't amber like the debris warning. It was a soft, steady blue.

// SUBSPACE MONITOR ACTIVE. //
// LOW-ENERGY TACHYON BURST DETECTED. //
// SOURCE: PROXIMATE. //
// ANALYSIS: AUTOMATED SIGNATURE. //

My systems triangulated the source. It wasn't coming from the wreck.

It was coming from something else, something that had just entered the sensor range of my external probe. Something that was moving under its own power, and on a direct course for the derelict freighter.

I was fifty meters from the 150 Aether core, my thrusters poised to make the final adjustment. I froze all movement, becoming just another piece of silent debris.

The signal was active. And it was getting closer.
