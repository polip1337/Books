# Chapter 6: Here be Giants

Zara slept.

In the pristine, newly pressurized cockpit of the *Unsinkable II* She engaged a security harness in the pilot's chair and was tucked in.

I wondered if I missed sleeping. As a human, I liked to sleep. It was a hard-earned reward after long hours wrestling with designs or debugging systems. Now, there’s no need for it—no fatigue to escape, no moment when my mind powered down. Ideas of sleep tugged at me, a piece of the life I left behind.

Now, as a Core, I was always on, but it didn't really bother me. It felt like the space between the monitor and me got removed, but I was still me. I didn’t seem to process data at increased speeds. When I was calculating the course corrections with the single thruster, I miscalculated more than once.

Human error. Heh.

The carrier loomed nearby.

It looked differently when viewed through the external sensors as opposed to the more human perspective of the cockpit’s camera. It was a dead giant of metal, a frozen demigod of shattered armor and snapped structural members. Its original yellow-and-black paint was scarred by the violent blooms of weapon impacts and the deeper, more visceral wounds of kinetic penetrators. It was a corpse, and we were the carrion birds come to pick its bones.

I'm thinking of it as a body.

I shook my mental head, and unbeknownst to me my navigation lights blinked in sequence. 

Enough. Go through the plan again. Use your Core powers of not needing sleep for something useful.

 Oh, we had a plan. And what a plan it was. A terrifying, exhilarating, and deeply uncertain plan.

We decided to go for the carrier first, as we were already within the debris field, and going in and out was dangerous for the Unsinkable II. We’d recover the reactor then all the tech we could find to boost me back to flying shape and get out of here. If everything went well we’d be out of here with months to spare before IMPENDING DOOM crushed both us and the carrier.

There’s always bigger fish

I went over the plan again, nothing to do but to wait for Zara to wake up.

Zara. I enjoyed having her onboard, a reminder of the human (Alien? I didn't even ask her race.) connection I’d lost. I’d really like to trust her, but the look she gets when she talks about money and how much she could get for me… At the same time, she's too useful to dismiss. 

I went back into my system trying to find something to fix.

Zara woke up a couple hours later. She ate a tube of some nutrient paste brought over from Bane and got straight to work. 

"Alright, Alex, run the checklist one more time," Zara said, her voice tight. She was strapped into the pilot's chair, her fingers flying across a datapad she'd wired into my console. Her own ship was connected to the same console by a long wire snaking through my ship and the umbilical.

"The probe is in position, providing a live lidar map of the debris field between us and the carrier's primary engineering access point," I reported, my voice calm despite the frantic processing happening beneath the surface. Most of the processing was done by the Bane, its navigation system became my faithful minion. No more calculations by hand. Repairing my own nav bumped a couple of notches on my TO DO list

 I displayed the map on the main viewport—a twisting, convoluted path through a labyrinth of spinning metal and frozen shrapnel. We were near the Carrier, but the best access path was halfway down the ship’s length, almost a kilometer from us. "The path is stable for the next forty-seven minutes, based on drift projections."

"Good. My suit's telemetry is synced to you. You'll get my biomed readouts and a real-time position fix." She tapped the datapad. "The target is the engineering bay, deck 4, section beta. That's where the main power core is. According to the schematics in your database—which should be close enough—the core's emergency containment housing is a sealed unit. If it's intact, we hope you can get the 150 Aether contained within it. If it's breached..."

"If it's breached, we get the hell out, and cross our fingers it doesn't decide to blow up now," I finished. It was the biggest variable. A contained core was a treasure chest. A breached one could be a trap, its energy wild and unpredictable. “Which it really shouldn’t, it’s been here for 20 years. It's going to be fine, Zara”

"Right.” She was utterly unconvinced; we had a bit of a different outlook on risk. “So, step one: We fly you near the entrance. Step two: I get in. Step three: assess. Step four: if it's hot, I grab what we can from the surrounding systems and get out. If it's contained..." She grinned, a flash of white in the cockpit's light. "We hit the jackpot. Shame the Bane is too big to fly there."

I laughed and the cockpit’s light twinkled. “Chickening out on me, partner? We’ve been over this—it's the only way. We can't disturb the debris, no way to tell what the result of a cascade of collisions could be.”

She stood up, pulling on her helmet with a definitive *click*. "You're my eyes and ears out there, Alex. I'm counting on you to watch my back. This wreck is old, and old things... sometimes they aren't as empty as they seem."

A chill that had nothing to do with my environmental systems ran through me. "What does that mean?"

"Means I've seen scavengers go into hulks like this and not come out. Sometimes it's unstable structure. Sometimes it's leftover security systems running on backup power." She turned on the piloting console.

Overnight, with the Bane’s computer help, I created a simple adapter software that let Zara fly me with only the two thrusters. It was cumbersome, but worked in the sims. 

“Go. 45 minutes to clear the run.”

I was the mission control, the overwatch. I felt a profound and strange responsibility settle over me. I now had two lives in my hands—or rather, in my sensors and processors.

"Crossing the perimeter now," her voice came slightly strained. "Debris is thick. I'm going to weave through that torn hull section to the left."

"I see it. Your vector is clear. Watch for that spinning girder at marker two. Its rotation is erratic."

"Copy. I see it."

I watched my body dart through the graveyard, thrusters flaring in precise, economical bursts. She moved with a grace, always a step from disaster, a dancer in a minefield. My sensors tracked every move, cross-referencing our position with the probe's live lidar feed, watching for any piece of debris that might shift into our path. It was an immense strain on my systems, and I couldn't do it without Bane.

Slowly, we inched towards the target. We were flying by a big piece of hull armor when disaster struck.

An unaccounted for piece of debris slid from behind the jagged edge of the armor. We couldn't see it before because we only used one probe and had a single viewpoint. I didn’t even have time to get angry at myself for the stupid oversight.

//** SYSTEM LOG**//
//** PILOT ZARA USED EVASIVE MANEVOURS**//
//** THRUSTER POWER INCREASED BY 100% FOR 30 SECONDS **//

What?

My body flipped with far more agility than what I was used to, the piece of debris Zara tried to dodge scraped against my ventral armor. Fortunately the angle allowed it to pass by leaving only a couple of scratches.

“Hey, watch the paint!” I was happy to be alive.

“You’re welcome, buckethead.” She laughed with an edge of hysteria in her voice.

“What was that?”

“I told you, I was a level 14 pilot. Apparently 15 now, thanks to my great flying there. It comes with a skill or two.”

“Why didn't you tell me?” The cockpit lights tinged red.

“Come on, you don’t tell people your skills. It's common sense. I bet you didn't give me all the info on yourself” 

I blushed internally. I actually did give her everything since there was nothing I could hide and still be able to work with her. 

Oh, I did omit the reverse engineering thing.

“Fair enough.” I said, still hurt, but we had a job to do.


I fired a scan of the way and managed to get some aether on the way.

AETHER RESERVES: 0/50 -> 6/50

She reached the gaping maw of the carrier's engineering deck without further issue. It was a wound torn open by some cataclysmic internal explosion. "No signs of recent activity. The edges of the breach are cold. This happened a long time ago."

"Readings are nominal from here," I confirmed. "No energy signatures, no movement. Bring us to a relative stop."

After she completed the final maneuvers, she unbuckled and checked the power cell on the heavy plasma cutter she'd attached to the arm over her shoulder. "Just keep the channel open and watch for movement I might miss."

With a final nod, she cycled through the airlock. I watched through the external camera as she emerged, a lone, grey figure against the infinite black. She activated her suit's maneuvering thrusters and pushed off, a tiny speck heading toward the colossal wreck.

She drifted inside, and my world narrowed to the feed from the camera on her helmet. The interior was a cathedral of ruin. Conduits hung from the ceiling like metallic vines. Shattered control panels lined the walls, their screens dark and cracked. Frozen globules of what might have been coolant or hydraulic fluid floated in the microgravity, catching the light from her helmet in an oily glitter.

Atmosphere is void, as expected," she murmured, her voice a whisper in the silence. "Heading toward section beta. The schematic shows a main corridor... there." She pointed her light down a wide passageway, its walls scarred by fire.

She moved slowly, deliberately, grabbing what handholds were still left and using the mag locks on her boots when necessary. The only sounds were her breathing, the faint hum of her suit, and the occasional crackle of the comm.

"Zara, hold position," I said, my audio sensors picking up a faint, rhythmic vibration. "I'm detecting a low-frequency oscillation. Bearing... 270, relative to your position."

She froze. "Source?"

"Analyzing... it's structural. A large piece of wreckage, probably a deck plate, acting like a tuning fork. Something must have hit it recently. It looks stable, but be aware."

"Copy. Moving on."

She continued, deeper into the belly of the beast. The scale was immense. This ship had once housed hundreds, maybe thousands of crew. Now, it was a tomb. We passed sealed doors, their emergency lockdown protocols still engaged decades after the battle. What was behind them? Frozen bodies? Shattered equipment? I found myself both dreading and longing to know.

After ten tense minutes of navigation, she reached a massive, reinforced blast door. It was sealed shut, melted by heat.

Zara stared at it, her gloved hand resting on the scorched and pitted surface. "Well, you're not going to open yourself, are you?" she muttered, hefting the heavy plasma cutter from its mount on her shoulder. The weapon—or tool, depending on the day—was a brutish thing of carbon-scored steel and heavily insulated conduits. She ran a hand affectionately along its barrel. "Time to go to work, baby."

The high-pitched whine of the cutter coming to life made me decrease my pickup sensitivity. It felt eerie in the ship's overall silence. A needle-thin beam of plasma erupted from its tip, and she aimed it against the side of the blast door. Molten alloy bloomed under its touch, spitting globules of metal that glowed pure white, having nowhere to pass their thermal energy. They slowly drifted away casting multiple shadows on the walls. The actinic light flared, reflecting in her golden eyes, which were narrowed in concentration. 

“Arent you worried that hot floating metal is going to be an issue?”

Zara didn't stop her work “They would be, if they could touch me. The cutter has a low power repulsor field for work in microgravity”

“That does not seem very safe.”

“Yeah, it's custom. Cost me quite a bit but when you work solo you either spend on quality or you die.”


That made me stop. What kind of life do you have to lead to end up in the middle of nowhere, scavenging old battlefields, one equipment failure from an extremely unpleasant death.


My musing was interrupted by a new, insistent alert. Zara’s wrist scanner, which had been passively analyzing the environment, suddenly flared a cautionary yellow. She pulled back, the cutter's beam dying with a sizzling fade. "Hold up, Alex," she said, her voice losing its casual tone. "My baby here just gave me a second opinion. The composition of this slag… it's not just from the initial battle damage. This was re-fused. Recently. Someone didn't want anyone getting in here."

“Uhm, so what? You gotta talk me through it, I’m not and experienced scavenger”

“A deliberately sealed door on a derelict is never a good sign. It could mean a trapped atmosphere, a hazardous material spill, or a security system someone had tried—and failed—to contain. Or it could mean the core inside was so unstable that sealing it was the only smart option.”

She took a deliberate step back from the hatch. "The core's not going anywhere," she said, the decision firm in her voice. "I'd rather we had a few more systems online before we say hello. Let's see what other leftovers this place has. Your magic trick works with scraps. This whole ship is full of them." The plasma cutter was re-secured on her shoulder. 



"Copy that," I said. "The core can wait. Let's see what the pantry has to offer first."
