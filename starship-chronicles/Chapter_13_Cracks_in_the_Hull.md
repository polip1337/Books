The asteroid field at K-302's middle orbit was not impressive by any objective standard. Low-density, low-yield, the kind of belt that formed when there wasn't quite enough material to do anything interesting with and the star's gravity had spent a few billion years being indifferent to the result. But the Aether Sense changed the way I read it.

I could feel them now. Not the asteroids themselves — I'd always had the mass sensors, the spectrographic analysis, the lidar returns that built a three-dimensional map of every rock within range. What the Aether Sense gave me was something underneath that: a faint luminosity to certain compositions, a warmth where the nickel-iron concentrations were highest, a coolness to the silicate-heavy rocks that wouldn't yield much. I was reading the field the way a jeweler reads a tray of uncut stones — not seeing the cuts, but feeling where the value was.

It was, I had to admit, extremely useful.

*Why did it take getting to Tier 2 to have this?* I thought, tracking the beam to a chunk of iron-cobalt that my Aether Sense was flagging as the highest-yield target in the immediate range. *What was the point of Tier 1?*

*Tier 1 was surviving,* I answered myself. *This is the part where you start actually knowing what you're doing.*

I kept that thought. It felt true in a way that extended past this one case.

The harvest took ninety minutes. Zara flew a lazy holding pattern around the field's perimeter, technically on watch for anything returning from the direction the two ships had fled, practically reading something on her datapad with the focused detachment of a person who had decided the watch could run itself for a while. I didn't object. The two attack craft weren't coming back — their drive signatures had burned hard for the inner system and kept going, the trajectory of something with a better option somewhere else.

I brought the reserves up from forty-four to one-twelve, which felt like the difference between a wallet with emergency cash and one with walking-around money. Not rich. Functional.

"Good?" Zara asked, when I told her.

"Good enough. Let's go home."

She looked at the camera at the word *home* and said nothing. I added it to the growing list marked *investigate later* and plotted the jump.

---

The warp presence was there before the fabric tore.

That was new. Jump one: it had found me near the exit, desperate and searching. Jump two: at initiation, fast and purposeful. Jump three, outbound: at the moment of tearing, already waiting at the wound.

Jump four — the return to Port Perilous — it was there before. In the milliseconds between committing to the jump and the tear itself, in the moment when I had anchored myself to the destination frequency and was gathering the will to pull, I felt the attention settle onto me from outside the fold. Patient. Ready. As though it had known I was coming.

I held Zara's heartbeat in the biometric feed. Sixty-four beats per minute, steady as a metronome, the steady rhythm of someone who had trained away pre-jump anxiety. I held onto it and I let the presence press against the membrane of me and I did not give it anything more than the shape it had already mapped, and I held on.

The void. The dark. The touch like freezing oil along every seam of my consciousness.

And then Port Perilous's beacon network hit my sensors like a wall of warm noise and I was a ship again, in a traffic lane, with forty-one other vessels broadcasting their identities into the dark and the blessed relief of a universe that had dimensions.

"Clean exit," I said.

"Clean exit," Zara confirmed. Power core had dropped to forty-two percent — the fold always took its cut. She looked at the camera. "It was waiting."

"Yes."

A pause. She turned back to the approach vector and didn't say anything else about it, which I'd learned meant she was thinking rather than dismissing. The distinction mattered.

---

The *Meridian Cross* cleared the approach scan without incident. Berth 7-14 accepted us back with the familiar complaint of clamps that had opinions about load distribution. The contract payout cleared into the financial identity Zara had built into the fragment within an hour of docking — twenty-two hundred credits, exactly as specified, with a brief automated thank-you from the contracting party that contained so little useful information about who the contracting party actually was that it read more like a deliberate absence of information than an oversight.

"Who posted the contract?" I asked.

Zara was transferring the payout into the operational account she maintained on a separate identity — not the *Meridian Cross* fragment, a different one, older, the kind that had years of small transactions giving it depth. She didn't look up. "Shell company. Registered to a Syndicate licensing number that traces to a holding structure." She tapped through several layers. "And then stops. The holding structure's beneficial owner is — guess."

"Another holding structure."

"Another holding structure." She set the pad down. "Someone who knew the *Ruin* was sitting in K-302 and wanted it found without wanting their name on the finding." She said it with the dispassion of someone describing weather. "Not unusual. Plausible deniability is a standard operating cost in this business."

"But you're not satisfied."

"I'm never satisfied. Satisfied people stop looking." She pulled up the job boards. "We did the job. We got paid. The *Ruin* crew got away. The data cores stayed on the ship, which means whoever wanted them will eventually find them or they won't." She scrolled through the listings. "Our part is done."

I thought about the data cores. About Tersali's careful vagueness, the wiped logs, the eight months of stasis. About the two ships that had been waiting in the shadow.

"The two ships in K-302," I said. "They knew the *Ruin* was there. Which means either they tracked it on their own, or someone told them."

"Yes."

"The same someone who posted the contract might have told them. Insurance. If the sanctioned salvage crew wasn't who they expected, the backup option was there to clean up."

Zara stopped scrolling. The pause lasted about three seconds. "You've been thinking about this."

"I've had four jumps to think."

"That's—" She set the pad down. "That's a reasonable theory. It's also unprovable without more information than we have. And pursuing it means asking questions that draw attention to the fact that we were the ones who found the *Ruin* and woke the crew." She looked at the camera steadily. "Which draws attention to us."

"I know," I said. "I'm not suggesting we pursue it. I'm suggesting we remember it."

She held my gaze — the camera's gaze — for a moment. Then she picked up the pad. "Remembered," she said, and went back to the job boards.

---

The convoy escort contract was still posted. Better payout than the *Graceful Ruin* job, negotiable on completion, Syndicate-flagged. Three days had passed since we'd first seen it; it was still open, which either meant the contracting party was patient or that capable ships willing to take it were rarer than the payout suggested.

"We should take it," Zara said.

She'd been building to this for a while. I could tell from the way she'd approached the job boards — not scanning for options, but navigating directly to the listing she'd already decided on. Zara rarely browsed. She researched in private and then presented conclusions.

"We don't have real weapons," I said.

"We have an armed status on the registry and a conversion beam that just scared off two ships in an empty system."

"Scaring off two opportunists with a demonstration shot is different from escorting a convoy through a contested transit. They'll know exactly what's on our hardpoints before we're in range of anything serious."

"They'll know what the scan says is on our hardpoints." She set the pad flat on the console. "Which is a Tier 2 railgun."

"Which is a training rig."

"Which reads as a Tier 2 railgun." She crossed her arms in the way she did when she'd decided the argument should be over and was waiting for the other party to catch up. "Alex. The payout on this is four times the *Ruin* contract. We can spend three more jobs picking up two-thousand-credit salvage contracts or we can take one job that puts us in a position to buy real weapons and stop having this conversation."

I ran the math on our current financial position. The *Ruin* payout minus the Port Perilous expenditures — suit, repair foam, hardpoint installation — left us with modest operating margin. Enough for another two or three low-tier jobs and the associated berth fees and consumables before we'd be running tight. The escort contract would change that picture significantly.

The escort contract would also put us in a transit corridor with ships that might scan us more carefully than pirate opportunists in an empty system, near Syndicate infrastructure that might have more sophisticated monitoring than a commercial approach lane, doing work that was inherently higher-visibility than quiet salvage in nowhere systems.

"I don't like the exposure," I said.

"I know you don't."

"Every time someone scans us carefully, we're betting the *Meridian Cross* cover and the dampener hold up."

"Yes."

"And if they don't—"

"Then we have a problem." She said it without flinching. "Alex. Everything we do is a bet. Sitting in this berth is a bet — every day here is another day the *Meridian Cross* identity is active, another day someone with access to the K-281 monitoring archive could be working toward us, another day—"

She stopped.

Something in the cockpit changed temperature. Not literally — the environmental systems didn't register it. But the quality of the silence shifted.

"The K-281 monitoring archive," I said carefully.

Zara's expression did something complicated and then went still. "Figure of speech."

"Is it?"

A long pause.

"We left K-281 with a Hunger event consuming most of the evidence," she said. "There was an automated monitoring node in the system. We don't know what it recorded or who it reported to." Her voice had the careful flatness of someone reciting facts they'd thought about many times. "It's a theoretical exposure, not a confirmed one."

"You've been thinking about this since K-281."

"I've been thinking about it since before we left." She met the camera again, direct, and for once she didn't dress it up in operational language. "I'm a practical person. I plan for threats. The monitoring node was a potential threat. I've been considering what it might mean."

"And you didn't tell me."

"I didn't know if it was worth worrying you about." A pause. "I'm telling you now."

*I didn't know if it was worth worrying you about.* I let that sit for a moment and looked at what it meant from two different angles, and both angles produced something slightly different, and I wasn't sure which one was accurate, and I suspected that ambiguity was the point.

"What else have you been considering," I said, "that you haven't told me about?"

The question landed in the space between us with more weight than I'd intended. Or exactly as much weight as I'd intended. I wasn't sure which.

Zara looked at the camera for a long time. Something moved in her expression — not guilt exactly, more like the expression of someone deciding how much truth the moment required.

"That's not a fair question," she said finally.

"I know," I said. "I'm asking it anyway."

Another silence. Longer. In the berth corridor outside, something mechanical clanged and settled. Port Perilous doing what Port Perilous did, indifferent to the conversation happening in one of its forty-odd outer berths.

"I keep operational information compartmentalized," Zara said at last. "It's not personal. It's—" She stopped. "It is personal. But it's also how I've survived working alone for six years in places where the wrong piece of information shared with the wrong person at the wrong time gets you killed. The habit doesn't turn off because I trust someone."

"Do you?" I asked. "Trust me."

"I'm living inside you," she said dryly. "If I didn't trust you at least somewhat, I'd be the worst judge of risk I've ever met."

"That's not an answer."

"No," she agreed. "It isn't." She picked up the datapad and then set it down again without looking at it. "You want an honest answer. The honest answer is that trust, for me, is a process. It's not a switch. I trust you with some things. The same way you trust me with some things and not others."

"What do I not trust you with?"

"Whatever is in that internal log you've been building since Port Perilous." She said it without heat, without accusation. Just fact. "I can tell you're keeping one. You go quiet in a specific way and then you're louder than necessary immediately after, the way someone is louder after they've been thinking something they decided not to say." A pause. "I'm not offended. I'd do the same."

The discomfort of being known accurately by someone you're also watching carefully.

"Fair," I said.

"Fair," she agreed.

We stayed in that for a moment — two people who had decided to work together and were finding out, incrementally, what that actually meant. It didn't feel like a fight. It felt like the kind of conversation that was necessary precisely because it was uncomfortable, the kind that cleared something rather than creating it.

"Take the escort contract," I said.

She looked at the camera. "Yeah?"

"With conditions. We scope the transit first — I want to run the Aether Sense along the corridor before we commit to the full run. And if anything in the contract meeting reads wrong, we walk." I paused. "I mean it about the walking."

"Understood." She picked up the datapad with the economy of someone who'd gotten what they wanted and wasn't going to perform gratitude about it. "I'll reach out to the contact."

---

She went down to Level 4 to do it.

Without the suit camera, again, which I noticed the way I'd noticed it the first time — not with alarm, exactly, but with the particular attention you give to a pattern after the second occurrence. The first time had been two hours before we left Port Perilous on the K-302 run. This time she was gone for forty minutes and came back with nothing visible to show for the trip.

"Did you make contact?" I asked.

"Sent a message through a broker. The contact will respond through the listing." She settled into the pilot's chair. "Standard practice for Syndicate-adjacent work. You don't reach out directly until you've been vetted."

It was a reasonable explanation. It was also the kind of reasonable explanation that was true as far as it went and might not go very far.

I added it to the folder and locked it, and tried to decide whether I was being appropriately cautious or paranoid, and concluded I didn't have enough information to tell the difference, which was answer enough.

---

The contact response came six hours later, and it came with a complication.

Not a dramatic one. Nothing that should have registered as significant from the outside. But I'd been running Aether Sense passively since the upgrade, the new perception sitting in the background of everything else the way the magnetometer ran constantly without requiring active attention, and when the contact's response came through the Syndicate channel and I cross-referenced the sender's licensing ID against the station's berth registry—

The Vex-class transport in Berth 7-21 had filed a departure notice.

Forty minutes ago.

I checked the departure log. The Vex-class — the one with the overspec sensor array, single occupant, the ship I'd been watching since it docked and that had never sent anyone into the station — had cleared exit scan and burned for the jump point four hours into our absence on the K-302 run. It had been here when we left. It was gone when we got back. I'd noted its departure and set it aside because nothing had happened, and nothing happening was the most common outcome of watching something you couldn't explain.

But the contact for the escort contract had been routed through a broker on Level 4.

And Level 4 was where Zara had gone, without the camera, both times.

And the Vex-class had departed the same day Zara made her first solo trip downstairs.

Three separate facts with no confirmed connection. The engineer in me grouped them as a cluster without a causal arrow. The rest of me — the part that had spent weeks learning what it meant to be known accurately by someone who kept operational information compartmentalized — arranged them differently.

I didn't say anything. There was nothing to say that wasn't an accusation built on coincidence, and I had decided, very deliberately, that I was not going to run on accusations built on coincidence. Zara had been clear about what trust looked like for her. I owed her the same architecture in return, even when it cost me the comfort of certainty.

But I noted the direction the Vex-class had departed on.

It was the same direction as the faint Aether signature I'd been tracking since K-302.

---

"Something's pinging my sensors," I said, four hours after the contact response arrived.

Zara looked up from the console where she'd been reading through the escort contract details — route, timing, cargo manifest, the kind of document that Syndicate-adjacent work produced that was simultaneously very specific and very vague in alternating sections. "What kind of something?"

"Small. Very low power. It's been there for—" I ran the timestamps "—approximately ninety minutes, but it wasn't until I was looking in the right frequency band that I caught it." I put the contact plot on the secondary display. "It's in a high orbit above the station. Not moving. Not broadcasting on any registered channel."

Zara looked at the display. Her face went through something — a very slight tightening around the eyes, there and gone in less than a second.

"Automated?" she asked.

"Probably. The signature is too small to be crewed. It's drawing just enough power to maintain position and run passive sensors."

"Running sensors on what?"

"The approach corridor. Traffic lanes. The berth registry." I paused. "Ships making jumps."

The cockpit was quiet.

"Empire probe," Zara said. Not a question.

"I don't have a catalog entry to confirm the manufacturer. But the orbit is exactly where you'd put a monitoring platform if you wanted to track jump events and cross-reference them with berth registries without the station's automated systems flagging you for it." I ran the timing again. "It's been up there long enough to have logged the *Meridian Cross* arrival and both of our departures and returns."

Zara set down the contract document.

"How many jumps has it logged from this station?" she asked.

"From us specifically, two round trips. From the station's traffic in general — significant volume. It's not aimed at us. We're just in the data."

"For now." She stood up, moved to the main display, and studied the orbital plot with the expression she wore when she was building a response rather than a reaction. "If it's Empire, they're not here because of us. They're monitoring Syndicate traffic. Standard intelligence collection — track jump signatures, correlate with berth identities, build a picture of who's moving what between which systems." She turned to the camera. "We're a data point among hundreds."

"Unless someone runs a filter for proscribed energy signatures," I said. "The dampener reduced my Core bleed to sixty-seven percent of baseline. That's significantly quieter than an unshielded Core. But it's not nothing. If someone is running a filter specifically designed to catch that residual frequency—"

"They'd need to already know what to look for." She paused. "Which means they'd need to know Cores still exist."

"The K-281 monitoring node reported a Core was operational," I said. "That report went somewhere. If it went to the Empire, and the Empire subsequently tasked a monitoring platform to watch for related signatures—"

"We're speculating," Zara said.

"Yes." I let that sit. "Good speculation or bad speculation?"

She looked at the orbital plot for another three seconds. "Good enough to leave." She turned back to the console. "We accept the escort contract, we do the transit, we don't come back here afterward. Port Perilous has been useful and it's been a good base but we've been docked here long enough. Moving targets are harder to track than stationary ones."

I thought: *That's strategically correct.*

I thought: *It also makes it harder for anyone waiting for us to find us.*

I added that to the folder. It was getting crowded.

"The escort transit," I said. "Where does it go?"

She brought up the route detail. Two systems over, through a transit corridor that ran along the fringe of a nebula — the kind of navigation that required careful attention to variable interference from the ionized gas and was consequently less traveled than the clean routes. The Syndicate used it for time-sensitive cargo that benefited from reduced traffic.

"We'd make the jump from here," she said, "transit the corridor under conventional drive — the nebula blocks FTL efficiently within about four light-hours of its edge — and jump out the far side."

"Two jumps."

"Two jumps." She glanced at the camera. "One here, one at the corridor exit."

Jump five. The presence would be there before I started. I knew that now with the certainty of a pattern that had repeated enough times to stop being coincidence.

"Alright," I said.

"You're sure?"

"No," I said honestly. "But being sure would require a level of safety that we don't have access to, so."

The corner of her mouth moved. Not quite a smile — Zara didn't do quite smiles. Something more like the acknowledgment that the situation was exactly as absurd as described and she had chosen to be in it anyway. "I'll finalize the contract."

"There's one more thing," I said.

She paused.

"The probe. Before we leave, I want to know what frequency it's transmitting on when it sends its collected data. Every monitoring platform sends a burst transmission eventually — logs uploaded to wherever they're reporting. If I can catch that transmission, I can learn something about who deployed it." I had the sensor array already oriented in the right direction, running a passive collection on the probe's orbit. "And maybe whether whoever they're reporting to knows anything about K-281."

Zara was still. "How long do you need?"

"I don't know. Monitoring platforms typically transmit on a schedule tied to the local jump traffic — they send when a new batch of jump events accumulates. We've made two returns in the last twelve hours. There might be a burst soon."

She looked at the contract document, at the probe's orbital plot, at the camera. The calculation running behind her eyes was visible to me the way most of her calculations were visible — not the content, but the motion of it, how she looked when she was weighing costs and timelines.

"Eight hours," she said. "If you haven't caught a burst by then, we go without it."

"Eight hours," I agreed.

She went back to the contract. I turned every passive sensor I had toward a small dark object in a high orbit, and I waited, and I thought about what it would mean if the burst, when it came, was addressed to someone who already knew the name *Nova Drift.*

---

The burst came in four hours and nineteen minutes.

Tight-beam, compressed, aimed at a relay point several light-seconds away that would bounce it to wherever it was going. I caught the edge of it — not the content, the encryption was military-grade and I had no key — but the header frequency and the relay destination.

The header frequency matched an Empire military monitoring format that existed in the *Nova Drift's* old system documentation, buried in a classification index that the original ship's logs had recorded without context.

The relay destination pointed toward a system called Vethara Station.

Where the *Graceful Ruin* had been registered.

I sat with that for a long time.

The *Graceful Ruin's* crew had been carrying military-grade encrypted data cores from an unknown source to an unknown contact who had gone silent. The ship had been parked in K-302 by a contract posted through a shell company. Two ships had been waiting to intercept the salvage crew. And now a monitoring platform with Empire military header frequencies was relaying data to a station that was the *Ruin's* home port.

Each of these facts was explicable in isolation. Together they formed a shape I couldn't quite resolve, like a diagram drawn in a language I'd only just started learning.

I told Zara.

She listened to all of it without interrupting, sitting still in the pilot's chair with the close attention she gave to things that had weight.

"Vethara Station," she said, when I was done.

"Yes."

A long silence.

"I know Vethara," she said. Her voice had changed slightly — not dramatically, the way voices change in films, but in the way that someone's voice changes when they've stepped onto ground they know and don't want to be on. Flatter. More controlled. "It's Empire-adjacent. Not a military installation, but close to one. The kind of station that does a lot of legitimate commerce and a certain amount of other things that aren't documented." She paused. "I've done work there. Before."

"Before when?"

Another pause. Longer.

"Before I was a scavenger," she said.

I waited. The fan in the environmental control unit cycled. Someone in a berth two arms down started a cutting tool, the vibration traveling through the station's structural members and into my hull as a faint intermittent shudder.

"I was Empire military," Zara said. "Intelligence branch, technically, though the work was less refined than that description suggests. I flew missions for them for four years." She looked at the camera. Her expression was the careful neutral of someone presenting information they'd rehearsed presenting, which meant it had come up before, in her own mind, and she'd decided the shape of it. "I stopped three years ago."

"You deserted," I said.

"I completed my service contract and declined renewal," she said, precisely.

"Zara."

A beat.

"I left a mission unfinished," she said. "The definition of that depends on which side of the paperwork you're reading from. The Empire's side says I deserted. My side says I made a decision that their paperwork didn't have a form for." She crossed her arms, not defensively — more like she was holding the story together from the outside. "I've been in fringe space since. Far enough from Empire reach that the classification of my departure is mostly academic."

"What was the mission?"

She shook her head, not refusing exactly, more like declining. "Old history. Not relevant."

"It might be relevant," I said carefully. "If Vethara Station is both your former employer's adjacent territory and the destination of an Empire monitoring relay and the home port of a ship that was carrying encrypted data cores — those might be connected things."

"They might be," she said. "Or it might be a coincidence of geography. Vethara is a hub. A lot of things go through there." She uncrossed her arms. "Alex. I'm telling you this because the connection you found is real and you needed the context. I'm not telling you it means anything yet."

"Okay," I said.

"Okay," she said, and there was something in it — relief, maybe, or the exhaustion of having carried something that was now slightly lighter. "So. We take the escort job. We move. And if the Vethara connection becomes something concrete, we deal with it then."

"And if the Empire monitoring platform has flagged us?"

"We won't know until someone acts on it." She stood up. "Which is exactly why we don't sit here waiting to find out." She moved toward the crew quarters. "I'm going to sleep. Four hours. Wake me for departure prep."

I watched her go. I watched the corridor camera as she moved through it, the specific familiar cadence of her in my corridors, six weeks of her being aboard having made the sound and weight of her a baseline I now registered as normal in the way I registered the hum of my own power grid.

She'd told me she was Empire military. She'd told me she'd left a mission unfinished. She'd told me she knew Vethara.

She hadn't told me what was on the data cores.

She hadn't told me what Dresh had recognized when he looked at her — because the way he'd identified the Venture-class hull specs wasn't just expertise, it was the expertise of someone who had catalogued the vessels that came through that kind of work. And Zara had known he'd sold them something useful without asking why, which was the kind of knowledge that came from knowing the rules of a specific game from the inside.

I wasn't accusing her of anything. I genuinely wasn't. I had a folder full of unconnected facts and a commitment to not building accusations on coincidence, and I was honoring both.

But I noted the direction the Vex-class had gone.

I checked the Aether signature's bearing.

Both pointed toward Vethara Station.

And I ran Minor Repair on the last nine percent of my hull — slow, quiet, methodical, the warmth of it threading through the final micro-fractures from the debris field and the *Bane's* impact — and I thought about what it meant to trust someone with the things you needed them to know and hold everything else in a closed fist, and whether the fact that both of us were doing it was a problem or just the shape of who we both were.

**HULL INTEGRITY: 99 → 100/100**

Whole, finally. For all the good it would do.
