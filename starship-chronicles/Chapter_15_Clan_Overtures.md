# Chapter 15: Clan Overtures

---

**Chapter Summary:** The escort payout clears in a quiet fringe system, and a scavenger clan called the Ashwalkers makes contact — reputation travels fast when you've outrun a Hunger event. Their matriarch Voss proposes a joint operation on a derelict buried inside a dense nebula pocket: their intel, the *Unsinkable II*'s capability. The job turns serious when a structural collapse traps Zara inside the wreck and triggers a cascade failure. In the dark, with Aether burning through continuous repairs and no clean options, Alex and Zara have the conversation they've been building toward for weeks. She gets out. The clan tech integrates as shields. And one offhand comment from a clan member about Vethara Station lands in Alex's already-crowded folder with the heavy certainty of a fact that connects too many other facts.

The escort payout cleared six hours after we delivered the haulers to their contracted arrival — a quiet fringe system called Mav's Junction, which had a name suggesting history and an actual population of approximately four hundred people who ran a fuel depot and asked no follow-up questions. Twenty-eight hundred credits, negotiable having landed at the higher end of the range because *Haul Seven's* operator was apparently capable of gratitude when his ship arrived intact and his cargo didn't get inspected by an Empire vessel with a functioning sensor array.

We didn't dock at Mav's Junction. We took on fuel from a tanker drone at the edge of the depot's service range, paid in the *Meridian Cross* fragment's financial identity, and held position in the outer system while Zara slept and I ran the numbers on where we stood.

Forty-eight hundred credits total. Minus Port Perilous expenditures, minus fuel, minus the consumables we were working through. Comfortable. Not comfortable enough to stop working, but the comfort of having a margin between us and the kind of desperation that made bad decisions look reasonable.

I repaired the hull while she slept. One point, quickly, the warmth of it settling the electromagnetic stress fracture in the port maneuvering vane back into proper alignment. Small, satisfying, the closing of an open item.

**HULL INTEGRITY: 99 → 100/100**

Then I turned my attention to Aether Sense and let it run wide, the way I'd been doing reflexively since the upgrade — a passive awareness of the space around us, like leaving your hearing on rather than actively listening. The fringe system was quiet. No Hunger signatures. No engineered Aether. The faint distant signal in the direction of Vethara had strengthened marginally since K-302, or I was getting better at reading it, or both.

I let that sit and watched Zara sleep and thought about what she'd said in the debrief after the corridor.

*Is it possible someone in your former unit knew where to find you because someone told them?*

*Yes.*

She'd said it with the directness she used for facts she'd decided I was owed. Not an accusation of herself. Just a confirmation that the topology of the situation included that possibility, the same way a map includes roads you haven't traveled. She'd looked at me and said it and then said *okay* and meant it as a door closing rather than one opening — not refusing the subject, just ending this particular pass through it. We'd deal with it when it became concrete.

The problem was that everything about it felt increasingly concrete, and I wasn't sure whether I was building a case or building a suspicion, and I had noticed that the two felt identical from the inside until the moment one of them turned out to be right.

I ran a Minor Repair on a stress weld in the forward cargo section instead. It needed it. The Aether cost was three units and the result was solid and I went back to thinking about something more productive, which was the shield situation.

Shields: offline. The *Nova Drift's* shield emitters had been on my repair list since the beginning — not the beginning of the arc, the beginning of me, the first time I'd looked at my own systems in K-281 and found only red indicators and a strong thematic resemblance to a tin can. The emitters required a power draw I hadn't been able to sustain in the early months and a component integration that needed hardware I hadn't been able to source. I had the power now. I still didn't have the hardware.

One of those would change today.

---

The contact came in on a frequency I didn't have registered — not a Syndicate channel, not commercial, not military. Something older, a band that my system documentation noted had been common among independent operators in the fringe sectors before the Syndicate standardized communications in the region. Someone who knew the old bands, which meant someone who had been working out here for a while.

I woke Zara.

She read the message in the same thirty seconds I'd spent on it. A voice contact request from a ship called *Stubborn Persistence,* registering as an independent with no affiliation marker. The message text was two sentences:

*We know what you are and what you did at K-281. We have a proposal that benefits both parties. Respond if interested.*

"They know what I am," I said.

"They know what happened at K-281," Zara corrected, reading it again. "Which is not the same as knowing you're a Core, necessarily. A ship that outran a Hunger event and then dissolved an Empire drone in a transit corridor — that's a specific profile. Someone's been paying attention."

"That's either an intelligence network or very good passive monitoring."

"In the fringe? It's gossip." She sat up fully. "Scavenger networks run on information. Someone saw what happened at K-281 — probably picked up the same monitoring data we didn't know about — and it circulated. Two ships escaped a Hunger event, one of them had unusual capabilities." She looked at the camera. "The *Meridian Cross* cover doesn't mask what you can do. It just masks what you are."

"There's a distinction."

"A critical one. To most people, a ship with an Aether conversion beam is strange. Dangerous, maybe. Worth being cautious around." She paused. "To someone who knew what Cores were, it would be more than that. This message—" she tapped the pad "—says *what you are,* not *what you did.* That's interesting."

I'd noticed that too.

She opened a response channel. "Let's find out what they want."

---

The matriarch of the Ashwalker clan was a woman named Voss, which might have been a given name or a surname or something that had collapsed into neither after enough years of being the only name she needed. She appeared on the comm display as a face that had been shaped by decades in low-gravity environments and the specific economy of someone who had never had the luxury of an unexpressed thought. Somewhere past sixty by human baseline standards, though the years sat differently on her than they would have on someone who'd spent them in standard gravity. Her eyes were the unremarkable brown of someone who hadn't bothered with modifications, which in the fringe read as a choice rather than a default.

She looked at our camera feed — Zara's face, visible; my presence, not — for a moment before she spoke.

"You're running a fake transponder," she said. "The *Meridian Cross* is a dead identity — the actual ship decommissioned two years ago. Decent cover for automated scans. Not enough for someone who checks." A pause. "I checked."

"And you called anyway," Zara said.

"Because what you did in K-281 and in the Sela corridor are things the *Meridian Cross* couldn't do. So the ship is something else." Voss's expression didn't change. "I'm not interested in reporting you. I'm interested in whether you're interested in a joint operation."

"Depends on the operation," Zara said.

"There's a derelict." Voss leaned back slightly. "In the Sela Fringe, about three hours from where you're sitting. Dense nebula pocket — the kind the gas columns close around so completely that it reads as empty space from outside. We found it eight months ago and we've been working it in pieces, but there's a section we can't reach. The approach corridor is too tight for any of our ships." A pause. "Yours would fit."

"What's in the section you can't reach?"

"A defensive systems bay. Shield emitters, partially intact, rated for a vessel in your class range." Voss's eyes were steady. "We've been keeping it as a trade chip. This is us trading it."

Zara looked at the camera — not at the display, at the camera, at me. A fractional glance that asked a question I didn't need to answer aloud because the answer was obviously yes, we needed shields, and Voss knew that because anyone paying attention to the *Unsinkable II's* encounter profile would know that the ship had weapons and no defensive systems, which was a gap that would matter eventually.

"What do you want in return?" Zara asked.

"The rest of the cargo bay. It's outside the tight corridor — we could reach it but the extraction is heavy work, more than our people can manage safely alone. You help us pull it, we split the yield, you get the shield hardware at no additional cost." Voss tilted her head. "And we get an answer to the question we've been sitting on since K-281."

"What question."

"What happened to the carrier." Her voice was flat. "The monitoring data we pulled showed a Hunger event. Significant one, ate most of the field. But the ship that escaped—" She looked at the camera directly, not at Zara. At me. "The ship that escaped had capabilities that didn't match anything in the current catalog. We want to know what we're dealing with."

The cockpit was quiet.

"You're asking about me specifically," I said. Through the speaker, in my own voice, not the navigation-system register.

Voss didn't flinch. If anything, something in her expression settled, the way things settle when a suspicion is confirmed. "Yes," she said.

"I'll answer what I can," I said. "Some things I don't know. Some things I know and won't share. You'll get the parts that help you understand what you're working with."

"That's more than I expected," Voss said, and it was the closest thing to warmth her face offered. "We'll transmit the coordinates."

---

The Ashwalker clan ran six ships of varying size and vintage, all of them in the worn-but-loved condition of things that had been maintained with genuine care for longer than their manufacturers had intended. They orbited the nebula pocket's outer boundary when we arrived — a region of dense violet gas that my long-range sensors read as empty but my Aether Sense read as faintly warm with the mineral richness of a system that had been accumulating undisturbed for a long time.

I liked them immediately, which I noted as a bias and tried to correct for.

Their people came out to meet us in EVA suits with the practiced ease of a group that had spent years working in void, and I watched them through the external opticals and Zara's suit camera as the introductions happened — eight of them, different ages, different modification profiles, the kind of crew that formed in the fringe over years rather than being assembled for a specific purpose. A young man with the banded temple implants I'd learned to read as neural lace, running what I estimated was the most recent generation of hardware. Two women who moved with the subcutaneous precision of someone with skeletal reinforcement — not military-grade, the working kind, joints and spine augmented for the repeated physical stress of heavy EVA extraction work. An older man who kept his hands in his pockets and said nothing and whose eyes moved over me in the particular way of someone who had studied ship classes professionally.

That last one I kept an eye on.

Voss herself came aboard — through the airlock, into the cockpit, which I felt the way I always felt Zara's presence and had not yet felt anyone else's: a new weight in my corridor, new footsteps on my decking, a new heartbeat in my biosign range. She looked at the cockpit without comment, the way people looked at spaces they were filing away for later reference.

She looked at my core housing camera — the cockpit one, the one that was closest to my eyes in any functional sense — and she said, "Thank you for letting me in."

It was such a simple thing to say. I didn't have an immediate answer for it.

"You said you knew what I am," I said.

"I said I knew what you were. Past tense, technically." She sat down in the spare seat — there wasn't one, she stood against the bulkhead, and I dimly registered that I should probably think about crew seating at some point — "I knew about Cores. Not from public record. From an old woman in a dead station who had access to archives that the Council thought they'd destroyed." She looked at my housing sphere through the interior camera. "You're the first one I've seen."

"Have you been looking?"

"Not specifically. But I know what to look for now." A pause. "The Aether conversion beam. The repair capacity. The way the ship feels occupied rather than operated." She turned to Zara. "You knew when you found him?"

"When she found me," I corrected. "She was more competent about it than I was."

Zara's expression moved in the way it moved when she was choosing not to look pleased about something. "He had a gun pointed at his core for most of our first meeting."

"I had my own reasons," Zara said.

"You didn't shoot," I said.

"No," she agreed. "I didn't."

Voss looked between us with the expression of someone reading a document they'd already summarized. She didn't comment on what she'd read.

"The derelict," she said. "Shall we."

---

The dense nebula pocket was exactly what Voss had described — a region where the gas columns had closed around an older drift of wreckage with the slow patience of cloud formation, sealing it from external view. Inside it, my sensors worked better than they had in the Sela corridor: the gas here was less ionized, cooler, more like fog than plasma. The interference was a degradation rather than a blinding.

The derelict was a station, not a ship. Small — maybe forty meters in diameter — the kind that got deployed as a survey relay and then abandoned when the survey found nothing worth developing. This one had been hit by something at some point in its past, the impact damage clear in the buckled section of its outer ring. The Ashwalkers had been working the accessible sections for eight months, extracting what they could, and the marks of their work were visible: cut sections, carefully levered equipment, the methodical archaeology of people who knew what they were doing.

The tight corridor was on the station's opposite side — a maintenance channel that ran through the inner ring, wide enough for a suit but not for any of the Ashwalkers' ships. Wide enough for me, barely, if I retracted the secondary sensor array and went in nose-first at minimum thruster output.

"You're sure about this," Zara said, from her seat.

"No," I said honestly. "But the math says I fit."

"The math says you fit with four meters of clearance on each side. That's not the same as being sure."

"Four meters is generous in mining terms."

"We're not mining." She was already pulling on the suit — the good one, the mechanic's suit from Port Perilous — with the focused efficiency of someone pre-empting the next argument. "I go in first. Clear the approach. You follow on my position data."

"Zara—"

"I'm not sitting in the cockpit while you thread a needle blind." She sealed the helmet. "Camera's live. You'll see everything I see." The suit feed came online in my sensor array, her perspective joining the rest of what I could see. "Ready?"

The inside of the maintenance channel was a thirty-meter tunnel of original station alloy with the accumulated grime of decades and the Ashwalkers' careful lighting rigged along the upper curve — a string of portable emitters that turned the passage amber and made the shadows absolute. Zara traversed it in four minutes on the thruster pack, moving slowly, her scanner sweeping the walls for stress points and structural irregularities.

"Clear," she said, at the far end. "Shield bay is through the hatch on your eleven o'clock. Alex, the channel is exactly what Voss said — you've got room, but the middle section has a compression buckle in the upper wall. Two meters wide at the pinch point." She held her scanner up. "I'm measuring four point one meters across your beam at the secondary array housings."

"I retracted the secondary array."

"I know. That's what gives you four point one." A pause. "Come in slow."

I nosed into the channel.

The sensation was deeply strange. I'd been in the asteroid field, in open space, in traffic lanes and berth arms, but this was different — the walls of the channel visible on both sides simultaneously, their proximity registering in my hull sensors as a constant low-level awareness of *near things.* Like walking down a corridor with your arms out and feeling the walls without touching them. The proximity alarms I'd configured wouldn't trigger until contact was imminent, but the Aether Sense was reading the station walls at close range and the warmth of their mineral composition was on both sides of me at once, a new and peculiar experience.

*This must be what claustrophobia feels like,* I thought. *Except I have sensors telling me I fit, which should make it better, and somehow doesn't entirely.*

I eased through the middle section at minimum thruster output, the compression buckle in the upper wall visible on my dorsal opticals as a convex intrusion that passed forty centimeters above the training rig housing. Close enough that the Aether Sense registered the material composition of the station ceiling.

I fit.

"You're through," Zara said.

"I noticed," I said.

The shield bay opened ahead — a chamber about fifteen meters across, the station's original defensive emitter array mounted in a circular installation along the inner wall. Half of it was gone, cut away at some earlier point by someone or something, the edges of the damage old and cold. The remaining half was intact.

// COMPATIBLE TECHNOLOGY DETECTED: DEFLECTION SHIELD EMITTERS (PARTIAL ARRAY) //
// BLUEPRINT ANALYSIS AVAILABLE: 5 AETHER //
// WOULD YOU LIKE TO ANALYZE? Y/N //

"Found them," I said.

---

What happened next happened fast enough that I filed the sequence as a single event and only pulled it apart in retrospect.

The Aether analysis was running — five units, the familiar warm bloom of blueprint acquisition — when my hull sensors registered a sound they had no business registering in vacuum. A low, resonant groan transmitted through physical contact between my hull and the station wall.

Then a series of cracks, sharp and sequential, like a spine failing joint by joint.

The buckle in the maintenance channel hadn't been a fixed deformation. It had been load-bearing, a structural member that had been holding a section of the outer ring in tension for years, and the passage of my mass through the channel — predictable, calculable, the margin I'd accepted as adequate — had been the final variable in an equation that had been building for a long time.

The outer ring began to fold inward.

Not catastrophically. Not immediately. But the station was settling, the way old things settle when the last support gives, and the maintenance channel was settling with it, the four-meter clearance I'd measured from static geometry becoming three meters and then two and a half as the walls moved toward each other in the slow, inexorable way of structural collapse that had momentum but no urgency.

"Zara," I said.

She was already moving. She'd felt the vibration through her boot contacts before I finished her name, suit thruster firing, pushing her toward the shield bay hatch. "I see it. Can you back out?"

I ran the geometry. The compression buckle was now below my minimum clearance in the upper wall, the channel narrowed from both sides. Backing out would require a maneuver I didn't have the space to execute. "Not the way I came in."

"Other end of the channel?"

"The station's inner ring. If it's still intact." I was already scanning. "It is. There's an emergency access point on the far side — wider than this channel, rated for equipment transfer." A pause. "Two hundred meters through the station interior. I don't know the condition of what's between here and there."

"Go find it." She was through the hatch. "I'll be right behind you."

She wasn't right behind me.

The shield bay hatch failed sixty seconds after she entered — the channel's collapse transmitted through the station's frame, and the hatch's mechanical lock, corroded by decades and the vibration we'd just introduced, jammed in a partially-open position. Not sealed. Not open. Caught at an angle that would admit a suit but not a human in a suit operating a pry tool, which was what the situation called for, because what the situation called for was a pry tool applied from outside.

"Report," I said.

"I'm in. Hatch is stuck at about forty degrees." Her voice was absolutely level. "I have my cutters. Give me two minutes."

"The channel has one minute before the clearance reaches my hull."

"Then you have a problem and I have a problem and we're both working on them." The sound of the cutter came through the suit audio. "Keep moving."

I kept moving.

The station interior was a problem. Not impossible — the corridors were designed for equipment transfer, and the structural integrity varied section by section, each one a separate calculation that my sensors had to make in real time as I pushed through spaces that had been designed for things smaller than me and hadn't been maintained for at least a decade. Twice I hit sections where the ceiling had partially collapsed and I had to back up and find an alternate route. Three times my hull brushed contact with the walls — barely, barely, each time a spike in every proximity alarm I had.

**HULL INTEGRITY: 100 → 99 → 98/100**

And with each contact, the Minor Repair function triggered automatically — I'd set it to auto-run on contact damage, a reflex developed from weeks in the asteroid field — each cycle burning Aether at the precise moment I had the least of it.

**AETHER RESERVES: 83 → 77 → 71/200**

"How are you doing?" I asked Zara.

"The hatch is stuck on a corroded hinge, not the lock. If I cut the hinge I can push it, but I need the angle." A pause. The sound of something being repositioned. "How are you doing?"

"Navigating. Burning Aether on contact repairs. I have time."

"You always have time," she said. "Until you don't."

"That's a general principle, not specific feedback."

"It was general feedback. How is the route?"

"I found the transfer corridor. It opens near the inner ring junction." I ran the sensors ahead. "Forty meters. Some debris from the collapse, but clearable." I was already nudging it aside with careful thruster bursts, the debris dissolving into Aether when the conversion beam touched it — small pieces, minimal yield, but the clearance they made was real.

**AETHER RESERVES: 71 → 63/200**

Tight. The budget was running tighter than I'd planned.

"Zara. The hatch."

"Working on it." A grunt of physical effort. "Alex, if this goes wrong—"

"It's not going to go wrong."

"Alex."

I stopped moving. The debris field was half-cleared. The transfer corridor was twenty meters away. The station was still settling, the groan of it coming through my hull contacts at intervals now rather than continuously, which was either stabilization or the system finding a new equilibrium before the next phase.

"What," I said.

"If something goes wrong," she said, "and you have to choose between getting yourself out and coming back for me — get yourself out."

The cockpit lights — the ones in my own frame that she couldn't see from inside the station, that I'd been running as a passive habit since the early weeks — went a color I didn't have a name for. Not red, not amber — darker than both. The color of a decision being refused.

"No," I said.

"Alex—"

"No. I'm not accepting the instruction." I kept my voice even. It took effort. "We've had this conversation in a different form before and it ends the same way. I'm not leaving you in a collapsing station."

The hatch made a sound. Metal stress, the grinding complaint of something being forced beyond its design parameters. Then a crack, sharp and definitive, and the suit camera showed the angle shift — forty degrees to seventy, the hatch swinging open far enough for a suited figure to fit through.

"Got it," Zara said. "Moving."

I exhaled, which I couldn't physically do, but the relief found some analogue in the way my processing suddenly had room again. I kept moving through the transfer corridor. Twenty meters. Ten. The emergency access point was exactly where the station schematic said it would be, and it was exactly wide enough, and I came through it into open space on the station's far side like something being born, which was perhaps too dramatic a framing for what was essentially a ship reversing out of a space it had gotten stuck in.

Zara was behind me four minutes later, suit camera showing the inside of the maintenance channel as she traversed it in the opposite direction, the collapsed section passable in a suit even as it was no longer passable for me.

She came out of the station's emergency access point and found me holding position twelve meters away and she said nothing for a moment, just floated there in the violet-amber light of the nebula pocket, and then she said: "You went the long way."

"Yes."

"You burned Aether on repairs you could have avoided if you'd moved faster."

"Yes."

"That was—" She stopped.

"Necessary," I said. "It was necessary."

Another silence. The kind she produced when she was sitting with something that didn't fit her usual frameworks. I let it sit.

"I don't have a lot of practice," she said finally, "at people who don't take the exit when they could."

"I know."

"Six years solo. Before that—" She stopped again. The suit camera caught the nebula gas columns shifting, violet bleeding into amber at their bases. "Before that, the missions I flew. The people I worked with. The calculation was always — what's the optimal decision for the operation. People were variables." She paused. "I was a variable."

"And now?"

"And now I'm living in a ship that won't take the exit." She said it the way she said most significant things — dry, minimal, the weight of it carried underneath rather than displayed. "Which I find simultaneously reassuring and profoundly inconvenient."

The cockpit lights shifted — something gold, something close to the color of her eyes in a certain light, not intentional but not accidental either.

"For the record," I said, "I find it reassuring too."

She was quiet for a moment. Then: "I know." She fired her suit thruster toward the airlock. "Come on. We still have a job to finish and Voss is going to want her cargo."

---

The rest of the extraction ran without incident. The Ashwalkers' crew did what Ashwalker crews apparently did — methodical, competent, unhurried — and Zara ran coordination between me and the external team, and the cargo bay yielded what it had promised: mixed station equipment, survey instruments, several crates of sealed materials that Voss's people catalogued efficiently and without explaining, and the shield emitters, separated from the array by Zara's cutter and brought aboard in two pieces that my Aether Sense confirmed were still viable.

// COMPATIBLE TECHNOLOGY DETECTED: DEFLECTION SHIELD EMITTERS (×2, PARTIAL ARRAY) //
// BLUEPRINT ANALYSIS: ALREADY ACQUIRED //
// INTEGRATION COST: 35 AETHER //

Thirty-five. I had sixty-three. Tight. The conversion of the cleared debris had recouped some of what the contact repairs had cost, and the Ashwalkers' split of the cargo bay had an Aether component — compatible equipment I ran through the beam on Voss's instruction, with her agreement, which gave me eight units from pieces that weren't worth transporting physically.

**AETHER RESERVES: 63 → 71/200** *(+8 Ashwalker cargo conversion)*

Twenty-eight units remaining after shields. Enough for minimal operations. Enough to make it to somewhere I could top up.

I ran the integration.

The shields came up slowly — not the dramatic flare I'd seen in films, nothing cinematic. More like a system coming back online after a long dormancy: the emitters seating into the housing I'd cleared for them, the power coupling engaging, the field generators running their calibration cycle with the querulous energy of hardware that had been inactive for years and was making sure everything still worked before it committed to anything.

Then, finally, a new indicator in the status display that had never been green before.

**SHIELDS: ONLINE — FIELD INTEGRITY 34%**

Thirty-four percent was a long way from full coverage. The partial array meant gaps, the hardware was old, the calibration would need refining. But thirty-four percent was thirty-four percent more than I'd had this morning, and the first time I registered it I felt something that I didn't have a precise word for — not pride exactly, not relief exactly. The feeling of a gap in your awareness closing. The quiet satisfaction of a system that had always been offline finally, simply, being on.

"Shields are online," I said.

Zara looked at the indicator from the pilot's chair. "Thirty-four percent."

"It's a start."

She looked at it for another moment. "It's a start," she agreed.

---

Voss came back aboard for the debrief, as promised, and I answered what I'd said I'd answer. The K-281 Hunger event — what it had been, how we'd escaped, the mechanics of the Aether conversion and what it had done to the carrier's power core. She listened without interrupting, her expression doing very little, which I'd come to read as attention rather than disengagement.

When I finished, she said: "The Hunger events are increasing."

"We've seen reports," Zara said. Carefully.

"Not reports. I've seen three in the last eight months. We've been in this region that whole time. One in K-281, which you know about. One in the Sela Fringe, smaller, contained itself before it grew. One two months ago in a system three jumps from here that doesn't have a name, just a catalog number, and nobody goes there now." She looked at the camera — at me, specifically, the same way she'd looked at me during the initial contact. "You said the warp presence told you the Hunger destroyed the Cores."

"It conveyed something consistent with that interpretation. I'm not certain—"

"What does it feel like," she said, "when a Hunger event starts? To you, specifically."

I considered. "Wrong," I said. "Like a frequency below hearing that the ear still registers. Like something running in a direction the universe wasn't built to accommodate."

"And the Aether signature," she said. "Can you feel them with the Sense? The events, the potential ones, before they start?"

"I don't know. I haven't been near enough to a confirmed Hunger event since the Sense activated." I paused. "The one at K-281 was before."

"If you could feel them in advance," Voss said, "you'd be the only early warning system anyone has." She said it with no particular emphasis. Just a statement, placed in the room, available for whatever use we chose to make of it.

I sat with that.

"Are you suggesting something?" Zara asked, her voice neutral in the way that neutral voices were neutral when they were being careful.

"I'm telling you what I've observed. You can suggest things to yourselves." Voss stood. "The cargo split was fair. The tech was as described. If you want a communication channel for future operations, I'll leave a contact frequency." She moved toward the airlock. "One more thing."

"Yes," I said.

"There's a station called Vethara." Her voice hadn't changed. Same flat delivery, same absence of theatrical weight. "In the past two months, two scavengers I know have gone there for work and not come back. Not dead — their transponders are still pinging. Just not communicating." A pause, her hand on the airlock frame. "Someone there is building something, and they're keeping it quiet, and they're collecting people with useful skills." She looked at the camera one final time. "I thought you should know."

She cycled the airlock. The station's beacon blinked away on my sensors as the Ashwalkers began their departure sequence.

I kept very still.

Vethara Station. The Vex-class transport's vector. The Empire probe relay. The faint Aether signature I'd been tracking since K-302. Zara's former unit. Two scavengers gone quiet.

*Someone there is building something.*

I watched Zara at the pilot's console. Her hands were on the navigation interface, pulling up our next heading. Her expression was the professional neutral she wore when she was working. She hadn't reacted to Voss's last statement — not with surprise, not with the guarded stillness of someone receiving information they already had, just a small, controlled note-taking quality in the slight tightening of her fingers on the pad.

She knew.

Or she didn't.

I genuinely couldn't tell, and I had very good sensors.

"Where are we headed?" I asked.

"Away from Sela Fringe before the Empire incident report circulates far enough to flag us." She looked at the camera. "I'll plot the details in transit."

"Okay," I said.

I put Voss's information in the folder. The folder was getting less like a folder and more like a wall, facts arranged and connected, waiting for the one thread that would let me start pulling.

"Alex," Zara said, not looking up.

"Yeah."

"In the station. When I said if it goes wrong—" She paused. "I meant it as a practical instruction. Not as—" Another pause. "I don't give instructions I'm okay watching you follow. You understand?"

I did understand. It was the same thing she'd said about trust being a process rather than a switch, just arriving from the opposite direction. *I told you to leave me because I was afraid you'd listen, not because I wanted you to.*

"I know," I said.

She nodded once, looking at the nav console.

The *Unsinkable II* turned away from the Sela Fringe and ran its shields at thirty-four percent and kept its Aether close and flew on.
