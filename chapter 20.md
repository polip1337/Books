Chapter 20: The Ascent
The shaft was fifteen feet across and went straight up like the barrel of a gun pointed at the sky.
Zack grabbed the first rung with both hands, tested his weight, and climbed.
The ice was old and dense, not the brittle surface frost he'd scraped off railings in the lower districts. These rungs had been cast into the wall deliberately, each one a curved bracket thick as his forearm. His boots found the first foothold and he pushed up, then the second, then the third. The rhythm came quickly. Hand, hand, foot, foot. His breath came out in short white bursts that vanished into the dark above him.
Nyx was already thirty feet up, a small winged shape circling in the shaft's dim throat. Her eyes glinted when she looked down at him.
You climb like a man who's afraid of what's below him.
"I'm afraid of what's above me," Zack said. "That's different."
Is it.
He didn't answer. He climbed.
The first ten rungs were manageable. By the twentieth his forearms had begun to burn. The cold didn't help—it seeped through his gloves and turned his fingers stiff, made gripping feel like squeezing through wet leather. He focused on keeping his arms bent, not locking out his elbows the way you did when you got tired and started using bone instead of muscle. He'd done enough chin-ups in his old life to know that the moment you locked out was the moment you started losing.
His old life. Strange phrase. That had been four months ago.
There's a gap ahead, Nyx said. Eight rungs up from your position. One of them is missing.
Zack tilted his head and squinted. She was right. A dark notch in the sequence, the wall there smooth and flush where the rung's anchor had crumbled away. Beyond it, two more intact rungs, then another gap.
He kept climbing until he reached it. Up close, the break was worse than it looked from below. Three rungs gone in a cluster, not one. The wall between them was a bare stretch of black ice, and the nearest solid handhold was almost six feet above his current position.
He hung there a moment, fingers hooked over the last good rung, and did the math. He couldn't jump it—not from this position, not hanging vertical with his feet on a bracket four inches wide. He'd need both hands for the leap and nothing to push off from.
Well? Nyx said.
"I'm thinking."
Think faster. You're burning energy just hanging there.
He was. He could feel it, the slow drain of holding his own weight, the arms that had already been working for three minutes and were now doing nothing useful. He looked at the bare wall above the gap. The ice was smooth, but not glass-smooth. There were grain lines in it, stress fractures from centuries of settling, tiny ridges no deeper than a fingernail.
Not enough to grip.
But he wasn't working with just his hands anymore.
Zack pulled his aura inward, the way Aiko had shown him in those early, frustrating sessions that felt like trying to hear a sound that only existed in his peripheral vision. He felt the warmth settle into his palms—not fire, not yet, just heat organized into intent. He pressed his right hand flat against the wall beside the gap.
The aura pushed outward from his skin in a thin layer, and where it met the ice, the ice gave way. Not melting, not a flood of water—more like pressing a brand into wax. A shallow depression formed under his palm, shaped to his grip.
He made a handhold.
He made four of them, two per hand, working his way up the gap in careful moves, pressing each hold before trusting it, testing depth before shifting weight. The aura drain was real—he could feel it the way you feel the last twenty percent of a phone battery, not gone yet but counting down. When he caught the first solid rung above the gap and let the aura go, he stayed there a moment with his forehead resting against the wall.
Impressive, Nyx said. She didn't sound sarcastic. You're learning to be precise.
"Learning to be desperate," Zack said. "Those are different things too."
Sometimes they're the same thing.
He kept climbing.

The air thinned noticeably above the hundred-foot mark. It wasn't the dramatic altitude sickness of mountain peaks—they weren't high enough for that—but the shaft was narrow and the ventilation was whatever the ancient architects had bothered to account for, which was apparently not much. Each breath felt like it was doing sixty percent of the work it should. He started breathing through his nose on the rests, mouth on the pushes, rationing it the way Rei had taught him to ration stamina in their early training runs before all of this.
Rei. Currently unconscious in an inn bed with her arm wrapped in treated bandages and a pulse that had been stable when they left.
Don't, Nyx said.
"Don't what?"
You're doing that thing where you go somewhere else in your head. I can feel it through the bond. Stay here.
Zack focused on the next rung. "She's fine."
She's stable. That's not the same as fine. And thinking about it up here doesn't help her.
"I know."
Then climb.
He climbed.

The blockage announced itself as a change in the air. The thin cold current that had been flowing past him from above cut off, replaced by still, stale nothing. Zack looked up and saw it: a mass of ice that had collapsed across the shaft at an angle, not a complete plug but close enough. Chunks of the wall had calved inward at some point—old structural failure, the kind that happened to everything eventually—and wedged themselves into a rough diagonal dam maybe twelve feet thick. There were gaps in it, hairline cracks where airflow still barely moved, but nothing a body could pass through.
He hung below it and considered the problem.
Blasting it with fire was the obvious answer and the wrong one. The shaft above might be occupied. The shaft behind him definitely was—he'd feel it if the whole thing came apart.
He needed it narrow. Surgical. A channel just wide enough for a person.
The left side, Nyx said from above, her voice threading down through the cracks. There's a seam running vertically. It's stress-fractured already. You'd be following a line that wants to open.
Zack found the seam by touch. She was right—he could feel the give in it even through his gloves, the subtle wrongness of ice that had already decided it wanted to be somewhere else.
He positioned himself with his feet braced on two separate rungs and his back pressed to the opposite wall. Then he called up the flame.
Not Inferno Step, not a combat burst. He thought of it as a thread—thin, hot, controlled. Something like a torch flame rather than a floodlight. He pressed his palm to the seam and let it run.
The ice groaned. Then it hissed. Then a fissure opened along the vertical line, slow and reluctant, and the left wedge of the blockage tilted—just enough. A gap formed, maybe two feet wide. Cold water ran down the wall in a sheet and soaked his sleeve before he pulled back.
He went through sideways, shoulders angled, his pack scraping the ice on both sides. His left hand found a rung above and he pulled himself through and up.
The air above the blockage was different. Older. He didn't want to think too hard about what that meant.

He was maybe forty feet from the top when the scaffolding moved.
Not the rung under his hands—the scaffolding to his left, a platform the width of a door bolted into the wall at an angle. He'd been using it as a rest stop, one foot on the platform while the other hung free. Then the platform shuddered and he heard something that wasn't structural.
It was breathing.
The Ice Man had been crouched against the wall behind the platform, folded into a space no living thing should have been able to occupy, armored in the same pale material as the others below—Voidstone-threaded crystal plate—but intact in a way the others weren't. No corruption lines. No blackened cracks. Its eyes were closed.
They opened.
They were white. Not blank, not void-black like the corrupted ones. Just white, the iris and pupil both, like something carved from the same ice as the walls.
It moved faster than anything that size should move.
The platform lurched under Zack as the Ice Man unfolded, and for a half second his foot slipped off the edge and he was hanging by one hand on a rung four inches wide, dangling in open space with forty feet of shaft below him and an eight-foot armored corpse standing up three feet away.
The Ice Man raised something—a shortened pike, the shaft broken to half length, still usable—and swung.
Zack let go.
He dropped two rungs before his other hand caught. The pike rang off the wall where his head had been and sent chips of ice spinning down into the dark. He pulled himself back in against the wall, feet scrabbling, and the Ice Man tracked him, rotating with the mechanical patience of something that had been waiting for a very long time and didn't particularly mind waiting a little longer.
The space was the problem. The shaft was fifteen feet across, but between the scaffolding and the wall he had maybe four feet of workable ground. No room to circle. No room to build momentum. If he used a full Inferno Step burst he'd bounce off the opposite wall.
Above it, Nyx said. She was pressed to the wall near the shaft's top, as flat as she could get. The scaffolding bolt on the upper right. It's corroded.
The Ice Man stepped forward. The platform groaned under it.
Zack ignited both hands, not a controlled thread this time but the real thing, and threw himself not backward but forward—up and sideways, angling his body to land on the scaffolding itself, one step to the platform, then immediately up again to the rung above, then the next, two rungs at speed while the Ice Man turned and raised the pike for a downward strike.
He hit the bolt Nyx had indicated with one burning hand on the way past.
He didn't stop to see if it worked. He kept climbing, three rungs, four, and the scaffolding gave way behind him—not a collapse, a controlled tilt, the right side dropping while the left stayed anchored, and the Ice Man went with it, sliding sideways on the angled platform, the pike swinging wide. It caught the opposite wall with one massive hand and arrested its fall, still standing, still functional.
But Zack was already at the top.
His hands found the lip of the shaft—real floor, flat and solid—and he pulled himself through onto his stomach, rolled clear of the opening, and lay there for a moment with the stone cold against his face.
Move, Nyx said, landing beside him. It might be able to climb.
"Give me two seconds."
You have one.
He got up.

The level above was nothing like the temple below. No carved walls, no altar, no architectural intention. This was raw—a broad, open tier that had been part of the city proper, residences or workshops, the specifics long since stripped away by time and cold and something worse. The floor was cracked and the cracks ran in patterns that weren't random, lines radiating from a point deeper in the space like something had been pressing upward from below.
The air tasted like copper and old snow.
And there, maybe two hundred meters ahead through the broken maze of collapsed structures, a glow.
Not the clean blue-white of the tower's ambient ice. This was deeper—a pulse rather than a shine, a rhythmic throb of violet-black light that came and went in a slow, arrhythmic beat. Like a heart that had forgotten what order it was supposed to keep.
Zack stood there breathing, his burned palms still warm, his arms aching from the climb. The glow lit the underside of what remained of the ceiling above, and it lit the mist drifting through the ruins, and it turned everything in the space the color of a bruise.
Nyx landed on the floor beside him rather than his shoulder. She was staring at the glow, and her amber eyes had gone very still.
"The crystal," Zack said.
Yes. A pause. Something's wrong with it, Zack. The sound it makes—
"I can't hear anything."
I know. I can. She didn't elaborate, and the way she didn't elaborate told him the elaboration wasn't going to be good. Her wings folded tight. We need to move carefully.
The glow pulsed again, slow and sick and wrong.
"Yeah," Zack said. He checked his hands. Still warm. Still ready. "Carefully."
He moved forward into the bruised dark, Nyx close beside him, the crystal's light pulling them deeper into whatever the tower had become.
